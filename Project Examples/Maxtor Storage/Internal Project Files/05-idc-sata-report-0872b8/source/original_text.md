I D C  O P I N I O N  
High-capacity, low-cost disks will be welcomed into the server storage pool over the 
next few years as IT executives seek to contain costs by applying storage 
management policies that acknowledge not all data has the same value nor 
performance requirements. 
` IDC believes there is no place in the datacenter for lower availability at the 
application level simply because the storage uses capacity-oriented technology. 
` IDC believes the two most receptive applications for capacity-oriented storage 
are (1) mirrors and snap-shots for data protection as well as for facilitating 
backup to tape, and (2) as repositories for fixed content. 
` IDC believes that capacity-oriented technology will be embedded within arrays as 
a class of capacity to be managed through current tools.  Over time, capacity-
oriented storage shipments will reach a quarter or more of the storage array 
terabytes shipped. 
` IDC believes there is not currently enough space for multiple classes/tiers of 
storage products based on SATA or capacity-oriented disk drives.   Therefore 
this report analyzes the market as a single entity or tier. 
` IDC believes capacity-oriented disk storage poses little near or medium term 
threat to tape.  In fact the larger the disk capacity, the more data and the faster it 
must be transferred to tape for archival purposes.  In some cases, however, 
tape's role will be refined to strict archiving policies with little to no nearline role 
whatsoever. 
` IDC believes that lack of effective information management software, and 
concurrent lack of information policies in most organizations will be the major 
inhibitor for the aggressive adoption of tiers or classes of storage.

I N  T H I S  S T U D Y  
This study presents the results of fifty North American in-depth, one hour interviews 
conducted in October and November 2003.  The interviews were conducted directly 
by two of IDC's senior analysts.   The topic was use of storage classes or tiers in an 
environment where capacity-oriented disks were available for Storage Arrays.   Ten 
vertical markets including: banking, energy, financial services, health, insurance, 
manufacturing, 
media/broadcasting, 
pharmaceutical, 
transportation, 
and 
wholesale/retail were represented in the interviews.  Forecasts for High-capacity, low-
cost arrays and capacity-oriented disk drives for these arrays are included.

Charting the Path of Digital Storage and Information Evolution 
Hardware Consolidation 
We find that there is a stable dance within organizations wherein the business units 
and functional groups are constantly proliferating applications, servers and storage in 
support of their business mission (as well as the proliferation of infrastructure from 
acquisitions, mergers, and simply restructures within an organization).  Responsibility 
is frequently turned over to the IT function as the investment  is complete and the 
application moves into the production phase of its mission.  Thus, faced with constant 
proliferation, the IT function is in a constant  state of consolidation.  Consolidation is 
the potential savior for IT, reducing touch points and frequency of touch points and 
providing ever higher levels of service with fixed or reduced staffing. 
For the past decade, first monolithic, multi-host storage arrays (Including NAS)  and 
then Storage Area Networks (SANs) have been the IT investment focus on the 
supplier promise of solving much of the costs and pains associated with huge storage 
unit and capacity expansion.   
The largest storage consumers have several years of SAN success under their belts.  
Now that the basics of a common storage pool are functional and stable, users are 
looking to the next steps in ringing value out of their storage infrastructure, and 
dealing with continued capacity expansion.  
Generating Value from Stored Data 
The companies IDC interviewed depended on IT technology throughout its functions.  
Most were well underway to fully integrating suppliers and customers into the 
computer-based business model.

The growing applications were typically from generating and capturing more 
information as business is conducted and then doing extensive data mining on the 
data to tune business intelligence.   
In certain industries computer-generated data – health radiology (Health), seismic 
analysis (Energy), and ASIC semi-conductor (Manufacturing) circuit design was the 
heart of the business-value being created.   These organizations tended to have the 
largest storage capacities to manage.  Assigning a terabyte to a project or application 
is not unusual. 
Data Protection and Disaster Recover 
We found two strategies in place for data protection.  The more common was the 
result of a Business Impact Assessment (BIA) in which all applications were classified 
into a small set of categories.  Each category had a target restoration time.  Typically 
the most critical had a four hour recovery goal.  The second tier had a multi day 
recovery goal and the third tier did not have a specific goal.  The other strategy was 
for a super tier of continuous operation through a remote active-active datacenter and 
constant data synchronization.  Only the most crucial applications and most resource-
rich organizations invested to this level.  The active-active tier did not have a recovery 
process in that it was expected to never fail at the application level. 
Four-hour recovery is a goal generally obtainable only if recovery is from a disk 
snapshot or mirror.   We found many organizations already were staging data to disks 
for both rapid restoration and managing backups with small and non-existent backup 
windows. 
Datacenter Management 
Storage continues to grow in relative importance in the data center.  IT managers are 
benefiting substantially from SAN and NAS technology, however storage continues to 
be too people intense to manage.  The general expectation is that additional layers of 
software will slowly move storage management back into the background and IT 
managers are so budgeting.   
Where we are in the Evolution Process and Why this Research is 
Needed 
We believe that tiered storage, using capacity-oriented disk drives is a step on that 
path.  ILM, or Information Lifecycle Management is another step.  The bigger strategic 
goal is what we might call "Information Integration" – the re-structuring of an 
organization's information fully enveloping what is now called middleware, and 
document management, among others for a fully integrated/consolidated information 
infrastructure.  One organization we interviewed claimed to be there.

This section provides key findings of the research and a quick summary of the 
situation. 
Users Open to and Knowledgeable about Storage Classes 
Large organizations are seeking ways of continuing to gain efficiencies from their 
storage infrastructure under tight budgets and growing capacity needs.   Multiple 
classes of applications and classes of storage already exist in most organizations, 
having been identified from their efforts around backup, and disaster tolerance.  Most 
organizations with any history or substance have identified clearly two to four tiers of 
their IT infrastructure.  We note that application tuning on current storage arrays 
creates embedded classes or tiers of storage.

Lack of Clear and Enforced Policies is an Inhibitor 
We found our interviewees managing classes of storage within a serious constraint 
that has most organizations without active information management policies.  
Everyone considers their data to be most important.   Neither policies nor incentives 
are in place.  In fact, more than a few companies were considering implementing 
charge-back models for storage in order to curb end-user consumption.  The only 
products on the investment horizon that will make a dent in this inhibitor are the 
document management applications being largely installed for compliance objectives. 
Compliance Requirements are Setting the Stage 
The organizations with clear and active tiering and compliance efforts are those with 
long term compliance efforts.  These are the same organizations which must keep 
much of their data forever and must regurgitate it nearly instantly.  The document 
management programs will likely be the first source level of metadata to implement 
true data tiering and information life cycle management.   
Next Stage for SAN Exploitation 
Functional SANs are now three years old and more.  Our interviewees are actively  
looking to get better value from them.  We found many who were actively 
transforming their enterprise one-size-fits all storage strategy.  Tier two applications 
were being migrated to appropriate storage arrays from what are known as modular 
mid-range products.  Several noted increases in performance and no degradation in 
reliability by shifting to mid-range technology.  The continuing cost pressures makes 
most organizations very interested in capacity-oriented storage.   
Storage Software is Lacking 
Our interviewees praised storage suppliers for meeting their performance and 
reliability needs with today's hardware.  They nearly universally lamented the lack of 
corresponding storage software.  Nearly all expect improved software products and 
are expecting to devote increasing fractions of their storage budget to gain the 
results.   This lack of software will be an inhibitor to tiered storage that is not low-
hanging-fruit with obvious demarcations of  data classes and applications. 
Products Should Integrate Capacity and Performance 
Our interviewees were consistent in desiring a capacity storage class within the 
arrays they are now buying.  A few want separate products because of early buyer 
caution, but most place a very high value on continuing with their current storage 
supplier and are looking to have seamlessness with their storage software too, 
whether the drives are performance or capacity oriented.  
Capacity Does Not mean Giving Up Reliability 
Capacity-oriented storage does not mean unreliable storage.  The users want no 
compromise in data protection or application reliability to gain the cheaper capacity.  
IDC suggests designs that accommodate two drive failures per disk rank without 
application impact.

Market is Moving Now and Can Grow toward 50% of New Capacity 
A few percent of server terabytes are already parallel ATA-based.  Users report 
satisfactory reliability experiences.  They report a willingness to spend 14% of their 
2004 array money on capacity oriented arrays.   As they look to their future needs, 
they see nearly 40% being over served by today's high-performance storage typically 
based on SCSI and Fibre Channel disk drive technology.

Pressure Points 
Changes in Data Being Generated and Stored  
In most organizations the ability to create and store new data is taken as an unlimited 
right.  There are little to no constraints or policies about data creation, its agreed 
importance, and its time-line of importance.  As a result, our interviewees are faced 
with continuous and excessively expanding data storage requirements.  Supporting 
this has served the disk drive and storage systems suppliers in every year providing 
more-and-more storage capacity for less cost.  It has not served IT organizations well 
and represents a major storage market business opportunity. 
A careful examination of most organizations data stored will uncover substantial 
abuse of this apparent unlimited data capacity. 
` Personal files of documents, images, music and video 
` Replication of a specific file many times over, even when shared storage 
technology is provided 
` Old and obsolete versions of data with no or negative value 
` Data that is finer grained than required, thus taking excess capacity 
` Data that is refreshed (versioned) faster than is needed or useful 
Our interviewees note that their users now have the ability of creating 
vast amounts of data very easily.  Imaging, video editing, data mart analysis, are  but 
three that are the tip-of-the-iceberg.   The problem is that of this created, stored and 
protected data, less-and-less is believed to be really important and less-and-less is 
believed to even ever be accessed or used. 
The  current  range  of software products from the storage industry is a set  of  tools  
operating  levels  below the real business-level needs. Most IT executives we 
interviewed believe they are making reasonable use of the available data  
management tools and are managing the life cycle of stored data as well as can be 
done under current circumstances. 
What  is missing are the policies and procedures at management level that are  a  
prerequisite before technology tools or solutions can be applied to the  
infrastructure level.  Many organizations have active Document Management 
Systems being rolled out.  These may well be the upper level software in the tack that 
provides the missing meta-data to fully inform automated tiered storage and 
information lifecycle management implementation.

Changing Relationship of Storage Technologies 
Our interviewees noted a number of shifted relationships.  Sometimes these were 
explicitly acknowledged, at other times it was more implicit: 
` Disks and disk storage arrays have become very cheap.  Cost of operation 
and backup far exceeds the disk element cost itself. 
` For recovery, mirrored data centers for non-stop operation of the very most 
critical data. Recovery off disk snapshots for mission critical and recovery off 
tape for the rest. 
` Optical has taken a big hit in large and extra-large organizations.  A very few 
organizations plan on keeping current levels of optical technology or increasing 
their dependency.  Disk-based technology has or will take over. 
` Tape is rapidly shifting to archive use only.  In a few cases we found 
organizations thinking about sending archive tapes to the repository less often. 
The Nature of Disk Drives Requires Data Tiering 
IDC believes that today's large Ent-class disk drives (36-147GB capacity) are 
frequently internally tiered unbeknownst to end users.   The tuning done by monolithic 
storage array suppliers, in part, are actions to place hot virtual LUNs on a few of the 
outside tracks of the physical disk rank.   Less intensely accessed virtual LUNs are 
placed on the inside tracks of the disk.   The data transfer rates vary by 2 to 1 and 
more from these adjustments.  Greater acknowledgement of this existing tiering of 
data would tend to accelerate the adoption of more purposeful tiering at the drive-type 
level and forward the acceptance of capacity-oriented drives for storage arrays. 
Information Life Cycle Management Status 
We asked our interviewees about Information/Data Life Cycle Management.  Nearly 
all were able to articulate classes of applications and hence classes of storage 
capacity.   Many organizations had completed a formal process called Business 
Impact Analysis (BIA) for classification.   Typically we found stored data was  in one 
of four classes of restoration time frame:  
1. 
0 hours (not all organizations invested for this class)  to restore –  
2. 
N hours – typically about 4 
3. 
N days – typically 3 days 
4. 
All else – these applications had a best-efforts time line with no specific goal 
Within these protection classes, most of our interviewees reported generalized 
policies of (ILM) data migration, movement, and discarding.  The vast majority of 
policies were the result of legislated requirements.  Where there were no legislated 
requirements, many of these same executives lamented the inability to implement 
ILM due to the politics of everyone holding their data as most important and everyone 
holding that their data needed to be continuously online.

It would seem that in addition to enabling technology, successful Information Lifecycle 
Management will depend on senior level leadership at the CIO level and/or the 
leverage of legislated compliance-based mandates. 
Compliance – Where and What is the Impact 
Compliance programs for IT-based data have been in existence forever in vertical 
markets such as Finance, and Pharmaceuticals.  In these verticals we found 
compliance for Sarbanas-Oxlay well underway (if not complete).   In all other 
industries we found limited and half-hearted efforts described.  There was frequently 
described an initial effort followed by a wait for further clarification. 
An important consideration for IT suppliers to understand is that compliance initiatives 
do not, in general, originate within IT or the storage management function.   It was 
clear from our interviews that the IT and storage management function had a 
peripheral role in the entire process; did not generally see specialized storage 
requirements for compliance; and were generally in a response-mode to others. 
This research did not have as its purpose to fully understand how compliance 
decisions were being made and implemented.  However, we believe that serious 
compliance efforts include the existence of a Chief Compliance Officer and that 
frequently compliance requirements are initiated and managed under the CFO. 
Our interviews did uncover several interesting impacts that both HIPPA and 
Sarbanes-Oxlay are having: 
1. 
Document Management Systems are being installed as an overarching tool 
2. 
Manual business processes are being automated to capture data instantly at 
source 
3. 
IT infrastructure is being added for rapid (2 hour) recovery/retrieval of old 
material 
We found no interviewee who believed that the amount of data stored would decline 
because of compliance (Though one company had instituted a two week, then 
destroy mandate for all email as a knee-jerk limit liability response).   The typical 
range of increase in storage requirements was 30-60%. 
We also note that other geographies have their own sets of compliance rules and 
therefore  the markets around compliance will  vary with geographic region.

Storage Applications Most Likely to Integrate High-capacity, Low-cost 
Solutions 
IDC tested 4 specific applications with the end-users against the appropriateness of 
high-capacity, low-cost storage usage:  General purpose server/applications, Data 
protection, Fixed-content management, and Temporary or Scratch storage.  Each 
company was generally quite decided on which applications would or would not be 
appropriate for these lower-cost storage solutions. 
The assumption given to the end-user that the storage system would be leveraging a 
high-capacity, low-cost disk drive with slightly lower performance (7,200 RPM) and 
reliability (in terms of MTBF), yet possessed a compelling cost savings characteristic 
(in terms of $/GB – general 1/2 to 1/3 TOC savings).  While some interviewees chose 
not to believe the savings would be realized from a TOC perspective, most based 
their decisions on the assumptions as stated. 
We will address each application as presented in the interview.

F o r e c a s t s  f o r  C a p a c i t y - O r i e n t e d  S t o r a g e   
High on everyone's list of outcomes for this study is the estimated impact of lower-
cost storage on the storage system market, as well as on the component markets that 
ship into the storage system market, specifically, hard disk drives. 
Although the 'raw' results of the survey would intimate a fairly aggressive 2004 
spending (14% of all storage dollars) on high-capacity, low-cost storage, IDC believes 
that these results represent an 'ideal' or a 'perfect world' scenario.  In reality, we 
believe that such results should be tempered significantly based on a few important 
tendencies and realities that exist within the market place: 
` In corporate IT, there is always a strong tendency to lean towards status quo 
rather than to adopt 'fundamentally different' technology.  IDC believes "ATA in 
the enterprise" is a fundamental departure from the SCSI/FC status quo.  The 
'newness' of SATA frequently was mentioned as a concern regarding the 
adoption of SATA. 
` The legacy installed base of both SCSI and Fibre Channel disk drives in the 
enterprise is enormous in comparison to ATA and reinforces our belief that IT 
managers will lean towards 'status quo.' 
` Adoption of SATA almost always was based on similar availability and 
redundancy schemes that are ubiquitous among today's high-performance 
(SCSI/FC-based) systems.  Integrating such requirements could prove costly 
enough to motivate end-users to default to spending on legacy-type solutions 
(read SCSI/FC). 
` A resounding theme and inhibitor to tiered storage was the lack of availability of 
appropriate and efficient software management tools.  IDC believes that the 
software solutions offered today are indeed sub-par with respect to end-user's 
expectations.  This dynamic will undoubtedly hinder the implementation of tiered 
storage and adoption of high-capacity, low-cost systems.  
This is not to say that we disbelieve the intentions of those interviewed, but instead 
interpret their intentions with a conservative posture that we believe provides a 
realistic expectation for adoption of tiered storage and high-capacity, low-cost storage 
systems.   
With these conditions in mind, we proffer the following forecasts, Storage system and 
Disk Drive (Table 14 and 15 respectively), as IDCs most likely adoption scenario of 
high-capacity, low-cost storage.  Please note that in both forecasts, the assumption 
was that SATA would be the choice for high-capacity, low-cost systems.  This is not 
to say that other interfaces couldn't be used, but IDC chose SATA for simplicity sake.

Forecast Assumptions and Implications 
Associated with any forecast is a list of assumptions that impact the size and 
magnitude of any trend.  Please note the following assumptions and qualifications 
with the above forecasts: 
` ATA/SATA is assumed to be the preferred interface for high-capacity disk drives 
` The implication of a moderately growing non-ATA (read SCSI/FC/SAS) HDD unit 
forecast, especially in years 2006 and 2007, is well-noted.  Please consider the 
following qualifications with respect to this increase. 
 The overall external TB forecast is assumed to grow above 60% per year 
from 2005-2007.  This posture is based on historic trends and the lack of any 
evidence suggesting a departure from such trends.  Relaxing the expected 
growth rate would obviously soften the HDD unit forecast.

Linux Plans 
Part way through our interviews, it occurred to the interviewers, that the application of 
Linux was possibly a corollary infrastructure of Tiered storage.  That is, it was a 
hypothesis that organizations with aggressive adoption of Linux technology might be 
more open to capacity oriented storage than those not. 
We have analyzed the interview data and the results show neither a significantly 
higher percentage of storage array capacity being over-served nor are the absolute 
terabytes over-served correlated to the aggressiveness of Linux adoption.  
Hypothesis not proven. 
SAS Awareness 
Our interviewees, despite generally being interested in tiered storage, and up on new 
storage technology had virtually no awareness of the forthcoming companion to 
SATA technology, SAS. 
Of those we asked (34), 24 had never heard of SAS, 8 had heard of it, but couldn't 
relate it to SATA, and only 2 correctly understood it as a serial SCSI companion to 
SATA.  It was also confused with the legacy SSA technology by several interviewees. 
Storage industry suppliers will need to initiate major marketing campaigns to have the 
combined SATA/SAS  value proposition understood.

E S S E N T I A L  G U I D A N C E  
This section provides IDC's recommendations, guidance and other actionable advise. 
Warrantee – Smart Buyers 
The storage buyers we interviewed are initially put off over the prospect of a one year 
warranty.  However, even most of those who were unhappy at first, understood the 
fact that a product has to have a single warranty period.  Nearly all expected to make 
comparisons for capacity-oriented products with an added three or 5 year support 
plan/warranty priced in when comparing them to current enterprise products.  A one 
year warranty on capacity-oriented disk dries will not be a showstopper. 
Stigma of Low Reliability 
There is no market for compromised reliability or performance at the application level.  
Storage systems incorporating capacity-oriented disk drives will need to surround 
them with RAID and cache technology.   We would recommend initial products be 
designed to withstand two drive failures in an array rank without application 
interruption.  Data protection should remain during a failed drive rebuild. 
Failure Rates of Disks 
Eighty percent of the market is not phased by a probably increase in disk failure rates 
in capacity-oriented disk drives.  The other twenty percent see it as a problem for one 
of several reasons: 1.) the will make no compromise in quality at the component level; 
2.) they perform self-maintenance and do not want the additional interaction; 3.) they 
have remote storage locations that are not staffed and would incur support problems.  
Fail Over Requirements a Must 
Availability must be high at the application level.  This implies redundancy in 
controllers and links. 
Education is Required 
The combined value proposition of SATA and SAS together is unknown.  Most 
storage managers are not aware of the SAS technology.   
Integration into Current Products 
The biggest market for tiered storage is for classes of capacity within the storage 
arrays now being sold.   Only a minority wish separate array products.  Many of the 
latter category wish it initially as they are unsure of the technology's reliability.   The 
major issue for having multiple storage tiers within a single array is the desire for 
common and integrated management. 
Storage Software is Lacking 
The interviewees we spoke with were as satisfied with their storage hardware as they 
were dissatisfied with their storage software.  While this is not new, we believe that 
suppliers will find it useful to read the transcript portions regarding storage for both a

Organizations to Avoid 
We have identified, from our interviews, classes of organizations that will be late 
adopters (if ever) of capacity-oriented storage infrastructure.   We suggest avoiding: 
the insurance industry; public sector organizations without oversight or competition;  
companies in highly profitable niche markets without cost or revenue pressures.  The 
companies we interviewed in these categories were not only relatively uninterested in 
our capacity-oriented technology story, but even hostile. 
Organizations to Seek Out 
The better opportunities for capacity oriented storage technology are in general any 
organization with growing storage requirements and under competitive cost and 
pricing pressures.  This is most of the world.  Especially favorable environments 
include those markets that have large compliance requirements where they have to 
keep everything forever and be able to regurgitate any portion instantly.  They are:  
Financial;  Transportation, and Pharmaceutical.   We also note the research and 
development (Scientific Workload) portions of manufacturing and the analysis 
applications of the upstream petroleum companies in the Energy market are great 
capacity-oriented target markets.

Goals of Research Project 
Our goal was to simply deliver high value to our sponsors. This meant obtaining rich 
data to inform forecasts with "why".  It also meant having the ability to evolve and 
tune interviews based on learnings. 
We also wanted to be able to provide mid-project, top-line results and deliver final 
results in timely manner. 
Our core methodology was to use 50 in depth executive interviews with a very high 
qualification criteria.  We also hoped to pioneer a web-based con-joint process post 
each interview to provide a scientific basis for understanding subtle differences in the 
different needs and values of multiple applications for capacity-oriented storage 
systems. 
Screening Criteria for Interviewees 
We sought organizations in IDC's large (1,000-9,999 employees) and extra-large 
(10,000 + employees).  The final mix was in nearly equal proportions.   All 
interviewees had to qualify with more than 10 terabytes of enterprise storage 
capacity.  We also required them to have a Storage Area Network, and have heard of 
ATA.

The theory was that we were looking for organizations with leading edge needs and 
pain.   We were hoping to find interviewees who were already thinking about 
classifying their storage, and interested in discussing it.   We had great interviews. 
The other part of the selection was the belief that the needs of the very high end of 
storage consumers would trickle down to smaller IT organizations as average 
capacity continued to increase.  By interviewing  the biggest and brightest we would 
get a preview of what would be happening more broadly over the next five years. 
Pre-interview Data Collected 
Our methodology included a generous honorarium for participating.  We also required 
potential interviewees to complete three complex tables prior to the telephone 
interview.  These resulted in unprecedented details of each organization's storage 
workloads and installed capacity.  It also included details on the type of storage 
environment for each workload, and the speed of the disk drives for each workload.  
The second table forced each interviewee to access the degree of on-lineness of their 
overall data.  The  third table required the potential interviewee to identify the top 
several applications causing capacity growth and to estimate the rate of growth.  
Finally each interviewee received the interview guide well in advance of the actual 
interview and therefore had time to think about their answers and to gather additional 
data for questions where they were not personally informed. 
The result is an extraordinary level of quality in the data and a massive and broad set 
of information. 
Why Analyst Directed the Interviews 
Having gathered much of the quantitative data ahead of the telephone interviews, we 
wanted the telephone interviews to be in-depth.  We wanted to be able to follow up on 
statements and pursue interesting insights.   
These goals were only possible with deeply knowledgeable and skilled  interviewers.  
Our very expensive choice was to directly do interviews as a two-person team.  While 
one analyst at a time conducted the questioning, the other analyst listened and closed 
open and confusing aspects with clarifying follow up questions. 
Though the Interview guide was piloted, the initial interviews lead to some slight 
changes which were mostly additional questions near the end. 
Post Interview Conjoint 
We asked each interviewee to participate in a web-based con-joint process to 
distinguish differences in storage systems requirements for the four horizontal 
applications: general, protection, fixed data, scratch.  That effort has not yielded 
results at this time and we are pessimistic for the future.
