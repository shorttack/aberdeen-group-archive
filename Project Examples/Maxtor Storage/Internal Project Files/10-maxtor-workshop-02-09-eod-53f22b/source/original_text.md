challenges and opportunities:

Aberdeen’s ILM Recipe: Order is Critical
1.
Centralize (each) datacenter’s storage
•
NAS/SAN core storage over time are the way to go
•
Storage management economies on this core
2.
Create Pools of Storage on 3 Axes
•
Data: structured (DB); semi-structured (e-mail); unstructured (Medical images)
•
Use: active; inactive / fixed; changeable
•
Data Life Model: online, midline, nearline, offline
3.
Create life-cycle policies
•
Start with Existing storage management products/policies, particularly smart backup/restore
•
Add new ILM-oriented hierarchy-based products/policies
4.
Populate new/rehosted applications on Pools of Storage
•
Assumptions: (1)every application platform (hardware, app rev, etc.) changes within five 
years; (2) no forced migration before its time
•
New standard IT process for new/rehosted platforms mandates Pools of Storage 
implementation
•
Accelerate compliance-mandated app rehosting to Pools of Storage
5.
Drive economies of scale
•
Increasing homogeneity (e.g., storage in a box)
•
ILM policies become more sophisticated
•
Keep data in the most economic place for its “age”
•
Key Performance Metric: Operators (and DBAs)/TB of storage
6.
Implement Complex ILM-based storage (circa 2008)
•
Storage-related labor hours on production systems reduced by 80%
•
Storage allocation and reclamation works a lot like main memory
•
Full legal and audit compliance sign-off on automated ILM policy management

Strategy Risk: Danger Lies in the 
Misapplication of ILM
•
Defining ILM broadly, although correct, may confuse buyers 
as vendors try to include everything under the ILM umbrella 
and frustrate them as not all the necessary products and 
services are going to be available for some time
– In this case the term ILM is discredited and storage suppliers 
will lose the attention of buyers if they continue to try and 
perpetuate the term
•
Defining ILM too narrowly would simply lead to “old wine in 
new bottles” that would tend to sell specific existing (or soon 
to be released) products, but would divert buyers from the 
need to put in place processes that would require a range of 
products and services that would lead to much greater value
– In this case ILM “succeeds”, but is trivialized.
•
Conclusion: ILM is too big to fail on a definition

Aberdeen’s Recommended Approach to ILM 
— Maintain Balance
•
Laying out the vision of ILM as a roadmap for the future 
helps gain consensus and buy in so that enterprises can 
start putting the planning wheels in motion in the right 
direction
•
Focusing on an aspect of ILM that is realizable now and in 
the near future can enable IT organizational focus and get 
moving in the right direction with immediate tangible 
benefits
•
Aberdeen believes that this focus should be on Pools of 
Storage
• Pools of Storage is a concept that can be easily grasped
• Unlike ILM software, Pools of Storage can be implemented now
• Storage suppliers — as a whole — have to focus on using a 
common lexicon if they want buyers to be able to focus
• Pools of Storage has a value proposition that extends beyond 
the cost savings implicit in TCO and ROI

What the Market is Saying
•
Organizations are likely to consider an ILM program
– Only 15% have done so now, but another 41% said that they 
will consider in it the future [We have to make sure the 
future is now!]
– About a third (33%) answered that they have not considered 
such a program and 11% don’t know
•
The Pools of Storage concept is not a foreign one
– 36% said they were familiar, 50% said they were somewhat 
familiar, and only 15% were only a little, if any, familiar
•
However, organizations need a push to get them to actually 
adopt a Pools of Storage concept
– Only 19% said they were very likely to implement Pools of 
Storage in 2004, 43% said they were somewhat likely to, and 
38% said that they were unlikely to.
