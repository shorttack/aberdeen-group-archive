Preface

Value. That is what information technology (IT) executives demand from
their investment in storage — as well as the rest of the IT infrastructure.
Although IT budgets are thawing a little, 60% of the respondents in a
recent Aberdeen survey stated that managing storage within the current
budget and headcount constraints is one of their critical success factors
in managing the storage infrastructure.
Yet the rapid growth of storage results in increased stress on the storage
infrastructure. This stress translates into increased management
complexity, higher administrative costs, and the need to make sure that
storage assets are used more effectively.
To deal with storage stress, enterprises are planning a number of
initiatives — such as storage consolidation, revamping the current
backup/restore process, implementing or expanding a storage area network
(SAN), and implementing or expanding network-attached storage (NAS).
Enterprises must justify the resulting capital investments in storage
through a formal financial process. The Aberdeen survey shows that total
cost of ownership (TCO) and return on investment (ROI) remain highly
popular components of the necessary financial discipline to determine
investments.
Reducing storage stress and maintaining financial discipline play very well
into the strengths that Dell brings to the storage market. For years, Dell
has been recognized for its business model that relentlessly pursues a path
of constantly improving price performance. Dell then returns those
improvements in the form of increased functional value to its customers.
Storage is no exception. This Profile describes how Dell is going about the
process of adding value for its storage customers.

Executive Summary

Dell not only supplies business storage customers with disk storage systems
from its ongoing Dell|EMC partnership (well received by the market), but
also fulfills other key storage solution requirements with both network
attached storage (NAS) and data protection solutions, featuring both disk
and tape. Moreover, Dell delivers the necessary service and training to
support the customer’s storage hardware product and related software
investment.
Dell also recognizes that the needs for business storage extend beyond the
data center (where a Dell CX700 would typically reside) to reach to the
department (with, for example, the CX500) and on to the workgroup level
(with, for example, the CX300 and the AX100).
The one constant across Dell’s product range and organizational reach is
the application of principles derived from Dell’s supply chain management
(SCM) approach. Among these principles are three fundamental Dell
strategies — continue to deliver industry-leading value, drive standards,
and move high-end functionality downstream, from the high end to the
workgroup.
Dell’s ability to continuously deliver industry-leading value through
continuous improvement in the price/performance ratio of products is built
into its “DNA.”. Dell believes that passing along that value to customers
gives it a sustainable competitive advantage. Like other suppliers, Dell
takes advantage of inevitable declines in raw storage prices over time and
manufacturing learning curves. Dell’s key ongoing advantages over these
suppliers have been superior SCM techniques and the ability to quickly pass
cost reductions along to the customer.
Dell is making a big push to support IT-based standards. Among the
organizations that it is working with in storage are the FCLC (Fibre
Channel Loop Committee), Serial ATA II, Serial Attached SCSI, and SNIA
(Storage Networking Industry Association). As a for-profit organization,
Dell focuses on the business value that comes from a “standards-based
virtual feedback loop” driving improved technology and reduced costs. Each
new evolutionary level of standardization enables new users to drive an
increase in market size; market -size increases lead to new technology
refinements and declining costs; and technology refinements lead to the
next level of standardization — just what Dell wants to achieve for its
customers and the industry.
With a strong focus on delivering value to its customers, Dell continues to
work collaboratively with EMC to deliver enterprise-class features
throughout the entire Dell|EMC CX product family. Typically, new storage
functionality, such as SnapView, the point-in-time copy software for the
Dell|EMC CX product family, tends to be initially offered at the high end
of the product line (aimed at data centers). With Dell|EMC, this type of
functionality is offered from the entry CX product (CX300) through the high-
end (CX700) offering.
Combined with Dell’s ability to rapidly drive down prices, enables Dell
customers tocan afford the new functionality that much sooner. For storage
software, standardization allows mainstream user administrators to handle
the new features that much more rapidly.
The net result is continually improving storage product value for
customers.

At the core of the Dell storage product offerings are the fruits of the
Dell|EMC partnership (Figure 1). These solutions include modular Fibre
Channel (FC) disk arrays that are the storage building blocks of the IT
infrastructure. The focus is to deliver FC storage that supports the basic
business processes of the enterprise on a SAN.

A New Generation Takes Center Stage

In 2004, the new CX300 (focused on workgroups), CX500 (targeted at for
departments), and CX700 (designed for data centers) replaced their
predecessors — the CX200, CX400, and CX600, respectively. This third-
generation set of products from the Dell|EMC partnership demonstrates the
ongoing relationship’s ability to deliver investment protection. As an
example, when upgrading from a CX200 to a CX300, a customer only has to
replace only the disk controller on the existing array — thus paying only
for the new controller, not a whole new array. Moreover, there is no need
to migrate data from one disk array to another, which usually causes bottom-
line-impacting downtime.
With the new generation, storage buyers get more bandwidth and hence
greater performance for the same price — the CX300, the CX500, and the
CX700 are is 70% faster, the CX500 is 100% faster, and the CX700 is 25%
faster, respectively, than theirits predecessors — added value that shows
up in TCO and ROI calculations as the ability to avoid unnecessary
purchases of servers, software, and storage in order to scale. Many other
storage suppliers reactively pass on technologies leading to improvements
in price/-performance; Dell proactively drives even faster
price/performance improvements.

Adding Midline Storage to the Storage Mix

Midline storage (ATA disks with higher capacity, lower cost, and lower
performance than FC disks) is now available for Dell|EMC disk arrays. The
traditional online pool of storage uses FC disks for applications requiring
the highest performance, such as revenue-producing online transaction
processing solutions. The new midline storage pool can store fixed content,
which needs reasonably fast access, but does not have the same performance
demands as traditional online storage pools. Aberdeen research shows that
obtaining satisfactory performance at less cost is emerging as a major
buyer trend, which is satisfied by and the new midline products are
satisfying this demand.
Moreover, enterprises can also use midline storage also in the nearline
pool of storage that has typically been reserved for tape automation. Disk-
to-disk backup and continuous data protection techniques all lead to faster
recovery of key information — and therefore bottom-line-improving higher
availability.
A Dell|EMC array with ATA drives requires several FC drives to house the CX
product family’s FLARE operating system. The rest of the drives can be a
mix of ATA and FC drives. A combination of the two can allow the ATA drives
to be used for less expensive point-in-time copies.

AX100: Midrange Fibre Channel Meets Serial ATA

The latest addition to the Dell/EMC line is the AX100, a product concept
that Dell conceived by Dell to meet its customers’ needs for easier to use
Fibre Channel system, at an even lower price point than the CX line. The
Dell/EMC AX100 storage system can be used in a SAN (with a new Brocade
switch offered by Dell) or directly attached to servers.
This powerful entry-level array scales up to 3 TB on SATA drives, yet can
be easily set up with wizard-based tools. Furthermore, the solution
includes all the software that a customer needs to manage the deployment,
automate failover, and provide backup. Redundant features, such as dual
controllers and mirrored cache, ensure high availability for critical data.
Large companies with many remote sites and small organizations recognize
the availability and performance benefits of implementing SANs, but have
been deterred by the associated high complexity and cost. Dell has helped
addressreduce these concerns by partnering with storage industry leaders,
such as EMC, Brocade, and Qlogic, to create a solution that is
significantly lower in cost, easily installed by the customer, and scalable
with business growth.

Making Change Happen

The ability of Dell and EMC to work together has resulted in several
practical benefits for storage buyers. As EMC’s experience has helped Dell
to better serve the higher end storage customer, Dell’s experience has
helped show EMC how the small and medium-sized enterprise storage segment
can benefit from the Dell|EMC class of products. The CX200 (and now the
CX300 and AX100) came about through Dell’s work with EMC to recognize the
needs of the workgroup for this class of products.

Getting the Most out of Storage through Software

Dell works with leading storage management vendors to cover the broad
storage management needs of enterprises, including backing up/restoringe,
configuring and provisioning storage, and monitoring the health and status
of storage systems. Dell offers products from well-known software vendors
with long -established reputations, but also from newer companies whom that
Dell has qualified to meet its high standards for delivering products that
it can recommend to its customers. Of course, Dell partners with EMC on CX
family software products, such as Navisphere for device management and
SnapView and MirrorView for point- in- time and mirroring data replication
functionality, and for Legato software. BecausSince one size of software
does not meet the needs of all organizations, Dell aims NSI Double-Take at
data replication for smaller configurations.
Further evidence of Dell’s focus on reduction of complexity can be seen in
Dell’s SAN management software. Dell offers VisualSAN — an intuitive
network management software package that discovers, visualizes, and
monitors servers, switches, and storage systems. With a Web-based user
interface, managing SAN configurations, assets, and device status is a much-
 improved process. Dell|EMC VisualSAN customers also can have the ability
to leverage Dell Device Managers — a free, downloadable plug-in that
provides enhanced component instrumentation for Dell devices and
integration with Dell OpenManage server management software.
For backup/recovery software, Dell features Legato Networker. Dell also
offers Yosemite TapeWare for workgroups, VERITAS BackupExec for
departments, and VERITAS NetBackup Enterprise Server for the data center.
Dell recently added CommVault Galaxy to its backup/recovery software
portfolio to provide features that scale from departments to the data
centers.
Two key ways that Dell uses its business model for storage management
software are (1) simplifying the process of buying the software for the
enterprise through bundling, and (2) simplifying facilitating the use of
the software through tighter product integration. As a result of Dell’s
direct business model, end-user feedback is driven into future product
requirements for Dell’s software partners.

Bundling

Bundling is not new, but Dell views bundling through the lens of how it can
reduce the “friction” and complexity of the purchasing process. Many
storage software products have numerous model numbers, e.g., stock- keeping
units (SKUs)that vary by number of servers, processors, storage capacity,
and other variables. The result is confusion and complexity for the IT
buyer in deciding not only what to buy to meet today’s needs, but also how
to ensure that the storage management software purchases will accommodate
storage growth and storage infrastructure changes in the near future
without requiring a trip back to the budget well.
Bundling not only reduces the number of stock keeping units (SKUs) that a
customer has to evaluate, butit also reduces deployment complexity by
ensuring that all software components are included for the solution. Dell
bundles are based upon its experience with a large number of storage
customers. Bundling also makes more complex solutions available to broader
audiences.
Bundling is budget-friendly in terms of overall cost. Dell’s considerable
market volume opportunity for software vendors, combined with fewer SKUs to
manage, results in lower costs to the customer.

Tighter Product Integration

Another important way to simplify the use of multiple storage management
software products is through tighter product integration, which can be
visualized as minimizing the number of “consoles” with which an IT
administrator has to work. Each individual product has its own console, a
graphical user interface (GUI), by which an IT administrator can control
the running of the software. Moving closer to a single window to the world
through one interface console that links multiple products makes it easier
to manage and reduces the learning curve.
Not having to switch between consoles reduces “context switching” between
the look- and- feel differences of multiple GUIs as well as reducingand
lowers the number of facts that storage administrators the need to remember
too many facts — such as when similar words have a different meaning in two
products and what set of actions is necessary to bring about a desired
result. Dell is able tocan drive different storage software vendor’s’
products to work together as a single console because Dellby providinges a
compelling business opportunity as a reward for such cooperation. At the
same time, Dell continues to work with storage standards organizations to
define a common API and command line interfaces that allow software vendors
to easily integrate storage and server consoles.
The net result of bundling and tighter product integration is that the Dell
business model delivers price/performance for software as well as for
hardware.

Service — Go for the Gold

Dell strives to develop tools that can deliver a good level of service at
lower cost. For example, performing management functions remotely from a
Dell command center using a WebEx session is sometimes faster than sending
someone on- site. Dell also continues to devise metrics to allow analysis
and therefore reduction of the time to resolve a service problem, e.g., as
a metric to measure the efficiency of a problem diagnosis and metrics to
measure the efficiency of the escalation process. Whereas many suppliers
might be daunted by the thought of significantly improving the service
process, Dell views it as an opportunity to apply its SCM techniques.
That opportunity carries over to improving the multi-vendor problem
resolution process. Every storage system has multiple vendors thatwho have
a measure of responsibility for overall IT satisfaction. When an IT
organization has a storage-related problem, it wants a swift, constructive
resolution to the problem — not time- consuming and unproductive finger-
pointing between two or more vendors.
Dell emphasizes a partner engagement process with third-party vendors who
that agree to participate. This tight service integration provides
structured processes and agreements that drive multi-vendor accountability
and mutual commitments that improve customer experience.
Vendors thatwho agree to participate derive the market benefits of being a
key Dell technology partner. Dell and its partners execute Dell Service
agreements that cover timely execution guidelines, on- site service level
agreements, defined escalation management policies, and global performance
metrics. Dell is involved real- time in partner-identified issues;, as
Dell is notified of a problem at the same time that the partner’s support
organization is made aware of itnotified. Moreover, Dell support teams will
receive enhanced training from partners where applicable.
Problem resolution is important, but preventing problems from affecting IT
organizations in the first place improves IT satisfaction. A centralized
Enterprise Command Center that enables Dell to reach out electronically to
monitor and to take action from a distance prevents and solves manya lot of
potential problems without disrupting the performance and availability of
IT applications.
Finally, avoiding problems before they occur is a cost-effective approach
to improvinges efficiency and increasing customer satisfaction as well as
being less expensive. Dell does a root cause analysis for each problem
incident and keeps a historical database of incidents. Dell can spot
problems and then that lead to proactively carrying out preventive
maintenance.
Dell has an edge over many of its competitors in that it knows where all of
its storage systems are because since it sells directly. That knowledge is
useful for determining where the systems are that require preventive
maintenance.
The net result is that service is amenable to the Dell business model — and
Dell can deliver increased efficiency, customer satisfaction, and shared
cost savings.

Feeling the Power of PowerVault NAS

Having numerous file servers with dedicated storage scattered over a
network tends to result in problems — and not only underutilized disks. For
example, each server may require a tape drive. Thisat is not only
expensive, requirement but results in an administrative-time-intensive
burden for managing the backup/restore process. Moreover, scaling to meet
storage growth adds disproportionately to the expense and administrative
burden. With products such as Dell PowerVault NAS, consolidating file
storage centrally not only improves not only disk utilization and
scalability, but also enables a tape solution that reduces the
backup/restore process’s administrative burden.
The Dell PowerVault NAS family consists of the PowerVault 775N and 770N,
both of which to act as central file servers in the midrange, and the
PowerVault 745N, to which acts as an alternative to file cabinets,
microfiche, or optical jukeboxes at the entry -level. Windows-based systems
using CIFS (Common Internet File System), Macintosh systems using AFP
(Apple File Protocol), and Unix-/Linux-based servers using NFS (Network
File System) can access a PowerVault NAS.
All PowerVault NAS platforms use Microsoft Storage Server, which is based
on the Windows 2003 Advanced Server operating system. The use of Microsoft
Windows Storage Server 2003 instead of a proprietary NAS operating system
illustrates why Dell adamantly Supports supports standards — IT
administrators who use the latter face a learning curve, whereas very
little to no learning curve is required for using the formerthere is little
or no IT administrator learning curve compared to a proprietary operating
system. Moreover, Windows Storage Server inherits the extensive features of
Advanced Server, e.g., a rich set of server utilities, support of Active
Directory, and clustering solutions.
PowerVault NAS servers can support additional capacity through the
connection of a dedicated disk storage array. Furthermore, the two midrange
products (PowerVault 775N and 770N) can connect directly to Dell|EMC FC
storage arrays, and both can act as a gateway to SAN storage through an FC
switch — delivering scalability, flexibility, and investment protection.

Data Stored Is Data That Must Be Protected

Protection is aA fundamental requirement for any business data is that it
must be protected. Dell offers a wide range of tape products under the
PowerVault brand to meet these needs.
Recognizing that legacy tape media formats are still in use, Dell will sell
individual tape drives in a number of tape formats. For its tape automation
products, Dell limits the choices to DLT (Digital Linear Tape) derivatives
and LTO (Linear Tape Open) derivatives. Dell offers the PowerVault 122 tape
autoloader and two tape library solutions in the PowerVault 132T and 136T
tape libraries to address the entry- level and midrange markets. Dell also
offers an enterprise-class tape library solution with the PowerVault 160T.
In keeping with its standards focus, Dell offers only LTO-2 tape drives on
the PowerVault 160T, and in the future, Dell is likely to emphasize LTO
technology going forward on its other tape libraries. For backup and
restore software in the enterprise, Dell offers the choice of Legato
Networker, CommVault Galaxy, VERITAS Backup Exec, or VERITAS NetBackup
Enterprise Server.
