Aberdeen’s IT Professional Services Practice 
The Purpose and Goal of this Practice Area
The purpose of Aberdeen’s Information Technology (IT) Professional 
Services Practice is to assist Information Systems (IS) buyers to select the 
best professional services suppliers to meet their specific information 
processing needs.  Aberdeen helps IS buyers determine the evaluation 
criteria for choosing a professional services partner, as well as provides 
comparative analysis that enables IS buyers to compare and contrast the 
various services offerings of multiple services suppliers.
Aberdeen’s expressed goal is to streamline and simplify the decision making 
and selection process  enabling IS buyers to establish partnerships with 
best-in-class services suppliers.  To do this, Aberdeen provides IS buyers with 
in-depth analysis of a particular supplier’s strategic intent, market 
positioning, product and services offerings, and comparative strengths and 
weaknesses.  By so doing, Aberdeen hopes to provide services buyers with a 
clear understanding of a given professional services supplier’s level-of-
commitment and ability to execute in the particular practice areas 
mentioned above.  And by so doing, Aberdeen hopes to reduce the amount of 
time that IS management must devote to evaluating various suppliers and 
choosing the appropriate services supplier partner.
Structure
Aberdeen’s IT Professional Services practice has been structured in a matrix 
fashion: first, by key industry market initiatives; and second, by the type of 
professional services supplier (an example of this matrix can be seen in 
Figure 1).  Aberdeen has structured its professional services practice in this 
manner in order to enable IS managers to choose a supplier by: 
1) The kind of expertise and level of commitment a given professional 
services supplier can exhibit in a particular solutions field-of-endeavor 
(such as infrastructure or application-specific deployment); as well as by
2) The type of services supplier. 
The reasons for this structure are that IS buyers generally seek professional 
services to solve specific design, deployment, and/or management problem 
related to systems infrastructure or specific application initiatives, and IS 
buyers generally look to certain types of professional services organizations 
for those services.  For instance, an IS manager may need assistance in 
locating a supplier of Customer Information Systems solutions  and that IS 
manager may turn to the Independent Software Vendor (ISV) or an Original

Key Market Initiatives
Aberdeen surveys indicate that the most sought-after IT professional 
services areas-of-coverage include: 
1. Windows 2000 Infrastructure Services  services related to the design, 
deployment, and management of Windows 2000-based (also known as 
Microsoft NT) servers and desktops;
2. Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) and related Supply Chain 
Professional Services; 
3. Customer Information Systems (including Sales Force Automation and 
Call-Center) as well as related Data Knowledge; and,
4. Education (including classroom, computer-based, and Internet-based 
instruction).
Types of Professional Services Suppliers
Aberdeen segments the IT professional services marketplace into four 
distinct supplier types:

Independent Professional Services Suppliers (IPSPs)  Formerly 
known as systems integrators, this segment now does far more than work 
on systems/application deployment and systems interoperability issues.  
IPSPs can often provide business strategic planning services, 
infrastructure services, and systems/applications design, deployment, and 
ongoing lifecycle management services (including outsourcing).  
Examples of IPSPs range from Andersen Consulting, through Computer 
Science Corporation and Electronic Data Systems, to niche services 
suppliers like Tenfold and Keane.

Independent Software Vendors (ISVs)  Makers of software products 
are now becoming far more aggressive in the professional services space, 
building workforces capable of helping to deploy and tune a given 
software supplier’s suite of product offerings.  Examples of ISVs that have 
now established large professional services organizations include SAP, 
Baan, Microsoft, and Computer Associates.

Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs)  For the most part OEMs 
have focused on providing infrastructure-related services (such as 
network and systems design, applications deployment and tuning, 
lifecycle management), but many are now actively engaged in providing 
outsourcing services and some are involved in providing business 
strategic planning services.  Examples of OEM’s that provide basic 
infrastructure services include Hewlett-Packard, Compaq, IBM, Data 
General and the like.  IBM also provides business strategic planning

IBM’s Global Services: IBM Gets It
Preface
IBM gets it.  They understand that packaged solutions deployment and 
ongoing customer service  not hardware features/functions  have become 
the basis for competition in the commoditized computer platform world. 
The commoditization of systems platforms (Windows, Unix, AS/400, NT) has 
been great for IS buyers from a hardware acquisition perspective  the cost 
per transaction minute has dropped from almost $300 a minute three years 
ago on Unix and NT platforms to less than $50 today.  But commoditization 
has also created a void  there is a worldwide shortage of qualified IS 
professionals available to address issues related to deployment and lifecycle 
management of commodity platforms.  Likewise, there is a dearth of trained-
and-experienced consulting/service expertise that can assist IS management 
in the deployment of new Internet-based transaction processing, decision 
support, mail-and-messaging, workflow, and Enterprise Resource 
Management/Supply Chain applications to name a few. 
IBM has addressed this worldwide skilled IT labor problem from two 
perspectives:
1. IBM has built a very large professional services and support organization 
(numbering more than 110,000 skilled professionals) and has deployed 
this workforce in 164 countries across the globe.  IBM was early to 
identify the growing need for skilled IT labor  and has a considerable 
advantage over its competitors in terms of people and worldwide 
coverage; and, 
2. IBM has strategically focused its resources on resolving issues related to 
IT infrastructure deployment/integration, as well as on providing turnkey 
Electronic Business (E-business) application solutions.  In particular, IBM 
has built expertise in systems/applications integration as well as in the 
deployment of certain applications solutions (described in depth in 
section three  Core Products and Services).
In this Profile, Aberdeen evaluates the professional services offerings 
provided through IBM’s Global Services business unit.  We examine Global 
Services market and competitive position, its core products and services, its 
strategic focus and direction.  And, from Aberdeen’s perspective, we 
conclude that IBM is best positioned as a major supplier of IT infrastructure 
services  especially services related to the deployment of electronic 
business applications across multiple platform types.

Market Positioning
This section discusses IBM’s professional services from a strategic, core 
focus, organizational size, and delivery mechanisms perspective. 
Professional Services  Strategic Positioning
It is rare to find a very large enterprise that can clearly state its identity and 
link all of its products and services together under one, cohesive, unified 
message  a message that says who the company is and what it is trying to 
accomplish.  IBM’s message is “E-Business” (short for electronic business), an 
initiative that focuses on using computer technologies to automate business 
processes and functions  all of IBM’s marketing messages, its product 
strategies, and its services strategies have been designed to support this one 
common, central theme.
Typical E-Business solutions include the products and services needed to:

Conduct commerce electronically (both with consumers as well as with 
external business partners); 

Automate inter-company applications and processes related to mission 
critical functions such as human resources, manufacturing, distribution, 
finance, and sales; and,

Internet-enable mail-and-messaging systems and interdepartmental 
workflow.
But, in reality, E-business can be generally applied to any computing 
function that automates any computing or business process function  
internal or external  within or without the enterprise.

Technology

business strategic planning.  IBM is one of only a handful of professional 
services suppliers that is capable of providing high-quality, in-depth, 
strategic business planning services and corresponding IT design, 
deployment, and management services.  The company’s services strength lies 
in its ability to recommend a strategic course of action  and then follow

solutions  and services play a key role in the delivery of solutions to IBM

Competitive Positioning
Although many would expect IBM’s Global Services to compete regularly 
with other OEM services providers such as Hewlett-Packard and Compaq, 
IBM claims that its primary competitors are Andersen Consulting and 
Electronic Data Systems. 
Like IBM, Andersen Consulting is widely respected for its strategic business 
planning services  and for its solutions focus (solutions such as Electronic 
Commerce, Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP), Extended ERP, etc.).  And it 
is in the area of solutions deployment that these two companies are most 
likely to collide.  IBM has a focused initiative (called E-business) that 
concentrates on automating business functions using electronic commerce, 
ERP, the Internet, and other technologies to streamline business transaction 
processing.  Andersen is also structured both horizontally and vertically to 
address many of the same markets that IBM has focused upon.
IBM and EDS compete most strongly for outsourcing engagements.  EDS is 
the industry’s largest supplier of outsourcing services, but reaps almost one-
third of its $15.2 billion in revenue from one account (General Motors (GM)). 
But note, General Motors is looking to reduce its reliance on EDS  a factor 
that could spur even more intense competition between IBM and EDS as IBM 
claims its fair share of GM opportunities, and EDS strives to regain revenue 
lost.
Competition between Hewlett-Packard, Compaq, and IBM is primarily 
related to size (the ability to get the job done on a worldwide basis) and 
infrastructure services.  All claim to be experts in NT/Unix/MVS systems 
integration, NT and Unix systems and application deployment, 
communications/networking, systems/network management, and the list 
goes on and on…  But, as Aberdeen lifts up the hood and checks out these 
companies’ respective professional services offerings, Aberdeen finds very 
distinct differences in their ability to execute various IT initiatives  
especially from an infrastructure perspective. 
Succinctly, our comparative analysis and resultant findings indicate that:

IBM currently has the largest OEM service/support workforce, numbering 
more than 110,000 people.  When Digital Equipment’s service and support 
resources are combined with the resources already in place at Compaq, 
this number should approach 34,000 (the total number of people 
estimated in the combination of Compaq, Tandem, and Digital 
workforces). Hewlett-Packard is the number two OEM services provider 
 with almost 25,000 people.

What Global Services Customers Say
IBM’s professional services customers consistently state that the primary 
reason that they have purchased and continue to purchase professional 
services through Global Services is “reliability”.  IS executives have generally 
told Aberdeen that they have a high degree of confidence that IBM will get 
the job done, and that it will get done right. 
When asked about “value”, (specifically, are IBM’s services more expensive 
than services that can be obtained elsewhere?), IBM’s customers responded 
that IBM’s services pricing compare favorably to the prices of IBM’s primary 
competitors – Andersen Consulting, EDS, and OEMs such as Hewlett-Packard 
and Compaq.  But note, for many IS services buyers, pricing is a secondary 
issue  the need to get a solution in place, on- time, and in working order in 
many cases supercedes cost issues.  In other words, the reliable delivery of a 
solution in a timely fashion is often a more important consideration than 
cost.

Summary Observations
The purpose of an Aberdeen Profile is to present an objective overview of a 
supplier’s products, services, and strategies, such that Information Systems 
(IS) executives can quickly gain an understanding of a particular supplier’s 
position and product/services offerings.  By providing this independent 
assessment of IBM’s services strategy, its competitive positioning, and cores 
services offerings, this Profile seeks to assist IS managers in the professional 
services partner evaluation process. 
From a market positioning perspective, it is important to note that IBM is 
one of only a few companies that can credibly address high-level business 
strategic planning as well as information technology design, deployment, 
and lifecycle management needs.  Some of IBM’s competitors have services 
strengths in strategic planning or information technology – IBM is strong in 
both aspects.  From Aberdeen’s perspective, IBM is particularly strong on the 
information technology side of the equation.  IBM has considerable expertise 
in the design, deployment, and support of multi-tiered systems 
environments (desktops, midrange servers, and enterprise servers).  The 
company clearly understands two-tiered and three-tiered computing models, 
and has built a broad array of tools that enable applications to work 
cooperatively across multiple systems types (ranging from Microsoft 
Windows-based desktops, through Unix and AS/400 systems environments, 
through mainframe enterprise server environments).
