Description, Focus and Benefits
Customer Interaction Software (CIS) is a class of front-line business 
applications used by leading organizations to acquire and retain 
long-term, profitable customers.    CIS applications, also called 
front-office software, are used by employees when they are in 
direct contact with customers and prospects.    These systems can 
also be used by customers and prospects directly to gather product 
and service information, obtain quotes, place and track orders, 
solve product- and service-related problems and questions, and 
schedule service calls. CIS applications include:

External Customer Service and Support; 

Sales Force Automation; 

Telemarketing/Telesales; 

Marketing Automation; 

Sales Configuration; 

Field Service Management; 

Quality Assurance 

Internal Help Desk. 
What makes CIS so attractive is its ability to increase the revenues 
of those companies that use it.    Enterprises implementing CIS are 
pulling ahead of their rivals in terms of customer satisfaction and 
retention, new business development, and profitability.    Customer 
contact is not only more efficient, it is also more effective.    
Particularly important is the ability of multiple business units — 
marketing, sales, service, support and product development — to 
share and leverage the same set of customer, product and service 
information.
Aberdeen’s practice in this area covers the ISVs providing these 
client-server and Web-based applications; the systems integrators 
responsible for implementing the software; the activities of the 
systems suppliers with respect to CIS; and providers of

Executive Summary
Customer Interaction Software (CIS) is a class of front-line business 
applications used by leading organizations in all industries to acquire 
and retain long-term, profitable customers.    Forward-looking 
operational executives are actively seeking out the independent 
software vendors (ISVs) that provide these applications and are 
rapidly implementing next-generation systems based on this advanced 
software.    CIS covers a wide range of applications, including external 
customer service, external customer support, sales force automation, 
marketing automation, telemarketing/telesales, sales configuration, 
internal help desk and quality assurance.    What makes CIS so 
attractive is its demonstrated ability to raise revenues for those 
companies that use it properly.    It is increasingly apparent that the 
organizations using CIS are pulling ahead of their rivals in terms of 
customer satisfaction and retention, new business development, and 
profitability.
Because CIS enables user organizations to increase revenues, it is 
being rapidly adopted in virtually every industry.    In 1997, total 
revenues from CIS Independent Software Vendors (ISVs) increased 
66% to $1.7 billion.    Aberdeen expects this figure to grow to $6.7 
billion by 2001.    Simultaneous with this market growth is a market 
consolidation to a smaller number of $100 million-plus software 
suppliers.    Additionally, the transition to Web-based CIS 
applications is dramatically altering the competitive landscape, 
creating new market winners and losers.    Aberdeen research 
shows that Web capabilities; the ability to provide multiple, 
integrated CIS applications; vertical industry focus; and the facility 
with which these applications can be integrated with other 
Enterprise Business Applications (EBA), such as ERP, electronic 
commerce and decision support are determining which ISVs will 
survive in this market.
This Practice Summary provides the reader with Aberdeen’s insight 
and perspective into the future directions of Customer Interaction 
Software.    In addition to explaining the basic CIS modules and

Key Aberdeen Findings
·
Customer Interaction Software is unique because it 
increases revenues.    The prime motivating factor 
behind CIS is higher revenues — not reduced costs.    CIS 
improves sales and marketing efforts and allows 
companies to provide superior service to customers.    
Companies can convert more prospects into customers 
and then build long-term, profitable relationships with 
these individuals and organizations.    Existing customers 
return for repeat business — buying more often and in 
greater quantity.    This is in sharp contrast to most other 
applications, which are designed to improve internal 
operating efficiencies, and have the more modest hopes of 
cutting costs on administrative and production activities.    
The ability of CIS to generate revenue increases its potential 
impact on profitability by an order of magnitude.
·
The CIS market continues to grow rapidly.  The CIS 
market increased 66% in 1997 to $1.7 billion.    The market 
is expected to grow to $6.7 billion by 2001, making CIS one 
of the hottest areas in the information systems industry.

the ten largest CIS ISVs were responsible for 49% of all 
revenues.    This trend will continue and Aberdeen

to top $75-100 million in revenues in 1998 to stay in this 
category.

How CIS fits into EBA
Customer interaction software is part of the larger enterprise 
business application category.    As one of the four primary 
components of EBA, CIS is often closely linked to ERP, electronic 
commerce and decision support/data knowledge systems.    In fact, 
as shown in Figure One, the lines between each of the four areas 
can quickly become blurred.    For example, when a customer 
engaged in self service over the Web needs to order and pay for a 
replacement part, the CIS application must seamlessly merge with 
electronic commerce capabilities.    Additionally, such an 
interaction must naturally link to financial/billing applications as 
well as inventory, shipping and distribution systems, all of which 
fall into the ERP sphere of influence.    Finally, an organization may 
want to do some analysis on the success of its self service initiative 
and the associated cost savings/revenues generated.    Clearly, this 
requires the involvement of decision support/data knowledge 
facilities.

The Unique Advantages of CIS
The most basic reason organizations are rapidly deploying CIS is 
that it provides a mechanism for increasing revenues.    This is in 
stark contrast to earlier commercially-available applications, such 
as financials, manufacturing and human resources, which focused 
on using information technology to reduce costs.    Because CIS 
impacts revenues, the potential upside is much greater.    For 
example, a sales force automation application that increases sales 
by just 10% represents a financial impact of three times the entire IS 
budget for most corporations.    By focusing externally on the 
customer, CIS is able to achieve much higher returns on 
investments than applications that concentrate on simply 
improving invisible, internal processes.
The major advantages realized by companies that implement CIS 
are:
·
Ability to provide superior service. 
·
Improved sales and marketing.
·
Better understanding of customer needs and identification 
of new business opportunities. 
·
Optimization of service/support resources and maximization 
of service revenues. 
·
New cross-selling capabilities. 
·
Rapid entry into new markets. 
CIS Modules
Customer interaction software encompasses several inter-related 
applications that are used by different groups in a single enterprise.    
However, all these different applications are designed with one 
purpose in mind — to aid in the process of acquiring and retaining 
customers.
In order to help the reader better understand the market, Aberdeen 
has identified two macro categories of CIS applications. The first 
group is made up of applications that manage a company's 
relationship with the customer and improve customer satisfaction.    
The second category consists of applications whose primary purpose 
is to augment sales and marketing processes.    It should be noted 
however, that many user organizations are looking to merge cross-
selling capabilities into their customer support/customer service 
applications, thus combining both macro categories in a single system.

Customer Management Applications
External Customer Support
External customer support applications (also often called external help 
desks) are used to log, track, resolve and close customer issues quickly 
and efficiently from a telephone-based call center.    The goal of 
customer support applications is two-fold: to lower the cost of 
maintaining a support organization; and to retain existing customers 
through better support.    The idea is to turn the support organization 
from a cost center into a revenue engine.
Problems or questions entered into the system are no longer lost.    
Additionally, customer support applications provide online access to 
previously-identified solutions.    Issues not closed on the first call 
remain open, are noted as such by the system, and can be passed on to 
an expert if needed.    Customer support applications have escalation 
capabilities that issue alarms if a call remains unresolved past a certain 
length of time.    For companies that provide service plans, customer 
support applications also verify service contracts and determine billing 
amounts.

External Customer Service
While often used interchangeably, Aberdeen views the terms customer 
support and customer service as describing two different types of 
applications.    Both are used to log and track external customer events, 
but as described above, customer support applications are designed to 
aid support agents with the task of finding the answers to customer 
problems and questions.    The individual calling a support call center is 
looking for answers and information.    As a result, customer support 
applications are typically problem-centric and built to interact with a 
solution repository or knowledgebase that contains the answers to 
previously-encountered problems and questions.
In contrast, the individual contacting a customer service call center is 
not looking for answers, but rather is requesting that a service is 
performed or that a business transaction be initiated.    For example, to 
learn how to install a new modem in a PC, a person would contact a call 
center that involves a customer support application.    To transfer funds 
from a savings account to a checking account one would talk with a 
bank employee using a customer service application.    In the second 
instance does not need a solution to an unanswered question.    The 
customer knows what service he or she needs.    Instead, they want 
someone to execute the transaction. 
Thus, customer service applications are concerned with the delivery of 
the products and/or services that a business provides.    This different 
emphasis means that good customer support applications do not 
necessarily make good customer service applications.    Instead of 
interacting with a knowledge base, a customer service application is 
more likely to need to interface with some of the core production 
application of the enterprise.    In the example above, the customer 
service application would have to be linked to the bank’s depository 
application to perform the fund transfer. 
Internal Help Desk
Like customer support, help desk software has facilities for logging, 
tracking and resolving user problems.    In addition, help desk 
applications are often linked to network and systems management 
software.    Some organizations are now expanding their internal help 
desk beyond IS to support a wide variety of issues, such as employment 
policies and tuition reimbursement.
In Aberdeen’s opinion, the major difference between an internal help 
desk application and an external help desk/customer support 
application is not the individual features, but rather who the 
application is sold to in the user organization and with which third-

Field Service Management
Often used in conjunction with customer support applications, field 
service management software enables a support individual at the 
home office to schedule on-site calls by a service technician.    This 
software is typically used by companies that develop, sell and 
support manufactured products and represent a second level of 
customer support for customer issues and problems that cannot be 
solved over the telephone.    Thus, customer incidents are often passed 
on from a customer support application to a field service 
management system for eventual resolution.
These products use scheduling algorithms to optimize the deployment 
of field personnel and reduce downtime.    These systems also include 
or are linked to software for spare parts logistics and inventory 
control.    In this fashion, geographically dispersed service technicians 
who rarely visit the home office are ensured that the parts and 
equipment needed to support the customer are delivered at the right 
place and on time.    The ability to contact remote personnel in the 
field through a wide range of methods, ranging from the Internet to 
email to beepers, is an important aspect of these products.
Some ISV offerings also include capabilities for managing a 
centralized repair center, where equipment can be taken or 
shipped to be serviced.    Remote diagnostics and on-line manuals 
are also becoming more popular.
Quality Control and Assurance
This software provides a vital link between the support/service 
organization and the engineering/design team and is used to manage 
and improve the engineering and product development life cycle.    
When a problem appears to be the result of a product defect or when 
a particular feature or enhancement is repeatedly requested by

Sales and Marketing Applications
Sales Force Automation
More than just contact management for a single user, Sales Force 
Automation (SFA) software is used by teams to increase and accelerate 
customer purchases.    In addition to providing contact information 
(such as name, company, date of last meeting, previous sales history) 
SFA software has scheduling features that remind sales executives 
when they need to perform certain actions, such as follow up calls and 
product demonstrations.    With a shared database, SFA software keeps 
the members of a sales team aware of colleagues' actions.    Other 
features include product information (on both a company's own 
offerings as well as those from competitors), proposal generation and 
sales forecasting.
With SFA software, sales executives can determine what steps to take 
to meet quota and, more importantly, decide which prospects are 
most likely to be converted into customers.    Linked to reporting tools, 
SFA software allows sales managers to better understand where a 
sales force is being effective and optimize the use of scarce sales and 
marketing resources.
Telemarketing/Telesales
These applications have much of the same contact information 
features as SFA software, but are intended for inside sales and 
marketing representatives.    Features include automatic number 
dialing as well as customized call scripts that help new telesales 
representatives become productive quickly.    Literature fulfillment 
capabilities are also typically included.    Because these applications

Marketing Automation
Marketing Automation is a set of CIS software technologies that 
focus on marketing as a distinct software category tightly 
integrated with SFA and customer support.    Marketing Automation 
technologies enable the enterprise to manage the generation of 
new prospects, monitor the success or failure of campaigns, and 
deliver specific, targeted messages to customers and partners in a 
cost-effective and time-efficient manner.
Marketing Automation components include lead management, 
campaign management, marketing encyclopedias, and data 
analysis tools, all of which enable the management of leads from 
initial prospect through to the close of a sale.

A Transition in CIS Architectures
Aberdeen’s field research indicates that the market is entering a major 
transitional phase that will radically change the basic architecture upon 
which CIS applications are developed and deployed.    Just as the early 
1990s witnessed the change from mainframe and proprietary 
midrange-based software to client-server-based CIS applications, the 
architecture-of-choice is now starting to focus around internet 
technologies and the Web.    And, just as the shift to client-server offered 
tremendous advantages in terms of flexibility and functionality, so too 
does the move to a network-centric model.
At the present time, the majority of CIS applications being bought and 
deployed are still client-server-based.    These solutions are able to take 
advantage of graphical desktop systems; powerful, yet inexpensive 
servers running Unix or NT; flexible relational database management 
systems from companies such as Informix, Microsoft, Oracle and 
Sybase; high-speed local-area networks; and advanced software 
development tools for rapid customizations and enhancements.
Client-Server is Challenged
However, in talking with several leading-edge users of client-server 
CIS applications, Aberdeen has found them to be reevaluating the 
software architecture they will use in the future.    Many now favor a 
network-centric model in which end-users access CIS application via a 
browser.    IT and operational executives in these firms are considering 
a move to inter/intranet-based CIS applications when it comes time to 
overhaul their existing application portfolio.    Additionally, many of 
those firms that are only now starting their initial CIS deployments 
are thinking of skipping right over client-server and moving straight 
to the Web.    This change in opinion has occurred in just the last six 
months and is rapidly gathering momentum.
While Aberdeen firmly believes that the future of CIS will be closely 
intertwined with the Web, it is unrealistic to expect the entire industry 
to change overnight.    The client-server revolution began a decade ago 
and has only become mainstream in the last few years.    Similarly, the 
Internet craze will take time to move from media frenzy to 
widespread deployment of enterprise-scale applications.    This has as 
much to do with industry skill sets and organizational inertia as it 
does with available technology.    However, the advantages of Web-
based CIS are clear and compelling and software suppliers ignore this 
fundamental industry change at their peril.

Supplier Challenges
Provide a complete solution.
The successful CIS ISVs will be the ones that help combine 
application software, computing hardware, networking equipment, 
computer-telephony integration, problem resolution, pre-packaged 
knowledge bases and professional services into a complete 
package.    User organizations are simply not prepared to

will be required to provide by themselves all the products and 
services needed by the user.    Rather, Aberdeen sees partnerships 
with systems suppliers, professional services firms and providers 
of complementary software as the answer for many CIS ISVs.
Leverage outside resources through strategic partnerships.
A major bottleneck in industry growth is the ability of software 
suppliers to implement their applications.    These companies 
simply cannot hire experienced professionals fast enough to meet 
their customer’s demands for services.    Partnerships with systems 
integrators, as well as the professional services arm of systems 
suppliers, can alleviate this problem.    It is also important to build 
alliances with providers of complementary software. 
Shorten implementation times.
Because CIS applications generate revenues and provide a 
competitive advantage, a supplier’s value is directly related to how 
quickly its solution can be deployed.    The concept of a fixed time 
implementation schedule is very attractive to user organizations.    
Again, partnerships with outside professional services firms assist 
in meeting this market requirement.

have voluntarily contacted their supplier.    Companies that stress 
efficiency through shorter and shorter call duration are missing out 
on additional revenues.    Once the initial reason for a call has been 
resolved, customer service/support representatives should be 
encouraged to spend more time on the phone with clients.    This often 
leads to cross-selling opportunities or insights on how to improve a 
particular product or service.    Companies need to spend more time 
with their customers — not less.

enormous advantages, it also represents significant challenges,
