Electronic Commerce (E-Commerce) Defined
Electronic-commerce is the automation of business transactions 
and work flow between physically discrete organizations and/or 
between organizations and consumers.    Or simply, conducting 
business on-line.    Any transaction to or from the enterprise that 
involves trading partners and/or customers can be considered E-
commerce. 
There is a lot of excitement about E-commerce — both at the 
enterprise level and within the consumer ranks — because E-
commerce has the potential to create organizational efficiencies 
(and thereby lower costs), while also providing a new means for 
the enterprise to conduct business with buyers.    However, as many 
companies have learned, there’s more to building an E-commerce 
site than simply putting catalogs and order forms on the Web.    
When it comes to fully integrating E-commerce within an 
enterprise, there’s good news and there’s also some bad news.

(First) The Bad News 
Apart from the benefits commonly associated with initial Web Site 
implementations (e.g., marketing, exposure, customer feedback, 
etc.) — most enterprises are finding that the biggest payoffs require 
large investments and some truly significant integration efforts.    
Enterprises are finding it a greater-than-expected undertaking to 
build and maintain a fully integrated E-commerce operation.    No 
single software supplier solves all aspects of the holistic continuum 
of Internet-based sales and supply chain management.    So this 
means that enterprise Information Systems (IS) resources must be 
used to provide application, customization, and systems integration 
services — or another source of supply must be found for these 
services.
Aberdeen research indicates that few enterprises fully understand 
or have the resources needed to deal with the complexities 
involved in deploying electronic commerce solutions.    Some of 
these complexities include network redesign (both at the wiring 
plant level and at the protocol level); program customization and 
development (frequently using new languages such as Java); and 
database integration (frequently enterprise Oracle or DB/2 
integration with Microsoft’s SQL Server database environment).    
Further, many enterprises will find that they lack internal 
personnel that understand how to reengineer business processes to 
conduct E-commerce most efficiently, and lack expertise in 
developing program-to-program transactions with trading 
communities. 
And this fundamental lack of human resources and/or the 
expertise required to implement new/evolving technologies most 
often results in the enterprise looking to E-commerce Professional 
Services providers for manpower and expertise. 
Now for the Good News: The Anguish Is Worth the Effort 
E-Commerce really delivers for enterprises and their customers.    
The primary benefits of a successful E-commerce implementation 
are related to cost savings gained from the use of new technologies 
as well as from streamlined business practices, and customer good 
will.

to save money due to:

Why are E-Commerce Professional Services Required?
To put it mildly, E-commerce is still in its infancy; the technical and 
business factors driving it are in constant flux ¾ meaning decision-
makers face a business environment riddled with technological 
and/or standards-based blind alleys.    For many, the best-case 
scenario is that the solution you implement today will be merely 
too slow, insufficiently scalable, or cumbersome next year … but 
upgradeable via software and/or minor infrastructure 
modifications.    The worst case scenario is that you’ll have to re-
build from scratch.
Most IS departments do not have on-site, trained-and-certified 
experts who both understand the complexities of E-commerce 
implementation, and possess the skill sets to put a working E-
commerce solution in place.    Further, the best resources of many 
IS organizations are frequently occupied elsewhere looking to 
solve Year 2000-related issues; establishing and improving network 
connectivity for the enterprise via Intranets and Extranets; and 
implementing EDI, Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP), and 
Extended ERP (supply chain initiatives).
To augment existing Information Technology (IS) staff, as well as to 
overcome E-commerce deployment and integration issues, IS 
executives are now finding that the use of external, skilled E-
commerce Professional Services organizations is a must.    Experts 
are required to analyze existing business practices, address E-
commerce systems design issues, point to solutions which meet 
your E-commerce business objectives, and provide integration 
services to existing enterprise systems.

Defining E-Commerce Professional Services 
E-commerce Professional Services consists of a series of services 
designed to enable the integration and deployment of E-commerce 
solutions—and the services needed to maintain E-commerce 
systems throughout product life cycles.    E-commerce operations 
can be divided into two categories: business-to-business and 
business-to-consumer.
Business-to-Business
From a business-to-business point of view, the Web’s ability to 
enable automation of the full commerce cycle between 
organizations—from inquiry and order placement to invoicing, 
payment, delivery and support services ¾ is the key driver.    
Today’s high-end E-commerce systems are an amalgam of EDI and 
Electronic Funds Transfer (EFT), transaction processing, 
middleware, database technology, existing enterprise systems, and 
ERP-related applications.
Business-to-Consumer
From a business-to-consumer perspective, Internet-based 
marketing and payment systems are enabling start-ups and 
established brand names to reach new markets and cut costs.    The 
Web is also giving consumers tremendous new power by allowing 
them to quickly review competing products, compare features, 
pricing, and value, and complete the transaction with no physical 
intervention from any of the merchants involved.
Many business-to-consumer E-commerce systems use a Commerce 
Application Platform (CAP) as their foundation.    A CAP is a 
software bundle or group of integrated products that combines:

Planning—Strategy and Process Design
Strategy and Process Design are the precursors to E-commerce 
systems design, development and deployment.      Their purpose is 
to insure all E-commerce systems development work is integrated 
into the overall corporate strategy and associated technology-
enabled business processes.    Strategic services are considered 
“high level”—meaning that they are relatively expensive per 
consultant hour billed, and critical to the overall success of the 
company.    Few professional services firms specialize in this 
practice area, and even fewer can take an enterprise through all 
three of the major professional service areas.
Strategy Development
As the leading edge of Supply Chain Management, Strategy and 
Process Design looks deeply into overall corporate goals, objectives 
and mission, and develops information systems strategies to 
support them.    Detailed process designs are done afterward, but 
the overall functionality desired, and as well as the identification 
of key E-commerce architectural topologies to be formed, must be 
done here.    The relevant issue in this phase, include, at a


The desired mix of extranets, intranets, and internet;

Business Process Design
Technology-enabled business processes are designed or 
reengineered, based upon corporate strategies.    The work can be 
as “simple” as choosing the right industry best practices or as 
complex—and costly—as a total business-process reengineering 
makeover.    Much of this depends on the extent of ERP or ERP-like 
Business Process Reengineering (BPR) the company has engaged in 
prior to initiating an E-commerce strategy.    The core business 
processes will both greatly influence (and be influenced by) E-
commerce systems design and development choices.

Systems Design 
As recently as one year ago, this paragraph would have described a 
landscape where, due to lack of E-commerce experience across the

together discrete packages from multiple suppliers to arrive at a 
functioning “system.”    Today, you can find packages from 
individual suppliers that will get you 80-90% of the required

enterprise from the ground up, E-commerce systems design is, by 
definition, the imposition of a standardized solution on top of a 
pre-existing commerce and communications architectures.    The 
need for significant customization will not be eliminated, even 
when suppliers offer so-called complete E-commerce solutions.
