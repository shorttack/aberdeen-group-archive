Description, Focus and Benefits
Aberdeen’s Professional Services Research Practice follows the 
market trends of Professional Service Providers (PSPs) engaged in 
the planning, development, implementation, support and 
maintenance of Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) solutions.    
Enterprise Resource Planning is the umbrella term for packaged 
applications that support core-business operational functions: 
Finance and Accounting; Sales and Distribution; Manufacturing 
and Logistics; and Human Resource Management.
The Professional Services Research Practice concentrates on the 
value added services provided by PSPs.    Aberdeen continuously 
examines these services and distinguishes between the types of 
offerings, looking at the individual firm’s strengths and weaknesses 
in different technology and service groups.
The pace of ERP solution technology introduction has accelerated 
over the past several years and very few corporations have the 
ability to stay current on all of the issues surrounding new 
technology. 
Professional Services Providers play an important role to industry 
by serving in the capacity of business technologist, not only by 
assisting corporations in technology assessment and integration, 
but also in terms of consulting on the strategic business decisions 
related to technology. 
Through these services corporations are just beginning to leverage 
their information technology investments to more strongly 
compete in the marketplace.

Defining ERP and the Supply Chain
Enterprise Resource Planning is the master plan for organizing, 
integrating and managing enterprise applications such as Finance, 
Accounting, Manufacturing, Sales, Distribution, and Human 
Resources in a unified and efficient fashion.    It involves the 
centralization of data, integration of applications across 
organizations, re-implementation of business processes — and, 
hopefully, all of these efforts will result in a more efficient and 
more competitive organization.
But note, this definition describes ERP as an internally-focused 
initiative ¾ a series of products and processes that streamline 
enterprise information processing by enabling internal

organizations to interact across organization boundaries ¾ thereby 
resulting in greater information flow, fewer internal roadblocks, 
and various workflow efficiencies. 
The latest trend in ERP incorporates an external focus ¾ enabling 
the enterprise to automate transactions with external business 
partners.    This trend allows for the streamlining of external 
processes related to the acquisition of products and materials from 
external suppliers (the “supply chain”). 
To the enterprise, the supply chain is the collection of suppliers 
that provides the raw materials needed to build a finished product 
that is ultimately delivered to a customer.    By automating the 
supply chain, the enterprise can improve process flow; implement 
just-in-time inventory practices (that reduce costs to carry 
inventory in stock ¾ and provide better inventory control); reduce 
the cost of processing purchase orders; and ultimately reduce the 
cost to manufacture goods while providing better service to 
customers.

1. OEMs (Original Equipment Manufacturers);
2. Independent Software Vendors (ISVs); and,
3. Independent Professional Services Providers (IPSPs). 
What Services Do ERP Professional Services Providers Offer?
ERP Professional Services firms interviewed by Aberdeen (and 
summarized in the final chapter of this report) provide a 
combination of Planning, Implementation, Project Management 
and Support services. 
The professional services required to implement an ERP solution 
can get quite expensive, and vendors should weigh the pros and 
cons when considering engaging one.    Costs associated with ERP 
deployments are usually compared with implementation time, 
deployment experience, industry knowledge and an outsiders 
point-of-view when looking at ERP Professional Service providers. 
Along with all of the deployment and integration skills that 
professional services firms bring to engagements, perhaps one of 
the least obvious benefit that an outside firm brings to an 
enterprise is its ability to implement change within an

Planning — Strategy, Process Redesign and the IT Roadmap
For many enterprises, the initial phases of ERP begin with a 
“strategic rethink” of the enterprise’s goals and objectives, followed 
by business and organizational process reevaluation and IT 
architecture assessment.    This section describes activities in each 
of these phases.
Strategic Rethink
Strategic planning is the precursor for ERP systems design, 
development and deployment.    In this phase, an enterprise usually 
reevaluates its business plan and business objectives; reassess its 
competitive position relative to other suppliers in its industry; and 
reconsiders how the enterprise can provide better services (and 
thereby attract and maintain) to its customers.    During the 
strategic planning process, enterprises look heavily into overall 
corporate objectives to determine strategic intent, and also conduct 
an external assessment to determine strategic positioning. 
Business Process Reevaluation
In many enterprises, applications were written to service the needs 
of a particular business unit ¾ and frequently these applications

behaved in a “stovepipe” fashion ¾ they serviced the needs of the 
business unit vertically, but were not designed for horizontal 
integration with other business units within the same enterprise.    
One of the key advantages of today’s ERP solutions is that they have 
been architected to transcend organizational boundaries ¾ making 
it possible for applications written by-and-for one organization (for 
instance, Manufacturing Resource Planning) to share data and 
work seamlessly with applications written by-and-for another 
organization (for instance, Order Management).    And as a result of 
this ability to integrate applications, organizational efficiencies in 
data flow result.
To take maximum advantage of the cross-organizational 
capabilities of ERP-based software ¾ business processes are 
redesigned or reengineered based upon the corporate strategy.    
This work can be as “simple” as choosing the right industry best 
practices or as complex — and costly — as a total business-process 
reengineering makeover.    These “core” business processes are the 
foundation of ERP systems design and development ¾ their goal: to 
“seamlessly” integrate people, processes and technologies across 
multiple disciplines, in order to accomplish the corporate 
objectives. 
IT Roadmap
Information gained from the strategic rethink and the business 
process reevaluation is used to ensure all ERP systems applications 
are integrated into the overall corporate strategy and associated 
technology-enabled business processes.    In this phase, existing IT 
systems are reevaluated ¾ and initial architectural decisions are 
made regarding how to deploy the new ERP systems, and how to 
integrate those systems with applications that are already in place 
within the enterprise’s existing information infrastructure. 
In addition, customization efforts are considered in this phase.    
One of the benefits of using standard, off-the-shelf ERP packages is 
that many of the cross-organizational software and process 
changes have already been engineered into the ERP software 
packages ¾ making it easier to deploy these systems and to build 
cross-functional systems.    Further, by buying a standard package, 
much of the research and development effort, as well as future 
software updates, become the responsibility of the ERP software 
supplier ¾ and less of a burden on the IS application development 
and maintenance budget.

Implementation — Systems Design, Development and 
Integration
This phase relies on information gathered during the enterprise’s 
planning process to construct an integrated technology solution 
that makes use of various ERP modules.    In this phase, once the 
enterprise has determined the type of ERP software to be 
purchased or upgraded, professional services firms assist IS 
executive management in the overall design, development and 
implementation of the system.    Services provided are based upon 
the business processes to be followed, the types of modules to be 
incorporated, and the desired functionality of the system.
Systems Design 
In the systems design phase, designers usually:
1. Start by examining the existing network (the physical 
network and related protocols) in order to ensure a common 
basis for desktop-to-server and ERP system-to-other-server 
communications;
2. Follow this network assessment with an analysis of 
application requirements (user count, peak-loading, tuning, 
database linkage, and other considerations);
3. Design the structure of ERP system.    Designers evaluate ERP 
configuration options, user count projections for all 
applications, utilization/performance projections, high-
availability considerations, and other aspects related to 
systems operation; and,
4. Lay out the application design ¾ such that applications can 
run optimally on the chosen ERP platform, and so 
applications can inter-operate with other applications on 
other desktops and/or servers. 
In this phase, the most critical services that IS buyers should 
examine are the professional service provider’s Architectural 
Design Services, services related to Systems/Network/Application 
Customization; and Systems Integration Services.    The remainder 
of this section describes these services in greater detail.
·
Architecture Design Services — The architectural design 
phase reflects a blending of the enterprise’s information 
management philosophy (for example: should systems be 
centralized ¾ or distributed?; should the database be 
standardized ¾ and what database should be used?) with

future requirements. 
·

Systems/Application Development and Integration
Once the ERP system design is complete, the process of overall 
system development begins.    Although ERP solutions are 
“packaged”, they still require customization for each enterprise.    
ERP Professional Services firms have at their disposal a variety of 
ERP software supplier-supplied or in-house methodologies and 
tools to develop the system.    These methodologies and tools were 
designed to both efficiently build the system and also provide for 
management control over the development process.    For an in-
depth look at the various tools and methodologies, please refer to 
Aberdeen’s ERP Systems Practice Report.
Systems Integration
This phase is concerned with micro issues related to 
system/network implementation, and integration within the 
existing information infrastructure.    Systems Integration Services 
usually make use of information gathered in the assessment phase 
of system analysis to scope and analyze systems integration 
problems. 
In this phase, designers usually emphasize the use of specific 
industry standards to use to resolve inter-system communications, 
application interoperability, and data sharing issues.    Typically, 
designers look to find standards that enable systems 
interoperability (networking, Middleware, database, security, 
network management, etc.)

Once the ERP system has been tailored it must be integrated with 
other enterprise applications, as well as the other systems ands

ERP Project Management
Skilled project management expertise is crucial to the success of 
many ERP system/application deployment scenarios — especially in 
large-scale application deployment engagements.    To ensure that 
applications are implemented properly, and that systems are 
deployed in a consistent and replicable manner, project managers 
are usually assigned to coordinate various aspects of an ERP 
system roll-out. 
ERP project management involves scheduling, tracking, and 
trouble-shooting activities — and the services provider usually 
embraces a particular project management methodology in order 
to perform these tasks. 
Projects vary in scope.    Project managers can be made responsible 
for the roll-out of a specific phase of an ERP server implementation 
(for instance, a roll-out of a PeopleSoft human resources 
application); or, a project manager can have responsibility for the 
integration and roll-out of a complete manufacturing, distribution, 
financial, and human resources system.
In large OEM, ISV, and IPSP organizations, the role of 
implementation and related project management is frequently 
turned-over to field service personnel for the actual staging of the 
proposed system, as well as for activities related to the physical 
ERP systems and application implementation.    At medium-to-small 
IPSP organizations, the systems designers are frequently involved 
in the project management and roll-out of the systems they 
designed.

Chapter Four
Issues and Challenges
There are several new or evolving technologies — as well as 
existing problems (like the Year 2000 problem) — that are 
influencing the deployment and management of ERP systems.    
Some of these include:
·
The Internet;
·
Client/Server and Network-Centric architecture;
·
Transition to Open Systems;
·
The Year 2000; and,
·
Staffing.
In addition, mergers/acquisitions and outsourcing trends are 
expected to effect the services offered, and corresponding costs 
related to ERP solutions deployment and management.    These 
issues are discussed in greater detail below:
The Internet
The Internet is having a profound effect upon ERP systems and the 
professional services supporting them.    This has created better 
and more stable communications between enterprises and through 
the use of secure internets, intranets and extranets the supply 
chain has greater access to information and thus can more 
efficiently integrate products information, services, payments and 
other financial information.
Transition to Client/Server and Network-Centric
