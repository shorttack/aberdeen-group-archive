Description, Focus and Benefits
This Aberdeen Group Practice Summary is a deliverable in a 
comprehensive service that includes Aberdeen published research 
and customized professional services aimed at executives who 
must make strategic business decisions regarding Information 
Technology.
Enterprise Resource Planning is the umbrella term for packaged 
applications that support core-business operational functions: 
Finance and Accounting; Sales and Distribution; Manufacturing 
and Logistics; and Human Resource Management. Aberdeen has 
organized this practice to monitor those suppliers that deliver 
solutions against each of these individual core functions (best-of-
breed), and those suppliers that seek to provide solutions 
encompassing multiple operational functions (fully integrated). As 
part of Aberdeen’s research discipline, suppliers that provide 
critical components or value-added solutions to a given primary 
ERP function are also monitored. For example, within 
Manufacturing and Logistics, suppliers of Advanced Planning and 
Optimization (APO) technologies, Manufacturing Execution 
Systems (MES) and Maintenance, Repair and Operations (MRO), 
solutions are also followed. Following is a breakout for the three 
primary ERP application areas: Finance and Accounting; 
Manufacturing and Logistics; and Human Resource Management.

need to correctly manage, report and reconcile operational 
financial data and business position. Increasingly, best practice is 
encouraging the adoption of process oriented approaches to 
financial management and integration with non-accounting 
systems (order processing, inventory, manufacturing).
Benefits: Today's advanced financial solutions are setting the pace 
in the Enterprise Business Application market by leveraging key 
enabling technologies — business analytics, object-oriented 
technologies, and the Internet — and cross-system integration

Chapter One 
Executive Summary
Enterprise Resource Planning or ERP includes the core business 
components without which today’s competitive businesses could 
not achieve many of their mission-critical objectives.    Aberdeen 
sees ERP functionality spanning a broad spectrum of business 
activities:    finance and accounting; manufacturing and logistics; 
supply chain management; electronic data interchange; sales order 
management; inventory distribution; and increasingly, human 
resource management.
Today’s Information Technology (IT) executives and their line-of-
business management peers need to understand and objectively 
evaluate the wave of advanced-technology-based applications now 
coming available.    Products abound, but users need to evaluate the 
effects imposed by these products and their applicability to the 
business solution or capability sought, the impact on business 
operations and the overall enterprise strategy for the future.
Corporate success is increasingly being defined based on the 
incredible potential and impact of Internet technologies on core 
business applications.    Electronic commerce, Kanban and JIT 
supply chain execution, real-time synchronization of supply chain 
planning information ¾ especially as these capabilities integrate 
and extend core ERP function ¾ are bringing about fundamental 
new ways of doing business across and between enterprises. 
ERP business applications are the lifeblood of an organization, 
whether manufacturing, finance, healthcare, etc.    These 
applications both make sense out of and drive the informational 
requirements of the corporation: control of manufacturing, 
processing, industry-specifics, business execution, and sales and 
distribution.    The dilemma arises in the confusion inherent in 
business as management tries to sort out the criteria    for working 
within the options available.    The decisions surpass simple 
software selection as the impact of that selection (implementation, 
integration of legacy data, training, and long-term management of 
systems which are likely to be heterogeneous, distributed, and 
Internet-enabled) clearly add dimensions of complexity to what is 
already a challenging software application decision.

Summary of Findings
The ERP market’s high visibility is well-earned, as the very essence 
of competitive success hinges in part on the capabilities of these 
critical underlying infrastructures.    Vendors face the breadth 
versus depth dilemma, with only the very top-tier vendors 
attempting strategies which combine both.    Today’s consumers do 
not have the luxury of protracted implementations, and especially 
for the middle market, complex hardware and software 
integration, implementations that require droves of expensive 
consultants, and sky-high price tags.    And they often need the best-
of-breed in their market segment software plus the breadth and 
integration of another ERP solution.
To stay competitive, no company today can afford to run its 
business on piece-meal, stand-alone software packages; the lack of 
real-time “right answers” inherent in the patchwork quilt approach 
to business applications can kill a company in today’s market. 
Thus, the strong growth rate for ERP solutions will continue for the 
next half-decade.    Customers, though, will be savvier as consumers
—asking the really telling questions about the applicability of the 
solution to their industry.    Midrange and growth market 
consumers will look for the high-value packages, seeking the ease 
in implementation and installation lacking in the past.    Closely 
tied to business success will be the degree of integration achieved 
between legacy information, business applications, and the entire 
supply chain, driven by core requirements of agility, efficiency, and 
economy.

The Evolution of Enterprise Applications 
Enterprise management is being challenged with forecasting and 
planning consumer demand, reducing work in progress (WIP) 
inventories and supply-chain cycle times, and improving product 
quality ¾ all while increasing customer satisfaction and reducing 
overall costs.    One major obstacle is that the majority of 
manufacturing-based software solutions still in use today have 
continued fundamentally unchanged for the past twenty years.    
Back in the late-1970s and into the early 1980’s, manufacturers 
were in a headlong rush to implement Material Requirements 
Planning (MRP) and Inventory Control Systems (ICS).    The promise 
of these systems was better control and increased predictability of 
the production environment.    What these management systems 
did well was improve cost accounting by providing the capability 
to:

count and track the production process; 

establish standard setup and operating costs; and 

obtain a better picture of the true costs associated with 
producing product. 
Today, the problem facing many manufacturers is that these now-
antiquated systems have not delivered the expected efficiencies 
and margin improvements.    These MRP-based systems were never 
architected to provide demand forecasting or production planning 
and scheduling based on capacity.    The focus of those first-
generation systems was targeted solely at managing the flow of 
materials inside the plant, providing little insight into the overall 
manufacturing or enterprise-wide processes. 
Enterprise Resource Planning Comes Of Age
Over time, this dichotomy between what MRP did well and what it 
basically did not do at all became further exacerbated as 
manufacturers struggled to keep up with escalating customer-
driven demands and continued pressure to reduce costs.    In

response, the EBA suppliers began delivering solutions known as 
Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems.    These solutions 
sought to integrate MRP functionality in a broader operational 
context that included other key enterprise applications, like

view of overall business operations but required customers to

Moving Beyond The Enterprise 
In the early 1990’s as the industry moved to adopt practices like 
Just In Time (JIT) and KANBAN manufacturing, broader market and 
economic factors came to bear, and the increasing implications of 
multi-site and tiered manufacturing relationships became of 
considerable significance.    ERP solutions began to reposition their 
value propositions by attempting to bring the same efficiencies to 
the extended    supply chain as they had brought to the single 
manufacturing site.    The initial promise of supply-chain-driven 
ERP was found wanting when enterprises realized that in order to 
achieve the cross-company supply-chain efficiencies it was 
presupposed that that a single ERP solution was in place at every 
site. 
If You Can See It, You Can Plan It
Advanced Planning    and Optimization (APO) technology then came 
to the market.    This new technology holds the key to bridging the 
ERP and MRP gaps and putting enterprises ahead of the supply vs. 
demand-curve through better end-to-end cross-company, multi-site 
supply chain planning capabilities.    By using advanced scientific 
algorithms, these planning engines examine capacity, resources, 
schedules, and utilizations to derive the optimum plan for the 
movement of materials, goods and products, finances and labor.    
Once derived, these plans then provide feedback to the underlying 
enterprise operational systems.    In their most comprehensive 
implementations, some enterprises have replaced their MRP and 
Master Production Scheduling (MPS) functionality with APO.    At 
their most easily attainable implementations, enterprises have 
used APO to significantly reduce WIP and cycle times ¾ deriving 
enough profitability to more than fund the potentially multimillion

Infinite Resource Planning
What began as Aberdeen research into the multi-dimensional 
impact of Internet technologies on business and enterprise 
computing has led to startling and far-reaching conclusions.    
Aberdeen asserts that enterprises are on the cusp of yet one more 
industry-rocking, watershed event.    This event is being driven by 
the convergence of three major technologies ¾ Object-Oriented 
Technology (OOT), Internet technology and Network-Centric 
Computing Architectures (NCCs).
These converged technologies make possible what Aberdeen refers 
to as Infinite Resource Planning (IRP).    IRP is a new model for 
conducting business and inter-business operations.    With IRP, the 
true integration of cross-functional and cross-organizational 
enterprise business applications may now be achieved. 
Aberdeen believes that the economic and business efficiency gains 
embodied in IRP capabilities are so compelling that leading 
enterprises will fundamentally alter their hard-won best practices 
and processes, and that suppliers of advanced business solutions 
and technology will have to rethink and remake their products, 
functionality and architectures.
