Healthcare Information Systems
Description, Focus and Benefits
Aberdeen’s Healthcare Information Systems practice follows the technical 
developments and market trends of several dozen leading-edge HIS suppliers -- 
including independent software vendors (ISVs), systems suppliers and 
professional services firms. Aberdeen specializes in the market positioning and 
tactical challenges faced by both leading HIS suppliers and forward-looking 
care-providing organizations.
Healthcare information systems include traditional administrative and 
financial applications, as well as clinical/diagnostic systems. Aberdeen believes 
that it is the next-generation applications that merge clinical and 
administrative data that are driving HIS industry growth. Making integrated 
information accessible during a patient encounter -- where it can have a real 
impact upon the physician-patient encounter -- is revolutionizing the delivery 
of healthcare. The result is improved care delivery during a patient encounter, 
valuable information for management analysis, and reduced costs and more 
profits for care-providing organizations.

Summary of Findings
The overriding challenge facing the healthcare industry is how to improve the 
quality of care while reducing costs.    Like other industries, healthcare has 
“downsized” its workforce in an effort to reduce costs.    Now it is looking more 
aggressively at information technology as a means not only to stem or reduce 
costs, but also to improve patient care. 
Traditionally, the healthcare industry has relied upon conservative buying 
patterns and has not taken full advantage of the numerous advancements in 
information technology over the past two decades.    Aberdeen believes that as 
the financial models within the healthcare system continue to become 
increasingly demanding, managers will rely more on technology to control 
rising costs.    Both providers and payers are realizing that implementation of 
information technology will be one of the best ways for companies to 
differentiate themselves from the competition.

Chapter Three
Healthcare Industry Practice Defined
The Role of Information Technology in Healthcare
In an effort to hold or reduce healthcare costs while improving the quality of 
care, healthcare providers are implementing information technology solutions 
at a rapid pace.    Furthermore, in an effort to become more competitive, the 
industry is consolidating both vertically and horizontally forming Integrated 
Delivery Networks (IDN).    This consolidation brings new challenges.    
Combining disparate healthcare businesses into a streamlined operation is time 
consuming.    Inherent in the task of merging business operations is the 
challenge of combining the various information systems, including many older 
legacy systems. 
Generally, healthcare providers have been slower than others in implementing 
information technology; they want proven solutions.    Because Information 
Technology can impact both cost and quality of care, it does offer user 
organizations a competitive edge. Corporate restructuring means hospitals and 
other providers must integrate heterogeneous systems and retrain users.    This 
is a big investment!    One of the questions facing the CIO is which system, if 
any, should be the lead system. 
Although healthcare providers have lagged behind other industries in 
allocating funds for IT, they appear to be increasing their focus on information 
systems as a means of streamlining operations and reducing the overall 
operating increases.    Aberdeen estimates a typical healthcare provider’s 
budget is about 4% of the annual operating budget, with the majority of IT 
departments projecting staffing increases.    Budgets are expected to increase, 
especially for the newly formed healthcare delivery systems and networks.
Core Information System Components
Because the healthcare industry is undergoing a significant restructuring, 
software solutions are also changing and are placing a greater emphasis on the 
integration of information.    For example, in the past, hospitals stood alone and 
physician offices were separate entities or organized as small, independent 
groups.    Today, they are forming Integrated Delivery Networks to provide a 
continuum of care versus isolated, fragmented care.  Providers may be a

Business Applications
Business applications address the basic operations of the healthcare enterprise 
including billing, claims processing, accounting, and human resources.    They 
are typically home-grown, custom-developed by a systems supplier or a third-
party professional services firm, or come from an independent software 
vendor in the form of a packaged, but highly customizable application.    There 
is a trend for providers to purchase packaged applications and tailor the 
package to their needs versus developing solutions from scratch with an in-
house staff.
The first processes to be automated are typically financial applications, and 
many of the original legacy systems are still in use.    Aberdeen has identified 
several classes of business applications.    They are:

Finance:    Healthcare financing is becoming more and more 
sophisticated and today’s financial applications help care providers 
manage the standard business functions including billing, and 
accounts payable. 

Human Resources:    In conjunction with horizontal HR functionality, 
healthcare providers are taking advantage of generic HR applications 
that assist in activities such as resume tracking and automating time 
and attendance records of employees.

Managed Care Applications:    As managed care plans become more 
prevalent, providers need to track their actual costs for specific 
populations in order to negotiate contracts prudently.    Managed care 
applications serve as a tool to help providers manage their financials 
under new reimbursement models. 
Clinical Applications
Clinical applications that capture specific data relative to patient care are 
becoming more sophisticated as they incorporate more modern, easy-to-use 
interfaces.    Lab, radiology, and pharmacy information systems have been in 
existence for over 20 years and healthcare providers are consistently 
upgrading these systems to reflect the latest technology advancements.

Radiology: a solution that manages day-to-day operations of a 
radiology business including tracking orders, scheduling patients for 
exams, tracking films, and generating reports.    Voice recognition, bar 
coding, and touch screen are technologies that facilitate dictation of 
patient reports.    For easy storage and retrieval of images, PACS 
(Picture Archiving Communication Systems) also represent an 
important technology area.    Additionally, speech recognition 
technologies will soon become more commonplace.


Laboratory: a system that tracks specimens from requisition entry to 
final report.    On-line instrumentation interfacing of laboratory

Management Information Applications
In addition to standard business and clinical applications, healthcare providers 
are investing in Information Technology for Management Information.    Many 
applications use generic software and technology customized to the specific 
needs of the healthcare provider. 
Much of the value that management information applications offer can be 
attributed to the integration of clinical and financial/administrative data.    
Management Information applications are a new breed of software 
applications that put a premium on data sharing and on better organizing, 
integrating, and delivering information that is critical to managers in their 
decision making.    The ability to integrate clinical and administrative 
information enables more efficient, well-informed decision making at both the 
point-of-care and in behind-the-scenes decision making.
In effect, the ability to merge and dynamically access information has led to 
this rapidly expanding new class of applications giving healthcare 
professionals — from nurses to executive team members — immediate 
feedback on how their activities impact the profitability of the enterprise.    
These management information applications provide users with advanced 
analysis, reporting, and decision-support capabilities.    Aberdeen has divided 
management information applications into three macro segments:    electronic 
medical record, decision-support applications, and customer interaction 
software.
The Electronic Medical Record
The concept of an electronic patient record is frequently hyped by HIS 
suppliers — in theory an electronic medical record provides an on-line, real 
time clinical history of patients. These applications can be thought of as a 
collection of data points from various clinical and administrative feeder 
systems.

Aberdeen believes that patient care applications will continue to expand in 
functionality and technology, taking advantage of networking and data 
synchronization functionality.    The concept of a single electronic patient 
record shared across the enterprise will someday emerge as an operational and 
technical reality.
Establishing an electronic patient record based on this concept is a long and 
arduous task.    In tracking the progress of application vendors, Aberdeen 
research indicates that most of today’s electronic patient record solutions 
operate on a departmental versus an enterprise-wide level.    However, a 
number of application suppliers have developed an enterprise-wide version 
and healthcare organizations are beginning to implement the solutions on a 
limited basis.    One major obstacle to implementing electronic medical records 
is who “owns” the data.
In addition to computerized departmental functions and patient records, there 
are a number of innovative patient care applications being implemented by 
progressive care providers: telemedicine and patient education are two such 
applications.    Telemedicine offers care providers a means of assessing a 
patient from a distance using various monitoring equipment in conjunction 
with teleconferencing.    Web-based “store and forward” technology provides a 
low cost medium for telemedicine.    Patient education using information 
technology systems (such as CD ROM databases, videotapes, the Web) offers 
providers a quick, non-labor intensive means of educating patients.
Decision-Support Applications
In the thin-margined healthcare industry, where mergers and acquisitions are 
the norm, providers are not only reevaluating treatment programs based on 
the relationship of cost and results, but they are also looking at the value of 
prevention and education — while trying to map these clinical and economic 
criteria to a patient population and the bottom line.    To systematically examine 
these relationships, while also measuring performance against best practices 
and the competition, healthcare companies require data warehouse-driven 
decision support technologies.    Not only must data be integrated from 
numerous transaction systems into a single database, the data must be cleansed 
and scrubbed, then enhanced (classified, for example) and optimized for 
decision support access.
Today’s advanced decision-support applications are able to extract and merge 
important clinical, financial and administrative data from both clinical and 
administrative or financial applications.    Using an intuitive, graphical user 
interface, decision-support applications allow senior management to quickly 
chart and analyze patient care results and important cost information.

Customer Interaction Software
Customer Interaction Software (CIS) is a class of front-line business applications 
used by leading organizations in all industries to acquire and retain long-term, 
profitable customers.    Forward-looking healthcare executives are 
implementing CIS applications for external customer service, external 
customer support, sales force automation, and telemarketing/telesales 
applications. 
Data and information gathered through clinical and financial applications 
(running on legacy systems, mini computers, and/or client/servers) provide the 
data to the Electronic Patient Record, the Decision Support applications, and the 
Customer Interaction software.    In an optimal situation, a healthcare enterprise 
would have a system integrating all of these applications.    In our discussions 
with CIOs in large healthcare enterprises, Aberdeen research indicates that 
providers are establishing the infrastructure to build such systems, but none has 
achieved it to date.

Issues and Challenges
Impact of Major IT Trends
Client/Server—The powerful and flexible architectures, lower price points, and 
easy-to-use interfaces that are characteristic of client-server based information 
systems facilitated the creation and adoption of management information 
applications.    In addition, based on the concepts of open systems, many of today’s 
advanced HIS solutions are able to integrate data from different client-server-
based systems and to access legacy data that resides on the mainframe.    This 
enables care-providing organizations to share critical information on several 
levels throughout the healthcare enterprise as well as leverage existing technology 
investments. 
Internet:    The Internet is being used actively by consumers as an information 
gathering tool.    Numerous healthcare providers have developed a site on the 
World Wide Web through which consumers, or potential patients, can research 
local health plans, as well as individual physicians and specialists.    Aberdeen 
research indicates users are increasingly accessing the Internet for health-
related information, and the number of chat groups focused on specific medical 
diagnoses has expanded.    Physicians are active users of the Internet; we 
estimate that over 70% of all physicians use the Internet for research. 
Internet functionality will be taken to the next level when patient/customer 
transactions can be conducted online.    Progressive health plans will use the 
Internet for    enrollment and administrative maintenance.    Within the next 12-
18 months, Aberdeen senses that the more forward-looking health plans will 
provide online functionality for claims processing and payment.
Intranet: There is a widespread move in the market today to develop Intranets 
— the use of Internet software and networking protocols for internal purposes. 
Aberdeen believes that the implementation of Intranet applications will prove 
the viability of Internet-based technologies and will greatly enhance an 
organization’s ability to distribute data to its internal employees, partners, and 
external customers.    Internet technology offers a low cost approach to 
information sharing. 
Extranet :    Companies across a wide range of industries are etching plans to 
improve communication with their business partners through the development

products in order to form a seamless information flow to users.    Applications 
relying on wireless technologies have been implemented for inpatient 
medication and home health.    Aberdeen believes that advances in wireless 
technologies will become an increasingly critical communication medium 
across the industry, particularly as next-generation healthcare enterprises 
continue to operate in a highly distributed environment.
Speech Recognition:    The promise of speech recognition technology has been 
well-documented throughout the healthcare industry for quite some time.    
However, limited vocabularies, slow response times, and the need to pause 
between words has stalled acceptance and limited user success.    Aberdeen 
research indicates that significant progress has been made by several leading 
suppliers that have optimized discipline-specific applications with underlying 
voice recognition technology — producing dictation solutions that can interpret 
continuous speech.    IBM’s MedSpeak for Radiology is one such application.
Imaging Applications: Advances in imaging technology are allowing healthcare 
providers to electronically share records that have traditionally been 
distributed manually — including X-rays and CAT scans.    Lower price points 
are making leading-edge imaging technologies — such as Picture Archiving and 
Communications Systems (PACS) — available to smaller healthcare providers. 
Data Warehousing:    An important piece of many of today’s healthcare 
information systems solution is a data warehouse.    Data warehouses provide a 
foundation for complex decision support by integrating legacy and client-server 
based data and are typically built with an extremely large relational database 
measured in terabytes. These warehouses often reside on a high-end Unix 
server.    Decision-support applications are the front end to the data warehouse:

Supplier Success Factors
In order to succeed in this market, suppliers need to have applications that 
integrate with existing applications, as well as with applications supplied by 
other vendors.    Because healthcare providers have few resources (e.g. time, 
money) to devote to training, applications should be intuitive and provide well 
written on-line help. 
User Success Factors
Short-term successes are critical when implementing an HIS.    For both the user 
and supplier, phased implementation cycles produce the best results. 
Automation must occur within a single department or individual healthcare 
provider before it can exist across several care providing organizations. 
In addition, users must plan and implement sufficient training for all users.    
Gaining support of the user community is critical for a successful 
implementation.

Supplier Abstracts
There are thousands of suppliers and technologies supporting the Healthcare 
Information Systems (HIS) market.    Hardware, systems software, applications, 
networking equipment, the Internet, and existing legacy systems make 
evaluating information systems a difficult task.    In order to help healthcare 
professionals understand the types of services offered by Information 
Technology suppliers, Aberdeen identified the following four main categories:

Integrated Healthcare Information Systems Suppliers — This group of 
suppliers is comprised of companies that provide a comprehensive set 
of integrated healthcare-centric products and services. 

Independent Software Vendors (ISVs) — Aberdeen identified two types 
of ISVs.    The first provides niche solutions for the healthcare market 
(such as radiology, lab, managed care, etc.). The second group includes 
all the providers of generic software (eg human resources) and 
technology (eg voice) that target the healthcare services market. 

Professional Services Firms and Systems Integrators — This group 
specializes in integrating and customizing information technology 
solutions.    They are often called in to supplement the services of ISVs 
or to augment internal resources.

Systems Suppliers — Nearly all of the traditional systems suppliers — 
including Hewlett-Packard, IBM, Digital, Data General, Sun— work 
closely with the large HIS providers and ISVs.    Frequently they have 
sales, marketing, and engineering groups that focus on the special 
needs of the healthcare market.
