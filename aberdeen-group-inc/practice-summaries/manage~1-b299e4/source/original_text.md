Executive Summary
IT managers used to have very few choices as to how they built, 
managed and maintained their corporate networks.  Technical 
advances and market deregulation have created new ways to build 
corporate computer networks.  In addition to new networking 
technologies, there are many new types of telecommunications 
services that address the specific needs of corporate IT managers.
A network/IT manager has three ways to build a corporate 
network:

In-house construction of a private network;

Full outsourcing of network construction and operation; 
or,

Mix and match Managed Carrier Services (referred to as 
“Managed Services” throughout this report) to take over 
specific network functions.
Managed Services enable enterprises to focus on their core 
competencies.  This is important as small and large businesses 
alike face increased global competition.  Managed Services are an 
effective solution for enterprises that wish to rationalize their 
resources; increase network functionality; and create new business 
opportunities worldwide.  By 2002, Aberdeen estimates that the 
Managed Services market will total $36 billion, or 2.6% of the 
projected $1.4 trillion worldwide telecommunications services 
industry.
The globalization of business is changing how both small and large 
companies think about networks.  The Internet provides a way for 
people to share ideas and information across great distances, and it 
is rapidly becoming a business tool.  Small companies can now 
benefit from the same technologies that have enabled workers to 
collaborate from around the world.  Medium-sized companies can 
now behave like large companies, and large companies can lower 
the costs of their enterprise networks.
Aberdeen presents the following discussion on Managed Services 
to explain what these services are and how they can improve

Chapter Two
Trends in Corporate Networks
Corporate networks are vital to enterprise communications, 
because most companies are not limited to a single building or 
locale.  Workers need to communicate with their colleagues, 
partners and customers, regardless of geography. 
Traditionally, corporate communications have been divided into 
two components: telephony and data communications.  Telephony 
has been managed by a telecommunications department, and data 
communications have been managed by an Information 
Technology (IT) department.
In the 1990’s, data traffic has grown significantly, and it now 
comprises a large component of internal corporate 
communications.  IT managers now operate large wide-area 
corporate networks that connect branch offices and data centers 
around the world.  These networks are costly and, at times, 
inefficient.  They are also redundant, as they operate in parallel to 
a corporate voice network.
Large companies can benefit from consolidating their voice and 
data networks onto infrastructures that capitalize on network 
technologies such as: Asynchronous Transfer Mode (ATM), Frame 
Relay and Internet Protocol (IP).  This is a labor-intensive process 
that requires a broad range of expertise.  IT managers’ tasks are 
complicated by the variety of suppliers and service providers 
involved to build their enterprise networks.
Though these next-generation corporate networks are based upon 
new technologies, their composition has also been strategically 
altered by third-party solutions.  Examples of such solutions 
include Managed Telecommunications Services to replace network 
operations that have traditionally been sourced from within an 
enterprise.  Further, these particular Managed Services are not 
provided from traditional corporate outsourcing suppliers, such as 
Xerox or EDS, but, rather, they are provided by telecommunications 
companies whose core expertise lies in the operation of 
telecommunications networks.
With the incorporation of Managed Services, more and more 
networks are “virtual networks” within a service provider’s

The Early Years
Large enterprises have been the primary builders of proprietary 
corporate networks.  These enterprises have the financial 
resources to build out private networks and support a dedicated IT 
staff to oversee network operations, management and 
maintenance.
In the early 1980s, corporate infrastructures were cobbled together 
with a mix of proprietary-based hardware and applications.  Large 
enterprises relied on both T-1 landlines and Very Small Aperture 
Terminal (VSAT) satellite networks to link back remote branch 
offices to the corporate headquarters or to interconnect branch 
offices.  Further, these enterprises invested large sums of money in 
customized applications for internal messaging systems, inventory 
controls, invoicing and other functions.  In order to link dispersed 
sites, enterprises leased public wide area services to complement 
their internal infrastructures.
The first public wide area network services applied to voice 
communications only.  Interexchange carriers were the primary 
providers of such services, selling time division multiplexed (TDM) 
dedicated T-1 lines.  Enterprises linked private branch exchange 
(PBX) trunks across multiple locations into a virtual private 
network (VPN).  Voice VPNs bypass the public switched telephone 
network and allow four and five digit dialing between any phone 
on the corporate networks.  Since these connection costs are fixed 
on a per-line basis, enterprises are able to control their inter-office 
long distance telephony costs.
The virtual private network soon evolved to include virtual private 
data networks in the mid-1980s.  Data VPNs are also wide area 
network services.  These services interconnect dispersed computer 
networks to facilitate the exchange of enterprise applications.  Data 
transport services were first offered by interexchange carriers.  For 
example, AT&T, MCI and Sprint lease dedicated T-1 lines to the 
public switched data network and offer X.25 services while data 
service providers offer similar T-1 links (leased wholesale from 
carriers and repackaged) and services.

The late 1980s marked a fundamental shift in corporate network 
history.  During this time, technological advances and enterprise 
trends — facilitated by market deregulation — collaborated to 
expand providers’ service repertoires.  As new services reached the 
market, IT managers used them to replace traditional structures.
Technology
While T-1 leased lines are robust and secure, they are a redundant 
and expensive solution.  Considering that one T-1 dedicated 
connection ranges between $2,000 and $5,000 per month, 
enterprises that use separate connections for voice and data traffic 
pay for more capacity than they need.
X.25, Frame Relay and ATM services are alternatives to T-1 
dedicated connections.  They transport network data traffic over 
virtual circuits rather than dedicated, clear channel circuits.
This is important, because computer traffic is inherently bursty, 
commonly using about 10% of capacity.  When computers do send 
data between each other, they use as much capacity as they can for 
a short period of time (the burst).  Packet networks are best suited 
for this type of traffic, because they can consolidate bandwidth 
across multiple users and network segments, averaging the overall 
network traffic to some percentage of the available bandwidth.
Frame- and cell-based protocol solutions also facilitate dynamic 
network configuration.  They employ statistical multiplexing which 
increases the ratio between network access and backbone 
bandwidth from a 1:1 correlation to 10:1 correlation or higher.  By 
increasing the access to bandwidth ratio, statistical multiplexing:

Eliminates the need for separate connections for voice 
and multimedia traffic; and,

Allows more voice, video and data traffic to be carried per 
connection.
It is very difficult for IT managers to predict data traffic patterns 
on a long-term basis.  Statistical multiplexing allows a network to 
accommodate the bursty nature of data transmissions.

Technology Innovations: TCP/IP
The introduction of TCP/IP protocol networks — the Internet in 
particular — has had a resounding impact on how enterprises craft 
corporate networks.  The public Internet is a global phenomenon, 
connecting users from over 150 countries.  Many enterprises 
envision the Internet as an optimal data transmission medium.  
Rather than employ private TDM leased lines or public network 
Frame Relay, SONET or ATM solutions, enterprises employ IP-based 
VPNs, commonly referred to as intranets and extranets, to 
communicate with employees, business partners, suppliers and 
customers.
IP-based VPNs have similar characteristics to other VPN solutions.  
IP-based VPNs allow enterprise applications to be Web-enabled and 
can be viewed through a browser interface.  Also, IP-based VPNs 
provide for additional applications such as e-mail and internal 
enterprise Web sites to facilitate the dissemination of corporate 
information.  Intranets easily support internal Web sites for 
corporate telephone directories, human resource information, style 
manuals, in-house chat forums and message boards.
With the advent of the TCP/IP protocol, the Internet and its 
permutations, data VPNs have been taken to new heights.  
Aberdeen predicts greater adoption of intranet and extranet 
solutions in the next five years as security solutions for data 
transmission are improved (Layer 2 tunneling protocols).
Enterprise Attitudes
During the late 1980s, IT managers largely understood the 
outsourcing concept and many accepted it as a viable solution to 
their network needs.  Unfortunately, by the 1990s, they considered 
the original outsourcing contracts to be failures for not anticipating 
future enterprise growth and needs.  Early adopters of outsourcing 
solutions ended up renegotiating their service contracts or outright 
cancelled their contracts, citing poor services and differences in 
corporate culture as the main reasons for rejecting outsourcing.
After many early adopters revealed the litany of problems they 
encountered with outsourced services, IT managers concluded that 
they would like the option of contracting third-party services on a 
more limited basis. 
Rather than relinquish complete network control, IT managers 
opined that targeted network services, or Managed Services, such 
as Network Connectivity, Web Hosting  and Managed Firewall

Chapter Three
Managed Services Defined
As enterprises face increased competition on a global level, they 
are forced to improve production efficiencies and reduce 
expenditures on non-core functions.  Each enterprise strives to be 
Number One in its market by providing goods and services better, 
faster and cheaper than its competitors.  The corporate networks of 
the 1980s are largely categorized as a non-core enterprise function; 
however, they retain their status as strategic assets.  In fact, 
corporate networks are more important than ever as they greatly 
streamline internal operations, speed up time to market and 
improve enterprise efficiencies.
As small and large enterprises alike face increased competition in 
their respective markets, they must seek out ways to reduce 
internal costs, cut down production schedules and communicate 
quickly with dispersed corporate divisions.  Such efficiencies can 
be achieved by implementing and maintaining an efficient 
communications infrastructure.
Improved communications networks can provide direct and 
indirect savings.  An enterprise may reduce its network 
expenditures by replacing T-1 dedicated connections with Frame 
Relay circuits or, they may maintain their expenditures but receive 
increased network functionality which ultimately supports future 
profit-generating activities.
Managed Services enable better virtual voice and data networks 
than the previous available market solutions.  The vast 
improvements in frame and cell-based third-party networks, along 
with the advent of the public Internet and its permutations, give 
enterprises more options in crafting robust, flexible and redundant 
networks. 
It is important to note that network applications will remain the 
driving force behind how virtual networks are configured; how 
third-party service is evaluated; how network performance is 
determined; and how important the enterprise network is 
perceived internally.

Managed Services Defined
Managed Services are targeted network-related IT and 
telecommunication solutions that replace particular corporate 
network functions.  Managed Services leverage third-party 
infrastructure, resources and technical expertise to transport voice, 
video and data traffic.  These services support and enhance virtual 
private networks (VPNs). 
Virtual private networks are private enterprise voice and data 
networks configured within third-party network infrastructure 
and supported through public or private network services.  VPNs 
provide a communications structure for linking the corporate 
network to:

Dispersed enterprise networks; 

Telecommuters and mobile workforces;

Suppliers and business partners; and,

Current and potential customers.
Further, they improve corporate communications and efficiencies 
so that enterprises can dedicate maximum resources to their core 
competencies.

Future Trends and Challenges
While Managed Services are currently contracted to extend 
corporate network capabilities and off-load network 
responsibilities to third parties, the second wave will be spurred by 
the ability to contract switched virtual circuits (SVCs) and 
bandwidth on-demand.  Only service providers with high capacity 
networks will be able to extend this type of network functionality 
to end users.
Further, the Internet and its permutations will affect the growth of 
Managed Services since they provide for heightened network 
functionality.
Switched Virtual Circuits (SVCs)
Switched virtual circuits provide the most flexible network 
configuration possible.  The concept is much like making a regular 
telephone call.  A number is dialed and a connection is established. 
When the call is completed, the connection is terminated.  SVCs are 
very similar to dynamic connections that can adjust instantly to 
network traffic demands.  A connection is established according to 
traffic characteristics and torn down as quickly as a completed 
phone call.  Unlike its predecessors, permanent virtual circuits 
(PVCs), SVCs do not require parameters to be pre-set.  Once a 
transmission session is completed, the circuits revert back to a 
temporary state until the next session.
Bandwidth on Demand
Bandwidth on demand will require a customer to pay for the exact 
amount of bandwidth used.  The customer will determine how 
mission-critical the data is and how it should be sent.  The end user 
will set the transmission parameters based on network availability, 
network latency and quality of service concerns.  Infonet is moving 
toward this future reality.  The company currently offers the most 
comprehensive managed Frame Relay service (network 
connectivity).  End users are able to customize the committed 
information rate (CIR) parameters for each permanent virtual 
circuit employed, based on network delay, variance in delay, 
throughput and burst allowance.

While data VPNs existed prior to the advent of the Internet, the 
Internet has taken this concept to another level.  Web-enabled
