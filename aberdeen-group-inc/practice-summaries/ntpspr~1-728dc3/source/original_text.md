NT Professional Services ¾ Practice Definition
Microsoft’s Windows NT is an operating environment that is 
growing rapidly in terms of market share on the personal 
computer desktop, as well as in entry-level server, 
workgroup, and midrange server markets.
NT Professional Services are those services related to the 
deployment of NT-based systems, applications, and databases 
within an enterprise information infrastructure — as well as 
services related to the training of Information Systems staff 
and the user community.

The 5 Ss
As IS managers have told Aberdeen that they are rapidly 
discovering several inherent weaknesses of the Microsoft NT 
operating environment.    The most prominent of these weaknesses 
include what Aberdeen terms the “5 Ss”:
1.    Scalability
 
  (not only at the systems performance level — but 
also the scaling of the user population, the database, and related 
applications);
2.    Stability
 
  (availability);
3.    Systems Management
 
 ;
4.    Storage Management
 
  (especially related to automated systems 
back-up/restore, as well as to setting user limits on the use of 
disk volumes); and,
5.    Security
 
  (particularly in large, multi-domain environments).
And, adding to these NT operating environment issues, IS 
managers have also discovered complexities related to the 
integration of NT servers with other servers (Unix, AS/400, 
mainframes) within their existing enterprise information 
infrastructure.    And all of these NT shortcomings and integration 
issues contribute to NT’s perceived lack of reliability and suitability 
for enterprise-grade computing.
As a result of NT shortcomings, as well as related interoperability/ 
integration issues, Aberdeen surmises that the need for services 
related to NT infrastructure design and deployment will be in 
strong demand in the near term ¾ especially when Microsoft 
introduces its next revision of NT (Windows NT 5.0) ¾ a revision 
designed to address many of today’s NT shortcomings.    IS 
managers should plan accordingly.

The Sources for Professional Services ¾ Supplier Findings
Despite NT operating environment shortcomings, Aberdeen has 
found example-after-example of well-implemented, well-managed, 
reliable NT server environments.    And, when queried by Aberdeen 
about how they successfully managed to create their stable NT 
environments, most IS managers indicated that they made use of 
external NT professional services from one or more of the 
following organizations:
1. Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) system suppliers; 
2. Independent Software Vendors    (ISVs);
3. Independent Professional Services Providers (ISPSs) ¾ formerly 
called Systems Integrators ¾ these suppliers now provide 
business strategic planning services, training, project 
management, and a myriad of other services as well as systems 
integration services; or,
4. Resellers. 
Market Sizing ¾ Growth Analysis
Aberdeen’s forecast shows that in 1996, when NT platform revenue 
was $750 million ¾ NT Professional Services revenues reached $3 
billion.    (This figure can be corroborated by adding NT-related 
revenues as reported or estimated to have been collected by NT 
Professional Services providers in 1996).
Other factors that validate this estimate include:
1. In 1996, the lion’s share of NT platforms sold were either 
desktops or workgroup servers used for file and print services 
(a simple platform to install when compared to today’s more 
complex NT application and transaction server environments);
2. In that year, IS managers and systems suppliers told Aberdeen 
that NT deployment costs were low (at about $6 for every dollar 
spent on the platform) ¾ making the total services market about 
$4.5 billion ($750m X $6); 
3. But Aberdeen notes that not every dollar spent went to outside 
professional services firms.    Many rugged individualists 
prototyped their own NT servers ¾ so a percentage of the IS 
budget for NT systems deployment went for internal IS 
expenses. 
Aberdeen observes that, because few enterprises had staffs with 
direct NT deployment experience, external NT Professional

Services garnered almost 2/3 of the available NT deployment

In 1997, the worldwide NT Professional Services market reached 
almost $20 billion ¾ as typical systems/applications professional 
services costs rose significantly from the 1996 figures ¾ to $12 for 
every dollar spent on an NT platform.    Reasons for this large 
increase in NT Professional Services costs include:

NT moved vigorously into the application server space ¾ up 
from the less complex workgroup file and print server space;

Application suppliers such as SAP, BaaN, and Computer 
Associates released products on NT-based platforms ¾ but had 
little in the way of automated tools that would serve to simplify 
the installation, tuning, and management of NT server 
environments.    As a result, costs for deploying NT-based 
servers rose significantly; 

Further exacerbating this situation was the paucity of 
management tools needed to control and tune NT server 
environments.    Although Microsoft had announced its Zero 
Administration for Windows (ZAW) initiative ¾ the company 
did not deliver the key management products needed to reduce 
the cost-to-manage (often linked to cost-of-ownership) its NT 
environments ¾ leading to further escalation in the cost-to-
deploy and cost-to-manage an installed NT environment; and, 

Additionally, Microsoft introduced several NT enhancements 
that added to the complexity involved in deploying NT-based 
server environments ¾ most notably, Microsoft’s Cluster Server 
and Transaction Server environments.    The associated learning 
curve to exploit and deploy these technologies added to 
professional services deployment costs.

Market Positioning
The myth: Amdahl is a maker of big-iron — mainframes.
The reality: Amdahl is a systems integrator — a maker of CMOS-
based enterprise servers, Sun UltraSPARC-based Unix servers, and 
Intel-based NT servers — and a provider of cross-platform 
professional services and related systems support — with a 
particular emphasis on NT-to-mainframe integration products and 
services. 
As the base of Microsoft Windows NT desktop and servers 
increases within the enterprise, IS managers are looking for 
technologies and services that will assist them in optimally linking 
NT environments with existing mainframe data and enterprise 
application environments.    These IS managers are cognizant that 
although Microsoft’s Windows NT can run the latest/greatest, 
client/server applications that individual, workgroup, and 
departmental users are demanding ¾ they are finding that NT 
lacks the processing power needed to execute the enterprise’s most 
complex high-end, data-and-transaction intensive production-level 
applications. 
Most Information Systems (IS) managers are surprised to find that 
Amdahl — a major computer systems supplier most closely 
associated with Series 390 type enterprise servers — offers an 
extensive and distinctive line of Windows NT-based products 
specifically focused on providing optimized On-line Transaction 
Processing (OLTP) between NT-based servers and mainframe back-
ends.    These products include Amdahl’s line of high-performance 
NT server platforms (EnVista); and an Amdahl-created, high-speed

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group's 1998 practice summary on NT Professional Services analyzing the market for consulting and systems integration services surrounding Windows NT deployments. The report quantifies the NT professional services market at $3 billion in 1996 growing to $20 billion in 1997 and projects growth to $110 billion by 2001. It profiles fourteen service providers across four categories — OEMs (Amdahl, Compaq, Digital, Data General, HP, IBM, NCR, Tandem, Unisys), ISVs (Microsoft, SAP), IPSPs (Coopers & Lybrand, CSC, Deloitte, EDS), and Resellers (Entex, MicroAge).
