Information Systems for Retail 
Description, Focus and Benefits
Aberdeen’s Retail Information Systems practice follows 
Information Technology (IT) market trends and technical 
developments as it applies to retail organizations.    Aberdeen 
tracks numerous providers of IT products and services in this 
market, including application software suppliers, systems suppliers 
and professional services firms.    Aberdeen specializes in the 
market positioning and tactical challenges faced by retail solutions 
providers, as well as the opportunities and obstacles encountered 
by user organizations as they seek to apply new information 
technologies to business problems.    Aberdeen reviews retail 
information technologies from task-specific systems (Point-of-Sale, 
inventory management) to enterprise-class integrated systems.
The retail industry uses multiple channels to market to consumers. 
Aberdeen studies retail IT by subdividing the industry into three 
selling tracts: in-store, catalog /direct marketing and the Internet.    
Catalog houses, direct marketers and larger in-store retailers have 
continually invested in progressive technological developments, 
while smaller retail shop owners historically have been slow to 
implement IT advances.
Today all these channels are challenged with both opportunity and 
competition from an emerging segment — the Internet.    In 
addition to creating a presence and realizing revenue from a web 
site, retailers who adopt technologies such as store automation, 
workforce and inventory management, and data collection and 
mining will increase their ability to compete in the future.    With 
the construction and continual development of the Internet, 
retailers literally have at their fingertips the potential to reach 
customers globally.
Additionally, Aberdeen evaluates emerging technologies and their 
effects on the industry (such as the smart card which is 
reconstructing the retail payment infrastructure).    Aberdeen helps 
its clients understand IT developments and issues, so that they can 
effectively plan their investments in information technologies.

Executive Summary
Aberdeen Group's Retail Industry Service follows the information 
technology (IT) market trends and technical developments within 
retailing. Aberdeen defines retail as all transfers of goods and 
associated services for remuneration direct to the consumer.    
Major categories include:
·
General and mass merchandisers (e.g., department stores, 
discount clubs)
·
Specialty shops (e.g., apparel and footwear, electronics, 
florists)
·
Markets (e.g., supermarkets, convenience stores, 
groceries)
·
Drug stores and pharmacies
Aberdeen’s Retail Industry Service does not track eating and 
drinking establishments.
In general, retailers distribute to consumers through stores.    
However, Aberdeen includes within its research purview non-store 
retailing such as catalogs, home direct marketing, and e-commerce 
to consumers over the Internet.
From task-specific retail information technologies to systems 
integrated across the enterprise, Aberdeen reviews application 
software suppliers, systems suppliers and professional services 
firms as they relate to the retail industry.
IT managers within the retail industry face a daunting series of 
challenges.    Above all, they must leverage their technology 
investment from a cost center into a strategic tool to open up new 
revenue opportunities.    Meanwhile, suppliers must recognize the 
emerging business and technological needs of retail enterprises.    
They must be aware of changing practices and processes in 
retailing in order to plan and execute solutions to address these 
developments now and for the future. 
Aberdeen research has pinpointed the following major issues and 
directions within the retail sector:

Chapter Two
Summary of Findings
Retail has a reputation for conservative IT buying tendencies.    
Aberdeen, however, is convinced that IS executives in retail will 
rely heavily upon IT in the future.    The transformation has, in fact, 
already begun.
In the past, retail information systems tended to focus on broad 
financial record-keeping, usually with a connection directly to the 
interface with the customer (the point-of-sale).    Today, while the 
traditional foci remain in place, retailers increasingly recognize 
that the effective, innovative use of IT can cut costs across the 
enterprise, while generating new revenue.    IT, in other words, is 
more than a filing cabinet:    it can become a tool.
Retailers are moving toward a consensus that competitive 
advantage does not come from developing custom applications 
tuned to the unique environment of a particular company, but 
rather in effectively utilizing the information that rests in the 
system.    Aberdeen notes that this often entails a shift to industry-
standard systems (e.g., Unix, NT) and off-the-shelf software.
One reason for this change is to facilitate the integration of 
information, 
a task of special complexity and urgency in the retail sector.    
Information managers in retail face multiple tasks of integration 
that mirror and 
intensify one another:
·
The trend in the industry has been to integration, both of 
businesses (notably a series of mergers that continues in 
full force, for example, in drug stores) and in business 
functions (for instance, the one-stop-shopping goal of Wal-
Mart).
·
IS organizations must not only coordinate the disparate 
environments they have inherited from mergers and 
other forms of integration, but they have to bridge the 
disaggregated, underutilized pools of data within legacy 
systems and emerging technologies. 
·
Just as important, IS organizations are working to 
integrate across business functions (financial systems,

Key Aberdeen Findings
IT Investments
·
In 1997 the U.S. retail industry expended $11 billion on IT, 
a figure that represented about one-half of one percent of 
total retail sales.    Over 70% of this sum went to external 
suppliers of hardware, software, and services.
·
Retailers are investing in IT:    acquiring new applications, 
making enhancements to systems, and appropriating 
emerging technologies.    Nevertheless, most of a retailer’s 
IT budget continues to go toward maintaining existing 
systems. 
·
Retailers are making the investment in information 
technology for a variety of reasons:    to gain a competitive 
edge, to support future growth, to maintain current levels 
of operation, or to catch up to other retailers 
technologically.
·
Because retail is a low-margin business, efficiency is a 
condition of survival.    However, operational efficiency 
must not sacrifice customer service, which Aberdeen 
believes should become the goal of IT in retail. 
·
The ongoing transformation within retailing by the 
effective 
deployment of technology is drawing attention from IT 
suppliers who recognize a new, remunerative market to 
exploit.

Applications
·
Previously the application emphasis had been on custom-
developed software; however, retailers have come to 
realize that pre-packaged applications can meet most of 
their needs with a bit of tweaking.
·
Major buying criteria when purchasing retail applications 
include:    scalability, functionality, extensibility, vendor 
viability, response time, price, security, user friendliness, 
availability of development skills, provision of source code, 
installed base, timely delivery of new releases, frequency of 
new releases, and cost effectiveness to business benefit.
·
Key differentiators for software suppliers specializing in 
the 
retail industry include:    the level of integration with retail-
specific elements, the breadth of coverage where not fully 
integrated, the 
effective deployment of client-server technologies and 
interfaces, and Internet-enabled extensions.
·
The software industry is now moving to integrate vertical 
expertise with broad enterprise applications, like 
Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP).    However, much of 
this integration is still fairly generic to broad retail 
functions and leaves room (by in-house developers today, 
by new players in the future) for further tailoring to 
specific types of product and to different types of retail 
establishment.
·
Many of the established retail Independent Software 
Vendors (ISVs) are merging or partnering with the large IT 
companies, particularly the enterprise software suppliers 
that have recognized the need for vertical-specific 
technologies and applications.    The specialists know the 
retail landscape, while the big guys have the resources.

New Opportunities and Challenges
·
The Internet, on one hand, is emerging as a critical new 
channel of communication with consumers.    On the other 
hand, it often represents a competitive threat for retailers 
who do not incorporate it as a regular option. 
·
The Internet is also being explored as an inexpensive 
means of communication to share calendars and 
databases between 
retailers and the supply chain.    With this connection 
retailers are able to immediately perceive fluctuations in 
the supply chain and anticipate potential ramifications 
before they impact business. 
·
Many large retailers have taken significant steps to make 
their systems Year 2000-compliant and are striving to 
finish a year in advance to give themselves a testing 
buffer.    Unfortunately, other retailers, large to small, 
have lagged in addressing the problem. 
·
Major retailers have begun exploring revenue 
opportunities abroad.    This is easiest when the retailer is 
itself a brand-name that can attract customers, as with 
Wal-Mart’s expansion into Mexico.
·
This direct route is often impractical, since consumers may 
resent non-nationals seeming to dictate their choices.    For 
this reason, international retail is often run through quasi-
independent corporations that are locally staffed and

Infrastructure Management
Infrastructure management involves the supervision and 
implementation of core IS activities that are generally common to 
any business:    software development and management, hardware 
and software 
maintenance, network management, and security. 
Financial Systems
Financial systems plan, manage and control the financial assets of a 
company.    Typical subdivisions include:    accounts payable, 
accounts 
receivable, asset management, general ledger, payroll, cash 
management, human resources management, benefits 
administration, purchasing (non-merchandise), credit management, 
and sales audit.    Financial systems are not unique in retailing, but 
due to the nature of retail business, it is 
imperative that all retail-specific activities coordinate with them.
There are some retail-specific financial systems, notably credit 
management.    For instance, when a retail establishment maintains a 
private-brand credit card, the retailer needs credit management 
systems to oversee credit-related issues (e.g., application processing, 
authorization, and 
delinquency). 
Supply Chain Management
Supply chain management is a macro integration of information 
designed to stock retail establishments with product in the most 
expeditious manner.    It reaches back to the product supplier who, 
in 
a full supply chain management implementation, is privy to a 
retailer’s sales and inventory information.    It proceeds through 
the distribution of goods to the warehouse, into the retail 
establishment, and across the merchandise management and in-
store system functions.
Merchandise Management
Merchandise management systems focus on the control of product 
from inventory to store display.    Typical activities include:    stock 
replenishment, purchase orders and returns, price changes and 
promotions, and merchandise placement.
In-Store Systems
In-store systems manage the daily operation of a retail 
establishment.    The primary focus is typically the network of

Issues and Challenges
IT users within retailing face many of the same tasks, threats, and 
opportunities as other U.S. industries, but the issues bring a special 
urgency in retailing as IS executives balance margin pressures 
against the chance to draw higher sales volumes through IT.
Impact of Major IT Trends
The Internet
The Internet has spawned a vibrant e-commerce system selling 
apparel, beauty aids, bicycles, books, cards, cigars, computer 
hardware and software, other electronics, flowers, food, games, 
gardening items, movies and videos, recorded and sheet music, 
office supplies, outdoors and sporting goods, toys, wine and spirits, 
and many other items — even automobiles. 
The ease of transaction and the depth of product information 
available have already made the Internet a viable retail organ.    
More effective marketing, improvements in technology, and 
increased familiarity with the Web can overcome remaining 
consumer inertia.
A number of large retailers are attempting to leverage their high 
visibility by going onto the Internet.    The Web has spawned, for 
example, online versions of Bloomingdale’s, JC Penney, and Macy’s. 
However, the most effective Web sales engines to date continue to 
be specialty retailers (e.g., Amazon.com).
The e-commerce alternative is proving difficult for many retailers 
to 
integrate into their corporate strategy.    In particular, they often 
attempt to protect their existing franchise by running Web-based 
sales as a 
separate business (for instance, BarnesandNoble.com requires 
returns to be mailed back — buyers cannot take them to the closest 
Barnes & Noble store, even with proof of purchase).    Aberdeen 
believes that this segregation is not viable over the longer term. 
The Internet also helps retailers tighten the supply chain across all 
trading partners.    For example, some companies are giving 
product suppliers passwords into a structured part of their data

Transition to Open Systems
The retail sector was in general comparatively slow to embrace 
open

financial systems.    Aberdeen sees a wider recognition within the

Transition to Client-Server Computing
One reason Aberdeen expects NT to become the dominant open

computing.

likely to be segregated from the data on the proprietary backroom 
and in-store systems.    In many retail organizations client queries

operating platform are less relevant.    By the time many large 
retailers are ready to transition to a single operating environment, 
NT may be ready for them.
