Preface
Aberdeen’s Information Security Practice 
Description, Focus and Benefits
Information security is about self-managing risk in order to insure 
that production information, systems and networks are reliable, 
complete and available for authorized users to create value for the 
enterprise. Eliminating risk is impossible and impractical, but 
managing risk is necessary for critical production systems, 
networks, information and business procedures. Striking a balance 
between too little and too much security, with rapidly 
discontinuous market and technology change, is an on-going high-
wire act that can easily slip.
Security is hot! An arcane topic that was once confined to the IS 
basement has been transformed by line-of-business managers, 
executive managers and even board members in order to assure 
that the brand equity of the enterprise and the trust placed in the 
enterprise is not impaired. Running naked is no longer an option, 
since access through networked applications, interconnections and 
Web-computing components is now provided for partners, 
suppliers and customers.
Proactive users achieve appropriate risk-management through the 
deployment and management of security solutions intimately 
linked with IS procedures and information-handling policy. 
Effective security solutions and procedures can enhance the flow 
of information in order to support the business of the enterprise, 
instead of hampering business procedures. But security solutions 
span a complex range of point-solutions that are incomplete by 
themselves. In order to adequately manage risk, users often 
integrate security componentware with other security solutions, 
systems, networks and applications.
Aberdeen’s Information Security practice is a vital part of its 
Network Services research area. All Fortune 1000 enterprises 
spend money on security products. And most of the Fortune 500 
spend IS budget moneys on staff for specifying, integrating, 
deploying and maintaining information security, financial and

Chapter One
Executive Summary
A major change to the enterprise’s computing infrastructure is 
underway as suppliers, customers and partners across the Net are 
being integrated with production business systems and procedures. 
Unlike the earlier shift to client-server computing, the transition to 
web computing is easier and less expensive, because the enterprise 
is able to:

Reuse production systems, applications and historical 
data;

Simply integrate new web components into the existing IS 
infrastructure;

Gain market advantages by deploying customer and 
supplier applications in three months or less; and

Rapidly overhaul applications and even throw them away 
with less financial impact.
But, unlike client-server environments where the enterprise can 
afford to run naked, Web computing requires security in order to 
be deployed.    The problem, according to Information Systems (IS) 
and Line of Business (LOB) executives, is that information security 
is now the foundation for safely protecting production business 
information, relationships with customers and suppliers, and the 
reputation of the enterprise. 
IS decision makers purchase, deploy, and manage security 
solutions and hire service providers in order to: 1) regulate access 
to IS resources, applications and information; 2) identify and 
manage the risk posed by the use and misuse of computing 
resources; and 3) integrate, deploy and maintain security.
IS relies on information security tools and procedures in order to 
minimize or avoid risk to the enterprise from:

Compromised business relationships;

Lost business opportunity and market-timing advantages;

Loss of information and access to information;

Sources for Security Solutions
Security solutions are available to IS decision makers from 
suppliers of: 
1. Embedded component products such as systems, 
applications, and network systems; 
2. Component security products such as user credentials, 
firewalls, anti-virus and intrusion detection products; and 
3. Enterprise security solutions such as cross-platform 
identification and authentication servers, authorization 
and access control servers, and single sign on solutions.
Fragmented and Non-interoperable Security Solutions are 
the Norm 
Veteran IS executives state that security solutions for client-server 
computing are too fragmented, requiring expensive integration 
and configuration efforts before security can be deployed.    Worse, 
managing security across many non-interoperable security 
components, different business lines, and geographies is too costly. 
As a result, IS long ago refocused on managing security for critical 
information.
Security solutions are fragmented because each component 
supplier focuses on providing security for its product.    And, with 
increasing attention being focused on security, component 
suppliers are all too eager to fill demand for more security.    
Unfortunately, more component security means further 
fragmentation for IS decision makers.    The introduction of feature-
laden but non-interoperable security solutions is what veteran IS 
managers are steadfastly avoiding.

Shrinking Market Share: Stand-alone Firewalls
After reaching commodity-status, firewall-based network access 
controls are being embedded into systems, gateways and routers.    
The general-purpose stand-alone market for firewalls will change 
as buyers shift toward embedded solutions through the year 2000. 
The stand-alone firewall market will fragment into high-end 
solutions capable of handling extremely high throughout 
requirements and low-end solutions for small enterprises, home 
offices, small offices and individual users.
Increasing Market Share: Network Scanning and Intrusion 
Detection
Fed up with non-interoperable security-auditing systems, IS

solutions in order to better manage risk.    This category will go 
mainstream by 1999 with automated rules-engines, common 
enterprise security auditing databases, risk and threat databases, 
and vulnerability assessment engines for responding dynamically 
to events on the enterprise network.

The VPN Market Shakeout
Virtual Private Networks are currently fueled by rapid cost-
recovery as expensive leased lines are replaced by low-cost, private 
data exchange over the Net.    Being deployed by enterprises for 
linking remote employees and remote enterprise sites with 
headquarters, investments in VPNs are being recovered in one to 
three months.    But the VPN market will undergo rapid change by 
the year 2000.    The choices that users will be faced with between 
now and the year 2000 include: building VPNs, obtaining VPN 
services through telecommunications or service providers, and 
employing embedded VPN services with digital certificate systems 
and services. 
The Emergence of Less-painful Auditing
Auditing is currently one of the most painful and laborious 
activities for IS, because non-interoperable audit logs are written 
by each major computing component.    Consequently, today’s 
auditing is performed on a best-efforts basis, if at all.    New tools 
will emerge for automating the auditing task and integrating 
features found in network scanning and intrusion-detection 
solutions.    Moreover, some of the leading suppliers of intrusion-
detection and network-scanning tools will be bringing integrated 
systems to market that will accomplish similar objectives for IS.
The Dawning of Plug-compatible Security Systems
Prescient IS managers are demanding modular, plug-compatible 
security solutions, and a few suppliers are beginning to respond.    A 
few plug-compatible security solutions will emerge before 2000 that 
will enable IS decision makers to deploy:

Multiple I&A systems and user credentials for managing 
passwords, tokens, and digital certificates;

Integrated risk-management suites for anti virus, applets, 
network scanning, intrusion detection and audit;

Web-server based I&A and IAC systems to integrate 
employees, suppliers, customers, and partners; and

Single sign on solutions for providing suppliers, partners, 
customers and employees with seamless access to 
authorized information while providing IS with simpler 
management tools and better control.

Issues and Challenges
Transition from client-server to Network and Web-computing.    A 
major change to the enterprise computing infrastructure is 
underway.    But, unlike the change from host computing to client-
server computing, the transition to web computing is much easier 
and less expensive than earlier transitions because the enterprise 
is able to:

Reuse existing IS systems, applications and historical 
data;

Simply integrate new web components into the existing IS 
infrastructure;

Gain market advantages by deploying critical customer 
and supplier applications in three months or less; and

Rapidly overhaul applications and even throw them away 
without financial impact.
The problem and challenge for the enterprise is that security is 
now a central foundation for proceeding safely to web computing.
Integration of suppliers, partners and customers.    The business 
imperative to interconnect customers, suppliers and partners with 
the enterprise’s business procedures through Web front-ends and 
Net-based services is accelerating the demand for security 
solutions to manage the risk that the interconnection solutions will 
open gaping security holes that allow intruders to access the 
enterprise’s information infrastructure.    As a result, security has 
moved from the backwater of IS to the front desk of the steering 
committee and to the lips of senior executives of the enterprise.    
The challenge for IS executives is to articulate to the lines of 
business what is needed and why.
Integration of security-components.    IS has traditionally relied on 
component security solutions from system, network equipment, 
and 
packaged-applications suppliers to regulate access to the 
enterprise’s systems, networks, applications, and information.

The enterprise will move toward common I&A solutions for 
regulating user (employee, customer, supplier and partner) access. 
The challenge for IS decision makers is to select appropriate

Managing actual risk.    The opening of the enterprise’s IS
