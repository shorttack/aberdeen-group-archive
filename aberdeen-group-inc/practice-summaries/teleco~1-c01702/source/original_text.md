Chapter One
Executive Summary
Telecommunications is changing rapidly. Deregulation of world 
telecom markets has introduced a new generation of competitive 
carriers, challenging incumbent providers. The growth of the 
Internet and other data networks is expected to change the very 
nature of the public switched telephone network (PSTN), as 
telecom service providers migrate their costly time division 
multiplexed (TDM) network infrastructure to a packet network 
more closely resembling a traditional data network. Emerging 
carriers are adopting these new network technologies and may 
leapfrog the incumbents in a number of markets.
As carriers evolve their networks to packet infrastructures, they 
need to adopt technologies and strategies to integrate voice and 
data traffic both physically and logically. Physically, it is relatively 
easy to combine the traffic on a single network – in many cases, 
voice and data traffic traverse the same network, but they are on 
separate parts of the network that are logically disparate. Logically, 
carriers need to coordinate existing PSTN signaling, such as SS7, 
ISUP, and TCAP, with data networks, including the Internet. In the 
discussion of Internet, or IP-based, telephony, many of the issues 
are about coordinating signaling between the PSTN and public IP 
networks and supporting IP telephony services with the 
appropriate back-office software for provisioning and billing. 
Each of these issues is a new challenge for telecom hardware and 
software vendors to address. In addition to making the next-
generation public network function, technology suppliers will need 
to develop new schemes to ensure that the reliability and 
performance of the new network will equal that of the existing 
one. Currently, public data network segments operate at an average 
of 95% reliability, often with scheduled maintenance and down 
times — this amounts to 36.5 hours of down time per month. In 
contrast, the PSTN offers reliability in excess of 99.9999%, with 
network outages totaling 53 minutes for an entire year. This type of 
performance is what we expect from our phone system, and it is 
costly to provide; it is uncertain how costly it will be to provide this 
type of reliability in the next-generation PSTN. How the underlying 
technologies evolve, and how open their architectures become, are

Summary of Findings
Aberdeen has identified dramatic shifts in:

The number and type of telecom service providers;

The vendors of hardware and software to these telcos; 
and

The telecom services sold – specifically, managed services.
The telecommunications industry is changing rapidly. Incumbent 
service providers are under attack from smaller, more nimble 
competitors who enter the market with data services for Internet 
access, wireless services, and high-speed connections. Incumbent 
equipment vendors face a significantly more competitive 
environment, as entrenched and competitive service providers 
alike look to data communications vendors for next-generation 
infrastructure. In addition, even though carriers continue to spend 
large amounts of money with the traditional vendors, such as 
Lucent or Nortel, they tend to distance themselves from these 
vendors in public announcements.
The issue of public, at least Wall Street, mindshare is increasingly 
important for today’s telco. Financial markets have changed the 
way that they evaluate carriers. Today, a public network, or even a 
plan to build a public network, has inherent value. Networks that 
were once considered as costs to be written down over a 15 to 30-
year timetable are now considered strategic assets. Nowhere is this 
more evident than in the financial markets, where next-generation 
carriers like Qwest and Level3 have raised billions of dollars for 
their network build-outs.
The dynamics of deregulation have resulted in an increased 
number of telecom service providers. As the number of carriers 
grows at a steady rate of 10% a year in the U.S., the number of 
interconnects among these carriers grows exponentially. 
Conversely, the dollar value of these interconnects is dropping. 
This factor creates opportunities for software vendors and for 
billing clearinghouse service bureaus. Interconnection between 
carriers will drive efficiencies in billing and Operation Support 
Systems (OSS) management. This is one of the key factors driving 
the telecom software industry.

Moreover, there is a great deal of uncertainty about the future of 
telcos and the companies who supply them with technologies. The 
core premise of Internet Protocol services is that they are “less 
expensive” than traditional, circuit switched services. In many 
cases, this is a fallacy, as today’s IP services are heavily subsidized 
by the PSTN.
For example, the local telephone network is subsidized by long 
distance services — long distance companies pay a per minute 
“access charge” for access to customers connected via a local 
telephone company. This has been a good thing for consumers, 
because it enables low-cost basic telephone service. However, 
Internet users, who have become the largest constituent of local 
telephone users, do not pay the same subsidy that long distance 
customers do.
As the Internet becomes more commonly accepted as a 
telecommunications medium, delivering a variety of 
communications services, it will have to bear the true cost of 
building, maintaining, and operating the underlying transport 
infrastructure, be that fiber optic, copper, wireless, or satellite.
But the presumption that packet networks are significantly less 
expensive than circuit-switched networks is incorrect. Each service 
will have to stand alone as a public network service, drawing in 
components of billing, customer care, network management, 
provisioning, services platforms, and physical network 
infrastructure. The Internet has not changed this fundamental 
issue. While IP provides a common services interface, it does not 
simplify the technical complexity and business processes involved 
in delivering services to hundreds of millions of people.
Also, the packet revolution ignores the traditional 
telecommunications business model. In 1998, Private Line 
revenues in the United States alone topped $10 billion in revenues. 
Frame Relay revenues are $2.5 billion, and IP services account for 
another $300 million. This is problematic, because IP services 
dominate the mindshare of the marketplace, but Private Line 
dominates the revenues.

Scale Versus Features
Often the features that enterprises look for in building private 
networks are much broader than the features that are actually 
provided as public network services. Scalability and reliability are 
the primary features that carriers look for in public network 
hardware, whereas broader feature sets tend to dominate private 
network hardware.
Back Office Software
While network management is an important issue for both public 
and private networks, public networks are built for the large scale 
deployment of services. This requires operational support systems 
to manage the provision of services across the infrastructure and 
business support systems to manage billing and customer care. 
These issues are paramount to any public telecommunications 
service provider, because they relate directly to customer 
satisfaction and the subsequent revenues associated with a job well 
done.
Back office software issues are becoming increasingly important in 
competitive telecom environments, because the back office can be 
the deciding factor that determines profit or loss. Further, as 
competitive carriers stress customer service as market 
differentiator, they need ways to ensure that they can effectively 
deliver the services that they sell.

operation of transmission as well as switching and services 
networks. The transmission segment is rapidly changing as

Practice Summary
Managed Services:
IT managers used to have very few choices as to how they built, 
managed and maintained their corporate networks. Technical 
advances and market deregulation have created new ways to build 
corporate computer networks. In addition to new networking 
technologies, there are many new types of telecommunications 
services that address the specific needs of corporate IT managers.
A network/IT manager has three ways to build a corporate 
network:

In-house construction of a private network;

Full outsourcing of network construction and operation; 
or,

Mix and match Managed Carrier Services (referred to as 
“Managed Services” throughout this report) to take over 
specific network functions.
Managed Services enable enterprises to focus on their core 
competencies. This is important as small and large businesses alike 
face increased global competition. Managed Services are an 
effective solution for enterprises that wish to rationalize their 
resources; increase network functionality; and create new business 
opportunities worldwide. By 2002, Aberdeen estimates that the 
Managed Services market will total $36 billion, or 2.6% of the 
projected $1.4 trillion telecommunications services industry.
The globalization of business is changing how both small and large 
companies think about networks. The Internet provides a way for 
people to share ideas and information across great distances, and it 
is rapidly becoming a business tool. Small and medium-sized 
companies can now benefit from the same technologies that larger 
companies use to enable workers to collaborate from around the 
world. Large companies, on the other hand, can use cutting-edge 
technologies to lower the costs of their enterprise networks.
This is a dramatic shift from the private line and earlier packet 
data networks. The economics of managed services makes it 
possible for the smallest of companies to have the largest of 
networks, spanning global geographies.

The fabric of the telecommunications network is under attack. Two 
factors – the entry of aggressive upstarts in the telecom service 
market and the emergence of the Internet – have completely 
undermined conventional notions of public network architectures. 
The business plans of new carriers, such as competitive local 
access providers (CLECs), Internet service providers (ISPs), and 
various backbone service providers, require network 
infrastructures that are inexpensive, yet which offer maximum 
control for operations, maintenance and provisioning. As it 
happens, many of these needs may be met by constructing a 
network using IP at its base, with distributed intelligence and 
features. Instead of designing current-generation telephone 
networks around strategically located central office switches, high-
capacity trunks and low-cost access systems, today’s planners are 
deploying packet-oriented mesh networks with ATM backbones, 
subscriber databases, and programmable switches. 
Telecom Network Hardware
Stalwart telecom equipment vendors – Lucent, Nortel, Ericsson, 
Siemens, NEC, Fujitsu and others – stand at a crossroad. Where 
once data communications was processed for transmission over 
voice networks, voice will now travel over a network optimized for 
data. The annual CO software upgrades that constituted the meat 
and potatoes of the telecom equipment business will disappear 
rapidly, to be replaced by third-vendor software solutions running 
on open server and database platforms. Moreover, in the 
atmosphere of intense competition, even old guard telcos such as 
Bell Atlantic, Ameritech, and BellSouth will look to the new 
generation of vendors – Ascend, Cisco Systems, 3Com, and others – 
to help future-proof their billions of dollars of network 
investments.
Traditional voice switch vendors face the prospects of complete 
organizational overhaul. While the notion of abandoning older 
switch platforms in favor of ATM products, edge switches, and IN 
gateways may have some appeal, for most legacy system vendors 
this approach will be wholly impossible. Existing carrier customers 
will rely heavily upon their longstanding vendors to help make the 
most of their existing switches, and to develop migration strategies 
that will keep them abreast of their more agile rivals. Ironically, 
these carriers will lean on their traditional vendors for product 
support even as they turn increasingly to IP vendors for new 
products and new solutions. 
An additional challenge for the voice switching vendors will be to 
work with their traditional telco customers and with new carrier

For their part, data communications equipment vendors can now 
claim responsibility for nothing less than a telecommunications 
revolution. Yet their challenges have just begun to surface. The 
biggest challenge to this set is to engineer the kind of reliability 
and feature-richness that telephone users have come to expect – 
even demand – from the networks. The Internet was designed to 
provide a fail-safe, low-cost, and ubiquitous information resource. 
It was never intended, however, to deliver content in real time, 
whether that content is voice, data, video, or any other medium. 
Delays remain problematic; congestion persists. The 
unprecedented uptake of World Wide Web usage alone strains 
networks. Voice over IP has arrived, but it remains flawed, from 
the perspective of both the end-user and the carrier.
Suppliers of network transmission equipment have entered into a 
transitional period of their own: the advent of lightwave 
multiplexing. Wave-division multiplexers operate on a 
fundamentally different set of principles from their SONET/SDH 
predecessors. Essentially, instead of interleaving and extracting 
bundles of discrete signals electronically, WDMs do the work 
optically, breaking up the lightwave signals into precisely 
segmented color bands. The primary advantage of the new systems 
is their ability to multiply the signal-carrying capacity of fiber optic 
cables, reducing the need by carriers to install new facilities.
Computing Hardware
The transformation of the public networks from a centralized, 
proprietary circuit-switched system into one based upon 
distributed architectures, open standards, and packet-switching, 
should have set the computer world on fire. Curiously, the IT world 
remains tentative, even cautious. Part of this hesitancy 
undoubtedly stems from the complex web of relationships that 
computer vendors have forged over the decades with the 
traditional network hardware vendors, third-party programmers, 
and telecommunication system integrators. These relationships 
have been so important to the IT world, in fact, that vendors like

Yet opportunities for computer vendors in the new

Computer vendors also play an enormous role in telecom service 
platforms, even in traditional circuit-switched networks. Such 
common features as Enhanced 911 Emergency Dialing, Caller ID, 
Call Forwarding, and Credit Card Calling are enabled by fault-
tolerant computers. These computers run databases with 
subscriber-specific call routing and processing instructions, which 
are typically then connected to CO switches via the IN, a packet-
switched network. These same systems will be essential to next-
generation carriers. As these upstarts seek to take business away 
from older telcos, they will increasingly be challenged to provide 
the same levels of quality, reliability, and feature richness as their 
predecessors. The window for competing as low-cost service 
providers has already begun to close. Computer-based services 
platforms will provide the key to quality in the new networks.
Computers are also the key to improving operational efficiency in 
carrier organizations. Although they provide the platforms for 
facilities and service management, computers have not yet begun 
to fulfill their potential for integrating these processes with front-
office systems such as billing, marketing, and customer care. So 
many routine transactions – from new service orders to billing 
inquiries – still rely on interdepartmental memos and even faxes 
between dispersed locations. By integrating these functions, 
carriers stand to do more than just save money; they can improve 
service quality, minimize customer churn, and more aggressively 
sell features and enhanced services.

Software is the glue that ties together transmission equipment, 
switches, and computing platforms into the services that telcos 
ultimately sell to make money. As the market shifts, many in-house

Telecom software falls into two categories: front office services 
platforms and back office business and operations support 
systems.
Services Platforms
Services platforms encompass many of the customer-facing parts of 
the telecom network. For example, calling card services and 
cellular roaming are software applications that run on computing 
platforms within the PSTN. There are currently three different 
ways to deploy services distributed within the PSTN. This includes 
switch-based SS7 applications, Service Control Points (SCP) and 
Intelligent Peripherals, and programmable switches. As carriers 
develop strategies for Internet telephony, the IP voice “gateways” 
become services platforms as well.
Services platforms often comprise a computing platform, such as 
Unix, and a middleware for the development of actual services 
applications. The applications run, in a distributed fashion, on the 
network.
With the convergence of the Internet with the PSTN, we will see the 
increased importance of these services platforms as well as the 
infrastructure to coordinate between the two networks.  Software 
for directory services and signaling will be critical to the 
development of next-generation networks.
Management, Business and Operations Support
The network-facing components of the PSTN have traditionally tied 
into the back office operations of a telco. These operations fall into 
three categories: network management, operations support 
systems, and customer care & billing.
Network Management
Network management involves the activities necessary to monitor 
and manage the equipment that comprises a public telecom 
network. In many cases, a single T1 circuit will traverse a 
multitude of networks within the overall public network. Because

Operations Support Systems (OSS)
Operations support systems are the business and computing 
processes that support the ongoing operational activities of a telco. 
These systems automate many of the tasks associated with 
ordering, provisioning, and repairing the physical infrastructure of 
a public network. For example, if a customer were to place an 
order for telephone service, it is necessary to make certain of the 
following things:

That the customer has reliable credit;

That the service is available to the customer;

Which components of the network will be used to deliver 
service;

The status of those components – are they operational, or 
do they need repaired or upgraded?

If a service technician is needed, when can it be 
scheduled?

That an appropriate customer record exists with the 
proper billing information.
Basically, for an order to be placed, it is necessary for the service 
provider to know if, and when, the service will be available to the 
customer. Many operational support systems have functions 
similar to Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) applications for 
supply chain management.

software disciplines: Customer Interaction Software (CIS) and

Customer care relies on CIS and tools for sales force automation – 
this is the software that customer service representatives and 
account managers use to coordinate customer account information 
such as billing inquiries. The primary value of customer care 
software is in the way that it organizes and presents information to 
those who are the primary point of contact for customers.
Billing systems, while they present screens and information to 
customer service representatives, are primarily focused on the 
activities of rating and analysis. Rating is the process of applying a 
series of charges, called tariffs, to each and every transaction that 
occurs on a public network. These tariffs vary by location, time of 
day, date, and other carrier-specific information. Once these tariffs 
have been applied, carriers need to analyze and track the 
information. Analysis is particularly important in interconnect 
situations where one carrier bills another for services delivered 
across his network. With the growth in the number of public 
network providers, the numbers of interconnects between these 
carriers is growing exponentially.
In the past, many Internet Service Providers (ISP) lacked the 
infrastructure to provide accurate billing information, having used 
flat-rate pricing plans to draw customers. While flat-rate pricing is 
attractive in its simplicity, many ISPs are adopting more 
comprehensive billing systems to charge for transactions and 
services such as Internet telephony. Flat-rate pricing is most likely 
not the future for telecommunications services.

The telecommunications market is a chaotic one, constantly 
shifting in response to three major factors: regulatory 
environments, technological innovation, and applications trends 
(see Figure 4). On the regulatory front, government owned and 
operated monopolies known as post, telegraph and telephone (PTT)

holdings and allowing competition into the sector. The pace of 
technological innovation allows carriers to deliver more, in terms 
of service quality, reliability, bandwidth and utility, at lower cost.

were either cost-prohibitive or utterly impossible in the past. As 
end-user expectations grow, so do demands placed upon their 
service providers.

Regulatory Environments

Technological Innovation
Although technological innovation made its impact on the 
telecommunications industry during the monopoly era, PTTs were 
generally slow to change. Perhaps the most significant 
technological adoption program during this period was the advent 
of software-controlled digital switching, which enabled telephone 
companies to add revenue-producing features to their standard 
voice offerings, and empowered switch vendors to sell software 
upgrade packages up to three times per year. In this environment, a 
handful of vendors rose to global prominence: Alcatel, AT&T 
Network Systems (now Lucent Technologies), Northern Telecom, 
Siemens, Ericsson, NEC and Fujitsu. Other innovations — e.g., X.25 
packet switching, synchronous SONET/SDH transmission standards, 
frame relay, Signaling System 7 and Advanced Intelligent Networks 
— offered more incremental improvements to existing carrier 
operations, but did not change the fundamental business models.
More than any other technology, cellular telephony transformed 
the telecommunications industry, demolishing the conventional 
assumptions about telephony as a natural monopoly and catalyzing 
the current wave of competition. The development of cellular 
technology itself — an arranged marriage of state-of-the-art radio 
and computer techniques — did not shatter any paradigms. But 
with cellular technology came the rapid construction of telephone 
networks in parallel to those of traditional carriers and PTTs. This 
successful buildout demonstrated the viability of competition in 
the telecommunications services industry.

XDSL, hybrid fiber-cable) sought to increase the bandwidth

Existing Telecom Infrastructure
Today, telecommunications service providers operate a number of 
networks, including a time division multiplexed (TDM) voice 
network; various SONET and optical networks; data networks 
based on ATM, Frame Relay and X.25; and Internet Protocol (IP) 
networks. These networks are managed and administered 
separately, so that, even though an IP network traverses an ATM 
network, the two networks are not logically connected. As the 
telecom infrastructure evolves, these networks will need to be 
logically, as well as physically, connected to each other.
More fundamentally, it is expected that all traffic types (voice, data, 
and other media) will traverse a single public network type. This 
network will be modeled more after a data network than a 
traditional circuit-switched network. However, it is not clear 
whether the common network type will be ATM or IP. There are 
two major issues that will slow the development of a single public 
packet/cell network: cost and reliability. 
Cost is a controversial issue. It is commonly accepted that, because 
circuit-switched networks are connected for the duration of a 
session, and using bandwidth regardless of whether anything is 
actually being transmitted, packet networks are intrinsically more 
cost-effective than circuit switched networks. Telephones send the 
dead space in conversations as well as the actual words, the logic 
goes, whereas packet networks send only the words. However, the 
economics of deploying a highly available, feature-rich network 
make this reasoning insufficient at best. 
The cost to operate a network is driven by numerous factors, 
including labor associated with managerial, business and technical 
processes, and equipment maintenance and upgrades, among 
others. As protected monopolies, telephone companies have 
engineered high levels of reliability and availability into their 
networks. Carriers continue to invest in new service capabilities 
for their networks, so that they may attract new customers, grow 
revenues among existing ones, and head off defection to 
competitors. All of these costs escalate when a carrier builds 
special-purpose overlay networks. And while the incremental costs

to emerging service providers, such as CLECs and ISPs, because it 
enables service providers to enter the market with a new value 
proposition that the incumbent carrier has not offered. 
Next-Generation Public Networks
Packet-switched networks have long been important enablers in 
the public networks. Telephone companies, government-owned 
post, telephone & telegraph (PTT) organizations, and specialized 
service providers have long relied on X.25 and proprietary packet 
switching protocols to serve their customers’ data needs. Packet 
switching segments data messages into variable-length packets 
with address and administrative information. A stream of data (or 
any signal, in theory, including voice and video) is thus broken up 
into many smaller packets, each of which is transmitted separately 
on a network according to availability and cost. The X.25 protocol 
also provides for point-to-point confirmation of successful 
transmission. The stream is reassembled at or near its intended 
destination, usually by means of a packet assembler-dissembler 
(PAD), for use by the recipient. 
Because the proliferation of digital and fiber optic technologies in 
public networks largely eliminated the need for X.25 protocol’s 
error-checking, a new, faster protocol standard was developed 
called frame relay. Frame relay allows faster data throughput by 
doing away with the error-checking, and provides a good platform

accommodate voice as well as data.
A basic issue with telephone companies has been the need to 
maintain separate networks for voice and data. Apart from the 
sheer cost of overbuilding infrastructure, carriers have had to 
dedicate staff and technicians to different networks and manage 
networks on different platforms. With the maturation of ATM 
technology in the early 1990s, carriers now had a switching 
platform for a variety of service offerings, from switched voice to 
video backhaul. Indeed, carriers began to install ATM switches in 
earnest over the past five years. Yet because of regulatory 
provisions for calculating tariffs and fees, entrenched 
bureaucracies, and other factors related to business cultures, 
telephone companies largely still maintain separate voice and data 
networks. 
It was deregulation in services that provided the incentive for 
integrating these disparate networks into a single platform. The 
next-generation networks being deployed today are based on 
unified service platforms, although architectures and approaches 
still vary. Backbone companies and capacity resellers may design 
their networks on ATM platforms at their inception, in order to 
leverage the technology’s signal grooming and cross-connecting 
capabilities for broadband trunking and backhaul. ISPs that have 
adopted full-service strategies to meet the needs of their corporate 
customers invariably begin with IP networks. They, too, may 
benefit from ATM in conjunction with multi-protocol label 
switching (MPLS), which maps IP directly into ATM cells. 
Remarkably, even as their customers install the first wave of next-
generation networks, vendors such as Ascend and Omnia are 
further simplifying their architectures. The signal grooming and 
cross-connecting capabilities of ATM switches will eventually 
supplant the need for digital access and cross connect systems, an 
equipment mainstay for delivering special services. Fewer

simplified operations, administration and maintenance (OA&M),

Issues and Challenges
Will traditional equipment vendors develop new business models 
that are in line with the new industry? 
The telecommunications infrastructure industry began largely as a 
hardware business, concerned primarily with switching and 
transmission equipment. Soon after the advent of stored program 
control (SPC) switching, the industry transformed itself from a 
hardware business to a software business, with switches and other 
gear sold near cost, and profits guaranteed through annual 
upgrades of proprietary generic (operating system) software and 
feature software packages. In the wake of distributed functionality 
and data-centric network strategies, can these vendors maintain 
their proprietary systems? Or will they revert back to their former 
status as hardware vendors and work more actively with 
independent software vendors? Will other lines of business which 
tended to support switching operations – such as data 
communications, network management, signaling system 7, 
transmission, and access networks – now drive these vendors’ 
overall strategies?
Who will be the next-generation network providers? What will 
happen to incumbents? ISPs?
For all of the promise of next-generation network strategies, many 
issues remain unresolved. No satisfactory solution has emerged, 
for example, for guaranteeing the reliability and availability of 
traditional circuit-switched networks on IP networks. To provide 
the features now taken for granted on the PSTN, new carriers will 
need access to the SS7 and IN networks of the incumbent telcos. 
For their part, most ISPs have a long way to go before they can 
build the robustness into their networks that would allow them to 
compete head-to-head with the telcos. Even more challenging is the 
recent wave of backbone resellers, whose chief goal is to provide 
inexpensive bandwidth to other carriers. As the dust settles from 
attrition and consolidation, who will be left, and why?
