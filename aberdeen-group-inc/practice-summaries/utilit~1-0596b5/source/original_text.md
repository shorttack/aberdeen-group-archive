Information Systems for Energy Utilities
Description, Focus and Benefits
Aberdeen’s Utility Information Systems practice covers new 
technologies and related market events that affect demand for 
computer and telecommunications equipment, applications and 
services to electric and natural gas utilities.
The utility industry faces new pressures caused by radical 
restructuring and new competitive forces.    Although the utilities 
and their specialized vendors have historically developed their 
own IT applications, competitive pressures are creating market 
opportunities for enhanced IT solutions from a diverse set of 
sources.    Aberdeen’s energy utilities practice covers vendors that 
are well-established in markets for automated operational and 
billing systems, as well as vendors that are meeting new demands 
in rapidly growing IT application areas, such as remote metering 
networks and electronic transaction processing.
Aberdeen believes that existing utilities will gradually integrate 
existing, specialized applications over networks, by using new 
infrastructures and industry-tailored enterprise resource 
applications.    Despite buying constraints, immediate competitive 
realities of meeting customer and transaction-related demands 
require that CIOs buy or outsource solutions in a number of critical 
areas: billing, outage and work order response, call centers, 
customer information systems, inter/intranet and transaction-
enabled systems.



adopting new IT infrastructures? 


prevail at large utilities and at newer, operationally smaller 
companies marketing competitively priced power sources? 

Which of the enterprise resource planning vendors will 
dominate the market for more specialized, modular 
applications designed for utilities?

Chapter One
Executive Summary
Aberdeen’s Utility Industry Practice focuses on the critical role 
information technology plays in meeting the challenges of a 
dramatic transition — away from utilities’ status as vertically 
integrated, regulated monopolies, to become competitive energy 
companies.
Aberdeen’s role is to develop an understanding of information 
technology trends in the utility industry and to provide research, 
consulting, publications and presentations on key trends. The 
scope of coverage includes software suppliers and professional 
services firms offering key technologies for an industry-wide 
transformation. 
Energy utilities represent a large market opportunity; gas and 
electric utilities together account for over $300 billion in U.S. 
revenues. This is the last major U.S. monopoly to be deregulated — 
utilities are going the same route as banking, telecommunications 
and transportation.
To survive, utilities must restructure their businesses, while 
preserving their assets and cash flows. Utility CIOs and 
management executives understand that information technology 
will play a pivotal role in successfully responding to changed 
industry conditions.
For IT providers, these IT challenges represent targeted market 
opportunities based around emerging applications. Supplier 
strategies must address the requirements of a complex vertical 
market with unique business conditions, and integrate with 
specialized operational systems.
Some of the major business trends affecting the utility industry 
are:
Restructuring: Many utilities are selling generation 
plants and relying on market availability using private 
contracts and regional exchanges;

Summary of Findings
Aberdeen believes that utilities will gradually integrate existing, 
specialized applications over networks by using new 
infrastructures and industry-tailored enterprise resource 
applications.
Because profits from existing business depend on lowering costs, 
utility shareholders and managers will continue to seek ways to 
lower operational costs – especially through labor savings. As they 
have done for other industry areas, new enterprise resource tools 
will help utilities achieve greater savings and efficiency by 
coordinating finance and operations.
Buying conservatism will therefore affect many of the issues utility 
CIOs face, such as mainframe conversion to client/server 
architectures. However, immediate competitive realities of meeting 
customer and transaction-related demands are requiring CIOs to 
buy or outsource solutions in a number of critical areas: billing, 
outage and work order response, call centers, customer 
information systems, inter-/intranet and transaction-enabled 
systems.

enterprise-wide integration issues.
New ISVs looking to enter this market must work with 
existing systems. Utility companies will be very risk

established pricing and performance of these solutions, 
which are generally sold by specialized suppliers.

Aberdeen Utility IT Practice Defined
How Utility IT Trends Fit With Horizontal Applications
Aberdeen’s Utility Industry Practice covers the interrelationship 
that has emerged between utility-specific operational systems and 
the more horizontal types of applications common to all market 
segments. This includes the requirement for energy utilities — 
especially electric utilities — to integrate specific applications, such 
as outage management and response, operational controls, 
geographic information systems and other unique elements of 
utility IT. 
Utilities are upgrading specialized systems to meet new customer 
demands and create more competitive operational cost structures. 
Driven by Year-2000 purchasing, utility IT markets present new 
opportunities for integrators, developers and service suppliers.
Unique Utility Features
Utility transmission and distribution systems are high-power signal 
networks that require the use of customized, real-time control 
systems called “supervisory control and data acquisition” (SCADA) 
networks. These operational networks are specialized IT tools that 
recently have become more accessible to other enterprise 
applications.
New hardware and software systems, such as billing and metering 
distribution networks, represent the evolution of entirely new 
distribution systems for utilities. In turn, these operational controls 
must link to horizontal applications, such as customer systems, call 
centers, and enterprise management applications.
Utility Restructuring
Deregulation is driving a transformation of U.S. utilities from a 
patchwork of monopolies with guaranteed rates of return to a 
competitive market in which over $300 billion worth of electricity 
and natural gas business are potentially up for the taking. IT is

being used to implement competition, and to help utilities respond 
to new competitive pressures.
Figure 1 depicts the relationship of individual component phases of

the appearance of “power marketers,” companies who source and 
sell wholesale power and offer infrastrucure financing and other 
financial risk management solutions. Most often, these companies 
sell to commercial, municipal and large industrial customers. Many 
power marketers are unregulated units of utility holding 
companies. Some states have already passed laws allowing power 
marketers to sell at retail, which requires these companies to 
establish more sophisticated distribution presences.
Generation, also pictured in Figure 1, is increasingly being run as a 
separate business. Utilities in states that have deregulated are 
selling off some, or even all, of their generation plants; in some 
regions of the country, generation companies are selling their 
capacity on centralized exchanges that have been specially 
established to oversee regional power markets. 
Many traditional utilities are focusing on the asset-intensive 
business of overseeing transmission and distribution segments of 
their business. Such companies are casually termed “pipes and 
wires companies.” Although much of their regulated cash flows 
remain intact for the time being, these companies must restructure 
and enhance operational efficiency in order to ensure returns in 
the future.

Transactions
The third area of utility practice coverage at Aberdeen,

Wall Street in its drive to make electricity a commodity, as it has

Chapter Four
Issues and Challenges
Impact of Major IT Trends
This section examines the major issues and challenges that affect 
the utilities industry.
The Internet
The Internet is important to utilities because they will benefit from 
the existence of an inexpensive medium that links them to 
customers. For new energy services companies and power 
marketers, the Internet will provide a ready channel for marketing 
and making proposals, and for providing information-based 
services. 
A good example of this is the need for industrial and commercial 
customers to perform complex energy usage analysis using PCs. 
The Internet provides the natural channel for this information. For 
customers in the residential category, the benefits include a 
secondary access medium for bill and account information, as well 
as a development channel for utilities to use for cross-selling and 
new services like home security (notification of vacation times). 
Further benefits will accrue with the widespread availability of 
online payment mechanisms. Utilities and Energy Service 
Companies are therefore buying Web servers and linking 
mainframes to the Internet.
Year 2000
Time and date coding and date stamp problems arising from the 
year 2000 date change are causing IT concerns for utility 
companies, due to the complexity of addressing these problems 
across the vast network of systems they typically rely on. While the 
Year 2000 issues are requiring re-coding and accommodations for 
mainframes for internal business systems, it is real-time 
operational systems such as energy management software that 
raises the most dire possibilities for failures or automated 
shutdowns.

As industrial companies that rely on embedded real-time systems, 
utilities are having a difficult time assessing the effects of the date 
transition on these embedded systems for controlling generation 
plants and transmission and distribution systems. This is partly 
because they do not have experimental data for system-wide 
performance. This is driving the replacement or upgrading of 
existing energy management systems (EMS), forecasting and other 
operational systems and platforms. 
Transition to Open Systems
The transition away from proprietary models is partly due to 
revised cost expectations brought about by deregulation. Given the 
need to control costs, the availability of newly packaged, 
customizable applications and suites is an incentive for these 
companies to make the shift. 
Furthermore, vertical market suppliers’ focus on data access 
integration and object-based programming is providing ways for 
these open systems to move further into the specialized ground of 
technical communications. This shift is underway. However, a 
complete transition to new technology is impeded by the enormous 
capital investments in current information technology, as well as 
by a tradition of relatively slow IT change, with an emphasis on 
custom internal development.
Technical Computing
As for other technical and scientific computing contexts, utility 
operational control and maintenance systems have generally 
already made the client-server shift to a networked model. Some 
newer systems rely on state of the art, fail-safe servers on the mid-
to-high end, with massive storage capability. A typical 
configuration includes a number of RISC/UNIX workstations 
performing related functions within a control room. As these have 
become more distributed, there has been a countervailing trend to 
open up data access between machines, and to distribute 
processing into network nodes.
From an energy management systems (EMS) context, workstations 
typically serve as the platforms for custom-developed software for 
online and off-line control, modeling and analysis functions. 
Increasingly, these EMS systems are being replaced with next 
generation real-time software solutions that allow for greater 
centralized access to transactional data.

Reliability is a big issue, however, and real-time platforms will 
include a high degree of redundancy for fail-over, as well as 
multiple siting for sensitive, critical functions. The transition to 
new software and platforms, whether in the field or in the control 
center, includes older software and hardware often kept as 
backups. For mission-critical controls, new platforms are being 
added and new facilities are being consolidated within centralized, 
regional systems. This is saving the utilities some of the conversion 
effort and redundancy of meeting the new need for IT to arbitrate 
between operational and transactional systems. Platform 
replacement may also be driven by concerns over the need to 
replace systems that cannot practicably be prepared for Year 2000 
compliance.
The Utility Supply Chain
Among utilities and power marketers, there is a consensus for the 
idea that enterprise-wide integration is needed to optimize 
resource use and implement the kind of efficiencies that other 
industry sectors, such as retail or manufacturing, have enjoyed. As 
database access points become accessible, more information is 
being incorporated into centrally accessible databases. The 
requirement that databases be used to manage operational, 
business and transactional processes will drive this trend, and will 
provide opportunities for suppliers to implement object-oriented, 
multi-tiered enterprise exchanges of data.
Two elements are especially important for enterprise resource 
planning (ERP) application integration to work for a utility. The 
first is the integration of data from real-time, operational systems. 
The other is the ability to integrate formerly separate kinds of data 
into business-standard, RDBMS databases. Transactions and 
facilities management are two very focused, early consolidation 
areas for ERP in utilities.
Regulation Affects Replacement Strategies
IT functions within utilities have traditionally been split between 
oversight for operations systems that run real-time controls and 
management information systems, including customer-oriented 
systems like call centers. A new division entering into this picture 
is the need for completely separate IT platforms to drive the 
marketing and support functions for the unregulated and regulated 
subsidiaries of a utility.

This is an extreme interpretation of the need for utilities to place 
“Chinese walls” that prevent information exchanges between 
regulated and non-regulated subsidiaries. Due to industry 
consensus and the decisions of regulatory precedent, most utilities 
are following an extreme version of this rule for unregulated 
subsidiaries: all but the IT systems for payroll and general 
accounting are being placed on separate platforms.
Supplier Success Factors
Most suppliers to utility and other energy companies seek 
specialized channels and solution development strategies:

Suppliers must leverage existing industry competencies. 
This includes close attention to integration with the 
solutions of suppliers particular to the sector;

Service suppliers should offer integration and, if possible, 
outsourcing options. Utilities will be hard-pressed to 
control costs, and their competitors are accustomed to 
better operating efficiencies;

Suppliers must offer solutions that mirror economic 
priorities of the changing industry. For transmission and 
distribution companies, service and asset management 
will be key IT roles;

Energy companies will also use IT to access the 
transaction mechanisms that are driving commodity and 
futures markets for electricity and fuels;

Pre-packaged solutions will sell more effectively, and 
successful suppliers will provide object based models for 
linking industrial and business systems together, as well 
as using industry standards for data and communications.
User Success Factors
Users among utilities are increasingly pulling IT into a centralized 
management by a CIO, reporting to management for costs and 
strategic objectives. They are also integrating their enterprises 
more fully, and seeking to introduce new mechanisms to 
implement regulation and meet competitive requirements. This 
includes taking an approach that plans to implement enterprise 
planning, customer management and asset management software 
together.

Among the more progressive companies who implement new 
technologies, some are seeking to move further along the adoption 
curve in order to reduce prices and forge commanding consumer 
relationships. The successful among these will implement IT with 
an eye towards the future structure of the industry — and not 
merely enhance support for the current structure. 
As a key benefit, these progressive IT-user companies will be more 
likely to understand where profits lie and they will be better 
equipped to win them. Among them will be a mix: utilities focused 
on serving traditional transmission and distribution monopolies, 
power marketers as well as “energy service companies”. They will 
seek to leverage information technology in order to control the 
customer service channel for marketing power and service.
