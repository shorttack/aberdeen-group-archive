Systems & Architectures 
The Systems, Platforms & Architectures area covers computer 
infrastructure – the hardware and software below the application 
level that enables a system to support a wide range of corporate 
computing needs.    Thus, practices within the Systems, Platforms & 
Architectures area include storage management solutions, all types 
of computer hardware architectures – from laptops, network 
computers, and desktops to enterprise servers and mainframes – 
operating system and utility software, development tools, 
frameworks, and database management systems.
The advent of the Internet and the continuing decline in hardware 
prices has made most of these components more of a commodity in 
today’s computing; but the solutions of which they are a part are 
more vital to enterprise success than ever before.    Systems, 
Platforms & Architectures form the core and the bulk of these 
solutions.    Moreover, strains on IS resources from Internet and 
Year 2000 efforts mean that system infrastructure must be more 
scalable and flexible than ever before, to handle ever-increasing 
workloads and new technologies without requiring costly 
hardware replacements.    In order to assess any computing 
purchase – network, application, or service – the IS buyer must 
understand the pros, cons, and state of the art of today’s Systems, 
Platforms & Architectures technologies.
Technical Workstations are a component and distinct practice 
within Aberdeen’s overall Systems and Architectures practice area.
This Aberdeen Group Practice Summary is a deliverable in a 
comprehensive service that includes Aberdeen published research 
and customized professional services aimed at executives who 
must make strategic business decisions regarding Information 
Technology.

Chapter One
Executive Summary
Aberdeen’s Technical Workstation practice covers standalone, 
technical workstations running the Unix and Windows NT 
operating systems and supporting key, visually-oriented, 
technical/scientific tasks — especially Computer Aided Design 
(CAD), Mechanical CAD (MCAD), Computer Aided Engineering 
(CAE), Digital Content Creation (DCC), Animation and Visualization, 
and pre-press & publishing.    Business-critical applications that 
require technical workstations include solutions that visually 
represent and navigate large data sets; such as customer ordering 
patterns, display stock-behavior charts on a financial trader’s desk 
for quantitative analysis, and create and manipulate complex 3D 
models for automobile design and movie special effects.
Because technical workstations are no longer “islands unto 
themselves”, they are of increasing importance to IS buyers, not 
only to engineer bleeding-edge competitive-advantage products, 
but also for deeper information analysis as part of a 
comprehensive enterprise architecture.    The heavy visual content 
of the Internet, in particular, makes it feasible for IS to cost-
effectively create Intranets in which graphical information flows 
more freely to and from the technical workstation.    As a result, IS 
now has a golden opportunity to establish the technical 
workstation as the cutting edge of enterprise computing; a 
powerful visualization tool for a wider variety of competitive-
advantage decision support needs.
In 1998, the key question for IS buyers is what part of their 
technical-workstation tasks to continue to allocate to Unix 
workstations, and what part NT-based solutions can now handle. 
From a raw performance perspective, NT workstations are 
approaching, if not overtaking, the performance of low-end and 
midrange Unix systems, and at a significantly lower price point.    
However, Unix workstations’ long experience and customized 
software in specialized tasks still makes them the system of choice 
in many areas.
Aberdeen finds that NT is gaining at a rate faster than many users 
appreciate.    There is a flood of enterprise-class applications and

Technical Workstations Defined
Technical workstations are positioned at the very high end of 
standalone systems.    Figure 1 shows the wide range of standalone 
system choices now available to the IS buyer.
As a result of this wide range of choices, IS buyers increasingly find 
that other desktops, and in particular the PC, can handle many 
sophisticated technical and creative tasks for which the technical 
workstation was the only alternative in the past.    At the same time, 
Unix and NT workstations now offer Intranet software that allows 
closer connection of those sophisticated tasks to other business 
processes than ever before.

The technical workstation is best defined as a “desktop plus”:    a 
PC-like architecture plus added Hardware — especially graphics 
cards, to speed up visualization tasks; and Software — especially 
compute-intensive visualization software; but also task-specific 
software such as CAD-drawing data or object management, 
publishing workflow, or editing-room cut-and-paste.    Increasingly, 
today’s technical workstations also include popular desktop 
software for communicating with the enterprise as well as for 
linking the workstations together in a “global design network”.
Today’s technical workstations fall almost invariably into one of 
two groupings based on their operating system:    Unix 
workstations and Windows NT workstations.
Unix Workstations
For the past ten years, Unix as an operating system running on a 
RISC-based platform has dominated the technical workstation 
marketplace.    However, there remain only a few notable Unix OSs 
on the technical desktop — Hewlett-Packard’s HP-UX, Sun 
Microsystems’s Solaris, IBM’s AIX, and Digital Equipment 
Corporation’s Digital Unix.    Unix workstations are targeted at 
creative and technical users of graphics- and data-intensive 
applications in market segments ranging from two-dimensional 
performance, such as simple CAD, to high-performance three 
dimensional markets such as Animation and Scientific 
Visualization.
NT Workstations
In late 1995, the technical workstation market was given a lower-
end alternative to pricey Unix workstations — Microsoft’s 
Windows NT running on Intel’s Pentium microprocessor.    
Aberdeen research shows that the low end and midrange of the 
technical workstation market is increasingly moving towards 
platforms based on Microsoft’s Windows NT.    Almost all of these 
workstations run on an Intel IA-32 architecture microprocessor, 
with a small minority running on a Digital Equipment Corporation 
Alpha microprocessor.    As with Unix workstations, NT 
workstations are also targeted at creative and technical users of 
2D/3D graphics- and data-intensive applications.
The success of NT workstations is due to tremendous 
improvements in the processing power of Intel- and Digital Alpha-
based microprocessors, gains in 3D-graphics-rendering capabilities

The Advantages Of An Integrated Solution
One of the key advantages of Unix (and even more so of NT) over 
workstations based on customized operating systems (which can 
have better performance) is its ability to link workstations together 
and connect them to the outside business.    This, in turn, allows 
enterprises to connect key engineering and design processes to 
overall business processes.    For example, global car companies 
now can not only coordinate design in America and Europe but 
also coordinate the design process with the enterprise’s ERP 
systems. 
Integrating data-visualization tools with an enterprise’s data 
warehousing offers even greater advantages.    By allowing deeper 
data mining, the technical workstation can deliver immediate 
bottom-line benefit:    for example, by targeting prospects for a new 
Internet telephone service more effectively by simulating and 
visualizing their behavior over time.

Impact of Major IT Trends
The following are major industry trends that Aberdeen is tracking 
with respect to their impact upon the technical workstation and/or 
Network Computing industry: 
?
Year 2000.    Some potential Year-2000 problems exist for 
workstations.    For example, Microsoft NT 4.0 Workstation 
is listed as “compliant with minor issues,” and can be 
made Y2K compliant with Microsoft’s Service Pack 3, and 
free NT Year-2000 QFE fixes.
?
The Internet.    The proliferation of the Internet has had a 
major impact on both technical workstation computing 
and network computers.    For workstations, the Internet 
is a common medium for displaying — and, increasingly, 
manipulating and editing in real time — data and large 
model assemblies between users in different geographic 
locations, working in heterogeneous environments.    
Internet-based cross platform technologies, by sharing 
data in real time, decrease time-to-market — and 
ultimately increase a company’s profits.

Market Position
In 1997 Hewlett-Packard (HP) experienced dwindling market share 
for its Unix workstations — B-class, C-class, and J-class systems — 
eroded by both NT workstations and the gains of some of its Unix 
competitors.    As a result, HP finished the year a distant second in 
Unix workstation sales to market leader Sun Microsystems.    
Consequently, due to this market share loss as well as HP’s strong 
commitment to and showing in the NT markets, reporters and 
analysts alike have questioned HP’s commitment to Unix 
workstations. 
In 1998 HP is responding to the confusion by stepping up the 
performance of its Unix workstations and reinvigorating its 
strategies for growth in the Unix market.    Key ingredients to HP’s 
Unix success will be: (1) a commitment to Unix workstation 
price/performance parity with Sun Microsystems; (2) a focus on 
making it easier for Independent Software Vendors (ISVs) to tune 
their software to capitalize on the visual features and functions 
that exist in HP’s advanced VISUALIZE graphics; and (3) a 
commitment to continued investment protection (for example, HP’s 
latest generation of Unix — HP-UX 11 — is a 64-bit implementation 
and is IA-64 ready).
Aberdeen Conclusions
On the bright side for HP, the combination of its Kayak NT 
workstation sales and its Unix workstation unit sales for 1997 
positioned HP as the overall technical workstation market leader

Market Position
IBM’s RS/6000 finished 1997 in the number three position for unit 
sales, behind both Sun Microsystems and Hewlett-Packard.    On the

difficulties in matching the raw performance of other suppliers’ 
systems.    The advent of a 64-bit PowerPC chip therefore marks the

chipset are likely to continue to lag in performance.    Furthermore, 
RS/6000 sales rely heavily upon IBM’s installed base of Dassault’s 
Catia application suite, but with the recent announcements of 
Catia’s upcoming availability on the NT platform, questions have 
arisen as to where IBM-Catia installs will take their business — 
RS/6000 or IntelliStation.

increasing pressures not only from Sun and HP in Unix markets 
but also from IBM’s NT workstation family — the IntelliStation.

graphics rendering, manageability, and cost, and Aberdeen 
suspects that by the end of 1998, IBM’s IntelliStation will outpace 
the RS/6000 in unit sales.    As these market dynamics play out, IBM 
will be increasingly challenged to convince customers that IBM’s 
Unix solution is the appropriate platform to meet their needs.    
Meanwhile, the dwindling market share of Apple’s PowerPC-based 
Macintosh line means that IBM must spread its RISC investment 
over fewer chips.

Market Position
Silicon Graphics, Inc. (SGI), long known for its 3D graphics 
performance in such high-end technical market segments as 
visualization and animation, has seen its Unix workstation sales 
over the last 24 months undercut by Sun, HP, and NT-on-Intel 
workstations (which compete more favorably in price and 
price/performance).    Consequently, SGI finished 1997 with 
decreasing market share — as well as financial and management 
turmoil.
Its riposte has been twofold:    introduction of new and more 
powerful low-end Unix workstations, and announcement of an 
intent to supply NT workstations.    While the new Unix 
workstations provide attractive price and price-performance, they 
run counter to an increasing perception by users that Unix is of use 
only at the very high end and for legacy workstation applications.
Aberdeen Conclusions
SGI faces some significant uphill battles over the next year as it 
transitions to a dual NT/Unix strategy.    SGI must convince its 
installed base of the viability of SGI as a company as well as SGI’s 
continued commitment to the Unix platform.    In addition, SGI 
must increasingly provide compelling reasons to justify the 
astronomical price/performance ratios that much of SGI’s current 
product lineup continues to offer — or spread the new pricing 
throughout SGI’s workstation line, which will have a serious 
negative impact on SGI’s margins.
SGI also needs to continue to expand beyond its traditional 
workstation markets — government, entertainment, and education. 
However, given SGI’s constrained resources and the new popularity 
of NT workstations, any attempt to attack these markets with Unix 
rather than NT workstations is unwarranted and unlikely.

Market Position
In 1997, Sun Microsystems made significant sales gains at its Unix 
rivals’ expense, ending the year as the number one seller of Unix 
workstations by a greater-than-two-to-one margin over HP, its 
nearest competitor.    What allows Sun to sell its Unix machines so 
successfully are (1) strong channel programs and ISV relationships, 
(2) a loyal installed base, (3) the strength and reliability advantages 
that Sun’s platform possesses (a robust OS in Solaris and a 
powerful processor in SPARC), and (4) high-performance 3D 
rendering and visualization technologies. 
Aberdeen Conclusions
From Aberdeen’s perspective, Sun’s market-leading position in 
Unix workstation sales is sustainable for at least the next 12-18 
months, although sales to new customers should see some erosion, 
especially at the low end.    Sun’s major challenge will be to 
continue to increase the performance of its Unix workstations to 
match Pentium/Merced gains.    Sun will also need to continue to 
aggressively price its technology at the low end in the face of rock-
bottom NT workstation prices, in order to give its customers a 
strong cost-justification for staying with Sun.
