SUMMARY 

Objectives: 

Aberdeen Group was asked by Bull Corporate Strategy & Marketing to survey users of Ingres and Sybase software for high-performance online transaction processing applications and to determine their satisfaction levels. 

Aberdeen's first objective was to identify leading-edge users of both Ingres and Sybase systems. We used various sources (online database searches, both historical and current customer references, and Aberdeen client contacts) to establish a listing of customers we wished to interview. 

Aberdeen's second objective was to separate customers' real satisfaction levels about Ingres and Sybase from the marketing puffery that often appears in the trade press. It has been Aberdeen's experience that many customers will overly praise a supplier's products in return for "special" attention and support. The survey guide was designed to establish the contact's position within the enterprise and why and what the contact might report. In addition, Aberdeen is well known in the U.S. for doing in-depth interviews of users for eventual public comment. As a result, many MIS executives feel very comfortable reporting to Aberdeen anonymously what they will not directly tell their supplier or their supplier's competitors. 

SUMMARY - Objectives 

Aberdeen's third objective was to compare the responses from Ingres and Sybase users on a normalized basis. At this time, there are no production users who are equally proficient and experienced with both product sets. However, through the acquisition, 

development and implementation process users will have different reactions to similar circumstances based upon the capability of their products. 

Aberdeen's fourth objective was to analyze the results of this survey in the perspective of trends in the RDBMS field, Aberdeen's historical knowledge of Ingres and Sybase -- including extensive interviews with the management's of both firms, and a recent briefing of Bull's organization and strategy provided to Aberdeen by Bull during the Hanover Fair, 21-22 March 1990. 

## SUMMARY 

Findings: 

#1. Even the most advanced users of Ingres and Sybase software are not using the products in an environment today that matches the performance expectations of a mainframe data center. The majority of customers who acquired Ingres and Sybase for high-performance online transaction applications (several hundred users online, inexpensive/dumb terminal connections, several gigabytes of randomly accessed online data) are still in the process of developing applications based upon either Ingres version 6 or Sybase version 4. Applications based upon these two releases are just now being completed and released to production. The solid successes of Ingres and Sybase for leading edge applications are based upon user experience with Ingres version 5 and Sybase version 3 for decision support applications with typically less than 100 users online. 

#2. At this stage in the application development life cycle of Ingres version 6 and Sybase version 4, Ingres customers are extremely satisfied with their product choice while many Sybase customers, even though they are meeting their project requirements, would prefer to be using Ingres' products. (In particular, Bull might note that the lead article for MIS Week for 26 March 1990 reports that Salomon may be moving its IMS-based applications to Sybase -- but Aberdeen's interview with Salomon shows that Salomon wished it had chosen Ingres instead of Sybase.) 

## SUMMARY - Findings 

#3. Customers of both Ingres and Sybase products require outside consulting help for application development. While customers are satisfied with both Ingres' and Sybase's ability to provide technical support for their products, neither firm has the resources to help users during the application development stage. Issues that were brought up include the need for user staff to understand the SQL/Relation Data Base model, client/server architecture, front-end workstation/PC processing versus back- end mainframe processing, and 4GL capabilities and limitations. 

#4. Ingres has been consistently been the poorer marketer when compared to Sybase (and Oracle -- volunteered by contacts, not asked). Sybase was the first RDBMS supplier to show a vision of high-performance online transaction processing and to tailor 

its core products specifically to win customers. This won Sybase its leading-edge customers that we interviewed. In contrast, Ingres does not provide its customers with the stream of marketing materials they feel are necessary top justify their choice of Ingres to non-technical executive management. 

#5. The first releases of Sybase products are on Sun Microsystems workstations. As a result, Sybase support staff has advised customers that to complete applications as soon as possible they should use Sun workstations for development and then port to the target machine -- DEC, Pyramid, or Stratus. 

SUMMARY - Findings 

#6. All customers surveyed believed that their supplier, whether Ingres or Sybase, did not have the resources necessary to provide the product functionality and support services that were currently envisioned. 

#7. No customer interviewed by Aberdeen has problems with the reliability or throughput of the systems. In addition, no customer intended to return to flat file or hierarchical data management systems. Customers understood they were on the leading edge of technology and believed fully that they would be the first to obtain benefits from improvements in hardware, software and communications technology. Customers implementing these products were doing so to obtain competitive advantages in their marketplaces and were satisfied with the results to date. 

SUMMARY 

Conclusions: 

Aberdeen believes that Bull has the resources, capabilities and desire to provide its customers with a common, state-of-the-science RDBMS -- no matter what the customer's platform choices are. In addition, Aberdeen believes that many potential customers for Ingres and Sybase systems would look to Bull as the larger, more stable, more qualified supplier to provide assistance in developing and implementing applications based on an SQL/RDBMS and the client-server architecture. As a result, we believe that it is very important for Bull's own customer credibility to select the RDBMS supplier that has the most reliable and stable of products and has not marketed capabilities beyond what is deliverable in the near term. Based on these assumptions and this survey only, Aberdeen believes that Ingres is the preferred supplier and Ingres 6.3 the earliest version of the RDBMS that should be selected by Bull. 

If Bull believes that it needs a partner with greater marketing capability, Sybase would be the preferred choice. However, Aberdeen is firmly convinced that the only hardware platforms Sybase is pro-actively supporting are Sun Microsystems and PS/ 2-OS/2. We do not believe that Sybase has the resources or desires to support its 

product set across Bull's wide family of platforms. 

While we did not specifically ask about Oracle in the survey, almost every contact volunteered that they had rejected Oracle due to basic architectural weaknesses

The result has been that Ingres and Sybase customers have all used third-party consultants to provide application development support. SUMMARY - Survey Analysis 

Customers believe that Ingres version 6 provides all the growth path they can implement today (especially with Knowledge Manager) and are looking forward to adopting the joint Cadre/Ingres CASE tools in the future. 

All Ingres end-user customers covered by this survey used consultants to help in develop applications. 

No hardware manufacturer was part of the acquisition decision making process. 

The strongest negative comments expressed were: the need for a more robust dictionary, better manuals and better marketing support. 

Ingres users believe that the deficiencies that they experienced with version 5 have been corrected in version 6. Version 6 has gained Ingres a tremendous amount of additional customer satisfaction and loyalty. 

Sybase Customer Satisfaction 

All Sybase users have experienced some reliability problems (as opposed to 

Ingres where none were reported), but these problems have been well within the threshold of pain that was anticipated. 

Sybase was acquired by the majority of users surveyed out of frustration with Oracle's inability to deliver the performance and functionality required for online transaction processing applications. 

SUMMARY - Survey Analysis 

The majority of customers surveyed acquired Sybase because it would provide a "special" feature: ATD-American wanted Fortex Support; Hill A&E/Artsoft was promised special support for front-end graphics displays because it was in the same geographic location as Sybase; Salomon Brothers needed a DB2 gateway ahead of Sybase's normal product schedule and Pyramid support; Stratus needed Stratus operating system and multi-processor support. 

All the Sybase customers surveyed were satisfied that the product set has met their specific objectives that were established at the time of acquisition. 

Sybase users rate their support as satisfactory. Users believe that Sybase would provide greater support if it had the resources. 

How Ingres Customers View Sybase and Vice Versa 

Ingres customers viewed Sybase as too young a company with too immature a product at the time of acquisition. Sybase customers viewed Ingres as behind the times with version 5 and without a clear vision of high-performance, online transaction processing at their time of acquisition. 

Today, both Ingres and Sybase customers believe that Ingres version 6 is equal to or superior to Sybase version 4.

## **Based on the survey findings, here are a few ways they likely influenced the development of future relational database management system (RDBMS) products:** 

## 1. Focus on Reliability and Stability 

The survey found that Ingres users were more satisfied than Sybase users, with some Sybase users wishing they had chosen Ingres instead. A key reason cited was that Ingres version 6.3 was seen as more reliable and stable compared to Sybase's offerings at the time. [1] 

This likely reinforced for RDBMS vendors the importance of reliability, stability and maturity in their products, especially for mission-critical online transaction processing applications. 

Vendors would have prioritized hardening their core database engines and ensuring high uptime. 

## 2. Improved Application Development Support 

The survey noted that both Ingres and Sybase lacked resources to provide comprehensive application development support, forcing customers to rely on third-party consultants. [1] 

This highlighted the need for RDBMS vendors to invest in application development tools, frameworks and services to reduce reliance on external consultants. Subsequent RDBMS releases likely included better IDEs, visual tools, sample applications and documentation to improve the full application lifecycle experience. 

## 3. Enhancing Query Capabilities 

While not explicitly mentioned, the differing query languages between products (e.g. Ingres using QUEL vs others using SQL) was likely seen as a hinderance to adoption. [1] 

Vendors would have doubled down on standardizing and enhancing their SQL implementations to provide a consistent and rich querying experience across their product lines. This prepared them for the increasing adoption of SQL as the standard database query language. 

## 4. Broadening Platform Support 

The finding that Sybase's initial focus was just on Sun workstations likely made RDBMS vendors aware of the importance of supporting multiple hardware platforms and operating systems from the start. [1] 

Subsequent RDBMS product releases expanded their certified platform matrices to run on a wider range of server, desktop and eventually cloud infrastructure to meet customer needs. 

## 5. Scalability and Performance 

While not achieving mainframe-level performance yet, the survey noted customer confidence that Ingres and Sybase would get there through technology improvements. [1] 

This provided an impetus for RDBMS vendors to continue enhancing the scalability and performance of their products to handle higher concurrency, larger data volumes and more demanding workloads as hardware capabilities improved over time. 

In summary, the survey insights helped shape the product roadmaps and priorities for major RDBMS vendors in the late 80s and 90s by highlighting user needs around reliability, 

application development, querying, platform support and scalability that they could improve upon in future releases. [1]
