Appendix A In-Person Interview Script and Presentation 101 PREFACE 

This is the final report of a study conducted by Aberdeen Group, Inc. for Apple Computer USA Marketing.  Aberdeen was asked to examine the market for clientserver computing (also called cooperative processing), with an emphasis on terminal front-ends to existing mainframe applications (sometimes called non-invasive frontending or frontware). 

We conducted eleven interviews of large mainframe-size sites in three U.S. geographic regions to determine current attitudes and plans for cooperative processing, and attitudes about Apple Computer and its competitors.  The interviews included cooperative processing decision makers in the banking, brokerage, government, insurance, retail, transportation, and utility industries. 

The Executive Summary section contains an overview of the findings of our study, along with Aberdeen Group's conclusions and recommendations. 

The Detailed Findings, Conclusions and Recommendations, section two, contains our detailed research findings, conclusions for Apple based on the findings, and recommendations for Apple's marketing programs.  This section also contains Aberdeen Group's analysis and recommendations regarding Apple's U.S. marketing programs aimed at improving market share at large, mainstream commercial accounts. 

Section three, the Frontware Applications section, discusses a number of real and hypothetical uses of non-invasive front-ends.  These examples are designed to enhance Apple's understanding of practical uses for frontware. 

The Interview Contact Summaries, section four, contain the complete record of the inperson interviews conducted at eleven mainframe sites.  This material weighs heavily in our research findings and should be read by marketing staff for insights into the current thinking and attitudes of a cross-section -- but not a scientific sample -- of mainframe buyers and recommenders of cooperative processing technology. 

Appendix A contains the slide presentation and script used during the interviews to discuss the road to cooperative processing and Apple's role in front-ending. 

I.  EXECUTIVE SUMMARY The Market for Frontware and Client-Server Computing 

The market for non-invasive front-ends to mainframe hosts (frontware) is small and mixed in buyer's minds with the also-emerging move towards cooperative processing using the client-server model. Aberdeen defines cooperative processing as desktop client-based display, decision support, transaction validation and communications to a server. 

Buyers are aware of frontware, and intend to evaluate it -- along with potential cooperative processing client platforms -- as early as this year.  However, the market is immature and buyers do not have the background or experience to weigh the merits of frontware supplier claims, particularly technical evaluations. 

We believe the frontware pilot application jump-start program recently announced by Apple is a necessary marketing program which will obviate drawn-out technical sales campaigns while positioning 

Apple for early revenues from production application volumes at successful pilot sites.  However, we are concerned Apple is not setting its sights high enough by planning less than 25 pilots in 1991.  The Fortune 1000 needs a 1991 wake-up call that clearly indicates Apple is ready, willing, and able to compete for enterprise commercial workstation business. 

Our research shows no vertical industry bias.  We conclude the market for frontware applications is cross-industry and its applicability is enterprise-dependent.  We suggest broad-based marketing programs such as seminars in order to reach a wide audience with Apple's frontware capabilities messages. 

The cooperative processing market is likely to mature only a year or two after frontware does in 1993. We conclude that the frontware market and the cooperative processing market are maturing in parallel. We see enterprises choosing frontware for existing applications which need little changing, and the client-server model for new applications or re-engineered applications.  We suggest that Apple market its frontware capabilities in conjunction with its cooperative processing capabilities as it is likely enterprises will see suppliers who can provide both. 

As our research indicates many major enterprises are on a path towards evaluating and selecting cooperative processing -- and frontware -- workstations over the next eighteen months.  We believe it is imperative that Apple position itself to be considered for both frontware and client workstations because these workstation decisions by MIS will establish buying patterns for the rest of the 1990s. 

## The MIS View of Apple 

Over eighty percent of corporate America is committed to and generally satisfied with the IBM PC.  This group appears unwilling to consider Apple for future client-server and front-end projects, and is likely to choose the IBM PC architecture for future applications.  We see substantial corporate inertia surrounding the IBM PC, and this should not be a surprise to Apple Computer. 

Our research shows no clear attributes to organizations which have chosen or might choose Apple. This market fragmentation makes it difficult to target marketing programs. 

We are concerned with the numerous myths and old history repeated about Apple to us.  If corporate MIS really believes that Macintosh is a toy computer, that it has no substantial business applications available, and does not connect to mainframes or PC-compatible LANs, then we believe Apple has some severe credibility problems which must be resolved before Apple's cooperative processing messages are even heard. 

## What MIS Wants From Apple Computer 

Few MIS executives interviewed by Aberdeen Group had ever had a presentation to them made by Apple.  They are quite ignorant about Apple's general capabilities, let alone Apple's workstation possibilities.  In contrast, IBM is either on-site daily or making frequent information updates to these (potential) buyers.  The clear remedy is more frequent and aggressive direct marketing activities. 

Apple has a good reputation in MIS as a quality company.  We believe Apple can capitalize on this reputation. 

We were surprised that several executives we spoke to were surprised -- to the point of being emotional -- when they learned from us a quick overview of Apple's present capabilities as a workstation.  They wanted more information, wondered why Apple wasn't calling on their enterprises, and left unstated the fact that they had never been given any choice but an IBM PC architecture because they were unaware of Apple's capabilities. 

It appears Apple's media coverage is being ignored by many MIS buyers as irrelevant to them. 

MIS is satisfied with buying from and being supported by the dealer channel of distribution. 

At two sites where Apple had invested a significant selling, the company was rewarded with (or soon will be) significant business volume. 

Other Significant Findings 

With the installed base of 5,000,000 IBM 3270s declining at an estimated 15%-20% a year, a market exists for desktop PCs which replace 3270s. 

Newly purchased PCs are today almost always connected to a LAN.  Large MIS sites have typically invested heavily in Novell Netware, so Apple connectivity to Netware is an entry hurdle -- and many MIS buyers are unaware that Macintosh is supported by Netware 286 today. 

Aberdeen anticipates a rapid growth in the market for departmental communications gateways running SNA SDLC to the IBM mainframe host.  We believe Apple may have a price-per-seat advantage using Macintosh with gateways from Avatar, DEC, and Tri-Star, and we recommend Apple focus on multi-port connectivity costs and multi-session configuration issues versus IBM PC options. 

PCs are purchased today through an entrenched corporate buying procedure which is unique to each enterprise.  Every enterprise requires a different sales and marketing strategy. 

Microsoft Windows 3.0 is the client software environment most frequently mentioned as the likely target for client applications.  OS/2 is used by those requiring multithreading or committed to IBM's OfficeVision/2.  Unix workstations are non-starters for corporate MIS, but Unix servers are attracting increasing interest due to their price performance advantages, particularly for RDBMS-based decision support. 

As of November, MIS executives were expecting budget increases ranging from no increase up to the 5% rate of inflation. 

Large sites expect to develop client-server application development expertise inhouse over time. 

IBM is showing virtually all of its utilities and packages with front-ends, blessing Easel Corp. as the frontware-of-choice.  IBM's SAA architecture for cooperative processing from PS/2s to CICS/DB2 is still two years away. 

Software AG's Natural Connection has Macintosh support for client CASE tools as well as a 4GL for mainframe host application development.  We recommend an evaluation by Apple of the potential for expanded joint marketing with SAG of the Natural Connection for Macintosh products. 

## II.  MAJOR FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 

## THE MARKET FOR FRONTWARE AND FRONT-END COMPUTING 

## Findings 

1. The frontware market over the next three years is limited, maturing, but still unstable. 

2. We size the market for frontware software at under $50 million in 1990, growing at 20%-25% in 1991. 

3. Corporate buyers are aware of frontware as a class of products. 

4. Buyers are unaware of the wide range of technical capabilities offered in frontware products. Easel and InFront are most frequently mentioned. 

5. IBM has been touting Easel as not only the client development toolset for future client-server applications, but also the non-invasive front-end to retrofit over existing applications. 

6. Only one large existing Apple site (University of Texas) was aware that Apple with Mitem's MitemView is a viable platform for front-ending mainframes. 

7. While numerous sites mentioned they had or would evaluate frontware for their enterprises, only Delta airlines had implemented an application with a standard PC (Prudential-Bache's system used proprietary CTOS machines). 

8. Merrill Lynch stated they were fearful that they could never manage the host application version control rigorously enough.  They worry that a mainframe screen change could crash all the frontware PCs.  [Unfortunately, true client-server applications make the problem even worse]. 

9. There is no strong indication that frontware is a phase during the transition to client-server-based cooperative processing. 

10. Aberdeen surveyed mainframe sites in the banking, brokerage, government, insurance, retail, transportation, university  and utility markets.  Our assumption was that the financial services industry was most likely to embrace frontware due to the competitive nature of these businesses. Our research does not support this assumption. 

11. We conclude that the market for frontware applications is cross-industry and enterprise-specific. 

## Conclusions 

Targeted vertical marketing programs are inappropriate due to the cross-industry and enterprise-specific nature of the frontware market. 

Apple must use broad-based marketing programs such as 

seminars to locate and educate corporate buyers. 

Buyers do not understand the range of capabilities in frontware products so they are unlikely to respond positively to a technical sell. 

As discussed in the next Major Finding, buyers are likely to overlap front-end computing with cooperative processing computing. 

Apple is not generally recognized as a potential supplier of 

frontware hardware and software. 

Aberdeen's conclusion is that a marketing program combining Apple's frontware and client-server capabilities is the prudent course: 

o Frontware (non-invasive front-ending) is best suited to existing applications which will change little.  The market for frontware will likely mature in two years -- just ahead of the market for true cooperative processing using the client-server model; 

o Application re-engineering using frontware competes 

with new-application development using the client-server model for the same 10%-20% of the MIS budget allocated for "new application development"; 

o Frontware and client-server are mutually exclusive. Aberdeen believes MIS will either retrofit a mainframe application with frontware or rearchitect the application using a client-server architecture. 

Implications 

It is likely many buyers will confuse frontware with more advanced client-server development tools. 

The recommended broad-based marketing programs are likely to be more costly and less effective than if vertical market programs could be employed.  We see no alternative. 

THE EVOLUTION OF THE MARKET FOR COMMERCIAL CLIENT-SERVER 

## COMPUTING 

Findings 

1. There is no headlong rush to client-server.  We estimate well over 95% of potential applications at mainframe-size sites are still using dumb-terminal -- usually 3270 -- communications to the mainframe host from either 3270's or PCs using terminal emulation. 

2. Most believe 1994 is the time when their organizations will be fully up to speed with clientserver technology and that the client-server market reaches maturity. 

3. Client-server is not for everyone nor is it a sure thing.  Our research showed a fair amount of skepticism on the ultimate productivity value of client-server, concern over the costs and lack of available technology, and general agreement that data entry jobs (e.g. retail sales clerk) would benefit least from client-server using GUIbased clients such as Macintosh. 

4. There are multiple accepted definitions for client-server, a source of confusion.  Buyers think they know what it is, and suppliers all claim to have it.  Further, frontware is likely to be mixed in with client-server in buyer's minds.  Lack of market maturity is causing a failure to communicate. 

5. The multiple technologies needed to support open client-server computing are not mature. Corporate buyers are seeking to mix and match PCs, LANs, communications gateways, and database servers in any and every way.  The technology, particularly interoperability software, will not be generally available until early 1992 -- and perhaps later. 

6. In spite of the technology uncertainties, buyers are proceeding to evaluate and select client hardware and software platforms in 1991. 

7. IBM is neither leading nor following.  No other hardware suppliers -- with the possible exception of Hewlett-Packard with NewWave -- are in contention to lead this market with proprietary solutions. The market will proceed with or without IBM, but will grow more quickly once IBM gets its act together. 

Conclusions 

Apple must move quickly to establish its presence in the market for frontware- and client-based application workstations.  Even though technology barriers and slow buyer acceptance will impede market maturity until the mid-1990s, buyers are now or will shortly evaluate their options for new desktop platforms. 

It is imperative that Apple position itself to be considered for both frontware and client workstations because these workstations decisions by MIS will establish buying patterns for the rest of the 1990s. 

Implications 

A targeted non-invasive front-end marketing program not only misses the client-server target but leaves buyers with the impression Apple can only solve part of the problem. 

## MIS VIEW OF APPLE COMPUTER 

## Findings 

1. A significant portion -- over 80% -- of the main-line MIS market is strongly committed to the IBM PC architecture.  The bulk of MIS market appears unwilling to even consider Apple for future clientserver and front-end projects.  They have already made a choice based around the IBM PC architecture. 

2. MIS (Management Information Systems) is generally satisfied with the IBM PC and its likely future.  We see substantial organizational inertia surrounding the IBM PC. 

3. MIS is comfortable with IBM PC issues: sizing, installing, buying, upgrading, applications, local area network (LAN) connectivity, host connectivity.  Apple threatens the status quo with unknowns and higher support costs. 

4. Those organizations which will use Apple or would consider Apple are hard to characterize and therefore will be difficult to market to.  There is severe market fragmentation. 

5. MIS believes incorrectly that Apple cannot connect to standard PC networks from Novell, 3Com and Banyon.  The "Apple Connects" program seems not to have connected.  There is considerable outdated information and misconceptions about Apple's capabilities in the MIS environment. 

6. MIS believes a lack of applications seriously hinders Apple's potential applicability for their organization.  For many purchases, MIS seeks business solutions, not base platforms. 

7. The MIS view of Apple is polarized by enterprise.  Apple is either a recognized player by MIS, or else is totally ignored.  There is little middle ground. 

8. As a company, Apple enjoys a favorable reputation as a quality company during a period when MIS buyer's are seeking a flight to safety -- dealing with only the highest quality, financially strong, and reputable suppliers.  Aberdeen believes Apple's quality reputation is one of its understated assets. 

Conclusions 

The general MIS attitude about Apple Computer should not be 

surprising. 

The likelihood of converting a large fraction of the heathen IBM PC-oriented MIS masses to Macintosh is nil. 

The mis-information about Apple connectivity, applications, and general applicability in a large MIS organization can and should be addressed with concerted marketing programs, but will take time. 

Implications 

Apple's market share in this decade is unlikely to exceed 15% of the business PC market, with large MIS shops putting up the largest resistance to Apple's marketing efforts. 

WHAT MIS WANTS FROM APPLE COMPUTER 

## Findings 

1. Few MIS buyers are familiar with what Apple can do today.  Many believe myths and stereotypes that the Mac is a toy computer, is overpriced, and that it cannot network with Pcs or mainframes using industry standards.  There is considerable missing information and wrong information present about Apple within corporate MIS. 

2. Few interview respondents had ever had a presentation made to them at their site by Apple. Our interviewers saw a high degree of emotional response from interviewees (Continuum and USAA) who were surprised to learn the impressive extent of Apple's capabilities.  They want to be called on and to be sold to. 

3. While Apple has a good corporate reputation in MIS, the business justification for Apple Macintosh vs IBM PC-compatibles is not apparent.  MIS wants sound productivity and cost/benefit information. 

4. Where Apple has done a significant and long-term selling job (Delta and University of Texas), the company has been rewarded by MIS management with significant business. 

Conclusions 

While Apple computers are sold through the dealer channel, the selling to corporate MIS needs to come from Apple's own Large Account Marketing organization. 

The strong emotional response noted in some interviews indicates some corporate buyers understand the tough road to cooperative processing using the IBM platform ... and they are looking at alternatives. 

Our conclusion is that Apple's product, support, and field sales issues are of minor importance.  The real issue is that buyers believe myths and old truths about Apple, are unaware of the connectivity, frontware and client capabilities, and of the business applications now available on the Macintosh. 

Implications 

Apple's media coverage is not reaching the large-enterprise MIS buyer.  These buyers can only be sold to directly and over time. 

Before Apple can market its frontware and client workstation 

capabilities to MIS, Apple sales must overcome substantial buyer resistance hurdles centered on broad product knowledge and corporate capability issues. 

ATTITUDES ABOUT APPLE COMPUTER ARE POLARIZED 

## Findings 

1. We observed a polarized buying pattern.  At 10%-20% of the surveyed sites, Apple was an established supplier with a healthy installed base.  MIS was positive to enthusiastic about Apple. 

2. However, the other 80% of the large enterprises we surveyed had such a low visibility on Apple that when asked how many Macintoshes were installed, replied they either didn't know, didn't care, or that "a couple were around somewhere." 

## Conclusions 

The polarized buying patterns constrain Apple's marketing strategy options.  Clearly, the easiest course is to grow usage at sites where Apple is already a recognized. 

The hard -- and necessarily expensive -- course is to improve corporate America's attitude towards Apple as a viable supplier for mainstream workstations. 

DISTRIBUTION CHANNELS 

## Findings 

1. Even the largest organizations routinely buy IBM PCs from the same dealer channels, such as Businessland, that sell Apple computers today. 

2. The exception is multi-thousand unit buys which are bid directly (Prudential Bache and Merrill Lynch). 

3. Dealers also provide support to the large enterprise Infocenter. 

4. A PC supplier such as Apple is expected by MIS to provide pre-sales information and post-sales backup support to the dealer. 

Conclusions 

Based on the lack of MIS interest in and knowledge about Apple, we conclude that dealers are taking orders for IBM PCs, rather than selling what's best for the customer, and not educating the market about Apple. 

## Implications 

Dealers have few incentives to sell Apple when the customer calls and says "IBM PC".  Dealers cannot be realistically expected to objectively sell Apple under these conditions. 

If Apple business practices cannot provide dealer incentives to counter walk-in IBM PC business, then marketing programs conducted jointly with dealers are necessary. 

TRENDS IN TERMINAL USAGE AND CONNECTIVITY 

Findings 

1. The installed 3270 base of about 5 million is declining at an estimated 15%-20% per year through 1993.  PCs, generally IBM-compatible, are viewed as the replacement of choice. 

2. The process of 3270 divestment will be gradual throughout the 1990s for two reasons.  First, the capital costs involved with replacing 10-20,000 devices at large sites is staggering.  Second, data entry jobs are not perceived as benefiting greatly from a PC, particularly a GUI-based PC. 

3. New PCs are almost always connected in a LAN as they are installed.  Older, existing PCs may be left standalone.  LANs are being connected to mainframe hosts, an important precursor of mainframe-based client-server systems. 

5. Large sites agree with IBM's recent conclusion that client-to-server connections are best routed through a departmental communications gateway server running SNA SDLC as a means of governing throughput and controlling the potential for unmanageable numbers of simultaneous mainframe sessions.  This suggest Apple should reinforce its relationships with Avatar Digital Communications Associates and Tri-Data Systems -- or supply multiport SNA gateways directly. 

6. Many mainframe MIS executives believe Apple's connectivity is limited to AppleTalk, and that the AppleTalk is not a robust enterprise-capable LAN platform.  Before Apple can run with client-server, it has to walk with connectivity in buyer's minds. 

7. Aberdeen research shows Novell's Netware to be the dominant LAN operating system today. The ability of Macintosh to connect to Netware 286-based servers in not generally known by MIS executives, a hinderance to Apple. Further, Novell's delay in delivering Mac connectivity to Netware 386 servers will increasingly hurt Apple as the Netware 386 installed base grows rapidly in 1991. 

Conclusions 

A market exists for desktop PCs which replace 3270s. 

Old-style "3270 add-in board" solutions to mainframe connectivity may quickly transition to gateway server topologies. 

Apple must be widely known as having excellent connectivity to Novell LANs. 

Implications 

Apple may have a LAN cost-of-ownership advantage using 

multi-port network adapters such as Avatar, DCA or Tri-Data's.  This advantage, coupled with improved Netware 386 connectivity, would be favorably viewed by corporate MIS. 

CORPORATE PC POLICY 

Findings 

1. The approval of PC purchases is made at levels ranging from individual departments all the way up to a DP Policy committee.  It strictly depends on the individual enterprise. 

2. The approval of PC suppliers is generally made at a high organizational level, is rarely changed, and is implemented by Purchasing.  However, some organizations allow flexibility in buying IBM PC clones for individual projects. 

3. Approved PC suppliers invariably include the IBM PS/2, sometimes include Apple and Compaq, and rarely includes PC clones. 

4. MIS Infocenter managers say they don't want the support problems associated with clones. 

5. Large enterprises tend to be more rigid than small organizations. 

6. PC sales and installation are handled by dealers at all but the over-5,000-PC enterprise.  Buyers seem generally pleased with dealer support and see no reason to change distribution channels, a plus for Apple. 

Conclusions 

Apple requires a custom sales and marketing strategy for every 

enterprise. 

Apple will have to sell high into increasingly intransigent management to earn approval as a supplier of record. 

Implications 

Only concentrated marketing and selling at targeted enterprises is likely today to gain Apple the status of a general-purpose corporate PC, alongside IBM's PS/2. 

USING PCs IN PRODUCTION APPLICATIONS 

Findings 

1. Based on user's definitions of what users want from the so-called client-server model, Aberdeen Group defines a client-server application as one where the PC client performs data entry, display, editing, and message-handling (the transaction) to a back-end database server. 

2. Very few client-server applications fitting the above definition are installed today.  Those that exist are mission-critical and highly customized to the enterprise's needs. 

3. PC-based client-server is practically non-existent in 1990. 

4. Large organizations are generally following IBM's lead in 8believing the clientserver model will dominate application design by 1994. 

- 8 

5. The acceptance of client-server in not universal.  Some believe there will be little productivity benefit to data entry workers, while conceding benefits to casual knowledge workers. 

6. Most expect new applications will use the client-server model -- or, at the least, will use desktop computers as the human-interface platform. 

7. The majority of large organizations surveyed expect to make substantial, longterm technology decisions by the end of 1991 regarding client-server.  They are considering tools now and some will implement pilot applications beginning in 1991.  Thus, the market window is immediate. 

Conclusions 

Buyers who have active projects are choosing their tool-sets over the next year, making 1991 a milestone for decisions that will set standards for the rest of the decade.  Apple should strongly consider an early program to establish the company as a leading player with deliverable client-server solutions today. 

The market is immature.  Buyers are selecting technology with 

the real deployment volumes delayed until the mid-1990s. 

Client-server and cooperative processing are imprecise terms 

which are rapidly becoming marketing buzz words with no useful meaning. 

Apple should invest heavily in 1991 marketing programs to ensure the company's client-server capabilities are understood, and to generate pilots for mid-1990s production systems. 

Implications 

Delaying an active client-server marketing program in 1991 may lock Apple out of a generation or more of corporate systems. 

## BUYER'S EVALUATION OF SOFTWARE ENVIRONMENTS 

During the course of our in-person interviews, we asked for off-the-cuff comments on the following six client software environments. 

Findings 

Microsoft Windows 3.0 - Is regarded as the most desirable platform for client development in 1991.  Most consider it adequate for the job. 

Hewlett-Packard NewWave - Is not widely known, but gets support from the most technically knowledgeable as not only the elegant version of Microsoft Windows 3.0, but as having a rational approach to open server computing. 

IBM OS/2 - Only those who require multiple tasks (e.g. multithreading) or are diehard fanatics for the yet-undelivered OfficeVision/2 are interested in OS/2. 

SCO Open Desktop - This Unix software environment is virtually unknown by the corporate commercial application developer> 

Sun Open Look - Sun is gaining commercial market share where multiple active windows are required, such as in foreign exchange trading applications. Otherwise, Open Look is a non-starter. 

Apple Macintosh - Is not being seriously considered except at those sites which already have made a commitment to Apple or are being actively sold by Apple. 

Conclusions 

Microsoft Windows 3.0 is surely Apple's first, second, and third 

competitive priority. 

The fact that Apple is not being actively considered or asked to bid on the long-term enterprise client hardware platform at many sites is indicative of the entrenched mentality surrounding the IBM PC. 

Aberdeen continues to see almost no desire for Unix desktop systems in corporate America for use as production clients.  We do see rapidly growing interest in Unix servers and in Unix (particularly Sun) workstations as software development platforms. 

OUTLOOK FOR MIS BUDGETS IN 1991 

Findings 

1. Those surveyed who knew the entire MIS budget picture report that budgets will range between flat and up 5% -- the rate of inflation. 

2. No major MIS resources will be devoted to client-server technology alone.  However, key applications which benefit from the client-server model will likely move forward with the selection of base technology, software tools, and pilot-program hardware platforms in 1991. 

3. MIS will develop experience in client-server through in-house projects.  MIS does not expect to outsource client-server projects to avoid the learning curve.  However, we believe that the industry will generally stumble in implementing pre-1995 clientserver systems.  This is likely to cause a backlash as buyers react to being oversold -- yet again -- on immature technology. 

4. Outsourcing application development to systems integrators such as Andersen Consulting for key new applications continues to grow.  However, Aberdeen research shows that these contracts frequently use conservative -- that is, old -- development methodologies and conservative hardware platforms.  We observe little client-server development work by the system integrators today, but considerable planning is underway. 

Conclusions 

As of November 1990, MIS was looking for a so-so budget year in 1991.  There were no indications a recession is causing drastic budget cutbacks. 

The complexity and immaturity of cooperative processing 

technology, coupled with MIS's lack of experience with this new technology, will cause MIS to use system integrators to help jump-start their in-house efforts. 

## IBM IS SAYING ONE THING AND DOING ANOTHER 

## Findings 

1. IBM is demonstrating virtually all its software products with add-on graphical front-ends.  This serves to whet the market's appetite for GUI front-ends. 

3. While IBM has blessed Easel as the frontware of choice, knowledgeable users are not impressed, and unsophisticated users are still unfamiliar with it.  Delta airlines reported that IBM bid Easel on a major program outsourced to IBM, but reversed course a month into the implementation because IBM foresaw implementation limitations and potential performance bottlenecks in Easel's interpreted pseudo-code. 

3. Corporate MIS has figured out that SAA is all fluff and no substance.  This does not seem to have any adverse consequences towards IBM. 

4. IBM is still two years away from demonstrating true cooperative transaction processing using OS/2 Extended Edition (EE) clients with CICS/DB2 mainframe applications. 

5. We believe IBM has no choice but to deliver the PC-based client capabilities promised with OS/2 EE as part of SAA, in spite of the industry conclusion that OS/2 is dead. 

Conclusions 

IBM continues to market mediocre products superbly. 

We believe that IBM is, yet again, likely to cause a market backlash because the carefully crafted IBM frontware-based demonstrations are far beyond the capacity of most organizations to implement on their applications today.  This places Apple in the awkward position of having to expose the IBM emperor with no clothes. 

Until IBM delivers the transaction integrity features which support networked client-server transaction processing with ensured recoverability, the market for mainframe client-server cannot mature.  Decision support client-server applications should be the primary focus through 1993, with a secondary focus on the capability to support development of production transaction processing using the client-server model. 

Implications 

Transaction processing represents an over-$30 billion market for hardware and software.  Most 3270 seats today are used for production transaction processing applications.  These seats cannot be easily replaced, except with frontware-based applications, until IBM solves its SAA communications and data integrity problems circa 1993.

Georgia DOT is primarily an engineering-oriented site.  They have few Macintoshes because they believe Macintosh is not a credible general-purpose engineering workstation.  Thus, our direct research for this project does not support the theory of a special relationship. 

2. Other Aberdeen research suggests Digital sites have a bias against IBM products, believing Digital's VAX/VMS is superior to IBM's midrange and mainframe offerings.  Digital sites often "march to a different drummer".  We believe this bias may extend to PCs, but would require additional research. 

3. There are a number of connectivity products which integrate VAX and Macintosh today. 

## Implications 

If Digital VAX sites are biased against IBM PCs, then marketing Apple PCs to VAX sites may have a higher than average success rate. APPLE COMPUTER AND SOFTWARE AG 

## Findings 

1. Software AG (SAG) has a client software product called Natural Connection which operates on both IBM PCs and Macintosh in conjunction with SAG's Adabas DBMS and Natural 4GL products. 

2. Two survey sites (Delta Airlines and University of Texas) were users of Natural.  The university also extensively used Natural Connection on Macintoshes.  Delta is about to add Macintosh to the list of MIS-approved workstations.

## Conclusions 

We recommend an evaluation by Apple of the potential for expanded joint marketing with SAG of the Natural Connection for Macintosh products. 

Implications 

As MIS increasingly embraces CASE tools for application 

development, Apple's ability to run de facto industry CASE tools such as IEW and Excelerator will become a greater stumbling block to MIS sales. 

III. APPLICATIONS OF FRONTWARE 

This section examines a number of real and hypothetical business applications which use PC-based frontware as a non-invasive front-end to mainframe applications.  Aberdeen breaks these applications into three distinct classes:  Coordinator, Data Interchange, and Enhanced Display. 

## Coordinator 

The Coordinator class uses frontware to gather information from multiple applications or remote computers.  The benefits to the users of Coordinator frontware are: 

- Single login, single request transaction -- no more logging in to multiple applications sessions, each with unique login and data query transactions; 

- Data consolidation -- no more writing down or printing information from one screen before moving on to the next screen to get all the required data; 

- Faster answers and higher productivity -- because data 

consolidation and single login eliminates human computer "busywork" overhead; 

- Potentially lower mainframe host utilization -- because the frontware can consolidate data quickly and release host resources faster. 

Coordinator-class applications are useful to two classes of computer users, casual users and knowledge workers. 

Delta Airlines implemented a Coordinator-class application to assist its flight crews, who are casual computer users.  The flight schedule, crew assignment and weather data are stored on two different Delta machines.  One uses the cryptic PARS transaction coding, while the other machine uses standard CICS conventions.  Delta found pilots were frequently using the wrong login protocol for the machines they were accessing, resulting in lost pilot productivity and intense frustration.  The Delta frontware system automatically establishes simultaneous sessions on the two different computers, and uses a GUI to speed pilot data selection and display. 

Merrill Lynch has numerous applications accessed by remote branch offices. 

Because each application may run on a different computer, it is now necessary to dedicate terminals to specific applications such as cash management.  There is considerable duplication of coaxial cables and data links.  A frontware application will consolidate applications so that brokers, branch managers, and back office applications will all use a common front-end to a branch LAN-based server.  The server will have the gateway connections to the remote application host computers. 

Apple Computer's SAMS application is a classic example of a Coordinator-class application for knowledge workers.  We recommend highlighting Apple's success in front-ending SAMS. 

Data Interchange 

We define Data Interchange as frontware which translates or transforms mainframe data into a useful display form and optionally passes it to another PC application for additional processing or storage. Data interchange is most effective when used with the color GUI of most PCs. 

The benefits to users of Data Interchange are: o Easier comprehension due to color and GUI; o 

Ability to transparently use mainframe data in PC applications and vice versa; o Intelligent data 

storage by the PC of mainframe data, promoting local databases. 

A stock trader workstation receives information feeds from numerous sources.  The data is typically price quotes or news tickers (e.g. Dow Jones or Reuters news wires).  Profitable stock trading depends on reacting to market information before the competition -- literally in seconds.  By executing local trend algorithms, graphing price quotes, highlighting significant changes in color, and sounding an alarm on a triggered event, a trader's sensory bandwidth can be enhanced. 

The U.S. Navy is implementing a paperless human resource information for job postings using Easel on IBM PS/2's with touch screens.  The casual users whose use the system  are unaware of the mainframe host as the source of the data. 

Executive Information Systems are specialized decision support systems.  EIS systems combine Coordinator-class and Data Interchange-class frontware to both hide the host data gathering and to make the information content high, primarily by sorting, summarizing, graphing, and display-byexception. 

Office Information Systems use data interchange with PC-based word processing, desktop publishing, database, and spreadsheet applications in conjunction with e-mail across host-based enterprise mail networks.  IBM's OfficeVision/2 and Apple's MacPROFS are two examples of such office information systems. 

## Enhanced Display 

Enhanced Display applications improve the display content of host data, but add little if any additional value.  The so-called Pretty Face applications use frontware to add color and GUI-based symbols like radio buttons and sliders to replace 3270 function keys. 

Enhanced Display applications are easiest to implement but essentially add no more value than mere terminal emulation. 

IBM is demonstrating enhanced display frontends to many of its mainframe utilities, conditioning the market to enhanced display frontware.  However, more sophisticated frontware such as OfficeVision/2 which adds significant value to data at the PC is being routinely delivered late. 

Aberdeen is concerned that the market will begin to lump all frontware into the Enhanced Display class, slowing acceptance of the truly useful Coordinator and Data Interchange classes of frontware applications.
