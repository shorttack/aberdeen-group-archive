Objective and Methodology: 

Aberdeen Group has been asked to advise Encore Computer Corporation on potential merchandising strategies and themes to increase Encore's marketshare for the justshipped Series 91 and the soon-to-be-shipped Series 93 within financial and information services organizations.  For purposes of this report, financial organizations include banks and brokerage houses and information services include enterprises that sell online information. 

Aberdeen Group conducts on average 25 user interviews each month.  Based upon the history of these interviews, the experience and expertise of Aberdeen's principals, and extensive discussions with Encore executives, Aberdeen recommends the following merchandising strategy. 

Our objectives in this report include describing for the Series 91 & 93 in both the financial and information services marketplaces: 

- market opportunity, size and growth 

- recommended market strategy 

- key positioning and differentiating messages 

- product issues:  base, specials & futures 

- distribution program recommendations 

- competitive overview 

- sales program recommendations 

We believe that Encore sales, public relations and marketing communications groups should be able to use this document as a basis for gaining awareness of both Encore's and the Series 91 & 93's capabilities to meet the needs of financial and information services enterprises.  However, we wish to note that this document may be relatively difficult to comprehend on a first reading.  Not only is the document the embodiment of multiple meetings between Encore and Aberdeen but much of the basis for recommendations that are stated in the beginning are discussed in more depth at the end.  Unlike a good mystery novel, we would prefer to begin with the "who did it" and leave the why and how until the ending.  And like a good executive report, we have emphasized conclusions and recommendations over long, drawn out proof theorems. 

Aberdeen encourages any Encore executives who have questions about this report after reading it to contact Aberdeen Group principals Peter Kastner or John Logan directly. 

Finally, the overall objective of this merchandising plan is to aid Encore's field sales and support organization by creating awareness of the unique capabilities of the Series 91 & 93 among MIS decision makers at leading-edge financial and information services organization as well as the independent systems integrators that work within both industries.

## Symmetric Multiprocessing (SMP) 

An SMP architecture allows one-to-many processors to operate in parallel under the control of a single OS image.  By adding processors, and SMP can easily expand vertically.  The OS efficiently dispatches work to as many processors as are available. SMP is considered an effective technique for minimizing response times and maximizing throughput by minimizing the time a task spends waiting for CPU cycles. 

## Clustering 

While SMP systems can show excellent performance growth, horizontal growth also has benefits.  Clustered systems are composed of multiple processor nodes (each of which can be SMP).  Each cluster node has an OS running independent tasks, and nodes are connected by a communications link. 

Clustering allows a node to specialize — for example, processing only certain kinds of database queries.  Specialization can mean improved aggregate throughput performance when compared to the overhead of systems of multiple, unspecialized nodes.  For example, a small node specializing on queries for customers "A" to "E" can often outperform a large node which has the overhead of looking at all customers. Clusters can also be used to achieve higher availability levels — a key buying criteria for the financial and information services market. 

## Memory-Coupling 

Applications and OS's on clustered systems usually communicate using messagepassing protocols.  There is overhead on sending and receiving nodes associated with preparing, sending, and waiting for a reply associated inherent in clustered systems.  Further, synchronizing events on clustered systems can itself become a bottleneck — witness Digital's distributed lock manager. 

Encore's reflective memory makes communications between clustered nodes behave as if tasks were communicating through memory on the same machine.  Reflective memory can be shared by an entire cluster, without the contention and overhead of message-passing. 

By copying application state information to reflective memory to another node, memory-coupling can also make programs more survivable and can improve overall application availability. 

## Single System View 

System management in clusters is typically complicated by having multiple, independently operating computers.  Encore's Adaptive Computing model transparently brings a single-system view of all the resources on the cluster to both programs and systems operations.  The benefit is less complex (hence more reliable and quicker to implement) applications, and less operator training and intervention. 

## High Availability 

Adaptive Computing contains several attributes which can improve availability for mission-critical applications: 

● Clustering splits work over multiple nodes.  If one node fails, others continue, and can restart the failed node's tasks on the survivors; 

● Memory-coupling allows application restart/recovery data to be easily passed to a backup node; 

● Processing nodes and I/O nodes in Adaptive Computing clusters can communicate via redundant RMS systems, and I/O can be directed through dualported disk controllers to mirrored disks.  Adaptive Computing supports configurations with no single point of failure.

## Banking Industry 

Banking is a worldwide market with concentrations in major financial centers.  The two major bank segments are retail and wholesale banking.  Adaptive Computing will be attractive to banks because of its ability to handle the largest share of present bank computing:  online transaction processing (OLTP) and decision support. 

Bank OLTP purchases began rebounding in the second half of 1989 from a slight decline in 1988 induced by low-to-no profits, and that momentum is continued into 

1990.  Banks are among the leaders in the downsizing trend, in spite of the predominance of mainframes. 

Domestic banks are individually struggling to be survivors in a massive shakeout which will see half their current number gone within five years as the irreversible interstate banking movement rolls on.  On the retail side, use of debit cards, longer hours, instant interest-rate responses to market conditions, and even more ATMs all impose more demand on existing OLTP systems for those that have them, or requires new ones for those that don't. 

In wholesale banking, any bank expecting to gain or even maintain marketshare among large industrial and commercial accounts must deploy sophisticated, networked OLTP systems to compete for international lending, cash management, correspondence banking, trust accounting, mortgage lending, corporate trust and depositories. 

Banks are no longer able to viably maintain their batch computer systems installed in the 1970s.  Leading banks have re-evaluated their information networks and are implementing more cost-effective solutions that combine lower overall labor costs, greater security, faster response to customer requests, on-line tellers at all branches, and integration of all information on any account at one authorized point. 

Lastly, banks are increasingly using automated decision support systems — often tied to a relational database — in order to make decisions more economically, efficiently, and consistent with increasing regulatory reporting requirements. 

The result of these trends is that banks are now continually upgrading -- funds permitting -- their computing systems from centralized batch processors housed in dedicated data centers to on-line information networks --  representing a prime area of demand for Adaptive Computing. 

## Brokerage Industry 

Information is the product of the securities industry.  Information must be collected, analyzed, dispersed and acted upon 24-hours-per-day around the world.  The global market from London-to-New York-to-Tokyo-to Zurich never rests.  To catch the paperthin margins on increasingly large and risky transactions, computers trade with computers with little or no human intervention. And with such highly leveraged stakes involved, brokerage and securities firms feverishly pursue the latest in OLTP technology. 

The financial community is still in shock from the market crash of October 1987.  Even though the crash was partly caused by automated systems, Wall Street has renounced its renunciation of computer-based trading schemes by making them even more sophisticated.  Also, the market managers added substantial capacity as a result of the unprecedented crash transaction volumes in order to forestall any market perturbation caused by lack of transaction-filling capacity. 

With the industry in a protracted shakeout, it collectively sees even more automation as a way to gain individual competitive advantage.  We see the following benefits of Adaptive Computing as most attractive to the brokerage industry: 

- The brokerage industry has the highest degree of real-time requirements of the non-communications commercial markets.  Encore has great expertise in custom realtime systems.  Adaptive Computing promises excellent commercial real-time computing capabilities versus the competition; 

- Most systems are proprietary and mission-critical.  Encore has the reputation for custom systems and the capability to configure an Adaptive Computing system to virtually any requirements; 

- Further, buyers are looking for a competitive advantage, not another off-the-shelf solution; 

- Availability ranges from high to fault-tolerance.  The ability to configure Adaptive 

Computing systems in a redundant fashion is important, and the capability to use Reflective Memory for application checkpointing gives Encore an attractive offering for a non-fault tolerant supplier.  Where fault tolerance is mandatory, it appears conceivable to be able to install FORMS on the VMS bus of a Sequoia system.

## Information Services Market 

Information-for-a-fee services have matured rapidly in the 1980s, and competition is driving the search for new or improved information delivery vehicles.  In order to speed full-text retrieval services, Dow Jones installed a massively-parallel Thinking Machine.  Large service providers like ADP, Dow Jones, Mead Data, OAG, MCI Mail and Compuserve require up to 150 TPS from 10,000 on-line subscribers. 

The kinds of computing typically done by information services — timesharing, database retrieval/display, and (increasingly) graphics/imaging — are all strong product features of Adaptive Computing.  There is an emphasis on connectivity, but breadth of communications protocols is also important. 

The information utility market is extremely fragmented, making general marketing difficult and potentially unrewarding. The information sold ranges from specialized fresh produce availability (a tiny niche) to generalized utilities such as CompuServe. Low cost modems and the general availability of cheap packet-switching networks has finally opened the information business to the masses. 

The market is very cost-sensitive and amenable to fixed cost-lowering options such as Adaptive Computing in order to keep unit costs down.  Dow Jones News Retrieval, for example, is losing customers due to recent price increases, and Nynex is shutting down its information service gateway after losing ten million dollars over the past two years.  We believe cost and competitive pressures will continue. 

Large information service providers frequently have multiple, networked computer systems.  Our research suggests the computers are purchased based on a bestinclass buying decision at a given time.  With an innovative and extensible system, Adaptive Computing has the opportunity to be the best-in-class platform for early1990s information applications. 

Perhaps the fastest growing segment of information services is the intelligent network applications of the telecommunications market, a market Encore is analyzing outside the scope of this report.

● Encore needs to capture only a very small share of the financial and information services hardware expenditures initially to be successful in terms of Encore's current nonexistent position in these marketplaces — Encore does not need to be all things to all buyers at this time; 

● The advanced technology theme will reinforce Encore's commitment to its installed base of realtime customers that it will not dilute resources to focus on the general commercial marketplace; 

● Encore can build on the advanced technology theme over the next three years with ongoing performance and customer success announcements.  The advanced technology theme has a long life in the financial and information services industries that respect stability and continuity on the part of their mission-critical hardware suppliers; 

● Encore has neither the depth of staff nor the breadth of third-party alliances to be a general systems supplier to either the financial or information services marketplaces.  Therefore, traditional market positioning statements that appeal to the security blanket that many suppliers seek to promise prospects could not possibly be used by Encore; 

● Encore's field sales force can quickly determine which suspects are real prospects because they have a need for the benefits of "dirty computing" and not be trapped in endless rounds of commodity price cutting against competitors that can do the same timesharing computing functions as standalone Series 91 & 93s can. Coordination of Merchandising and Field 

This merchandising plan has been created specifically with the objective of supporting the Encore field organization.  The merchandising effort is intended to motivate financial and information services decision makers to consider Encore and the Series 91 & 93 as the potential basis for new information processing applications.  But just having an advanced technology is not enough to capture a sale — the Encore field organization must be prepared to meet the pre-sales, post-sales and implementation support needs of financial and information services industry MIS decision makers. 

Encore management should consider three field issues that need to be addressed in concert with this merchandising plan: 

● The Encore field force has little experience in selling into financial and information services accounts and believes it has been misdirected several times in the past; 

● Financial and information services accounts demand that their hardware and systems software supplier of record also be able to offer systems integration services to customize each specific installation.  Systems integration services today is a very broad umbrella term that includes application development, networking and interoperability support for non-Encore systems, capacity planning, disaster recovery services and even strategic technology planning.   In financial and information services accounts, Encore will need to lead in both coordinating the efforts of other firms and supplying support itself.  Encore must be prepared to act as prime contractor; 

● Encore must be careful to have completed enough field sales training and to have developed enough systems integration alliances to satisfy the customer requirements of a successful merchandizing campaign. 

Proposed Series 91 & 93 Messages 

Aberdeen recommends that Encore introduce the Series 91 & 93 as multiple packaged systems connected by RMS under the theme of Adaptive Computing. Encore will introduce Adaptive Computing as the style of computing for the 1990s that allows MIS managers to quickly and effectively modify their data center systems to rapidly accommodate the changing needs of their enterprises. 

A sample press release might have as its theme, "First introduced to the commercial marketplace by Encore Computer in 1991, Adaptive Computing takes advantage of the best features of older technologies such as symmetric multiprocessing and Unix and transforms them into a data center system that can be easily adapted to provide users with sophisticated, new applications that require realtime response.  Encore's ongoing research into reflective memory systems (RMS) has led to the technology breakthroughs required to make Adaptive Computing a reality in today's data center." 

Aberdeen recommends that the minimum configuration would be a base model that we have dubbed the Encore Adaptive 291.  This would consist of two dual-processor Series 91s (one for computing and one as an I/O channel), 16 MB of RMS and 4GB of DASD for $250,000. 

The specific benefits of Adaptive Computing that will be stated in Encore marketing communications are the unique abilities to: 

- Search large amounts of data and update multiple applications faster than with any other system; 

- Move data transparently into multiple applications at memory-speed transfer rates; 

- Provide interoperability among multiple, different applications transparently through RMS; 

- Add additional dedicated systems as the number of applications grow; 

- Merely add processors as required to match the needs of growing applications; 

- Service thousands of simultaneous online users with fast response times; 

- Manage a single systems view of multiple systems; 

- Distribute the system over a 3 km area; 

- Create a fault-resilient system through redundant processors and RMS. 

To differentiate itself from all other competitors, Encore needs to emphasize the technical capabilities and user-enterprise advantages of building data center systems around the use of RMS.  Aberdeen knows of no other computer manufacturer that is following an architectural strategy that is similar to RMS.  Most are caught up in merely providing the functionality on Risc/Unix systems that was previously available on CISC/Proprietary O/S ASAP and are planning the addition of Risc/Unix multiprocessing systems to be released between now and 1993. 

Further, we expect a flurry of suppliers will announce mediocre cluster capabilities (including Sequent, which is using Ethernet as the cluster communications backbone). With media attention focussed on opens systems clusters, Encore has the ability to stand out with truly superior and different technology. 

If Encore religiously preaches the benefits of Adaptive Computing, it has the opportunity to establish a new agenda for data center MIS executives similar to what Sybase has done with client-server for the RDBMS (relational database management systems) marketplace. 

In particular, the Adaptive Computing message will reach a receptive audience within the financial and information services markets.  Both marketplaces are under increasing pressures to reduce costs but, due to the time critical nature of revenue capture, be ready to expand and/or modify services instantly to meet changing user demands. 

Finally, we strongly recommend that Encore not use the term "Open Systems" in its marketing messages.  We believe that users now believe open systems is synonymous with "low-priced", "Unix", and "commodity box".  Encore cannot make the type of gross margins it needs to grow the business if it is perceived as merely yet another open systems provider. 

## Product Issues 

At the base level, there is very little to recommend the Series 91 & 93 computer systems to the financial and information services marketplaces.  True, they are hot boxes, but Data General has already claimed the leading Motorola 88000/Unix hot box position at price levels that Encore management does not desire to be locked into.  And MIS executives in the financial and information services industries rarely, if ever, buy just a hot box — they need to make long-term commitments to a supplier's current products and vision of the future. 

## Encore Adaptive Series Packages 

Packages consisting of multiple Series 91 & 93 processors with reflective memory can be value priced to the financial and information services industries — yet allow Encore 

the option of pricing basic systems components at the appropriate price levels for its traditional realtime marketplaces. 

Aberdeen recommends that the base level package consist of two dual-processor Series 91s with 16 MB of reflective memory and 4 GB of DASD for approximately $250,000.  While the price can always be discounted when appropriate, this would be the minimum financial commitment that Encore should expect even for a serious trial data center system based upon a technological sale for a financial and information services mission-critical application. 

In addition, Encore should place in its catalogue a range of packages up to the largest technically feasible system.  For example, an Adaptive 893 consisting of 8-System 93s, 1 GB main memory, 4-64MB RMSs, and 12 GB DASD system priced at approximately $5 million.  Note that as packages become larger and more complex, the price based upon a simple parameter such as $/MIPS should actually increase from the base level price. 

## Communications 

The product area of greatest concern for Encore in the financial and information services industries will be communications.  Both industries are heavily dependent upon communications, including IBM's SNA protocols and application specific protocols.  For example, one supplier of LAN-based systems to the financial industry has over 100 engineers dedicated to providing and supporting communications protocols that are required by customers. 

At a minimum, Encore must be able to support IBM's major SNA protocols, including LU2.0, LU6.2 and SNADS.  In addition, we would recommend that Encore license and resell DECnet compatible networking software from Bell Atlantic's Community division if it wishes to operate in conjunction with currently installed VAX systems. 

In addition, Encore should begin both developing greater in-house communications expertise and making partnerships with smaller custom consulting firms that can provide application specific protocols for customers under Encore's general supervision. 

Again, Aberdeen cannot over emphasize how important the ability to supply individual customers in the financial and information services industries with the communications protocols each needs will be to Encore's success.   And the issue is not necessarily the cost of the communications protocols but the ability to deliver on time, assure quality and reliability of the communications software, and support it into the future. 

## RDBMS 

The ability to quickly develop and modify applications is extremely important to the financial industry.  And the chosen vehicle to accomplish application developments is increasingly RDBMSs from the major hardware suppliers (IBM's DB2, DEC's Rdb, Tandem's Guardian and Teradata's SQL) and the independent software vendors (Ingres, Oracle and Sybase). 

Encore does not have the resources to develop and support its own world-class RDBMS.  Therefore, it is appropriate that Encore develop as close a working relationship as possible with all three of the major independents.  Due to the multiple system and RMS architecture of Adaptive Computing, this working relationship will require expertise on Encore's part as to how specifically to develop RDBMS applications that take advantage of the unique technological capabilities of the system. 

We highly recommend that Encore explore technology-sharing agreements with its two major RDBMS partners, ASK Ingres and Oracle, to optimize RDBMS performance in the clustered topology of Adaptive Computing.  We believe that the I/O-managing Series 91(s) should also be executing the database read/write, logging, and archive 

processes of the RDBMS.  Oracle has recently announced version 6.2 with improved cluster support.  The objective is to harness the distributed processing power of an Adaptive Computer with optimized DBMS performance and throughput not available from a vanilla port of the RDBMS engine.  In short, the loosely-coupled RMS-based systems must be tightly-coupled from an operational standpoint and tuned for database throughput. 

Lastly, we firmly believe Encore must early on demonstrate the claimed benefits of Adaptive Computing through performance benchmarks — starting with the TPC-A transaction benchmark. 

## Distribution Tactics 

Enterprises in both the financial and information services industries that would make the commitment to Encore for what would soon be millions of dollars of systems as envisioned under the Adaptive Computing architecture would require direct sales contact by Encore for the period covered by this report. 

Teradata is a similar, recent role model.  Teradata's first sales were to a limited number of customers such as Citicorp and Bank of America.  These sales were all direct sales.  As the capability of Teradata's system became appreciated by the industry, Bull and NCR decided to OEM their systems — but not until the product had been available for approximately three years. 

Even where Encore has the opportunity to form alliances with systems integrators and value-added resellers, it will be required to sell directly to financial and information services MIS executives itself to establish credibility over the planning period. 

As a result, we recommend that Encore hire sales representatives who have experience in selling to the financial and information services industries.  Specialized sales reps have the potential of shortening the amount of time (by at least 12 months) 

that it would take Encore to make the initial key introductions with decision makers in both the financial and information services industries. 

## Competitive Overview 

The Series 91 and 93 packaged as Adaptive Computing will have to compete against both established suppliers and other new starts in both the financial and information services marketplaces. 

Aberdeen research indicates that the technological leader to beat in the financial services industry will be the IBM S/390 running MVS/ESA with applications developed under CICS for DB2.  In the information systems marketplace, the major technological competition will be Tandem Computers providing 24-hour availability and extensive communications support.   In addition, Digital Equipment Corporation's VAXcluster, Teradata and Sequent will represent competitive alternative technological threats to Encore's Adaptive Computing. 

## IBM S/390 and DB2 

IBM continues to position the high-end S/390s running MVS/ESA with applications developed using CICS and targeted for its DB2 database as the most powerful system available for financial institutions' data centers.  (The multi-million dollar acquisition and support costs are too high to even bother pitching to most information services enterprises.) 

Aberdeen believes that IBM is developing its own version of Adaptive Computing — but instead of RMS it will employ a dedicated Risc-based multiprocessor database engine running DB2 that will service multiple S/390s. 

If Encore can conclusively show that it can outperform a high-end S/390-720 for commercial computing, Aberdeen believes that sales opportunities could easily surpass Encore's most optimistic expectations. 

Encore's strengths: Low hardware and support costs, expandability, excellent price/ performance, choice of RDBMSs, application interoperability — all equalling the ability to give the financial institution that successfully implements Adaptive Computing a competitive advantage against those who stay with high-cost, inflexible S/390s. 

Encore's weaknesses: Limited support resources, minimal communications capabilities, and lack of credibility in the financial marketplace. 

## Tandem 

Tandem is continuing to make inroads into the information services.  Information service decision makers are especially attracted to Tandem's high-availability continuous processing capability, range and quality of communications protocols supported, and modular expansion growth path. 

Tandem is currently migrating from its proprietary architecture to a higher performance system based upon MIPS Computing Systems Risc processors and Unix.  We believe that this product transition will not be complete during the planning period and we believe that this effort is absorbing any engineering resources that Tandem may have to replicate the functionality of Adaptive Computing within the planning period. 

Tandem derives almost half its product revenues from the financial markets.  It is wellestablished with excellent references, its sales force is well-versed in the industry's nuances, and fault-tolerance is prized for certain applications. 

Encore strengths: Low hardware and support costs, excellent price/performance, extremely fast end-user response even when searching through large amounts of data.  Encore's Adaptive Computing has the opportunity to be the low-cost, highperformance database search and retrieval system to installed front-end Tandem systems. 

Encore's weaknesses: Limited support resources, minimal communications capabilities, no fault tolerance, and lack of credibility in the information services marketplace. 

## DEC VAXcluster 

DEC's VAXcluster has set the technological leadership role for loosely-coupled systems for the last decade.  Controlling the individual VAXes and disks in a VAXcluster is a device known as a Star Coupler on smaller clusters or an HSC controller on larger clusters.  These cluster control devices are dedicated computers. Encore's technological leapfrog is the use of RMS to perform many of the same functions as a cluster controller but much faster. 

DEC will claim that Encore's Adaptive Computing is just a pale attempt at imitating the functionality of a VAXcluster.  Aberdeen recommends that Encore respond by claiming that Adaptive Computing is a generation of technology beyond the VAXcluster and fulfills the functional specifications that DEC claimed for VAXcluster but never could effectively deliver (i.e: single-systems view, fast access to data located anywhere within the cluster, and node growth with no end-user response degradation). 

Encore strengths: Unix systems in a cluster, faster end-user response time, low hardware and support costs, excellent price/performance, beginning of new generation of systems while DEC is phasing out VAX, and interoperability of applications. 

Encore weaknesses: Minimal communications capabilities. 

## Teradata 

Teradata was started to provide Citicorp with a means to access large amounts of data for decision support applications.  The Teradata system is composed of multiple Intel 80X86 processors that are acquired as modules to perform specific tasks such 

as I/O and processing.  In addition, the Teradata system has an integrated SQL RDBMS.  Note that the typical Teradata system is configured at $1M-$1.2M and then has $.5M of upgrades over the next 18 months. 

Encore will find that Teradata has already made inroads into the largest banks for data retrieval applications.  And many prospects within the financial services industry will initially perceive the Adaptive Computing architecture as being in the same class as the Teradata.  Their first question will be whether the Series 91 & 93 can provide their enterprises with the ability to run OLTP applications that Teradata has promised but never been able to deliver. 

Encore strengths: Industry-standard RDBMS with Ingres that is far superior to Teradata, already on the Risc performance curve that Teradata must jump to eventually, ability to grow by adding full systems — not just processors and modules, price/performance. 

## Encore weaknesses: Minimal communications capabilities. 

## Sequent 

Aberdeen has found Sequent in the financial services institutions as a potential replacement for large mainframes.  Technologists within these institutions explain that multiple Sequents could be connected via high-speed fiber optic cables to create a single-systems view of an OLTP application.  In this scenario, each Sequent would handle one portion of the application and communicate with the others only when there was a need for a cross reference check with a database managed by one of the other systems.  The advantages are the use of a standard RDBMS, growth path, ease of modifying applications compared to a mainframe running CICS, and much lower cost. 

The large majority of Sequent's sales are for medium-sized applications where the user needs more power than a microcomputer such as an Altos or NCR Tower but does not want to pay the price of a DEC VAX.  As a result, Sequent has not announced a vision of high-end computing comparable to Encore's Adaptive Computing. 

Encore strengths: RMS for interoperable applications and fast response time compared to Sequent's interconnection with fiber optic cable, and RDBMS pointers and higher-performance Risc processors compared to Sequent's CISC Intel 80X86. Encore weaknesses: None.

## Appendix A Financial Applications 

## Banking Applications (Retail) 

On-line banking - integrates demand deposit accounting (checking), deposit instruments, installment and mortgage loans, and customer relationship data. Application software is a prerequisite in this market.  In the U.S., commercial banks have not implemented on-line banking and are unlikely to in the next five years. There is no pressing market demand to do so.  U.S. savings and loans have traditionally been on-line at the teller level and have implemented automated teller machines for off-premise services.  There are 25 large S&L's requiring more than 50 TPS for their systems.  However, the problems of S&L's are widely known. 

Internationally, banks in Europe and Japan are more centralized an on-line than their U.S. commercial counterparts.  Very large mainframes are the norm, requiring up to 125 TPS.  In Europe, the 1992 economic changes will probably loosen the national computer vendor strangle-hold on large bank computer systems, creating an opportunity. 

Relationship accounting - customer information systems aggregate data from individual bank applications into a relationship repository.  The intent is to allow personnel to view all of a customer's activities at the bank.  While widely desired, these systems have only been installed at the largest banks — or else the 

functionality is rudimentary.  The decision support characteristics of a relational database are ideal for this application, but RDBMS performance has been so poor (until recently) that these systems could not be price/performance justified.  While application performance requirements in TPS are modest, the processing necessary to manage complex decision support queries in real-time is high. 

Automated teller machines - local banks require up to 15 TPS.  Nationwide ATM networks like CIRRUS require 25 TPS or less.  This market is maturing.  Volume requirements are higher, to 60 TPS, in Europe due to the widespread use of debit cards for electronic funds transfer (EFT).  The darling market of the 1980s, we do not expect future growth at anything close to the historical rate. 

Credit and debit card - This market demands high throughput, expandability, and very high availability.  Card processing is quite lucrative because financial institutions charge a transaction fee on every one of the millions of transactions processed daily. In spite of the strength of the entrenched card processors, Mastercard and Visa, Sears started Discover in the mid-1980s and AT&T's wildly successful Universal card broke into the business in 1989.  We do not believe market-entry restrictions by Mastercard and Visa will stop other new competitors. 

Card applications have typically required multi-million dollar mainframes with faulttolerant front ends.  Volumes range from 100 TPS regional nodes to 300 TPS central switches.  Adaptive Computing has the benefits of excellent 

price/performance, mainframe-caliber throughput — plus, high availability, and good connectivity. 

## Banking Applications (Wholesale) 

International funds transfer - large money-center banks generate 10K-15K transactions per day (less than 1 TPS) in this high-profile application.  Security and availability are key requirements. 

Cash management - allows corporate cash managers the ability to move monies around the globe.  Timing is critical, volumes are a modest 20 TPS in large systems, and information retrieval and presentation is a critical factor in these applications. 

Foreign exchange - purchase and sale of foreign currencies.  Up to 40K transactions per day (1 TPS). 

Interbank clearing systems - time-critical bank-to-bank item transfer of checks, drafts, and notes among members.  Volumes to 200 TPS peak.  Examples include CHIPS (the NY clearing house) and FedWire, which handles Federal Reserve Bank transactions.  While this applications appears very attractive, there are only a handful of clearing house systems in place — it is not an attractive market. 

## Brokerage/Securities Applications 

Retail trading - multiple computers manage the flow of information from the broker's desktop through wire rooms to the exchange floor and back.  Volumes are substantially governed by the retail broker's ability to enter transactions;  75 TPS is a peak load at a large brokerage firm. 

Mutual funds - shareholder systems and securities accounting system volumes are up to 100 TPS.  The stock ticker plant application (usually on a fault-tolerant computer) maintains real-time database and position information for the institution's wholesale business.  At the start-of-day, opening quotes drive volume to 150 TPS.  These transactions require minimal database activity. 

Program trading - about 400 firms engage in program trading.  A typical program involves watching a basket of the Standard and Poor's 500 stocks and making continual mathematic calculations against the underlying options.  This application requires the ability to maintain the security "basket" database, run the linear equations 

necessary to make the buy/sell determination, and execute the trade through an automated interface to the trading floor.  This can involve the quick purchase or sale of 500 stocks;  each stock is an individual transaction.  Because program traders are competing with other large institutions, speed and accuracy become competitive advantages. 

Commodity trading - foreign currency, bond, and commodity trading involves international markets with intense competition.  Individual institutional traders use rapidly changing spreads between markets to make minuscule profits (or losses) on large volumes of transactions.  Computers are increasingly used to manage multiple market databases, determine profitable spreads, and signal traders to initiate the transaction.  Volumes range to 75 TPS. 

Information providers - Reuters, Telerate and other securities information providers deliver securities market information over proprietary networks to customers.  The systems require numerous (over 50) information input sources and the capacity to communicate 1-2M bytes per second over up to 2,000 communications lines. 

## Insurance Applications 

Claims Processing systems - these custom-tailored applications at large insurance companies generate volumes to 60 TPS.  None have been implemented with an RDBMS to our knowledge.  Aberdeen field research shows numerous insurance companies are reimplementing this core application — a once-in-a-decade opportunity. 

Agent Support - agent/broker online support for customer records, policy information, price quotes, etc.  Volumes to 40 TPS against many databases. 

Risk Management - online ad hoc management of claims data to adjust risks to rates. Large databases with decision support query volumes. 

Policy Systems - inquiry, billing, payments and renewals with online access by local or remote agents.  Volumes to 35 TPS.  Highly customized or sourced from specialized ISMs such as Policy Management Systems or Continuum. 

Actuarial - this computation-intensive application has traditionally been done on large mainframes in batch or background.  An opportunity exists for on-line actuarial decision support. 

Imaging - we believe the insurance industry will increasingly embrace digital imaging and the use of object-oriented databases.  The on-line storage and retrieval capacities of these systems at the major insurance companies are conjectural but have the potential to be very large.  Further, our research shows the insurance industry moving towards implementing imaging systems over the next four years. Appendix B

The report outlines the market opportunities, recommended strategies, key positioning messages, product issues, distribution program recommendations, competitive overview, and sales program recommendations for these systems in the financial and information services industries. The report emphasizes the concept of "Adaptive Computing," which leverages technologies like symmetric multiprocessing, clustering, memory-coupling, and high availability to offer superior performance and flexibility compared to traditional midrange and mainframe systems. 

## ## Recommendations for Computer Technology Buyers 

The document advises computer technology buyers, particularly in the financial and information services sectors, to consider Encore's Series 91 and 93 systems due to their advanced technological capabilities. Buyers should focus on the benefits of Adaptive Computing, such as high availability, superior expansion, investment protection, and better price-performance ratios. The report suggests that buyers look beyond commodity RISC/Open Systems and evaluate the unique advantages offered by Encore's Reflective Memory Systems (RMS) and Adaptive Computing architecture. 

## ## Analysis of Technology 

The technology discussed in the document, particularly Adaptive Computing, is highly relevant to mainstream computing in 2024. The concepts of symmetric multiprocessing, clustering, and memory-coupling are foundational to modern cloud computing and distributed systems. The document's focus on flexible, scalable, and high-performance computing solutions aligns well with current trends in edge computing and hybrid cloud architectures.
