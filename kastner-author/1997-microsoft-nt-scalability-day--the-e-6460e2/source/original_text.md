Microsoft NT Scalability Day: The Emperor Has
No Clothes
"Any business of any size can now run its enterprise applications on Microsoft
software and industry-standard hardware", declared Bill Gates as he kicked off
Scalability Day in New York. With a multi-million dollar extravaganza,
Microsoft claims it can now do it all with NT and BackOffice. We suggest
skeptical Information Technology buyers ask "when, how, with whom, and
why."
Microsoft to World: We Want It All
Aberdeen's Market Viewpoint, Microsoft/Intel: Undisputed Leadership in
Departmental Price/Performance in November 1995 said Microsoft's NT had
successfully jumped from workgroup to departmental-class applications
supporting around 250 users. In July 1996, we called NT Server 4.0 "a giant
step forward." Now, Microsoft says NT Server and BackOffice are ready for
even the biggest enterprise application loads – with lots of demo proof-points.
While there is no doubt that NT will be better in 1998 than it is now, we remain
unconvinced that NT is a panacea from desktop to enterprise – particularly
complex application environments at Fortune 500-size enterprises. Let's not be
distracted by marketing hype from reality – or caught by the Big Lie.
Carl Sagan Would Be Proud
The late astronomer famous for quotes on the enormity of the cosmos would be
proud of the bigger/better/faster superlatives hyped to demonstrate the
superiority of NT Server for the largest enterprise applications. But the so-
called proof points bear close analysis:
100 Million Internet Hits Per Day: a single two-processor Pentium Pro
with NT and Internet Server processed 100M hits in a 24-hour day, a hit
load one-third greater than Microsoft's own. We believe this benchmark
was unrealistic of real-world HTTP volumes and was unreasonably spread
evenly over a 24-hour day. Our challenge: let Microsoft run its HTTP
traffic at www.microsoft.com on one server – any one server;
"The One Billion Transactions Per Day Benchmark demonstrates the
performance of a computer system large enough to manage the daily
banking activities of a quarter of the world's population": Not really.
This benchmark has the features of the long-obsolete TPC-A test, and is a
far cry from a true bank application's functionality. We'll keep our money
in a real bank;
1.8 Million E-Mails a Day with 50,000 Users per Server: purports to
show the enterprise scalability of Exchange Server. But wait, the e-mails
are all Internet POP3 and SMTP, the work-load is spread over 24 hours –
ignoring normal e-mail peaks, and the product will not ship until Q1-1998.
Many Exchange customers have told Aberdeen (and the press) that they
cannot scale beyond a few hundred Exchange clients — a better view of
enterprise reality today;
A Terabyte of Imagery Data: Microsoft will sell satellite images over the
Internet starting in October using a future version of SQL Server. All of
SQL Server's competitors have supported large databases and relational
objects for some time. No big deal;
Support in SQL Server for Very Large Memories (VLM): a future release
of SQL Server on a future release of NT Server will support VLM. OK,
but Oracle and Informix have done this for over a year on Digital
Equipment's Unix on Alpha.
For Now, Look to Microsoft's Partners
NT and BackOffice need the capable help of Microsoft's partners for
enterprise-class software and services. These partner solutions and substitutions
are the only prudent way to push NT into high transaction rates, high
availability, fault tolerance, mainframe connectivity, systems management, and
enterprise-level services. Moreover, the ever-increasing performance of Intel's
microprocessors was given scant credit for NT scalability. Microsoft is
schizophrenic about partnering, wanting all the kudos and revenue but unable
to supply the best-of-breed products and services in all categories all the time.
Beware of all-Microsoft solutions.
Why a Scalability Day?
Scalability Day demonstrated for us how far NT has come – but also how far it
has to go. If Microsoft's objective was to demonstrate technology prowess, we
are underwhelmed. And a little disappointed that so much of the demonstrated
capabilities depend on next year's software. Scant help for executives designing
for today's IT projects.
We suspect that one unstated reason for Scalability Day is the continuing saga
of reported NT scalability debacles. Too many projects – often done by the line-
of-business with little IT support and oversight – push the envelope of real-
world, mid-1997 NT capabilities. The results are increased business risks,
career failures … and a black eye for Microsoft's championing of scalability
that users cannot obtain outside of a performance laboratory. If that's the case,
we believe Microsoft's Scalability Day we-can-do-anything demonstrations
will distance the company even further from scalability reality. And that could
open up a hard-to-close trust gap with the very customers that Microsoft is
trying to please.
Bottom Line: NT is Still "Not There"
We believe that NT remains best suited to departmental applications, and have
had considerable difficulty finding sites with more than a few hundred users.
Yes, many of the world's applications are now NT-capable. However, the real
enterprise high-end legitimately remains the province of Unix, the AS/400, and
even the mainframe.

### Abstract

Peter S. Kastner of Aberdeen Group delivers a pointed critique of Microsoft's Scalability Day event in New York, where Bill Gates declared NT and BackOffice ready for any enterprise. Kastner dissects five benchmark demonstrations as unrealistic or misleading, argues NT remains best suited to departmental (not enterprise) applications, and warns that Microsoft's all-or-nothing platform claims risk opening a 'trust gap' with enterprise customers. He concludes Unix, the AS/400, and even the mainframe legitimately retain the enterprise high-end.

**Actual Outcome 1:** Windows NT enterprise trajectory
- **Result:** Windows 2000 (2000) substantially improved enterprise scalability; NT Server licenses grew 50% faster than competitors in 1998 per IDC; but Unix/Linux retained enterprise high-end through 2000s. Kastner was correct that NT was not enterprise-ready in 1997 and that it would improve by 1998/1999. Linux eventually won the server market.

- **Year:** 1997
- **Confidence at time:** medium (based on customer reports)

**Actual Outcome 2:** Exchange scalability outcome
- **Result:** Exchange 2000/2003 substantially improved scalability; Exchange became dominant enterprise email platform with hundreds of millions of seats by 2010; the limitation Kastner documented was real in 1997 but was resolved by Exchange 2000
- **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes:** Kastner's 1997 assessment was accurate for mid-1997 but Exchange improved significantly with the 2000 version.

**Prediction 3:** Trust gap warning
- **Claimed:** Scalability Day demonstrations will open a 'hard-to-close trust gap' with enterprise customers
- **Year:** 1997
- **Confidence at time:** medium

**Actual Outcome 3:** Microsoft enterprise trust outcome
- **Result:** Mixed outcome: Windows 2000 eventually won over many enterprise skeptics; however, Microsoft's enterprise credibility gap persisted through the 2000s, particularly in financial services and government using Unix/mainframes. Linux rather than Windows ultimately became the enterprise default in data centers.
- **Confidence:** medium
- **Notes:** The trust gap Kastner predicted manifested but was eventually partially overcome by Windows 2000/2003 improvements.
