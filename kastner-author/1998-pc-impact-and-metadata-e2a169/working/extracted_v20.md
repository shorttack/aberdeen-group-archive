## **Don’t Fall for the Siren-Song Price of Under-powered Desktop PCs** 

_IS executives have been captivated by the siren song of sub-$1,000 PCs, thinking they could save millions on corporate desktop-PC buying.  And why not?  Buyers are seeing twice the CPU clock speed and twice the memory of a couple of years ago at bargain basement prices.  But recent Aberdeen research indicates many present and almost all future corporate desktops are better suited to more, not less._ 

## **Is Less Best?** 

The economics are compelling and simple; if astute IS managers can mandate less costly standard personal computer platforms, then they can easily save their enterprises millions of dollars a year at Global 1000-size organizations. The kind of decision that results in fat bonuses.  As a result, many organiza-tions in 1998 have been lagging behind Intel’s new product introductions that are now up to 450MHz in speed in favor of less powerful platforms.  Some are even buying home computers that sell for around $1,000 in volume thinking “these seem to do the job OK.”  Until now, Aberdeen had little evidence to argue the fallacy of less is better. 

## **What A Corporate Desktop Does Is Changing** 

Three years ago, a new desktop PC would typically run at 150 MHz and have a generous 32MB of main memory. Enough to run Windows 95, Office 95 and perhaps an e-mail client and file-sharing over a 10 Mbps LAN.  The PC cost in volume was over $2,000 per unit. Performance was quite acceptable with 32MB of memory but barely adequate with 16 megabytes of memory. 

Fast forward to late 1998.  The basic corporate desktop’s workload looks on the surface about the same as three years ago.  But research at Aberdeen Group shows a considerable increase in the loads placed on our own desktops.  What was a superb PC a year ago — say a 233 MHz Pentium II with 32 MB — is becoming just adequate, and everything older is struggling — sometimes badly.

- _**Net**_ :  TCP/IP and NetBEUI; 

- _**Suite**_ : Office 97; 

- _**Browser**_ : Internet Explorer 4.0;

## **Where Are The MIPS Going?** 

Sure, we can blame feature-bloated versions of software.  But the reality is that there has also been an explosion in services expected by desktop users and by IS managers: 

- Intranet, Internet and Extranet support including background updates (e.g., channels and “push”); 

- A tidal-wave of electronic mail; 

- Numerous shared-access network connections to workgroup data and applications; 

- Desktop systems management capability to improve remote access and help desk diagnosis. 

A lot of these MIPS-consuming services are occurring in background tasks invisible to the user.  Moreover, the tasks take lots of main memory — the virtual memory usage on my own PC is about 60 MB — so 32 MB of memory is now too little. 

## **Aberdeen’s “Welded Case” Model For PC Buying** 

Aberdeen’s own PC planning demands a 3-5 year asset life.  Like most IS buyers, we hate the cost and aggravation of hardware upgrades because it means the laborintensive opening of the desktop’s case to install more memory or a processor upgrade.  Multiplied by lots of desktops and it’s problematic.  We try to buy more PC power than is required at purchase knowing that software upgrades will inevitably consume the excess over the useful life of the machine. 

## **What Is The Best Choice For The Future?** 

Like many IS executives, as CIO I had hoped to save money for the company by buying Celeron-class PCs or 233 MHz Pentium II inventory close-outs. 

But our internal research, discussions with IT clients, and a view towards the near future convinced us that such a decision would soon prove to be a false economy.  The Microsoft-Intel 1999 PC Standard specification calls for a mini-mum of a 300 MHz CPU and 32 MB of memory.  Experience has shown that the minimum leads to less than satisfactory real-world performance that rapidly deteriorates over time — have you tried a 486 PC with today’s software lately?

The document from Aberdeen Group, dated September 10, 1998, argues against the trend of purchasing sub-$1,000 desktop PCs for corporate use. The report recommends investing in more powerful PCs to ensure longevity and better performance, suggesting a 400 MHz Pentium II with 128 MB of memory and a 17” monitor as the optimal configuration for corporate desktops. 

### 2. Summary of How Computer Technology Buyers Should React 

Computer technology buyers should avoid the temptation of purchasing low-cost, underpowered desktop PCs. Instead, they should invest in higher-end models that can handle the increasing demands of modern software and network services. This approach will maximize the useful economic life of the PCs, reduce the need for frequent upgrades, and ultimately save money in the long run.

The principle of investing in more powerful hardware to future-proof against software demands remains relevant. Modern enterprises continue to prioritize robust hardware to support increasingly complex applications and services, including AI, big data analytics, and cloud computing.
