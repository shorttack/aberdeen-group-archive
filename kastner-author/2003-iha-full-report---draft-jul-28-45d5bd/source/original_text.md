### EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**The Digital Electronics Market is Maturing Rapidly**

The lifestyle use of digital electronics, led by digital cameras, is becoming pervasive among all age groups and economic backgrounds. It was easy to fill the slots in the two cities (Boston and San Diego) where focus group and individual direct interviews (IDIs) were held. Aberdeen views the market as more mature from a receptivity and likely future buying pattern basis as a result of this study than we would have conjectured prior to the study.

**It Often Starts With a Digital Camera --**

The explosive growth of digital cameras, combined with the ubiquitous availability of home PCs and Internet access, is a clear market stimulant. The Internet as a keep-in-touch lifestyle element is absolutely true. Once acquired, digital cameras are used in place of traditional film cameras, but with two technology twists:

- Digital photos are e-mailed to friends and family, and printed locally. E-mail is a key behavioral driver.
- A measurable minority of consumers take on photo manipulation and enhancement as a hobby. They are comfortable with this.

**-- or Music on MP3s**

Young people are highly likely to store and use music digitally. They make MP3s, store them on CD-ROMs, and use them as personal entertainment on MP3 players or to share with friends. All ages are beginning to store legitimate music collections on PCs, and some are thinking about transferring photos and videos to PC digital storage.

**Digital Data Manipulation Goes Mainstream**

Digital manipulation of data, be it songs, movies, or photos, is clearly becoming mainstream. A majority of consumers are comfortable with using digital electronics products, and a minority report that in the past year they have significantly increased the time spent creating digital content (e.g., PhotoShop) as a hobby or as a means to author content (e.g., home digital movie making). Thus, data transfer from the capture device to the display/print/PC manipulate device becomes a recognized and routine way to deal with digital data.

The implications for a robust transition to digital electronics from traditional consumer electronics are obvious. However, this study did not determine the boundaries of the transition timeframe, which we would put at a decade from now.

**Data Content Portability Rises in Importance**

Almost all consumers are aware that digital data content is portable. MP3 players and digital cameras with memory cards were frequently cited as examples. The ability to move data from one device to another is considered a key advantage to digital electronics products. One reason is the ability to take the content outside the home, where it can be used at work or play (e.g., an MP3 player) or shared with friends and family (e.g., photos on a memory card).

**The Memory Card is a Recognized Digital-Data Content Carrier**

Primarily due to the ubiquity of digital cameras, the vast majority of consumers was both aware of and/or knew they had a memory card. The implication is that memory card what-is-it-used-for education is not required as part of a marketing campaign.

However, the majority were unable to identify the type of memory card they owned. This lack of recognition of the taxonomy of memory cards -- the card's market and technology strengths and weaknesses -- becomes an inhibitor to the market adoption of SD memory cards. Moreover, the memory card choice to date has been driven by the product the card is used in: consumers are not yet buying digital electronics products based on compatibility with existing memory cards that they already own.

**Perceived Advantages and Disadvantages of Memory Cards**

The focus groups did not have much difficulty identifying and reaching consensus on the advantages and disadvantages of memory cards.

The advantages are: small size; capacity choice; re-usability; and improving price points.

The disadvantages are: small size can be lost; variety of incompatible formats; price, particularly in larger capacities.

**Memory Card Capacity: More is Better**

Retail stores apparently are getting better at advising buyers about the small capacity of memory cards installed at the factory. A significant minority report they bought higher capacity cards so they could store more photos or songs.

Once the focus groups had studied a chart of memory capacities versus content capacity (e.g., how many photos can the card hold?), they strongly chose higher capacities for hypothetical future purchases -- mostly in the range of 256 MB to 1 GB where price was not a consideration. Given a choice of personal cards for a family versus an investment in a "big" capacity card, consumers choose one big card.

**The Digital Electronics Future for Memory Cards is Bright**

When shown a demonstration of the interchangeability of content between cameras, camcorders, printers, PCs, PDAs, DVDs and other digital electronics products, the most frequent response was "wow!" Focus group participants were visibly excited about the prospect of using memory cards as a medium for content storage and transport.

Importantly, consumers in the panels easily made the connections needed to ensure a more widespread adoption of memory cards. Aberdeen believes the main connected dots are:

- Standardizing on one memory card format ensures compatibility across a wide variety of digital products. Note: the SD memory card is one contender but not the assured winner since card type is not well understood.
- Many electronics products are appearing with memory card slots, and consumers are comfortable with moving a memory card(s) from product to product.
- Consumer's current electronic products do not have memory card slots, and consumers are unwilling -- not surprisingly -- to throw out what they have just to get memory card capability. The transition will take time, which we put at the better part of a decade to complete.

### SUMMARY OF KEY FINDINGS

- Respondents own and use a wide range of digital technology, with the vast majority owning: desktop computers, printers, and digital cameras. Other digital technology owned by some respondents includes: video camcorders, DVD players, PDAs, scanners, MP3 players, photo printers, and laptops.

- Transferring digital data is important to many respondents, particularly to San Diego respondents and "early adopters" in the Boston market. Younger respondents participating in the individual direct interviews (IDIs) who regard it as a leisure time activity, also consider transferring digital data very important. Older respondents (particularly in Boston) seem reluctant to state this is not a very important activity to them, but several report they have limited technology (for financial reasons) or that they use both digital and non-digital means of meeting their communication needs.

- Digital data is transferred by various means, depending on the type of data and the available resources:
  - digital photos are most frequently transferred to PCs via a cable or wire link, although a minority of respondents use memory cards,
  - video footage is most frequently transferred by connecting the camcorder to the VCR and transferring the footage to VHS tapes,
  - music is most typically transferred by downloading it onto a PC and then burning CDs.

- Only a few respondents across the focus groups and IDIs have in-home local area networks, which they occasionally use for transferring data. Wireless transfer of data is limited to PDAs.

- Many respondents find it quick and easy to transfer images, particularly photos, from one technology to another. After the initial set-up, most report they simply connect a wire from their camera to their computer through a USB port, and click on an icon. Transfers from camcorders are also considered straightforward, while music is regarded as the most difficult type of digital transfer because of the amount of computer memory it requires and the time it takes to both download the material and burn CDs.

- Respondents who express dissatisfaction with how they transfer digital data typically focus on the following: the need for additional equipment purchases, lack of sufficient USB ports to use several peripherals simultaneously, and complex, poorly-written instructions that are hard to follow. In addition, some respondents are dissatisfied with the capacity of the memory cards in their digital cameras and the difficulty of transferring pictures from their memory card while traveling.

- Many respondents are unable to suggest solutions for the difficulties they face transferring digital data, but a few respondents feel wireless transmission should be the ultimate goal. This will reduce the amount of equipment needed and steps required to accomplish desired data transfer.

- Research participants living in family households report that some household members are uninvolved in the transfer of digital data. Typically, one adult head-of-household, along with one or more teenaged/young adult children, engages in this activity. Whether it is the male or female head-of-household who becomes involved in data transfer varies from household to household. In contrast, young adults living with their peers, and young marrieds, typically indicate everyone in the household transfers data.

- Those living in households where some members are reluctant to become involved in digital data transfer feel the best way to induce participation is to simplify and shorten the process.

- Most respondents have few concerns about the storage of their digital data. Typical practices include storing photos on PC hard drives (and occasionally, CDs), storing video on VHS tapes, and storing music on CDs. No one participating in this research uses memory cards to store digital data.

- Most agree they have little difficulty retrieving digital material if they have taken the time to label their files or devices. A few respondents express some concerns about the number of CDs or videotapes they need to store. Respondents who download music are most likely to say they have less hard drive memory than they'd like, because music files are so large. Most respondents, however, feel storage space is not an issue for them at present.

- Usage of memory cards varies among the research participants, with Boston early adopters and younger respondents in all groups/IDIs most likely to be users. Digital camera owners are also likely to say they use memory cards (although not all are aware they do), along with those using PDAs. Many respondents cannot say what brand of memory card they use. Sony Memory Stick users appear to be most brand-aware.

- Memory card users feel the medium has several advantages, including: small size/portability, varying capacities, and reusability. A few respondents also feel declining prices and compatibility with various technologies are advantages of memory cards.

- Memory card users also feel they have two key disadvantages: their small size makes them easy to misplace or break and they come in a variety of formats. In addition, some respondents consider them expensive and/or lacking in sufficient capacity.

- Reactions to the SD Memory Card demonstration are largely positive. The product has a high "cool factor" for some respondents, primarily because of the many products it works with. The card's "versatility," (e.g., ability to work with a wide range of technologies and manufacturer's products) is considered a key advantage by many respondents. Other benefits widely recognized by respondents include: small size/portability, the availability of various (including, large) capacities, and speed. Some respondents, particularly those in San Diego, feel the SD Memory Card could replace other storage devices, such as CDs and videotapes, saving users both time and space.

- The key drawback of the SD Memory Card is that users need to own compatible equipment. The more compatible equipment a user owns, the more he or she can benefit from usage of the memory card. Many research participants indicate they currently own no compatible equipment, and the need to acquire such equipment is viewed as a drawback for the memory card. Another key drawback is the memory card's size, which is perceived to be notably smaller than many currently used by respondents. In addition to the risk of loss, or having children and pets swallow the small unit, respondents are also concerned about the ease of labeling the device so they can easily retrieve material stored there. Finally, some respondents have concerns that even if adapters are/become available, the card will not function as quickly as it does with state-of-the-art equipment, depriving them of one of its key benefits.

- Some respondents, particularly those from the Boston market, can identify specific ways they are likely to change their use of digital technology. Those mentioned most frequently include: taking more digital photos, creating family photo albums to store on the memory card, and downloading more music.

- Most respondents, however, do not feel the SD Memory Card would change their usage of the digital technology they already own because it is not compatible with the memory card. Many express reluctance to acquire technology that could be used with the SD Memory Card because they are unsure it will become the "industry standard," instead of being eclipsed by a newer, better technology.

- Many respondents will take a "wait and see" attitude toward purchasing the SD Memory Card. Boston respondents, particularly, express concern about whether this product will be widely accepted, or quickly fade away.

- Some respondents in all groups, as well as adults participating in IDIs, are skeptical about purchasing the memory card (and the equipment needed to maximize its usefulness) without assurance it will be in the market over the longer term. However, the key barrier to immediate adoption of the SD Memory Card is respondents' lack of compatible equipment.

- Respondents' qualms about purchasing equipment compatible with the SD Memory Card range from income constraints, to concerns about the long-term status of the technology, to satisfaction with their current manner of transferring digital data. Overall, San Diego respondents and teens participating in the IDIs are most likely to say they are interested in purchasing the SD Memory Card.

- Respondents have great difficulty articulating what they would be willing to pay for the SD Memory Card. Past purchasers of any memory card sometimes use the prices they paid as benchmarks. Others, consider the cost of other storage media (such as DVDs, videotapes, or CDs). Overall, respondents are prepared to pay less than the anticipated prices of the product at various capacities. In addition, they expect product prices to decline over time.

- If purchasing the SD Memory Card, most respondents would opt for a larger capacity card (to meet their largest anticipated need) over several smaller ones. On an aided basis, however, some respondents with children, as well as some who use a wide range of technologies, acknowledge that several smaller cards could be more advantageous.

- The vast majority of respondents feel SD Memory Card capability would be a minor consideration in their choice of new digital technology products. However, many would regard it as a "tie breaker," opting for products with the capability over very similar products without the capability. However, having acquired one product with SD Memory Card capability, they would be more likely to seek the capability in subsequent digital technology product purchases.

- Respondents are divided in their opinions about whether they would pay a premium for products with SD Memory Card capability. A substantial proportion would be willing to do so, although how much of a premium they would pay is unclear. Quite a few of these respondents indicate it would be in the 10-15% range, although some would pay more. On the other hand, a notable proportion of respondents claim they would not pay a premium. Many of these respondents indicate they would wait to see if the product became an established standard, and then expect the capability to be widely incorporated in new digital products (at no additional charge). In general, respondents do not expect products with SD Memory Card capability to be any more difficult to use than those without it.

- Respondents expect the SD Memory Card would be widely distributed through retail and on-line sources. Consumer electronics stores such as Best Buy, computer stores, office superstores, warehouse clubs, and mass merchants (like Target and Wal-Mart) are all expected to sell SD Memory Cards, along with any websites selling digital technology. In addition, non-traditional outlets, such as toy stores, grocery stores, audio/video stores, and pharmacies are all expected to eventually carry SD Memory Cards (much like they currently sell film).

- SD Memory Cards are expected to be displayed in the storage and accessories section of consumer electronics stores, as well as with each type of product in which they can be used. Consumers want to face no difficulties finding memory cards, whether they come into a consumer electronics store expressly to purchase one, or whether they are primarily purchasing a product that uses it.

- Teams of respondents in the various focus groups agree the SD Memory Card has the following key benefits: size, portability, universality (versatility), capacity, ease-of-use, and speed. Along with mass media (TV, radio, print) and on-line advertising, along with direct mail campaigns, most teams highlight the importance of product demonstrations as a means of communicating the memory card's key benefits. They believe in-store demonstrations, continuous loop videos, infomercials, and opportunities for respondents to get "hands-on" exposure to the SD Memory Card are the best way to communicate what it can do for users.

### Background and Purpose

**The Internet Home Alliance**

The Internet Home Alliance (IHA) is a trade association of companies involved in digital home electronics. For this project, members of the alliance are primarily interested in more effectively marketing the SD Memory Card, although a home electronics retailer who distributes memory cards nationally also participates in the consortium.

**Secure Digital Memory Cards**

The SD memory card is a portable storage product that can be used to transfer digital information (including still images, video, music, and text) from one device to another. It allows users to gather information via one device and then edit, share, or output it on another device.

**Project Scope**

In order to maximize the effectiveness of its marketing strategies, the IHA commissioned primary qualitative market research to better understand the target audience's needs, expectations, and buying behaviors. Specifically, the goals of this research were to explore the following issues:

1. how the target market currently meets data transfer needs, the pros and cons of current methods, and identification of any unmet needs,
2. reactions to the SD memory card, potential uses for the card, and comparisons to other options for data storage/portability,
3. identification of the product features/characteristics of greatest value to potential users and the likelihood that use of the SD memory card would change behavior with respect to use of memory cards and/or host products,
4. potential uses for the additional storage capacity provided by the SD memory card,
5. likelihood to purchase SD memory cards at various price points and the impact of SD memory card purchases on the purchasing of peripherals which utilize them, and
6. target audience expectations of the purchase experience (e.g., where/how will they be sold and where will they be displayed in a retail store environment).

**Methodology**

Qualitative research was the chosen methodology for addressing the issues facing IHA because of the opportunities it provides for open-ended exploration of topics and issues. A total of six focus groups were conducted. Three were held in the Boston area (on June 9 and 10, 2003), and three in San Diego (on June 23 and 24, 2003). In addition, 11 in-depth interviews (six in Boston and five in San Diego) were completed among parent/child dyads or triads.

Respondents qualified to participate in the focus groups met the following qualifications:

- Own at least one of the following digital devices (purchased for home use): Video Camcorder, Personal Digital Assistant (PDA), Printer which uses a memory card, Digital Camera, HP Media Center PC, Notebook PC with a memory card, MP3 player with a memory card slot
- Are the primary user of at least one of the digital devices owned by the household
- Are aware of memory cards
- Are between 18 and 70 years of age
- Live in a variety of household settings (e.g., living alone, living with spouse/partner and/or children etc.)
- Are not "Early Adopters" of technology (based on four batteries of attitudinal questions)
- Are not employed in advertising, market research, public relations, or in sales, marketing, or marketing communications for a manufacturer, distributor or retailer of consumer electronics or technology products
- Have not participated in a market research group discussion during the past six months

One Boston group consisted of respondents who qualify as "Early Adopters" based on four batteries of attitudinal questions.

Respondents qualified to participate in the in-depth interviews met the same product purchase/usage qualifications as focus group participants (outlined above). In addition, they met the following criteria:

- Live in a household with a child/children who is/are 11 to 17 years of age
- Have at least one child who also uses one or more of the target digital devices
- Able to participate in the interview along with one or more qualified children (and possibly, a spouse)

Tabulated respondent questionnaire data is included in Appendix A of this report. Copies of the screeners and discussion guide are included in Appendix B of this report.

**A Caution**

The results of this study are presented as observations or hypotheses, not as incontrovertible facts; the sample sizes were small and not representative in a statistical sense. The hypotheses which follow should be checked against quantitative data on the same subject and/or verified by additional quantitative or experimental research.

### DETAILS OF FINDINGS

#### I. Usage of Digital Technology

**A. Types of Technology Used**

1. Consistent with screening requirements, respondents in all focus groups and IDIs own and use a range of digital technology. Types of technology most commonly owned by participants include: desktop computers, printers, and digital cameras. In addition, some respondents own Palm Pilots, video camcorders, DVD players, scanners, MP3 players, laptops, and/or photo printers.

2. Desktop computers are owned by virtually everyone participating in this research and are used for a wide range of purposes, including personal, business, and school-related functions. Desktop computers are often the lynch pin upon which a user's integration of digital technology depends.

   > "The computer linking in personal use is as an entertainment center, information center, a communication center." (San Diego male)

   > "I think the thing I use most is my PC. I'm on it from dawn until dusk, and my cell phone, too." (Boston male)

3. After computers, cameras are the digital technology most likely to be owned by participants in this research. Many respondents make extensive use of their digital cameras, primarily for personal reasons, but sometimes for business purposes as well. Digital photos are considered easy to review, store, and share. This is particularly important to respondents living far from their families and those with children or grandchildren. Digital photos (and the ability to e-mail them) play a critical role in connecting generations.

   > "I take a lot of pictures. I have a digital camera that takes still pictures and movies, and I e-mail them. I have a lot of family and friends out of the area." (San Diego female)

   > "My mother's side of the family lives in Korea, we can send them pictures of my baby sister. My grandfather actually hasn't seen her in person yet, but he knows what she looks like because of the photos we send." (Boston male)

4. Digital video camcorders are most likely to be owned by families raising children. The key motivation for owning this equipment is to chronicle the growth and development of children, along with their participation in various activities.

5. Quite a few respondents across these focus groups and IDIs download music from the internet, although only a few have MP3 players. Many younger respondents (primarily those under 35, including teens interviewed in the IDIs) download music from the Internet and then burn CDs.

6. Some respondents in these focus groups also have PDAs, which are often used primarily for work/business purposes. More male than female respondents use PDAs in both the Boston and San Diego focus groups.

**B. Transfer of Digital Data**

1. Many respondents claim that transferring digital data is very important to them. San Diego respondents and Boston Early Adopters are more likely to consider this activity "very important" than others.

2. Digital data is transferred by various means, depending on what type of data it is and the available resources. Photos are most likely to be transferred via some type of cable or wire link, although a few respondents transfer photos by moving memory cards.

   > "I connect the camera to the computer with a cord that goes into a USB port. The icon just comes up on the screen and I press it." (Boston male -- IDI respondent)

   > "I do it [transfer photos] with a memory card. You plug in the reader, you stick a memory card in, and it just pops up on my computer. It's beautiful." (San Diego female)

3. Comparatively few respondents transfer video, and those who do are primarily living in family households. Transfer of video footage is most commonly done by connecting a video camcorder to a VCR (via a cable) and transferring the footage to VHS tapes.

4. Transfer of music is primarily a matter of downloading from the Internet onto a PC, and then burning CDs.

5. A few respondents report they have local area networks in their households and occasionally use them to transfer digital information. Wireless transfer of digital data is only done with PDAs.

6. Participants generally agree it is quick and easy to transfer digital information from one technology to another. Some also appreciate the ability to modify what they have transferred and the speed with which they can have access to photos and music.

7. While many respondents are very satisfied with the process they use to transfer digital information, others express concerns about equipment purchases, multi-step processes with poorly written instructions, difficulty transferring photos when away from home, and dissatisfaction with the quality of photos.

   > "When something you're trying to do doesn't work well and you have to go out and buy another piece of equipment, or when you have to upgrade something because it doesn't communicate well with something else. It just never ends." (Boston female)

   > "For all of us elderly people who started late with the technology, it's a lot harder." (San Diego male)

8. One solution to digital data transfer problems suggested by a few respondents across the focus groups is to develop a wireless capability.

   > "I think we definitely need some sort of remoting capability." (Boston male - Early Adopter)

   > "Too many wires and cords all over the damn house everywhere, get rid of all the wires! Do it without wires." (Boston male)

9. Respondents living in family households often report that not everyone in their household is involved in transferring digital data. In contrast, respondents living in households of unrelated individuals often say all or most household members transfer digital data.

10. Most respondents living in households where some others shy away from transferring digital material feel the best inducement for involving other household members would be simplifying the process.

**C. Storage of Digital Data**

1. Most respondents use their computer's hard drive as storage for their digital photos. Some respondents transfer photos to CDs, either as a back up or because they do not have adequate space on their hard drive.

2. Music downloaded from the Internet is typically transferred to CDs (unless a respondent has a MP3 player) to save space on the hard drive.

3. Respondents store most video by transferring it to VHS tapes. A few respondents have transferred video to CDs, and isolated respondents store video on their hard drive.

4. No one in these focus groups uses memory cards for permanent storage of any digital data. Memory cards are considered too expensive and too easy to lose for archival purposes.

5. Respondents generally report few digital information storage problems. Running out of space on their computers and having a large number of CDs or tapes to store are the issues mentioned most often.

**D. Digital Technology Information Sources**

1. Respondents rely on many different sources of information to learn about developments in digital technology. The most frequently cited source is word-of-mouth recommendations from friends, family, and co-workers perceived to be knowledgeable.

2. Another very important source of information is the Internet. Many respondents go online to learn about new products or to get additional information about products they may already be using.

3. Some respondents consider consumer electronics retailers and computer stores to be good sources of information, but others do not. Most note they ultimately go to local stores to see the products of interest to them.

4. Mass media sources of information (e.g., newspapers, magazines, and television) are used by some respondents. Mail order catalogues serve as a source of information for some others.

#### II. Current Usage and Impressions of Memory Cards

**A. Usage of Memory Cards**

1. Awareness and usage of memory cards vary across the focus groups and IDIs. Boston Early Adopters and younger respondents in all groups/IDIs are most likely to be aware of memory cards, as are users of digital cameras.

2. Many respondents cannot say what brand of memory card they are currently using. Sony Memory Stick users appear to be most brand-aware.

3. Respondents using memory cards most commonly report they use them in their digital cameras and/or PDAs.

4. Most users of memory cards in digital cameras never remove their memory card from the camera, since all transferring of images is done through a cable connection.

**B. Advantages of Memory Cards**

1. One advantage mentioned very frequently is the small size, and associated portability, of the cards.

2. Memory cards come in various capacities, allowing one memory card to potentially hold a great deal of digital data.

3. The reusability of memory cards also appeals to many current users.

4. A few respondents, primarily in the San Diego focus groups, feel declining prices and versatility are advantages.

**C. Disadvantages of Memory Cards**

1. Many users feel the memory card's small size is both an advantage and a disadvantage because it makes the card easy to misplace or break.

2. Memory card users who are aware that memory cards come in a variety of formats find this to be a disadvantage.

3. Some memory card users mention either price or capacity as disadvantages.

#### III. Post-Demonstration Reactions to SD Memory Card

**A. Perceived Benefits**

1. After viewing the SD Memory Card demonstration, some respondents clearly feel this product has a high "cool factor." The most frequent response was "wow!" Focus group participants were visibly excited about cross-device content interchangeability.

2. A key benefit is the card's ability to be used with many different types of technologies. This "versatility" sets the card apart from others in the category.

3. Another advantage is the SD Memory Card's small size, particularly in relation to the amount of digital information it can store.

4. Quite a few respondents feel the SD Memory Card could help them save time in transferring digital information.

5. A few respondents feel another advantage is that it could replace other storage devices.

**B. Perceived Drawbacks**

1. The key perceived drawback is the need for users to have compatible equipment.

   > "Right now I don't think we have anything but the Palm that can use it." (Boston female - IDI respondent)

2. Many respondents express concern about the small size, perceived as both an advantage and disadvantage (risk of loss, choking hazard for children/pets, difficult to label).

3. A few participants express concerns about compatibility with older equipment and ease of maintenance.

**C. Expected Impact on Usage of Digital Technology**

1. Some respondents, particularly in Boston, can identify specific changes they would make in their use of technology.

2. Many respondents indicate they would not expect their usage of current technology to change because their devices are not compatible with the SD Memory Card.

**D. Purchase Intent and Pricing**

1. Quite a few participants would take a "wait and see" attitude before deciding to purchase the SD Memory Card. Many express concern about whether SD will become the industry standard or be eclipsed by a newer technology.

2. The greatest barrier to purchase intent is that most respondents do not currently own compatible equipment.

3. San Diego respondents and teens participating in the IDIs are more likely to say they are interested in purchasing the SD Memory Card.

4. Most respondents have great difficulty articulating how much they would be willing to pay. They expect prices to initially be high and then decline.

   > "I really can't tell you what one of these would cost. I bet it's a lot though, because it's new. Usually the prices come down after something's been around for awhile." (Boston female - IDI respondent)

5. Some respondents consider pricing in terms of the cost of alternative media (DVDs, CDs).

6. With operational descriptions of capacity, most would select a larger capacity card (512MB+) but would be willing to pay considerably less than expected prices.

7. Most respondents would want to purchase one larger SD Memory Card instead of several smaller ones, primarily to avoid running out of space during travel or family events.

**E. Impact of SD Memory Card Capability on Digital Product Purchase Decisions**

1. Most respondents claim SD Memory Card capability is not likely to be a key factor in digital product purchase decisions. However, it is considered a "tie breaker" when two products are otherwise equal.

2. Most respondents agree that if they already had one product with SD Memory Card capability, they would make it a priority to have this capability in subsequent purchases, creating a network/ecosystem effect.

3. The vast majority expect products with SD Memory Card capability to be just as easy to use as comparable products without it.

4. Many respondents would be willing to pay a 10-15% premium for SD Memory Card capability. However, a sizeable segment would pay no premium, expecting it to eventually become standard.

#### IV. Retail Environment

**A. Purchasing Sources**

1. Respondents expect to purchase SD Memory Cards in consumer electronics stores (Best Buy, Circuit City), computer superstores (CompUSA), office superstores (Office Max, Staples), mass merchants (Target, Wal-Mart), and online.

2. Some respondents expect distribution to ultimately extend to toy stores, grocery stores, warehouse clubs, drug stores, camera stores, and audio/video outlets.

   > "Well given the way our technological industry works, within a year or two of its coming out, I'd expect to find it in Walgreen's and CVS." (Boston female)

**B. In-Store Positioning of SD Memory Cards**

1. Most respondents expect SD Memory Cards in the storage/accessories section, alongside CDs, floppy discs, and other memory cards.

2. Most respondents would also expect the SD Memory Card to be displayed with any digital equipment that uses it (cameras, printers, PDAs, MP3 players, etc.).

   > "It should be almost like a product placement in a movie. If I'm buying this great DVD player that has a memory card, I'd almost want to see some hanging there." (Boston female)

#### V. Communication of Key SD Memory Card Benefits

**A. Key Benefits**

1. Teams across the focus groups consistently identify these key benefits: size, portability, universality (versatility), capacity, ease-of-use, and speed.

   > "We said size, convenience, compatibility with many products, speed, it's easy to travel with." (Boston male)

**B. Communication Strategies**

2. Respondents suggest Internet-based marketing, mass media (TV, print), and promotions including mailing small-capacity sample cards to device owners.

3. Many teams emphasize that product demonstrations would be the most powerful communication strategy. In-store demonstrations, continuous loop videos, and hands-on experiences are recommended.

   > "We'd go into a bunch of stores with like TV/VCR units, with an endless broadcast loop. Set up near where the units were for sale, and show what it can do." (Boston male)

### Abstract

A qualitative market research study commissioned by the Internet Home Alliance (IHA) examining consumer adoption patterns for Secure Digital (SD) memory cards. Based on 6 focus groups and 11 in-depth interviews conducted in Boston and San Diego in June 2003, the study explores how consumers transfer, store, and use digital data; their awareness and perceptions of memory cards; reactions to SD card demonstrations; purchase intent and pricing expectations; and retail environment preferences. Key findings include digital cameras as the primary driver of memory card awareness, USB cable transfer as the dominant method over card removal, a prevalent "wait and see" attitude toward SD card purchases, willingness to pay a 10-15% premium for SD capability, and strong recommendations for in-store demonstrations as the most effective communication strategy. The study identified size, portability, universality, capacity, ease-of-use, and speed as the six key SD card benefits perceived by consumers.
