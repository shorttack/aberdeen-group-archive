## Original Document Text

Intel's Centrino: Don't Man the Barricades

Intel's Centrino is an evolutionary step in wireless notebook computers that is likely to cause all sorts of reactions, some revolutionary. It's critical for IT executives to sort out the benefits from the gotchas of mobile employees. Unfortunately, we see signs that more than a few IT shops are willing to opt out on Centrino's chief benefit, wireless computing.

What's Centrino and Why Should I Care?
Hey, we went on vacation last month so we can understand that you might have missed the opening salvo in Intel's $300 marketing launch of Centrino. The TV and newspaper ads and the billboards in Times Square, for instance. The key points are: Centrino is an all new, integrated set of electronics from Intel that consists of a mobile Pentium 4, a chipset, and an 802.11-compatible wireless Ethernet adapter and radio. This integrated platform was built from the ground up for long battery life -- over five hours -- and better performance than today's Pentium 3 and Pentium 4 mobile processors. All the major notebook computer companies are rolling out Centrino products, at attractive, mainstream prices. Consumers version of Centrino will untether people to compute out on the sundeck. Enterprise versions will enable information workers to keep up to date while on the go -- the so-called always on, always available mantra.

Centrino is the most important introduction that Intel, the industry's dominant microprocessor supplier, will make in 2003. Besides the glitz of the ad campaigns, the company is spending tens of millions of dollars to accelerate a worldwide network of wireless access points -- so-called hot spots -- where 802.11-enabled computers including Centrinos can get on the Internet for all the reasons that the Internet has taken a role in our lives.

The evolutionary fact of Centrino is simply that other 802.11 and wireless approaches exist in the market. It is the clout, market-awareness building, and wireless infrastructure-building muscle of Intel that makes Centrino's introduction not just another product launch but a milestone in the adaptation of computers into our lives. Mark down March 12th, 2003 on your computer history calendar.

The revolutionary aspects of the Centrino notebooks and the wireless-everywhere world that will follow imply consequences that deserve consideration. We'll leave the question as to whether the initial wireless public infrastructure will provide a consumer-friendly quality of service for another day. The immediate question for IT decision-makers is what to do about all those wireless notebooks that are about to invade the corporate campus?

The Wireless Invasion is at Hand
The scenario is simply stated: Centrino notebooks come with wireless connectivity for free -- it's part of the platform and you won't save any money by deleting the 802.11 part on the order form. That means future notebooks, which represent a quarter or more of personal computer units sold to enterprises, will virtually all come standard with wireless Ethernet connectivity. In and of itself, that is not a problem. However, 802.11 requires time, effort, and money from the IT department to make wireless networking secure. And any employee can walk down to CompUSA on Saturday and come back with a $90 wireless access point (AP) that connects their notebook to the corporate wired network. All they have to do is plug the AP into a ubiquitous RJ45 wired LAN receptacle using a $3 CAT-5 cable. Unfortunately, this wireless AP also let's anyone within about 150' also have access to the corporate network if the AP is not properly secured. This unobtrusive exposure of the corporate network can be a breech in an otherwise well-managed and secure network. This is why such wireless access points are called "rogues".

Centrino Wireless: Banned in Boston?
Faced with the prospect of rogue access points, many IT executives are reacting by banning wireless notebooks because they are the reason wireless access points are being deployed by users outside of the IT department's domain in the first place. One notebook supplier expects more than a quarter of its business Centrino notebooks to ship with explicit IT department instructions to remove the wireless radio mini-card at the factory -- permanently neutering the Centrino's ability to work wirelessly on the corporate campus or anywhere else.

A Better Solution
Banning wireless is about as effective as keeping hormone-charged teenagers apart. Occasionally successful but never assured. Recent developments make wireless security and authentication much more practical. Cisco is making its LEAP protocol available to the industry. Centrino is LEAP-aware. And Microsoft's Windows Server 2003 can be hardened for wireless environments, as Microsoft has proven on its own campus and elsewhere. Services are readily available to investigate and secure a wireless campus..

I believe Centrino notebooks -- and the wireless network they work best in -- should become one of the standard IT infrastructure building blocks. PricewaterhouseCoopers says that the additional initial costs of wireless notebooks can achieve a return on investment in six months or less. Aberdeen Group research indicates that a campus-bound meeting-attendee can be productive an extra 30 minutes a day. Task workers such as physicians can see an hour a day in productivity improvements in conjunction with digital record-keeping. Senior executives, sales and support people will quickly subscribe to public wires services -- just like they were expensing cell phones well before a corporate policy was put in place.

Taking the wireless out of Centrino leaves the buyer with -- longer battery life, a nice but not consequential benefit. This artificial barricade to wireless will be impossible to police. The better solution is to face the inevitability and desirability of a wireless world and embrace it.

--Peter S Kastner
