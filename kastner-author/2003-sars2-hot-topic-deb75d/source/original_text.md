# SARS May Impact Global Electronics Industry

**Study ID:** 2003-sars2-hot-topic-deb75d
**Authors:** Russ Craig; Peter Kastner
**Date:** 2003-03-28
**Type:** Hot Topic
**Publisher:** Aberdeen Group

## Original Document Text

The SARS (Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome) epidemic may have a dramatic impact on the global electronics industry. Asia Pacific consumed an estimated 37% or $52 billion of 2002 semiconductor production. The Semiconductor Industry Association (SIA) estimates that Asia Pacific will consume $65 billion (38%) in 2003, with the Peoples Republic of China (PRC) alone representing 11%.

Over 85% of all WiFi (802.11) gear is made in Taiwan and the PRC. Sony is moving all PlayStation manufacturing to the PRC. Eight new PRC 8-inch semiconductor plants are under construction with NEC, Philips, Oki, Elpida, and Infineon among the companies that have committed to new PRC fabs.

Motorola has temporarily closed its Singapore cell phone plant after 305 of its workers were quarantined. CTIC had a corporate shutdown when 6 employees who had returned from Beijing via Hong Kong were diagnosed with SARS. Compal has barred employees from traveling to Hong Kong and the PRC.

Sony has postponed a Taiwan procurement conference, citing the Iraq war rather than SARS. Broadcom, Cirrus Logic, Conexant, and National Semiconductor all maintain applications engineers in the Asia Pacific region.

The PRC is a source for many of the components that go into today's electronics products. For example, a laptop computer cannot be sold without a PRC-made power supply. If China were quarantined, it would mean "nuclear winter" for the semiconductor industry.

Just-in-time manufacturing practices make the electronics supply chain inherently vulnerable to pandemic disruption. Companies that have rushed into the PRC to take advantage of low manufacturing costs now face the possibility of severely disrupted supply chains. The dependable supply of components has been an overlooked rule in the industry's drive to minimize costs.

The PRC government's handling of the SARS crisis raises serious concerns about transparency. Their deceit regarding the severity of the outbreak will not be forgotten by their trading partners. Western companies doing business in or through the PRC should factor government transparency risk into their supply chain planning.

Authors: Russ Craig and Peter Kastner, Aberdeen Group, March 28, 2003.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group hot topic report warning that the 2003 SARS outbreak could cause major disruptions to the global electronics supply chain concentrated in Asia Pacific. The report documents that Asia Pacific consumed 37% ($52B) of 2002 semiconductor production, with 85%+ of WiFi gear made in Taiwan and PRC. It catalogs facility closures (Motorola Singapore), travel bans (Compal), and corporate shutdowns (CTIC) already occurring, and warns that a PRC quarantine would mean nuclear winter for the semiconductor industry. The analysis identifies just-in-time manufacturing as inherently vulnerable to pandemic disruption and notes critical single-source dependencies on PRC components.
