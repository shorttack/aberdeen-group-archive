### Dell: Applying Its Business Value Model to Storage

#### Preface

Value. That is what information technology (IT) executives demand from their investment in storage -- as well as the rest of the IT infrastructure. Although IT budgets are thawing a little, 60% of the respondents in a recent Aberdeen survey stated that managing storage within the current budget and headcount constraints is one of their critical success factors in managing the storage infrastructure.

Yet the rapid growth of storage results in increased stress on the storage infrastructure. This stress translates into increased management complexity, higher administrative costs, and the need to make sure that storage assets are used more effectively.

To deal with storage stress, enterprises are planning a number of initiatives -- such as storage consolidation, revamping the current backup/restore process, implementing or expanding a storage area network (SAN), and implementing or expanding network-attached storage (NAS).

Enterprises must justify the resulting capital investments in storage through a formal financial process. The Aberdeen survey shows that total cost of ownership (TCO) and return on investment (ROI) remain highly popular components of the necessary financial discipline to determine investments.

Reducing storage stress and maintaining financial discipline play very well into the strengths that Dell brings to the storage market. For years, Dell has been recognized for its business model that relentlessly pursues a path of constantly improving price performance. Dell then returns those improvements in the form of increased functional value to its customers. Storage is no exception. This Profile describes how Dell is adding value for its storage customers.

#### Executive Summary

Dell not only supplies business storage customers with disk storage systems from its ongoing Dell|EMC partnership (well received by the market) but also fulfills other key storage solution requirements with both NAS and data protection solutions, featuring both disk and tape. Moreover, Dell delivers the necessary service and training to support the customer's storage hardware product and related software investment.

Dell also recognizes that the needs for business storage extend beyond the data center (where a Dell CX700 would typically reside) to reach to the department (with, for example, the CX500) and on to the workgroup level (with, for example, the CX300 and the AX100).

The one constant across Dell's product range and organizational reach is the application of principles derived from Dell's supply chain management (SCM) approach. Among these principles are three fundamental Dell strategies -- continue to deliver industry-leading value, drive standards, and move high-end functionality downstream, from the high end to the workgroup.

Dell's ability to continuously deliver industry-leading value through continuous improvement in the price/performance ratio of products is built into its "DNA." Dell believes that passing along that value to customers gives it a sustainable competitive advantage. Like other suppliers, Dell takes advantage of inevitable declines in raw storage prices over time and manufacturing learning curves. Dell's key ongoing advantages over these suppliers have been superior SCM techniques and the ability to quickly pass cost reductions along to the customer.

Dell is making a big push to support IT-based standards. Among the organizations that it is working with in storage are the FCLC (Fibre Channel Loop Committee), Serial ATA II, Serial Attached SCSI, and SNIA (Storage Networking Industry Association). As a for-profit organization, Dell focuses on the business value that comes from a "standards-based virtual feedback loop" driving improved technology and reduced costs. Each new evolutionary level of standardization enables new users to drive an increase in market size; market size increases lead to new technology refinements and declining costs; and technology refinements lead to the next level of standardization -- just what Dell wants to achieve for its customers and the industry.

With a strong focus on delivering value to its customers, Dell continues to work collaboratively with EMC to deliver enterprise-class features throughout the entire Dell|EMC CX product family. Typically, new storage functionality, such as SnapView, the point-in-time copy software for the Dell|EMC CX product family, tends to be initially offered at the high end of the product line (aimed at data centers). With Dell|EMC, this type of functionality is offered from the entry CX product (CX300) through the high-end (CX700) offering.

Dell's ability to rapidly drive down prices enables Dell customers to afford the new functionality that much sooner. For storage software, standardization allows mainstream user administrators to handle the new features that much more rapidly.

The net result is continually improving storage product value for customers.

In 2003, Dell and EMC extended their original five-year agreement to co-brand storage products for another two years (through December 2008). This commitment demonstrates the long-term value that both companies derive from the relationship.

Dell recognizes that data processing requirements are distributed across the enterprise and that many of these requirements are file oriented. Therefore, Dell offers a family of NAS server solutions under its PowerVault brand. Moreover, enterprises need to protect the data on their NAS and SAN storage, so Dell offers a number of PowerVault tape backup products and tiered recovery solutions.

#### A New Generation Takes Center Stage

In 2004, the new CX300 (focused on workgroups), CX500 (targeted at departments), and CX700 (designed for data centers) replaced their predecessors -- the CX200, CX400, and CX600, respectively. This third-generation set of products from the Dell|EMC partnership demonstrates the ongoing relationship's ability to deliver investment protection. As an example, when upgrading from a CX200 to a CX300, a customer has to replace only the disk controller on the existing array -- thus paying only for the new controller, not a whole new array. Moreover, there is no need to migrate data from one disk array to another, which usually causes bottom-line-impacting downtime.

With the new generation, storage buyers get more bandwidth and hence greater performance for the same price -- the CX300, the CX500, and the CX700 are 70%, 100%, and 25% faster, respectively, than their predecessors -- added value that shows up in TCO and ROI calculations as the ability to avoid unnecessary purchases of servers, software, and storage in order to scale. Many other storage suppliers reactively pass on technologies leading to improvements in price/performance; Dell proactively drives even faster price/performance improvements.

#### Adding Midline Storage to the Storage Mix

Midline storage (ATA disks with higher capacity, lower cost, and lower performance than FC disks) is now available for Dell|EMC disk arrays. The traditional online pool of storage uses FC disks for applications requiring the highest performance, such as revenue-producing online transaction processing solutions. The new midline storage pool can store fixed content, which needs reasonably fast access, but does not have the same performance demands as traditional online storage pools. Aberdeen research shows that obtaining satisfactory performance at less cost is emerging as a major buyer trend, and the new midline products are satisfying this demand.

Moreover, enterprises can also use midline storage in the nearline pool of storage that has typically been reserved for tape automation. Disk-to-disk backup and continuous data protection techniques all lead to faster recovery of key information -- and therefore bottom-line-improving higher availability.

A Dell|EMC array with ATA drives requires several FC drives to house the CX product family's FLARE operating system. The rest of the drives can be a mix of ATA and FC drives. A combination of the two can allow the ATA drives to be used for less expensive point-in-time copies.

#### AX100: Midrange Fibre Channel Meets Serial ATA

The latest addition to the Dell/EMC line is the AX100, a product concept that Dell conceived to meet its customers' needs for easier to use Fibre Channel system at an even lower price point than the CX line. The Dell/EMC AX100 storage system can be used in a SAN (with a new Brocade switch offered by Dell) or directly attached to servers.

This powerful entry-level array scales up to 3 TB on SATA drives, yet can be easily set up with wizard-based tools. Furthermore, the solution includes all the software that a customer needs to manage the deployment, automate failover, and provide backup. Redundant features, such as dual controllers and mirrored cache, ensure high availability for critical data.

Large companies with many remote sites and small organizations recognize the availability and performance benefits of implementing SANs, but have been deterred by the associated high complexity and cost. Dell has helped address these concerns by partnering with storage industry leaders, such as EMC, Brocade, and Qlogic, to create a solution that is significantly lower in cost, easily installed by the customer, and scalable with business growth.

#### Making Change Happen

The ability of Dell and EMC to work together has resulted in several practical benefits for storage buyers. As EMC's experience has helped Dell to better serve the higher end storage customer, Dell's experience has helped show EMC how the small and medium-sized enterprise storage segment can benefit from the Dell|EMC class of products. The CX200 (and now the CX300 and AX100) came about through Dell's work with EMC to recognize the needs of the workgroup for this class of products.

#### Getting the Most out of Storage through Software

Dell works with leading storage management vendors to cover the broad storage management needs of enterprises, including backing up/restoring, configuring and provisioning storage, and monitoring the health and status of storage systems. Dell offers products from well-known software vendors with long established reputations, but also from newer companies that Dell has qualified to meet its high standards for delivering products that it can recommend to its customers. Of course, Dell partners with EMC on CX family software products, such as Navisphere for device management and SnapView and MirrorView for point-in-time and mirroring data replication functionality, and for Legato software. Because one size of software does not meet the needs of all organizations, Dell aims NSI Double-Take at data replication for smaller configurations.

Further evidence of Dell's focus on reduction of complexity can be seen in Dell's SAN management software. Dell offers VisualSAN -- an intuitive network management software package that discovers, visualizes, and monitors servers, switches, and storage systems. With a Web-based user interface, managing SAN configurations, assets, and device status is a much-improved process. Dell|EMC VisualSAN customers also can leverage Dell Device Managers -- a free, downloadable plug-in that provides enhanced component instrumentation for Dell devices and integration with Dell OpenManage server management software.

For backup/recovery software, Dell features Legato Networker. Dell also offers Yosemite TapeWare for workgroups, VERITAS BackupExec for departments, and VERITAS NetBackup Enterprise Server for the data center. Dell recently added CommVault Galaxy to its backup/recovery software portfolio to provide features that scale from departments to data centers.

Two key ways that Dell uses its business model for storage management software are (1) simplifying the process of buying the software for the enterprise through bundling and (2) facilitating the use of the software through tighter product integration. As a result of Dell's direct business model, end-user feedback is driven into future product requirements for Dell's software partners.

#### Bundling

Bundling is not new, but Dell views bundling through the lens of how it can reduce the "friction" and complexity of the purchasing process. Many storage software products have numerous model numbers, e.g., stock-keeping units (SKUs) that vary by number of servers, processors, storage capacity, and other variables. The result is confusion and complexity for the IT buyer in deciding not only what to buy to meet today's needs but also how to ensure that the storage management software purchases will accommodate storage growth and storage infrastructure changes in the near future without requiring a trip back to the budget well.

Bundling not only reduces the number of SKUs that a customer has to evaluate but also reduces deployment complexity by ensuring that all software components are included for the solution. Dell bundles are based on its experience with a large number of storage customers. Bundling also makes more complex solutions available to broader audiences.

Bundling is budget-friendly in terms of overall cost. Dell's considerable market volume opportunity for software vendors, combined with fewer SKUs to manage, results in lower costs to the customer.

#### Tighter Product Integration

Another important way to simplify the use of multiple storage management software products is through tighter product integration, which can be visualized as minimizing the number of "consoles" with which an IT administrator has to work. Each individual product has its own console, a graphical user interface (GUI), by which an IT administrator can control the running of the software. Moving closer to a single window to the world through one interface console that links multiple products makes it easier to manage and reduces the learning curve.

Not having to switch between consoles reduces "context switching" between the look-and-feel differences of multiple GUIs and lowers the number of facts that storage administrators need to remember -- such as when similar words have a different meaning in two products and what set of actions is necessary to bring about a desired result. Dell can drive different storage software vendors' products to work together as a single console by providing a compelling business opportunity as a reward for such cooperation. At the same time, Dell continues to work with storage standards organizations to define a common API and command line interfaces that allow software vendors to easily integrate storage and server consoles.

The net result of bundling and tighter product integration is that the Dell business model delivers price/performance for software as well as for hardware.

#### Service -- Go for the Gold

Dell strives to develop tools that can deliver a good level of service at lower cost. For example, performing management functions remotely from a Dell command center using a WebEx session is sometimes faster than sending someone on-site. Dell also continues to devise metrics to allow analysis and therefore reduction of the time to resolve a service problem, e.g., as a metric to measure the efficiency of a problem diagnosis and metrics to measure the efficiency of the escalation process. Whereas many suppliers might be daunted by the thought of significantly improving the service process, Dell views it as an opportunity to apply its SCM techniques.

That opportunity carries over to improving the multivendor problem resolution process. Every storage system has multiple vendors that have a measure of responsibility for overall IT satisfaction. When an IT organization has a storage-related problem, it wants a swift, constructive resolution to the problem -- not time-consuming and unproductive finger-pointing between two or more vendors.

Dell emphasizes a partner engagement process with third-party vendors that agree to participate. This tight service integration provides structured processes and agreements that drive multivendor accountability and mutual commitments that improve customer experience.

Vendors that agree to participate derive the market benefits of being a key Dell technology partner. Dell and its partners execute Dell Service agreements that cover timely execution guidelines, on-site service level agreements, defined escalation management policies, and global performance metrics. Dell is involved real time in partner-identified issues; Dell is notified of a problem at the same time that the partner's support organization is made aware of it. Moreover, Dell support teams will receive enhanced training from partners where applicable.

Problem resolution is important, but preventing problems from affecting IT organizations in the first place improves IT satisfaction. A centralized Enterprise Command Center that enables Dell to reach out electronically to monitor and to take action from a distance prevents and solves many potential problems without disrupting the performance and availability of IT applications.

Finally, avoiding problems before they occur is a cost-effective approach to improving efficiency and increasing customer satisfaction. Dell does a root cause analysis for each problem incident and keeps a historical database of incidents. Dell can spot problems and then carry out preventive maintenance.

Dell has an edge over many of its competitors in that it knows where all of its storage systems are because it sells directly. That knowledge is useful for determining where the systems are that require preventive maintenance.

The net result is that service is amenable to the Dell business model -- Dell can deliver increased efficiency, customer satisfaction, and shared cost savings.

#### Feeling the Power of PowerVault NAS

Having numerous file servers with dedicated storage scattered over a network tends to result in problems -- and not only underutilized disks. For example, each server may require a tape drive. This expensive requirement results in an administrative-time-intensive burden for managing the backup/restore process. Moreover, scaling to meet storage growth adds disproportionately to the expense and administrative burden. With products such as Dell PowerVault NAS, consolidating file storage centrally not only improves disk utilization and scalability but also enables a tape solution that reduces the backup/restore process's administrative burden.

The Dell PowerVault NAS family consists of the PowerVault 775N and 770N, both of which act as central file servers in the midrange, and the PowerVault 745N, which acts as an alternative to file cabinets, microfiche, or optical jukeboxes at the entry level. Windows-based systems using CIFS (Common Internet File System), Macintosh systems using AFP (Apple File Protocol), and Unix-/Linux-based servers using NFS (Network File System) can access a PowerVault NAS.

All PowerVault NAS platforms use Microsoft Storage Server, which is based on the Windows 2003 Advanced Server operating system. The use of Microsoft Windows Storage Server 2003 instead of a proprietary NAS operating system illustrates why Dell adamantly supports standards -- IT administrators who use the latter face a learning curve, whereas very little to no learning curve is required for using the former. Moreover, Windows Storage Server inherits the extensive features of Advanced Server, e.g., a rich set of server utilities, support of Active Directory, and clustering solutions.

PowerVault NAS servers can support additional capacity through the connection of a dedicated disk storage array. Furthermore, the two midrange products (PowerVault 775N and 770N) can connect directly to Dell|EMC FC storage arrays, and both can act as a gateway to SAN storage through an FC switch -- delivering scalability, flexibility, and investment protection.

#### Data Stored Is Data That Must Be Protected

Protection is a fundamental requirement for any business data. Dell offers a wide range of tape products under the PowerVault brand to meet these needs.

Recognizing that legacy tape media formats are still in use, Dell will sell individual tape drives in a number of tape formats. For its tape automation products, Dell limits the choices to DLT (Digital Linear Tape) and LTO (Linear Tape Open) derivatives. Dell offers the PowerVault 122 tape autoloader and two tape library solutions in the PowerVault 132T and 136T tape libraries to address the entry-level and midrange markets. Dell also offers an enterprise-class tape library solution with the PowerVault 160T. In keeping with its standards focus, Dell offers only LTO-2 tape drives on the PowerVault 160T, and in the future, Dell is likely to emphasize LTO technology on its other tape libraries. For backup and restore software in the enterprise, Dell offers the choice of Legato Networker, CommVault Galaxy, VERITAS Backup Exec, or VERITAS NetBackup Enterprise Server.

#### Aberdeen Conclusions

Dell lives and breathes the simple economics of the price elasticity curve where lower cost (a benefit to the customer) yields higher volume (a benefit to Dell). Therefore, Dell wants to effect storage price reductions that occur more rapidly than they ordinarily would.

For customers, Dell's standards orientation makes it simultaneously easier and less costly to integrate Dell storage into a heterogeneous storage environment. This approach provides customer choice by lowering the risk and "friction" of a proprietary technology lock-in.

After watching Dell's storage product line grow in complexity, breadth, and sophistication over the past five years in order to meet the storage needs of large enterprises, Aberdeen notes that Dell continues to leverage its success from the small to medium-sized business (SMB) segments, where ideas such as storage software bundling reduce operations and buying complexity. Dell's storage team now deserves a place at the business discussion table of large, medium-sized, and small companies and government agencies -- and workgroups of any size organization.

By viewing Dell as a storage integrator and not as an OEM, an enterprise can easily understand the company's efforts to simplify the selection of disk arrays, tape drives, and storage software in subtle ways that give customers operational flexibility and the agility to react to change. Lower cost of ownership benefits customers, whereas simplification directly lowers the costs and complexity of Dell's service obligations.

Price improvements alone are not enough; Dell seeks to improve the price/performance ratio. Thus, in the latest generation of Dell|EMC CX disk arrays, the price numerator stays the same, but the performance and capability denominator improves. Dell also views improving functionality and moving it to a broader range of products as performance improvements. For example, the CX300 and the AX100 have the SnapView point-in-time copy capability, whereas the CX200 does not.

The net result of Dell's approach is that Dell can move as rapidly as any supplier to make its storage products less expensive, better, faster -- across the spectrum of its Dell|EMC family products, PowerVault NAS products, and PowerVault tape automation products. Storage buyers seeking to plug value into their TCO and ROI calculations should like the way the Dell solutions look.

### Historical Context

- **Dell-EMC Partnership**: Extended through 2008 as noted in the document. Dell ultimately acquired EMC Corporation in September 2016 for approximately $67 billion, forming Dell Technologies. This was the largest technology acquisition in history at the time.
- **Brocade Communications**: Partnered with Dell for SAN switches as noted. Brocade was acquired by Broadcom in November 2017 for approximately $5.9 billion. The Brocade brand continues under Broadcom's infrastructure software division.
- **VERITAS Software**: Mentioned as a backup software partner. VERITAS was acquired by Symantec in 2005 for $13.5 billion. In 2016, Symantec spun off the information management business as Veritas Technologies, now operating independently.
- **Legato Systems**: Acquired by EMC Corporation in 2003, becoming part of EMC's software portfolio. The Legato Networker product eventually became EMC Networker and later Dell EMC Networker.
- **CommVault**: Mentioned as newly added to Dell's backup portfolio. CommVault (now Commvault) continues to operate independently as a major data management vendor.
- **Standards-Based Storage**: The document's prediction that standards-based storage would prevail proved correct. SATA became the dominant interface for capacity-tier storage, and industry standards continued to drive down costs as predicted.
