AMD’s Acquisition of ATI: Upsetting the Equilibrium 
Market Event 
 
Advanced Micro Devices’ (AMD) $5.4 billion acquisition of ATI Technologies looks like it’s all 
about the consumer graphics market. In fact, AMD is upsetting the competitive equilibrium 
among AMD, ATI, Intel, and nVidia. As a result, we expect less innovation and higher prices in 
enterprise volume servers and workstations. 
 
Aberdeen Analysis 
 
Introducing the Players 
AMD 
AMD is the upstart microprocessor manufacturer that used to compete on price with larger rival 
Intel. But in the past two years, it has grown market share in volume x86 servers to 
approximately 20% with the Opteron processor, while improving its position in notebooks and 
desktops on the performance of the Athlon 64. AMD’s corporate workstation market share has 
tripled in the past three years. 
ATI 
ATI is one of the two major graphics chip makers, competing with nVidia. ATI is the leader in 
discrete notebook graphics products, and a strong second-place competitor to nVidia in discrete 
desktop graphics cards. ATI also makes chipsets (e.g., core logic silicon for input-output), and 
silicon for cell phones, digital televisions, and videogame consoles, notably Microsoft’s xBox 
360. 
Intel 
Intel is the microprocessor market leader in volume servers, and desktop, and notebook 
microprocessors. The company is vertically integrated in that Intel also designs and 
manufactures graphics capabilities into its volume desktop and notebook chipsets. However, 
Intel works closely with ATI and nVidia to deliver mid- to high-performance graphics 
capabilities for mainstream notebooks (i.e., portable workstations) and workstations. 
nVidia 
nVidia is best known for its graphics processors, which are used in notebooks and desktops for 
consumers and enterprise clients, including virtually all enterprise workstations. However, the 
nVidia nForce brand of chipsets powers more than 40% of high-end AMD-based desktops, and 
75% of recent AMD Opteron servers. 
The x86 Ecosystem Until This Week 
The microprocessor wars pit AMD versus Intel. Neither company makes high-end graphics 
chips that power the graphically intensive enterprise applications such as CAD, life sciences, and 
mapping. AMD and Intel need ATI and nVidia to supply that capability. Both AMD and Intel

The graphics wars pit ATI versus nVidia at the high-end, and both against Intel at the low end.

The Dance Card Changes 
Microprocessor-maker AMD is taking out graphics powerhouse ATI. The stated direction of the 
merger is that AMD and ATI work together to create more tightly integrated designs that could 
offer better performance, lower cost, and lower power consumption. This will start being seen in 
late 2007 and completely in 2008. 
Implications for IT Infrastructure Planners 
Microsoft Vista Requires Greater Graphics Power 
There are no immediate changes needed to 2006 purchasing specifications caused by the 
impending merger. However, Microsoft’s Vista operating system, when launched in 2007, will 
require considerably greater 3D graphics capabilities to operate with all its features than 
corporate buyers have encountered with Windows XP over the past five years. Exactly what to 
buy is unclear since the Vista code is still not ready for performance testing, and the fall rollout 
of new microprocessors from AMD and Intel has yet to occur. Buyers should plan to test 
Windows XP and Vista on dual-core computers in Q1 2007 and assess the graphics horsepower 
required. 
AMD/ATI Get More Integrated  
The accretion to AMD in doing the merger is closer integration that will come by tightly 
integrating the microprocessor and graphics processor and using AMD’s HyperChannel 
technology. But this will not result in a new merged architecture before 2008. Buyers should 
plan for business as usual in 2007, purchasing the separate microprocessor and graphics 
capabilities needed for a given computing role. 
Intel Ups the Integrated Graphics Ante 
The handwriting is on the wall for Intel: Dramatically improve integrated graphics over the next 
18 months to keep competitive with the anticipated AMD/ATI silicon merger. Buyers should 
closely monitor Intel’s integrated graphics capabilities over time, especially under a Windows 
Vista workload. If Intel’s integrated graphics underperforms, add-in cards from ATI or nVidia 
can deliver greater performance, but at a higher unit cost per PC or notebook. 
HP has the Most to Gain and Little to  Lose 
Among the top-tier PC and server OEMs, HP has the most to gain or lose from the merger. HP 
has the broadest and deepest set of desktop, notebook, and x86 volume server products using 
AMD, ATI, Intel, and nVidia products. It’s likely that HP will be a showcase provider of the 
new, integrated AMD/ATI architecture late next year, and will gain if AMD/ATI silicon is a hit. 
But if AMD slips, HP still has a strong Intel-based product line for corporate buyers.
