# Intel's IA Continuum Strategy Anticipates a Much Broader Computing Market

**Author:** Peter S. Kastner, Aberdeen Group
**Date:** circa 2010-02-01
**Source file:** 2010 Intel IA Continuum WP-1 2.rtf

Intel's IA Continuum Strategy Anticipates a Much Broader Computing Market

If you understand Intel's strategy for this decade, you'll also understand where computing is headed and why.  The answers are important to Information Technology (IT) planners, consumers, the technology press, and investors.

Don't Look Back at Intel
The IBM PC, with an Intel microprocessor as its brains, was launched 28 years ago.  The PC business over time vaulted Intel into a top technology-company position.  The Pentium brand is one of the most-recognized in the world.  And we all remember the bunny suit ads.  But the mass-market fuzzy perception of Intel as the "PC chip company with some related businesses" is far outdated.

True, Intel is aware of the global Total Addressable Market (TAM) for silicon; approaching a billion processors a year in round numbers.  And turning sand (silicon) into $50 to $3,000 microprocessor chips at 60% margins is a great business.  But Intel's fortunes no longer rest on the growth of the global PC market, as is the perception.  Growth now comes from many markets, some of which Intel is creating from scratch, and some that are just growing in new places.

Look for New Markets That Need Acceleration
In the past decade, Intel quietly metamorphosed from a technology leader to a technology-leading marker creator.  For example, in order to boost adoption of mobile, Internet-connected notebooks -- which are in 2010 approaching 60% of the PC market -- Intel invested heavily in 2003-2005 in the creation of WiFi wireless access points through businesses and communications carriers around the globe.  WiFi is an industry standard, yet Intel invested hundreds of millions to standardize the infrastructure, software, and standards.  Today, we take WiFi access mobility, often free, as a given.  WiFi adoption would not have happened as quickly without Intel's coordinated investments to grease the skids for the rapid, global adoption of WiFi notebooks.  With ubiquitous WiFi as a given baseline technology, new generations of mobile devices such as netbooks, tablets, and smartphones are exploding into the market.

We'll discuss the key market-acceleration catalysts including consumer electronics, systems on a chip, the datacenter, high performance computing, PCs and notebooks, and the channel in more detail below. They are part of Intel's strategy, are receiving enormous investments, and will blossom as technology markets this decade.

The 2010's Computing Continuum
Tomorrow's computing is a continuum that ranges from smartphones to mission-critical datacenter machines, and from desktops to automobiles.  These devices represent a TAM approaching a billion processors a year, and will explode to more than two billion by the end of the decade.  Of that, traditional desktop microprocessors are about 150 million chips this year.  The devices are the easy part.  The hard part is gluing all the devices together across the Internet in a seamless fashion.  Building this computing continuum and supplying the resulting markets is Intel's technology (and marketing) challenge for the decade.  As the company says, "it's no longer about personal computers, it's about making computing personal."

The Intel Architecture (IA) Continuum
IA is Intel's architecture and instruction set that is common (with feature differentiation) in the Atom, Core, and Xeon microprocessors already used in the consumer electronics, desktop and notebook, and server markets, respectively.  These microprocessors are able to run a common stack of software such as Linux or Microsoft Windows.  IA also represents the hardware foundation for hundreds of billions of dollars in software application investments by enterprise and software application package developers that remain valuable assets as long as hardware platforms can run it -- and backwards compatibility in IA has protected those software investments.

To meet the widely varying requirements of this decade's computing continuum, Intel is using the DNA of IA to create application-specific variants of its microprocessors.  Think of this as silicon gene-splicing.  These genetically-related processors will extend Intel into new markets, and include instruction-set compatible microprocessors:

Atom, the general-purpose computer heart of consumer electronics mobile devices and soon smartphones;
Embedded processors and electronics as "systems on a chip" (SOC) with an Atom core and customized circuitry for controlling machines, automobiles, and industrial products;
Core i3, i5, i7 processors for business and consumer desktops and notebooks, with increasing numbers of variants for form-factor, low power, and geography;
Xeon server processors for workstations and servers, with multi-processor capable advances well into the mainframe-class, mission-critical computing segment.
Xeon datacenter infrastructure processor variants, the first of which, codename Jasper Forest, is aimed at storage systems, and with communications management a logical follow-on.

Can Intel Use One Architecture Across the 2010's Computing Continuum?
It is proper to question whether Intel is capable of such a massive expansion of its base microprocessor product set.  After all, smartphones have little in common with datacenter number crunchers, right?  Actually, they do have a lot in common as many variants are in essence "general purpose" processors.  Processor speed, wattage and power management, manageability, and chip geometry are all variables that are demonstrably well understood by Intel.

Moreover, Intel is the market leader at fabricating consistent (think "tick-tock" delivery process), reliable, high-volume silicon-based products.  It is also very adept at delivering the software tools needed to harness the hardware effectively.

At this juncture, only one technology company in the world could propose a common computing architecture that scales from consumer electronics devices to datacenters: Intel Corporation.  The company has the technology tools and knowhow, ability to execute, and vision to glue the billions of Internet-connected computing devices together in a single computing continuum.  Intel views succeeding in delivering a continuum of IA-based hardware and software to match the computing continuum as its brass-ring chance of the decade.

I conclude that IA, indeed, fits the market requirements of the vast majority of the decade's computing work requirements, and that Intel is singularly capable of creating the products to fill the expanding needs of the computing market.  The go-to market strategy for each new market builds on the commonality of the IA continuum.

Consumer Electronics: Building From Scratch
The explosion of the consumer electronics (CE) market over the past decade, married to the desire to put the power of the Internet into every device, has created an explosive market dynamic that will approach 1 billion units a year by mid-decade.  CE product features this decade will include wireless connectivity, long battery life, intuitive user interfaces, instant on-off, and strong security.  Moreover, the rapid change rate of CE devices (think cell phones) virtually demands a flexible, building-block software base on which to build the next generation of products; starting engineering anew is not possible in a six-month product development cycle.

Enter Intel.  CE devices lush as set-top boxes or TV sets don't need and can't afford the hardware and (Windows) software of an old-fashioned PC architecture. Thus, the need for Atom, a simple IA computer that requires very little power.  This year's Moorestown iteration of Atom is good enough to garner a few smartphone design wins, where cost and battery life are paramount.  Next year's Medfield and subsequent iterations will take Atom down to sub-watt levels; what's needed for a long day of wireless Internet access.  But the Atom processor alone is not the whole CE solution.

To win in the burgeoning digital CE market, Intel is making two other major investments: software and systems on a chip (SOCs).

CE Software
Many digital CE devices are not well suited for Microsoft Windows, the dominant desktop, notebook, and netbook operating system, as exemplified by Microsoft's mediocre results after a decade's development of Windows Mobile.  Linux, an open source operating system alternative to Windows, is very fragmented among dozens of distros supported mostly by small software companies which are ill suited to the engineering and support needs of global, large CE manufacturers.  Two years ago, Intel began rallying the Linux community around a mobile-friendly version of Linux, named Moblin.  Lots of behind the scenes cajoling resulted in much more commonality of Linux kernel and utility versions across the Linux community.

Today, Moblin is available off-the-shelf for CE manufacturers to use in building Atom-powered, highly tuned devices.  Moreover, Intel is spending millions to train developers in Moblin best practices and to evangelize the engineering needs and requirements that Moblin solves.  It even opened an application store so that software developers can download routines they choose not to reinvent -- speeding time to market.

In February, Intel announced that cellphone giant Nokia's Meego Linux would be merged with Moblin.  This follows on to the 2009 acquisition of Wind River, a software development and CE system integration specialist with extensive experience in embedded, real-time, and CE systems.

In short, Intel has boot-strapped a global application software infrastructure that will make it easier and faster to deliver high quality, exciting digital CE products. With the ability to create customer-specific SOCs, coupled with a robust open-source CE-oriented software platform, Intel dramatically increases its total addressable market, and making the company one to watch as an emerging leader in digital consumer electronics.  Nevertheless, the CE market will require year's of concerted effort by Intel to win over old-style CE product-development mindset and beat well-entrenched competitors.

Systems on a Chip (SOCs)
A CE device made with fewer pieces of silicon can be smaller, cost less to manufacture, and use less battery power.  Those are all key design goals in the CE business.  That makes Atom, the standalone IA microprocessor, less attractive than some competitors because Atom must be surrounded by numerous support chips to make a CE device.  In 2009, the company geared up its organization to take on creating Atom-based systems-on-a-chip.  These SOCs are being built to customer specifications, to include parts like memory, wireless communications radios, and specialized input-output.  The chips will be fabricated at Intel's plants or by partner Taiwan Semiconductor.

Unlike the CE market, embedded product-development change cycles are glacial as product life cycles stretch out to a decade or more.  That suggests minor short-term gains for Intel, with growing and prolonged revenue streams later in the decade.  I am convinced that the embedded systems market is ripe for Intel's SOC-driven approach, as it brings time-to-market, life-cycle cost, and development labor cost benefits while lowering development risks.  Expect a billion dollar SOC market share for Intel towards the end of the decade.

Datacenter
The server chip run-rate is about 10 million processors a year in 2010.  That count will more than double this decade.  Since margins are high, server silicon is a highly attractive market.

Intel has lead in one- and two-socket server volumes for years.  These are the datacenter computing infrastructure building blocks that run the world's Internet applications.  They are the bricks for a scale-out architecture.  In the past few years, the shear rackspace of all these servers, and especially the electrical requirements, have become formidable issues, and in some cases, hard barriers to datacenter expansion.  Intel has responded by focusing less on raw compute power per chip and more on compute power per watt across the Xeon family of server and workstation microprocessors. This power efficiency is aimed at rack-level and server row-level efficiencies. Virtualization is another strong current trend that drives server consolidation this decade, since many servers in the past ran at low utilization rates.  Both power efficiency-driven and virtualization-driven datacenter refresh cycles will play out over the next five years.

Today's big hype trend in datacenter-class architecture is cloud computing.  Although definitions of the cloud are hazy (pun intended), there is merit to the concept of applications running on the Internet on server infrastructure that can scale as loads ebb and flow throughout the 24x7 work cycle.  Successful companies like Salesforce.com have bet the business on the ability to deliver applications from the cloud.  To support massive, highly variable workloads that the cloud architecture anticipates, Intel is extending the Xeon server microprocessor product line upwards with more cores per chip, and downwards with low-wattage versions that can be deployed by the hundreds without breaking the datacenter electricity budget.

What About Itanium in the Datacenter?
Skeptics will point out that Intel's Itanium architecture is not in the IA family.  Itanium is the number two selling architecture for mission-critical workloads, behind only IBM's mainframe and Power processors.  Itanium is not, in fact, instruction-level compatible with IA-based Xeon.  Software compiles differently than on Xeon.  However, the mission-critical features in Itanium, learned over fifteen years in the market, have increasingly appeared in Xeon, improving Xeon's reliablility-availability-serviceability (RAS) features.  This culminates in the latest Xeon high-end chips, Nehalem-EX, which share the same common I/O chipset.  There is Itanium DNA in Xeon, and that Itanium DNA indirectly improves all IA hardware.

Storage and Communications Controllers Plus Enterprise Solid State Storage Devices (SSD)
The continuing growth in storage is almost beyond comprehension.  A University of California report says the daily per capita data consumption is 34 gigabytes a day.  This works out to 3.6 billion trillion (i.e, zetabytes) bytes in 2008.  For the U.S. alone.  Obviously, managing all those bytes is an attractive market.

Intel has announced plans for a storage variant of Xeon (codename Jasper Forest) designed specifically for use embedded in storage controllers, a high-volume datacenter technology adjunct to Intel's core server business.  Moreover, Intel is investing to be a leader in solid state "SSD" disk drives for the enterprise and consumers.  Because SSD's are several times faster than hard drives in reading data, they work well in many datacenter workloads.  Intel will sell millions of SSD drives, where it makes the SSD controller, as lower cost trends push rapid datacenter adoption by mid-decade. Communications controllers are another datacenter opportunity.

Many-Core Future
Many science and engineering problems such as stress analysis and weather forecasting can be solved faster by breaking the problem up into pieces and running each piece on a processor.  This is parallel computing.  In November 2009, Intel had over 400 of the Top 500 Supercomputing Sites.  Yet this market is growing rapidly as the trend moves from computational science towards science discovery.  High Performance Computing (HPC) is an attractive market for Intel this decade, and thus offers multiple avenues to many cores.

Intel's datacenter R&D is transferrable to HPC, and will over time result in more cores per chip, higher density at lower electrical loads, higher I/O bandwidth, and faster memory bandwidth.  Intel has a long history of parallel software development and testing software tools that improve programmer productivity and code quality.

Intel's HPC R&D projects deserve attention.  Terascale is a testbed R&D project to put many, simple IA cores on a chip.  Intel demonstrated a 48-core version in December 2009, where each core can run its own virtual OS instance with TCP/IP network connectivity.  This version could be productized within a year.  It would be very useful for some applications that now use field-programmable gate array (FPGA) circuits.  It could also be used as a many-core cloud computer.

Larrabee is another R&D project worth highlighting.  Originally conceived as a discrete graphics board competitor to nVidia and AMD's ATI division for delivery by now, Larrabee was redirected towards HPC in autumn 2009.  Like Terascale, Larrabee has multiple IA cores and a network on a chip.  But Larrabee also has new instructions that can massively speed up visualization primitives.  These instructions accelerate games (the original product target), but also visual computing applications such as 3D petroleum reservoir models.  Larrabee would also be an excellent engine for computer-driven animation, the source of movie hits like Shrek and Avatar.  My conclusion is that Larabee is far from a write-off, as critics claim, and that it can become a significant source of Intel business.

Traditional Desktops and Notebooks
It is easy to overlook innovation and change in Intel's traditional PC market for desktops and notebooks.  However, two forces in particular are driving rapid innovations: PCs per Person (PPP) and emerging markets.

It wasn't a decade ago that developed economies reached apparent saturation of one PC per household.  Obviously, forces that include the Internet, wireless communications, the lower cost effects of Moore's Law and fierce competition kept personal computing growing.  Industry marketers now talk in terms of PPP.  In developed countries, family dynamics -- the after dinner fight to get online -- has pushed society towards one PPP at home.  Notebooks now represent over 60% of total PC sales units, and allow portability around the house as well as to work and in family activities.  The desktop, once the stodgy beige box, now comes as an all-in-one in the kitchen or close by the flat-screen TV in order to deliver homemade audio-visual content or, increasingly, download online content, especially videos.

The second huge force on the PC industry is emerging markets.  With six billion people on this earth, Intel has an addressable market of billions of units.  The rapid emergence of a middle class in numerous countries is driving economic forces that benefit Intel.

Intel's biggest seller in emerging markets is not one of the Core processors but the latest version of the venerable Pentium -- a worldwide top-10 brand name.  In many emerging markets, lower selling price is often an issue.  That drives Intel towards lower cost Atom-based PCs as well as lowering the chip count (hence, manufacturing costs) in reference designs.  However, in China, the social pressure is to buy way up the Core product line.

The January introduction of Westmere, the 32 nm fabrication of the Nehalem architecture, continues Intel's cadence of tick-tock changes over two-year cycles.  The new Core i3 and Core i5 Clarkdale family of dual core (four thread) desktop processors make Intel more competitive at the low end of the PC continuum.  At the high end, the Core i7-980x delivers 20 GHz over six cores (12 threads), and is by far and away the most powerful consumer chip ever produced.

The Arrandale generation of Westmere chips for notebooks will expand the portable market with a number of features.  The integrated graphics in Arrandale and Clarkdale are suitable for many video games and is tuned to deliver HDTV-quality graphics and video.  Improved power management means longer battery life, while Turbo Boost adds computing power when it is needed.  Reviewers are uniformly impressed with the capabilities of Westmere processors.

It is not difficult to foresee Intel's current technology building blocks in power management, Turbo Boost, threads, and integrated graphics driving PCs around the home that are thinner, smaller, more embeddable, and video friendly.  These capabilities will drive up the PPP ratio over the course of the decade, while making computing more personal.

In the office, Intel will soon cover all the bases of enterprise desktop and notebook management and security with its vPro technology for businesses.  The Fortune 500 are already convinced of the significant cost-of-ownership savings that vPro enables, and many have inserted vPro into their PC refresh cycles. The next major market for vPro is the global small to medium business (SMB) market, where IT staffing tends to be a critical issue.

The Channel is the Bottom of the Iceberg
The channel is an amalgam of market segments worldwide that include small PC OEMs, online Internet merchants like Newegg.com and Amazon, assemble-to-order PC malls in Asia, and do-it-yourself hobbyists.  On a global basis, the channel is about as diverse as imaginable.  Yet the channel is delivering enormous growth to Intel as PC, CE, and server demand accelerates worldwide.

The perception is that by following the DAHL (Dell, Acer, HP, Lenovo) companies, the world's largest PC manufacturers, one will have a handle on Intel's business since the DAHL companies buy so many chips.  The reality is that the large PC and server manufacturers, including the DAHL, represent only about a third of Intel's business by units.  It's the tip of the iceberg.  The channel now represents the bulk of Intel's business, and it's growing faster than the DAHL.

Challenges
The most difficult technology challenge that Intel faces this decade is not hardware but software.  Internally, the growing list of must-deliver software drivers for hardware like integrated graphics means that the rigid two-year tick-tock hardware model must also accommodate software delivery schedules.  Larrabee is an apparent example of mis-aligned hardware and software schedules.  The need for longer software development schedules, especially testing, is forcing Intel to have hardware working earlier in the tick-tock cycle.  This leads to better quality, but more software creates more inherent schedule and business risks for Intel. Also, by the end of the decade silicon fabrication will be under 10 nm, and it is a lot less clear how Moore's Law will perform in the 2020's.  Externally, software parallelism remains a sticky problem, as Intel creates more parallel threads in hardware, but programmers are slow to distribute their applications across that hardware.  This is a computer science problem of considerable enormity.  Intel is investing heavily with professional product developers, and its parallel software tools are widely used and admired.  However, that may not be enough to unblock this parallel software bottleneck.

The market challenges for Intel are also great opportunities.  About ten percent of cell phones are smartphones today, but that will grow towards 90% by the end of the decade.  The competitive feeding frenzy led by Apple's iPhone and now iPad will drive competition and innovation at an enormous rate.  Intel is likely to get a small piece of the pie over the next couple of years, but could, with considerable investment and sustained concentration, be a major player by mid-decade.  Surely that is the company's goal.  SOCs are another multi-billion dollar opportunity that will unfold slowly as product design cycles are much longer.  Yet autos alone are a 35 million unit opportunity growing to 60 million in late decade.  Intel's new market investments are best watched over a period of years; success will be hard to measure quarter-by-quarter.

The distracting mosquito that won't go away is government regulatory action.  Last fall, after settling a major dispute with rival AMD, Intel was slapped with an anti-trust suit by the U.S. Federal Trade Commission.  Intel was late to the table in making its case to governments around the world by lobbying and government affairs and is making up for lost time.  Unfortunately, a neutral observer would conclude that Intel, like other large U.S. technology companies including Microsoft, Apple, and Google, has been chosen for extra government scrutiny because it is big and successful.

Conclusions
The key computing market-acceleration catalysts in this decade include consumer electronics, systems on a chip, the datacenter, high performance computing, PCs and notebooks, and the PC/CE channel.  Intel is taking its proven IA platforms and modifying them to be competitive as existing markets evolve and as new markets such as smartphones emerge.  To do this, Intel discarded the PC beige box mentality in favor of application-specific form-factors and product requirements.  IA scales from handhelds to mission-critical enterprise applications, all able to benefit from a common set of software development tools and protecting the vast majority of the world's software investments.  Moreover, IA and Intel itself are evolving to specifically meet the needs of a spectrum of computing made personal, the idea that a person will have multiple computing devices that match the time, place and needs of the user.

Our conclusion is that Intel's self-interest in growth is pushing the company to innovate in new markets, which will benefit computer buyers.  But the company is also hugely invested in the worldwide growth of a middle class in emerging markets, which expands the universe of potential customers.

We will all look back a decade hence at how far personal computing has come in ten years.  The corollary is we should all be excited about what is to come in the next decade.


Bio
Peter S. Kastner is an industry analyst with over forty years experience in application development, datacenter operations, computer industry marketing, and market research.  He was a co-founder of industry-watcher Aberdeen Group in 1989.
