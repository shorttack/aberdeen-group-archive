## Original Text

New Consumer Electronics Categories Appear in Time for Christmas
Keywords: Consumer Electronics, Sony, Roku, NEC, fuel cell, laptop, ZVUE, Panasonic, Thompson, SD Memory Card

The march of technology continues, bringing pocket sized video players, advanced digital media players for use with TVs, and a laptop powered by a fuel cell -- all in time for Christmas this year or next year.

Innovation in Consumer Electronics Continues, but not from Traditional Players
Hand Held Entertainment, a company founded in February 2003, announced a pocket-sized video and music player in early September -- something Aberdeen was expecting from Sony. The device has a 2.5-inch color display and earphone audio output.  It runs 8+ hours on four AA batteries.  Licensed audio and video programming is stored on Secure Digital Multimedia cards using a proprietary compression technology.  A 256 MB SD card will hold 4 hours of video, enough for two full-length movies.  It will be sold through Toys R Us as a package including earphones and an SD card with one video title in October.  The truly startling thing is the price -- $99 plus the SD memory card of choice.  Additional audio and video content will also be available on preprogrammed SD cards.  For those people who want to create their own content, Hand Held will release a software application to convert MPEG-4 content files to a compatible format which can then be stored on a blank SD card.
Admittedly, this capability isn't unique, but at a new, lower price point.  Sony can store 4 hours of high-resolution video on a 1 Gig Memory Stick Pro card, which can be played on the new UX40 Clie.  Video programming can be captured using an accessory priced at $260.  Add the Clie at $599 and a 1 Gig Memory Stick Pro at $500, and you end up paying $1,359.  Of course, you get the other Clie functionality as well.  Panasonic and Sony are both expected to launch similar dedicated video players, but pricing is expected to be $300-$500 and up.
HDTV Personalized Eye Candy
Roku, a startup by the ReplayTV founder Anthony Wood, is launching a line of digital CE devices. The initial product is the HD1000, a high definition digital media player. The device supports both standard and HDTV with display of photos, art, playing of music with or without visuals, and "Live Art" which is animated still scenes as well as PC screens or applications.  The HD1000 converts that blank HDTV screen into an art gallery.  The list price for the player is $499.99, with "Live Art" pack at $69.99 each. The device has Ethernet and WiFi interfaces for content stored elsewhere as well as CF, SD, SmartMedia, XD memory card, and Memory Stick slots. The OS is Linux based and is supported by a development environment aimed at third parties.  The user interface is highly intuitive and easy for a non-technical user to understand -- expect Microsoft to move in this direction soon.  Product can be purchased at Magnolia Audio-Video and Tweeter Home Entertainment Group (including all Tweeter, HiFi Buys, Sound Advice, Showcase Home Entertainment and Hillcrest High Fidelity stores) in late October.  Alternatively it can be pre-ordered from www.rokulabs.com.
Fuel Cells Go Consumer
NEC announced a laptop computer powered by a water/methanol fuel cell mid summer.  On September 17th, they showed a prototype at WPC Expo in Tokyo.  It had a battery life of 5 hours.  They expect to have it on sale in 2004, and a version that will run for 40 hours in 2005.  Fuel cells are poised to be alternate power sources for all kinds of portable technology.  Rumor has it that a fuel cell powered cell phone will be available in Asia by the end of the year.
Aberdeen Conclusions
While the economy is still tenuous, consumers will find a variety of new gadgets available for Christmas.  Strong price competition and new competitors like Dell means traditional categories will be an even greater bargain than normal -- but all from non-traditional CE companies, as the global CE brands continue to fall by the wayside.
~ Russ Craig & Peter Kastner
