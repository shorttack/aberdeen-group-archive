Business PC Deals - March 2003
Aberdeen Group's Enterprise Reference Desktop PC
These are our current recommendation for mainstream desktop personal computers that offer the best combination of price, value, and longevity is:
    • Microprocessor: Pentium 4 at 2.4 GHz (533 MHz front-side bus)
    • Memory: 256 MB DDR SDRAM (minimum)
    • Hard Drive: 7200 RPM 20 GB or better.  (We price 40 GB for consistency)
    • Optical: CD-ROM
    • Floppy: Optional.  (We favor USB flash memory drives in 2003)
    • Monitor: 15" or 17" XGA-resolution LCD flat panel
    • Communications: 10/100 Ethernet or better.  No modem.
    • Operating System: Windows XP Pro service pack 1
    • Warranty:  3-year, next business day on-site repair
    • Software:  Antivirus and Microsoft Office XP Pro
Aberdeen PC Deals tracks managed enterprise PCs, with a focus on the Dell GX260, the Gateway E-4000 Special Deluxe, the HP Compaq Evo D510SB, and IBM's NetVista M42 8303.
Aberdeen's Reference Business Desktop
A general desktop is one aimed at mainstream white collar-workers and certain task-specific workers.  We have been advising clients to adopt the following specifications for their general business desktops:  
Form Factor - We like the small desktop form-factor (SFF) machines that have proliferated over the past year.  To us, the days of the big-box tower are over.  Who needs six drive bays at their desk anymore?  Small takes less desk space and saves on unused components, which lowers costs.  We see no downside for most users.  However, Dell's jewel-like SFF Optiplex 260 uses notebook components, and is thus slower than its slightly bigger rivals.
Manageability - These PCs are all manageable from a central help-desk/network operating center.  Consumer-grade PCs are not particularly manageable.  Moreover, by focusing on business computers, features such as disk image management are included.
Processor - Our judgment is that a 2.4 GHz Pentium 4 is the minimum processor for a purchase today.  We consider an AMD Athlon 2000+ as functionally equivalent for business applications.  Yes, a few dollars can be saved by buying a Celeron.  Our argument for a 2.4GHz or better processor is based on lab experiences with Windows XP Pro with Service Pack 1 and standard Office, Internet, and collaboration applications.  Looking out over 3-4 years, Aberdeen sees a proliferation of web services that will sap the capacity of less powerful machines.  While, 2.0 GHz is adequate, but 2.4 is basically a free upgrade worth taking. 
Operating System - Windows XP Professional is the only reasonable long-term choice.  Windows 2000 works well, and we use it throughout Aberdeen Group.  However, Win2K is halfway through its service life, according to Microsoft.  Therefore, a PC purchased today with Windows 2000 will run out of Microsoft support in 2006, during the machine's 3-4 year service life.  While we do not have many qualms about running a machine out of its OS service life, many large enterprises with custom applications will just not be able to take that risk. Apple Macintosh with OS X is viable, but PC Deals follows mainstream PCs because that is where the market is.  Linux is not ready for mass deployment on corporate "fat client" desktops.
Memory - While Windows XP Pro will run in 128MB, users will see a lot of page faults in a typical multi-window scenario.  We would opt for 256MB of memory for Windows XP.  Our tests show at least a 10% improvement (and sometimes 30%) in XP throughput with 256MB compared to 128MB.  With Windows 2000, 128MB will work somewhat better, but we'd still spend $60 for 128MB more memory.  For power users, 512 MB is more prudent than extravagant. 
Hard Disk Drive - The key to business desktop drives is speed, not size.  Our standard is 7200 rpm.  Higher speed gets the data to the starving processor faster.  While 40GB is common, 20GB of local storage will suit many users just fine.
Optical/Magnetic - A CD-ROM for loading software is all we can justify.  A CD-RW can be a security problem, but we think a CD-RW per department has a lot of valid uses.  A floppy drive is optional today.  If you delete it, you'll probably never miss it.
Monitor - Note that in our price comparisons below, we include no monitor.  The reason is that many desktops already have a decent monitor.  Our advice to the frugal would be to buy new PCs without monitors while sparing a couple of monitors to replace those that die over the service life of the PC.  But LCD flat panels are overtaking CRTs in 2003 - that's the clear trend.
A 17" CRT monitor has the best screen real estate for the dollar.  However, a 15" LCD flat panel is the equivalent of a 16" CRT, so it's a viable option.  A flat panel CRT is more restful on the eyes than the lowest-cost monitors available from the PC suppliers.  And LCD flat panels are now economically attractive, even at 17" and 19".
Communications - 10/100 Ethernet is the corporate standard. All our comparison machines include 10/100 Ethernet or better on the motherboard.  But note that Dell is including gigabit Ethernet (GigE) on desktops, servers, and notebooks, betting on a changeover to GigE over the next several years.  Our standard PC includes no modem, since wired or wireless Ethernet access to the Internet is commonplace.
Warranty - Our standard is at least a three-year parts and labor warranty with next business day service.  It only takes 3-4 machines worth of higher, four-hour response time warranty costs to pay for a spare machine and a rolling cart, so we would not gild the warranty lily, especially if there were an on-site IT group.
Productivity Software - Small and medium businesses of up to 500 employees will get the lowest prices by standardizing on Microsoft Office Small Business Edition at an imputed price of roughly $200 per seat.  Buy Office XP Pro for those users who will actually use Access and PowerPoint.  We have also configured an anti-virus desktop solution as a must-have.  However, even small businesses are now deploying server-based virus protection solutions, so a desktop-only solution is not the only solution.  For example, Dell recently started shipping a client license for Symantec's enterprise (server-based) AV software.

Large Enterprise PC Pricing
Large enterprises do not buy off the standard price list.  They go out to bid, typically with a laundry list of specific product and service features.  This makes comparing vendor pricing dicey at best.  Nevertheless, Aberdeen's research in March shows the same configurations as priced above for small business as: Dell Optiplex 260 at $884, the IBM NetVista M42 at $900 and the HP Evo D510 at $914 in quantities up to 50,000 PCs.  This is a price reduction of $50 since September, and includes a processor upgrade to 2.4 GHz.
The configurations used for the large enterprise pricing are the same as described above for small business with one expensive missing component:  Microsoft Office XP.  Businesses with more than 500 employees are no longer eligible to purchase Office XP Small Business Edition from their PC supplier.  Instead, they have to buy Office XP from their PC supplier, a Microsoft distributor, or with a corporate volume license.  Volume pricing for Office XP Pro is in the $300 - $320 range.
This pricing proves what Wall Street has known for a long time:  Microsoft's profits are built on Office sales to business.

Corporate Notebooks: Intel's Centrino Changes the Market
Intel's Centrino is an evolutionary step in wireless notebook computers that is likely to cause all sorts of reactions, some revolutionary.  Centrino is an all new, integrated set of electronics from Intel that consists of a mobile Pentium 4, a chipset, and an 802.11-compatible wireless Ethernet adapter and radio.  This integrated platform was built from the ground up for long battery life - over five hours - and better performance than today's Pentium 3 and Pentium 4 mobile processors.  All the major notebook computer companies are rolling out Centrino products, at attractive, mainstream prices.  Enterprise versions will enable information workers to keep up to date while on the go - the so-called "always on, always available" mantra.  And in their off hours, employees using Centrino will compute out on the sundeck.  
Centrino is the most important introduction that Intel, the industry's dominant microprocessor supplier, will make in 2003.  Besides the glitz of the ad campaigns, the company is spending tens of millions of dollars to accelerate a worldwide network of  wireless access points - so-called hot spots - where 802.11-enabled computers including Centrinos can get on the Internet for all the reasons that the Internet has taken a role in our lives.
The evolutionary fact of Centrino is simply that other 802.11 and wireless approaches exist in the market.  It is the clout, market-awareness building, and wireless infrastructure-building muscle of Intel that makes Centrino's introduction not just another product launch but a milestone in the adaptation of computers into our lives.  Mark down March 12th, 2003 on your computer history calendar.
The revolutionary aspects of the Centrino notebooks and the wireless-everywhere world that will follow imply consequences that deserve consideration.  The immediate question for IT decision-makers is what to do about all those wireless notebooks that are about to invade the corporate campus?
Your Next Desktop is a Laptop
But let's not throw out the Centrino baby with the rogue, wireless bathwater.  I believe Centrino notebooks - and the wireless network they work best in - should become one of the standard IT infrastructure building blocks.  
Cap Gemini Ernst & Young says that the additional costs of wireless notebooks can achieve a return on investment in six months or less.  Aberdeen Group research indicates that a campus-bound meeting-attendee can be productive an extra 30 minutes a day.  Task workers such as physicians can see an hour a day in productivity improvements in conjunction with digital record-keeping.  Senior executives, sales and support people will quickly become subscribers to public wireless services - just like they were expensing cell phones well before a corporate policy was put in place.  While I don't have the definitive ROI study on wireless notebooks to present today, neither have I heard from my clients that wireless notebooks are anything less than positive.
The perfect notebook is not yet in the market - but it will be by year end.  I'd start with the basic Centrino platform, and enhance it with next-generation silicon and a few micro-architecture tweaks.  That would be Intel's road-map placeholder called Dothan.  I'd marry Dothan with a convertible form factor and Microsoft's Tablet PC software.  That would allow for keyboard entry as well as hand-written note-taking and sketching.  With Microsoft's Office 11 and the forthcoming OneNote note-taking software, this go-anywhere notebook will be under five pounds, have at least four hours of meeting life before a battery swap or recharge is needed, and be a one-stop portable office.  I expect we'll see these notebooks this autumn from all the usual sources at prices under $2,500, including the Office licenses.  Not the cheapest notebook around, but one packed with productivity.  A platform I believe will represent more than 10% of corporate clients in 2004 - and that includes all the desktops too.
