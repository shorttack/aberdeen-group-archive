Gateway’s Acquisition of eMachines Bodes Well

In a bid to shore up its sagging fortunes, personal computer maker Gateway announced that it would buy privately-held eMachines in a $235 Million deal.  Aberdeen sees this as a shrewd move on Gateway’s part.  With this deal, Gateway expands their distribution channel, opening new channels in which their innovative products may be well-received.

Gateway, once the top online personal computer reseller in the consumer markets, has fallen on hard times over the last several years, primarily as Dell has become the gorilla in the North American personal-computer marketplace.  While Gateway has made a foray into the retail market with 170 retail showrooms, the results have not justified management’s confidence in the new strategy.  In short, while Aberdeen sees Gateway as one of the innovative leaders in delivering digital consumer technology, the company’s ongoing decline in core PC sales has made life difficult.

In a marriage made in San Diego County, California, Gateway announced that it is acquiring eMachines, another Southern California PC firm.  eMachines, since going private three years ago, has become the number two brand in retail stores in the United States.  In fact, eMachines’ success is one of the reasons that Hewlett-Packard became so price aggressive in the past year.  Staying mostly in the PC value segment (e.g., low price) E-Machines has built a quiet brand loyalty through sales at big-box electronics and computer stores.

There are three important takeaways Aberdeen sees on our initial analysis of this acquisition:

    • Acquiring eMachines, which has major shelf-space in PC retail havens such as Best Buy, Circuit City, and Comp USA, will — over time — allow Gateway to deemphasize, and likely exit, its own costly 170 store chain of showrooms.  This move will allow Gateway to fund more marketing and product development.
    • By using eMachines shelf space, Gateway gains an important opportunity to grow its already well-recognized national brand at retail outlets.  With eMachines strength in low-end machines and Gateway’s leadership in high-end machines, such as their all-in-one media center, the ingredients are in place to entice consumers with a full-line competitor to Hewlett-Packard.  In fact, we see Gateway trying to barge in with a second brand next to eMachines over time.
    • Lastly, and perhaps most strategically, Gateway can now leverage eMachine’s relationship with retailers to sell more of Gateway’s innovative digital consumer electronics products into the broader retail channel with less apparent competition from Gateway’s captive brand stores.  As the leader in plasma TVs for example, Gateway could gain a place at the electronics altar in the big box electronics stores.  This creates opportunities for higher volumes which would offset the distribution costs.

Overall, we believe Gateway’s acquisition of eMachines represents an astute and timely move by the company to expand its distribution channel with another national brand, while creating opportunities to accelerate Gateway’s digital consumer brand.
