September 5, 2001
HP's Consumer IT Leadership Needs More Focus On Consumers
HP's avowed strategy is to lead the market in consumer IT.  This includes access PCs, imaging products, and new consumer electronic products yet to be announced.  However, our experience is that HP consumer product support is less than world-class.  Unless more focus is placed on the consumer’s experience, HP could fail to achieve the leadership role that it seeks—and waste a lot of money doing it.  This InSight illustrates HP's problems based on our Aberdeen Laboratory experience with HP’s digital camera.
.
What consumers want is easy to explain
Simply stated, consumers want complex electronics and information technology to be simplified and made intuitive so that the out-of-box initial experience and repeat usage makes sense and becomes second nature.  These consumer requirements are well studied and result in considerable engineering in order to achieve just the right consumer satisfaction level.  In today's Internet world, consumers expect quick start instructions, searchable Web sites, and published answers to frequently asked questions.
The HP C315 digital camera
HP was savvy enough to give each analyst the gift of a C315 digital camera at a May conference.  As an amateur photographer who lacked prior experience with digital cameras, I looked forward to experimenting with HP's product.  And indeed, once installed and running, I have enjoyed using the camera for six months.  The photographs are high quality, the transfer process to my PC is straightforward, and HP's worthwhile but hard to find photography web site offers the ability to publish photographs for free, so I can easily spread them around to friends and family.
However, it would be difficult for me to recommend the HP digital camera line to others without some strong caveats about installation problems and ease-of-use.
Installing under Windows ME
The original installation in May had only one significant hitch.  The ACD See image display software had a missing .dll file, and was not usable as the primary image library.  Computer novices might be intimidated by a situation such as this where one software application fails to install completely but another is available and does basically the same job.  I was off and taking pictures within half an hour without the necessity of a call to customer support.
Installing under Windows XP
In October, I upgraded the photography PC to Microsoft's new Windows XP.  That's when HP and my problems came to a head.  I upgraded the PC "in place", the approach recommended by Microsoft.  Upgrading in place puts the new Windows XP operating system underneath all of the existing software applications and their settings.  The alternative to an upgrade in place is called a clean install.
HP's web site states that the C315 camera enjoys native support under Windows XP.  To use the camera withthe new technology that Microsoft has added to Windows XP, one need only connect the camera through a USB cable to the PC.  The web site is technically correct, but only under the condition that Windows XP is upgraded with a clean install.  And only if consumers avoid using the photo manipulation software which ships with the camera.
The consumer's frustration
The photo transfer software that ships with the camera interferes with the native support built into the Windows XP operating system.  As a result, attempts to move photos out of the camera and into the PC result in an error condition that prohibits the photo transfer.  The frustration builds after the consumer, in this case me, prints out the precise instructions from the web site and follows them to the letter—and to no avail.
Diagnosing the problem
The documentation that ships with the camera states that the digital media in the camera must be formatted before it can be used to store photographs.  This is normally done within the camera itself.  But under Windows XP, the camera and its digital media appears to Windows XP as a removable disk drive.  And disk drives, as every PC user knows, can be formatted.  So, eventually, I tried having Windows format the digital media in the camera, which failed with a severe error message.
Upgrading  the read-only memory
At this point, I had about one hour invested in trying to get the camera to work.  Moreover, I had a load of photographs that I wanted to save so I could to take the camera on an upcoming trip.  So, I decided to press on to the next approach.  I decided that, like a few other applications, the HP camera would be happier with the reinstallation of the application software.  At the end of the reinstallation, the software looked on the Internet for updates—a very good software practice.  Appearing before me: there is a newer version of the camera read-only memory.  I figured the six-month-old camera needed a ROM upgrade to support Windows XP but there was no indication of whether the ROM upgrade had anything to do with XP.
The ROM upgrade failed because the Windows XP formatting process, which Windows said had failed, had changed the camera media in some fashion.
That's when I called HP technical support—a single call that cost HP more than in the retail price of the camera based on industry help-desk research by Aberdeen Group.
HP tech support solved the immediate problem
I give high grades to the HP support technician who took my call.  He listened carefully to the steps I had taken, did a triage on the multiple problems, and talked me through the solutions.  We were done in about 45 minutes.
However, I could only transfer photos using some of the features of the HP application software.  In spite of the assurances of tech support and the web site, I could not get native Windows XP support to work.
The answer actually occurred to me a week later.  The application software that ships with the camera, even when updated with the latest changes over the Internet, is incompatible with Windows XP.  This is not documented in any place that I found on HP's support web site.
The solution to achieve native HP digital camera support under Windows XP is not to install the HP photo transfer software that comes with the camera.  Since this solution is not written anywhere, my industry experience tells me that there will be many unhappy HP camera owners who happen to upgrade to Windows XP.  And consumers, who happen to buy a new HP PC and digital camera together, and make the mistake of following the camera’s installation instructions, are likely to fall into the same consumer hell that I experienced.
Conclusions for HP
The economic law of consumer electronics is written in granite: you can't afford to take many customer support phone calls and still make money on mass-market products.  HP broke the law.  
By failing to document for consumers the procedures for dealing with a long anticipated upgrade to Windows XP, HP is bringing unnecessary calls to its support center while leaving the taste of an unsatisfactory consumer experience.
Our conclusion is that HP has a lot of work ahead of itself in order to achieve world-class leadership in consumer IT.
A Word about Aberdeen Laboratories
The mission of Aberdeen Laboratories is to qualitatively test the consumer’s experience with information technology products.  We evaluate real products as we think real consumers would.  
Our research is aimed at answering consumer’s questions about using information technology.  
Our experience is that many otherwise sophisticated technology consumers are more afraid of and less patient with information technology than many it suppliers would expect.  
Our services are aimed at assisting suppliers in improving the out-of-box experience and lifecycle of consumer IT.
Peter Kastner
