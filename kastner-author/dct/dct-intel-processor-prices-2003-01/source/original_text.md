# Analysis of Intel Processor Prices in PC Deals (January 2003)

## Original Document Text

﻿Intel Celeron processors consistently hold the lowest price point of under $600.00.  The August price decrease of the Intel Pentium 4 (P4) processor 1.8 GHz processor in PC Deals to under $900 sealed the Celeron PCs’ low-price position by pushing the Celeron 1.7 GHz into the lower price points.  Intel launched the Celeron 1.8 GHz processor on June 12, 2002, and quickly followed with the Celeron 2.0 GHz on August 26th.  In November, the Celeron 2.0 GHz processor entered PC Deals in the $600.00 price category, thereby solidifying the Celerons’ place in the low-end PC market.  While the Celeron 2.0 GHz processor’s appearance did not eliminate the Celeron 1.7 GHz processor from PC Deals, the 1.7 GHz processor costs under $450 in PCs and never rises from the bottom of the $600 category.  Without the introduction of faster Celeron processors, their prices remain unchanged, but Aberdeen would expect the elimination of the 1.7 and possibly the 1.8 GHz Celeron processors upon the release of a faster Celeron.  
August 26, 2002 also marked the release of Intel’s P4 2.6, 2.66, and 2.8 GHz processors.  But while the new Celerons pushed their slowest 1.2-1.4 GHz counterparts out of PC Deals within a month, the introduction of the newest P4s only lowered the price of the 1.8 and 2.0 GHz P4s $150 in the first month to the $900 price point.  The prices of the 1.8 and 2.0 GHz P4s remained at $900 for the next two months.
Intel’s P4 processors fall into two categories: those with the older 400 MHz front side bus and, beginning with the 2.26 GHz P4, those with the new 533 MHz front side bus.  A pricing division has emerged based on this variation in P4 performance ability.  Instead of the slowest P4 processors “dying” out of the market, such as the 1.8 GHz or 2.0 GHz P4s, these processors fill the price categories between the low-cost Celerons and the 533 MHz front side bus P4s.  Thus, while the 1.8 GHz P4 remains around the $900 price point, the P4 2.26 “disappeared” by October.  Of course, a 2.26 processor was still available in customizable PCs, but it was no longer a good value.  So the prices of P4s with the new front side bus continue to fall without much affecting the pricing of the old front-side-bus machines.
In January the conclusion to this rift in P4 composition begins as the 2.4 GHz processor falls, due to the November introduction of the hyper-threading 3.06 GHz P4, into the same price category as the 1.8 and 2.0 GHz P4s.  As experience would predict, the 1.8 GHz P4 has begun to disappear, but its 2.0 GHz sister processor remains a strong option on all middle to lower-end PCs.  The 2.4 and 2.0 GHz P4 processors really cannot stand in the same price category for long.  So, either the 2.0 GHz must drop in price or disappear, or the 2.4 GHz processor will disappear (because it cannot drop to a price below the inferior 2.0 GHz processor) as did the 2.26 GHz.  I predict that the end is near for the 2.0 GHz processor because currently, since the launch of hyper-threading technology, Intel produces three differently structured P4 processors.
Interestingly, the price decline of the 2.4 GHz processor is not representative of its 533 MHz brethren.  The 2.53 GHz P4 did drop in price since the release of the 2.8 GHz P4, but the 2.66 and 2.8 GHz P4s have remained in the same price categories for over four months.  The large price discrepancy between the newest 3.06 GHz P4 and a 2.8 or 2.66 GHz in a PC means that the 3.06 GHz does not compete with, and thereby lower the cost of, the other high-end P4 processors.  Until the emergence of the 3.06 GHz P4, it was difficult to construct a $3,000.00 PC, even when the 2.8 GHz P4 was new. technology.  Now, a 3.06 GHz P4 containing machine costs between $2,200 and $3,000, creating a completely new high-end price category.
Following the example of new front side bus v. old front side bus, I do not think that hyper-threading processors will eliminate the middle of the road 533 MHz processors, such as the 2.53 GHz P4, anytime soon.  
It is difficult to compare the pricing trends of summer and fall PCs with that of the holiday season.  Summer is a slow time for PC sales and the back-to-school season is when PC manufacturers and retailers compete most for the busiest PC sales time of the year.  That means that PC Deals through September showed a time when PCs were at their best deals.  Christmas, falling at the end of most companies’ quarters, became an opportunity for PC makers and dealers to recover some of their profit losses this year.  Aberdeen saw price increases from several PC sources, especially Dell, and precious few deals from national retailers during a time period we would have thought would be abounding with them.  The first week of January has not revealed much of a reversal of these price hikes, but if companies want to move these PCs in the slow spring quarter, prices will have to fall.

### Abstract

Analysis of Intel Celeron and Pentium 4 processor price tiers observed in Aberdeen's PC Deals tracking from August 2002 through January 2003. Documents the bifurcation of P4 into 400 MHz and 533 MHz front-side-bus families, the introduction of hyperthreading 3.06 GHz P4, and the mechanics of older CPUs being pushed down price bands by new releases. Predicts near-term obsolescence of the P4 2.0 GHz.
