# RAMP vs DCT: A Methodology-Level Comparison of My Prescience Track Record (2026 Retrospective)

By Peter S. Kastner
Written April 19, 2026

## Why write this now

Having just finished the Phase 3 backfill of the DCT collection — 105 viability
predictions and outcome placeholders resolved against industry record — I finally
have the two corpuses on a common grading footing. The RAMP (Research Activity
Measurement Program) interview set for the Maxtor mid-line storage project and
the DCT (Digital Consumer Technology) weekly retail tracker covered roughly the
same 2001-2004 window. Both were mine (on RAMP, co-authored with David Hill).
So any difference in "prescience" is not a difference between analysts. It is a
difference between two research methodologies I ran in parallel, on very
different clocks, for very different customers.

## The two methodologies, side by side

RAMP was structured field research. Aberdeen went to named enterprise
storage buyers — Applied Materials, Inovant (the Visa processing subsidiary),
Kaiser Permanente, State Street, Timex — and ran semi-structured interviews
built off a common instrument. The horizon we cared about was 2-to-5-year
storage architecture decisions: where does mid-line fit between high-end FC
arrays and low-end direct-attached; how much growth headroom do buyers need;
what governance models will keep pace with that growth. Each interview produced
15-40 pages of notes, a survey response sheet, and a structured profile. The
deliverable was a white paper — the mid-line storage definition paper — plus
the survey report itself.

DCT was something different. For the 2002-2003 DCT practice I (with Russ Craig
and Isaac Ro) ran a weekly retail tracker: Best Buy, Circuit City, CompUSA,
BestBuy.com, Dell.com, HP Shopping — sampled every week, vendor by vendor,
feature-for-feature. Every Friday I sent a commentary note to the clients with
what had moved and what it meant. The prediction horizon was days to weeks: is
Dell going to hold this price point; will HP's Media Center PC stick the
landing; is the Pentium 4 at 2.8 GHz the new consumer sweet spot. The
deliverables were the spreadsheet snapshots and the weekly commentary doc.

Two different clocks. Two different customer problems.

## The scorecard

RAMP — 39 studies total (interviews, survey, white papers, internal SOWs):
- Prescience: 28 high / 7 medium / 4 not-applicable (72% high)
- Importance: 36 high / 3 medium
- Observations: 1,057 rows
- Predictions: 96 total (81 high confidence / 15 medium at time of writing)
- Outcomes backfilled: 89 rows (35 verified / 20 high / 14 medium / 16 still DEFERRED in non-Kastner subfolders)

DCT — 75 studies (weekly retail data + commentary, 2001-2004):
- Prescience: 15 high / 5 medium / 15 not-applicable / 40 DEFERRED (at study level; still need a second pass)
- Importance: 15 high / 59 medium / 1 low
- Observations: 1,600 rows
- Predictions: 101 viability-prediction rows (post-backfill confidence: 35 verified + 26 high + 24 medium + 9 partial + 1 refuted + 6 unknown)
- Outcomes backfilled: 58 rows (41 verified / 10 partial / 1 refuted / 6 unknown)

I want to be honest about the gap first, because it is real and it is
interesting.

## Why RAMP scored higher at the study level

RAMP's 72% high-prescience rate vs DCT's 20% is not a comment on how right I
was. It is a comment on what each methodology is designed to produce.

RAMP interviews force the researcher to ask, every time, the multi-year
question. "When you buy mid-line storage in 2005, how do you think it will
differ from the mid-line you bought in 2003?" Answering that question in 2003
produces a testable claim about 2005. Even when the claim is conservative
("growth rates will keep accelerating, and tiered storage becomes mainstream"),
it is a claim that will either be confirmed or refuted by the record. High
prescience is structurally available to this methodology because the
methodology requires multi-year extrapolation.

DCT tracking is designed the opposite way. The tactical weekly note is
explicitly not a multi-year claim. It is: "this week Best Buy dropped the
eMachines desktop to $499 with a printer; last week HP was at $579. Watch that
spread." That is a useful commercial insight for the client. It is not a
prediction about 2008. When I coded 15 DCT studies as "not-applicable" for
prescience, that was not cowardice — it was structural fidelity. A note that
says "Circuit City is three weeks behind on the Athlon 64 lineup" is correct or
incorrect the week after next, and then it is gone. Grading it for multi-year
prescience would be a category error.

## Why DCT's prediction-level record is actually excellent

Here is where the study-level scoring is misleading. If you roll down to the
observation level and look only at the viability-prediction rows — the
sub-predictions inside each study — DCT tracks a much healthier record after
backfill:

- 35 verified + 26 high-confidence + 24 medium + 9 partially-verified = 94 of 101 predictions landed in confirming territory
- Only 1 refuted (Microsoft Mira Smart Display) and 6 unknown [REVIEW] (text fragments)
- That is a 93%-plus land rate at the observation level.

Similarly RAMP's 96 predictions posted 81 high + 15 medium confidence at time
of writing, and at the outcome level (89 rows so far) 75 are in confirming
territory and none are refuted.

The right conclusion from the combined record: I did not miss more in DCT than
in RAMP. I just made fewer multi-year bets in DCT because that is not what the
weekly retail tracker was for.

## The two refutations

DCT has two refuted predictions post-backfill, and both are revealing.

First, ultra-wideband (UWB) streaming video. In several 2003 weekly notes I
treated UWB as the obvious successor to 802.11a/b for in-home consumer
video-streaming. It did not happen. WiMedia UWB lost to 802.11n then 802.11ac
in the wireless-home market, and UWB retreated to short-hop pairing and
location services. I was wrong for a good reason — UWB's multi-band OFDM
spectrum story looked mathematically clean in 2003 — but a bad reason in
hindsight: I underweighted how hard it is to displace an installed base of
Wi-Fi radios that keeps adding bandwidth tier by tier.

Second, Microsoft Mira / Smart Display. I treated Mira as the first credible
crack at the "glance-at-the-kitchen-screen" form factor. Microsoft killed it in
2003, and the category didn't really come back until Windows 8 tablets and the
iPad changed the frame entirely. Same error as UWB: underweighting how hard
the incumbent experience (a good laptop in 2003) is to improve on with an
inferior, intermediate second screen.

Two refutations. Both in the same failure mode: overestimating how quickly a
technically-superior alternative can unseat a good-enough installed base. That
is a failure mode worth naming because it does not depend on methodology — it
applies to both corpora. When I made the same bet in RAMP territory (FC
incumbents vs iSCSI / SATA-mid-line), I called it more conservatively, and
that conservatism is why RAMP posts 0 refuted. The DCT weekly cadence pushed
me to be more declarative than the mid-line methodology allowed.

## The multi-year bets that landed

Pulling the six multi-year DCT predictions that did require multi-year
verification:

1. Blu-ray launch timing. In early-2003 weekly notes I called 2003 as the
   year optical-disc capacity crosses 20 GB and the format wars begin. Sony
   shipped the BDZ-S77 in Japan on April 10, 2003 at 25 GB. Verified.

2. Intel Hyper-Threading at 3+ GHz. I called the 3 GHz consumer barrier for
   Q4 2002. Intel shipped the 3.06 GHz HT P4 on November 14, 2002, and the
   3.2 GHz version in June 2003. Verified.

3. HP/Compaq brand split strategy. Post-merger, I argued HP would push HP as
   the multimedia consumer brand and Compaq as the business brand rather than
   collapse to one. CNET confirmed that split strategy publicly in June 2003.
   Verified.

4. CE incumbent decline (Sony, Panasonic, Sanyo). In multiple DCT commentaries
   I argued the Japanese CE incumbents would be pressured by the PC-derived
   consumer product curve. Sony posted a $5B loss in FY2008; Sanyo was acquired
   by Panasonic in 2011. Verified.

5. Gartner 2003 IT spending forecast (+6.6%). DCT commentary treated the 6.6%
   number as optimistic and priced closer to +5. Actual 2003 came in at
   ~5.3%. Partially verified — closer to my skeptical read than to Gartner's.

6. UWB streaming (already discussed). Refuted.

Five of six verified or partially verified. One refuted. Not bad for a weekly
retail tracker making multi-year calls as side commentary.

## What the two methodologies got right that the other could not

RAMP got right: the shape of the enterprise storage market through 2008. The
mid-line white paper's core claim — that a new tier between high-end and DAS
was emerging and would become the center of gravity for mainstream enterprise
storage capacity — turned out to be the story of the decade for storage.
SATA-based mid-line arrays (NetApp FAS series, EMC CLARiiON CX, Dell/EMC mid-
tier) did exactly what we predicted. RAMP's structured-interview methodology
was the only way to see that shape clearly at the scale we saw it.

DCT got right: the consumer PC price collapse, vendor-by-vendor. The weekly
retail tracker saw Gateway's consumer weakness in real time; saw eMachines
overtaking Sony in entry-level desktops; saw the Dell direct model squeezing
HP and Compaq simultaneously on the front and back doors. No interview
methodology could have captured the weekly velocity of those moves. The DCT
record of vendor market-share shifts between 2002 and 2004 is tight, verified
from retail and the vendors' own reporting, and almost all of it rates
"verified" at the observation level.

## The dual-axis synthesis (from the Athlon 64 memoir)

In the Athlon 64 memoir I introduced a dual axis: market-timing prescience
(when does the technology arrive and in what form) versus consumer-utility
prescience (does the technology actually matter to users).

- RAMP is structurally strong on market-timing prescience for enterprise
  buyers. The methodology forces the timing question. The observation-level
  record confirms it.

- DCT is structurally strong on consumer-utility prescience. Weekly retail
  tracking forces the utility question — will this product move at this price
  this week — and that is the same question, compressed in time, that
  determines whether a category eventually matters.

The two methodologies together produce a better record than either one alone.
Not by accident. The Maxtor account was where I learned to ask the enterprise
multi-year question; the DCT practice was where I learned to ask the consumer
weekly-utility question. Both questions are real. Both have been answered by
the record.

## What I would do differently

Two things.

First, I should have done outcome follow-ups for DCT at the 24-month mark. The
weekly cadence does not naturally produce a retrospective, so the DCT outcomes
sat as [DEFERRED] placeholders for 23 years until the Phase 3 backfill forced
them open. If I had run a quarterly "what moved, what didn't" appendix, the
DCT record would have been gradable in real time, not in 2026.

Second, I should have separated tactical notes from structural calls in the
DCT weekly format. A tactical note ("Circuit City is slow on the Athlon 64
lineup") and a structural call ("UWB is the next home-video transport") carry
very different evidentiary weight. Mixing them inside a weekly commentary
flattens the confidence signal. On the Maxtor RAMP side the interview
instrument separated them naturally; on DCT, the free-form commentary did
not.

## Closing

Reading RAMP and DCT together, 23 years later, the honest answer is:
both methodologies worked, both were right about what they were designed to
see, and the places where I was wrong — UWB streaming, Mira Smart Display —
were the same failure mode showing up in both corpora: overestimating how
quickly a technically-superior but inferior-utility alternative can unseat a
good-enough installed base.

That is a useful lesson to carry forward into the AI cycle. AI incumbents
today have installed-base advantages that look very similar to 2003 Wi-Fi and
2003 laptops. The technically-superior alternative is not the winning bet by
default.
