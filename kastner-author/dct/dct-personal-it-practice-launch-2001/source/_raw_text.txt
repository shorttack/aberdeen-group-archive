Personal Information Technology
Focus
Aberdeen Group’s newest practice area is Personal Information Technology (IT).  The practice was initiated due to the rapidly evolving market paradox, opportunity and convergence of:
    • The commoditization of personal computers (PCs) and lowered margins is driving an intense pressure on suppliers for larger market share, where the home consumer globally represents tens of millions of units;
    • Digital devices such as personal video recorders and MP3 players are replacing electromechanical devices such as VCRs and personal cassette players.  The digital devices are connected to or based on standard computer technology, and represent new, mass consumer markets in digital entertainment devices and content;
    • Office life is extending into personal timeand vice versa—as email and other activities go from home to office.  There are huge implications to business and public networks with security being just one factor.  And taking work home or on the road has created large markets for laptops and personal digital accessories (PDAs);
    • Digital technology is enabling new leisure activities and hobbies such as digital-based photography, music, and video production.  A computer is used for all of these new activities, creating new classes of peripherals and software;
    • Yet the market strategic goal of  “a computer in every home” is obsolete.  The consumer PC market in developed countries has attractive segments for add-on PCs, as each family member seeks his and her own computing experience.  This is driving broadband connectivity and the need for home networks that connect and share, as well as information services online;
    • Consolidating and segmenting all of these consumer digital devices is driving new PCs such as the home server, distinct from enterprise servers;
    • The impending switchover to digital TV and the arrival of third-generation wireless devices will drive new waves of consumer buying, where the existing name brands do not necessarily have assured market share;
    • The complexity of Personal IT begs for consumer-friendly products and the need for in-home services;
    • The complexity of integrating Personal IT—think of the devices and functions in a home network—and the wide variety of brands makes merchandising and distribution a challenge and a potential profit-killer;
Service Offerings
Aberdeen Group’s Personal Information Technology (IT) practice focuses on the consumer and office use of information technology. The practice performs market research and consulting to analyze technology, business, and marketing trends driving the use of desktop computer and operating systems, personal productivity software, and the use of IT in home and small office environments. The practice enables clients to make well-informed technology-centric decisions and to benchmark winning messages and strategies. 
The Personal Information Technology practice provides a full range of custom consulting services, market research, market education, and analysis to technology suppliers and service providers, and distribution end-points such as retailers. The practice helps emerging Personal IT companies understand the competitive landscape, create successful market positioning strategies, educate the market, gain visibility in the venture capital and investment banking communities, as well as to build strategic business alliances and partnerships.  For established Personal IT suppliers, Aberdeen Group provides go-to-market strategy, planning and positioning services and market research.  The Aberdeen Lab provides product evaluation and testing services aimed at helping clients deliver the highest-quality out-of-the-box experience to consumers.

Strategic Market Questions
    • What will change the dynamics of today’s replacement-only home computer market? 
    • Are microprocessor speeds far outdistancing the needs of consumer and SOHO users, driving down high-end microprocessor selling prices? 
    • Will home networks and consumer IT increase the number of computers per household?  What will drive this trend, and what are the implications for home networking? 
    • What new services will be delivered to the consumer, and what new product categories will these converging services create? 
    • Is advanced IT aimed at enterprises too much IT for consumer and SOHO users? How will the complexity of Internet-driven IT be simplified for home users who lack an IT staff?  How will services and support be delivered on a Quality of Service basis? 
    • When and how will the last-mile problem be solved in advanced countries? Will broadband deliver the promise of next-generation consumer applications and services?
    • How will new technologies such as digital photography and digital television change traditional leisure and entertainment spending habits?
    • How will a maturing Microsoft build its expanding consumer IT business?

Key Market Trends
    • Sub-$1000 new computers are being networked with older home computers so that every family member can do their own computing without conflicts.  The implication is that a home network and shared Internet access are required.
    • Microsoft’s Windows XP operating system will take advantage of the latest hardware and fastest broadband connections.  Will Windows XP drive a new burst of high-end PC growth?  Which version will SOHO buyers choose?
    • 802.11b wireless is rapidly growing to compete with wired home-networking technologies.
    • The computing power of advanced gaming computers rivals many traditional PCs, but they lack traditional operating systems and application support. Can Japan’s gaming computers catch the U.S.-based Wintel PCs by moving up-scale?  What challenges does Microsoft face with the Xbox?
    • IT suppliers are creating special business organizations dedicated to marketing consumer-simplified IT products outside their traditional sales and distribution channels, creating new brands.
    • Flat-panel LCD monitors have reached a consumer price point where they make excellent sense in desk-space-conscious SOHO environments.
    • Digital imaging is growing fast enough to materially impact traditional photography-based markets.  How many printers does a home need?
    • Home digital-convergence will accelerate as major IT suppliers view consumers and homes as an attractive new revenue source during difficult traditional enterprise-computing times.  But an enterprise brand does not automatically translate to consumer sales.
    • Traditional utility and energy service providers are taking a fresh look at home networking and home automation technologies to evaluate whether the technology can support existing lines of business.  But mass-market home automation is yet to come.

Peter Kastner Biography
Area of Focus
Peter Kastner manages the research business unit and is responsible for the content of Aberdeen's research products and consulting services. Using his extensive commercial Information Technology (IT) experience and the full gamut of Aberdeen Group research, Kastner actively consults with suppliers on executive level market research and positioning, industry analysis, strategic planning development, product planning, and competitive strategies.  He has served as ad hoc marketing counsel to technology startups, been instrumental in the strategy and execution of billion-dollar rollouts, advised senior executives in business strategy including mergers and acquisitions, and served as a technology market expert witness.

In 2001, Kastner initiated Aberdeen Group’s Personal IT practice area, which focuses on traditional PCs, small office/home office (SOHO) computing, and the convergence of consumer computing with Home Networking, entertainment, imaging, and messaging.
Experience
Kastner has over 30 years of IT industry experience in both user and supplier organizations. His expertise includes online transaction processing (OLTP), decision support, client/server architectures, distributed commercial systems development, e-Commerce, and commercial benchmark auditing and analysis. Prior to joining Aberdeen Group as vice president in 1988, Kastner was a marketing executive with Digital Equipment, Prime Computer, and Stratus Computer. For over a decade as a consultant and program manager with Arthur D. Little Inc., Kastner was actively involved in the management and execution of complex technology development projects spanning the manufacturing, banking and financial services, health care, and government markets and including mainframe, minicomputer, and desktop systems.
