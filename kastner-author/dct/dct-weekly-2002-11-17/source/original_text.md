This Week's Analysis of PC Deals
Pentium 4 with Hyper-Threading Arrives
Intel's Pentium 4 at 3.06 GHz with Hyper-Threading (HT) technology arrived November 14th.  HT adds a trivial number of transitors that add a measurable amount of performance boost for most users.  Thus, P4 machines with HT will perform much more crisply than the implied boost from a 200 MHz speed-bump over yesterday's king of the chips, the 2.8 GHz P4.
HT technology works by providing hardware support for a second executing compute task.  There is still only a single central processor unit (CPU).  But HT provides a second ready task with the hardware-assist needed to quickly swap from one task to another.  Without HT, the CPU spends a fair amont of its gigahertz on internal housekeeping when it is instructed by the operating system to switch from one task to another.  Think you only have a few tasks at a time running on your machine?  Guess again.  Take a look in Window's Task Manager.  Our work machine has 335 tasks and the home lab machine has over 200 tasks.  HT will allow the CPU to spend more of its available time in doing useful work tasks and less time spent on hardware-level task housekeeping.
How much better will your HT experience be?  Your milage will vary, of course, but our experience suggests many business and consumer users will see a visibly more snappy response from an HT machine - particularly a machine with lots of fast memory.  You'll be able to do more foreground work while simultaneously doing Web downloads, printing, file zipping, CD and DVD burning, spell and grammar checking, as well as the increasing mix of background "services" now prevalent in Windows XP.  Compared with the 50% boost in gigahertz between a Pentium 4 at 2.0 GHz and the new 3.06 GHz with HT, many application mixes show performance improvements more in the 80%-90% range.
Our long-term view is that a P4 with HT will make a great next desktop.  We see the HT technology pushing down the product line as Intel rolls out more speed improvements in 2003.  It will cost over $2,000 to get into a richly configured HT machine now, but we forecast 3.06 GHz machines will be in our $1,350 price band by next April 1st.  No fooling!
Please take a look at the custom-configured HT computers from Dell, HP, and Gateway in our special PC Deals section.
This Week's Buying Tips
The best deal we've seen on a new HT machine is at the build-your-own kisok at Best Buy.  About $1,450 gets you an HP 793 and a house-brand monitor.  For just a litle more, you could have a real deal compared to the other HT machines in the $2,000 category.  Another example of recent aggressive HP pricing versus Dell.
The release of new Intel processors with hyper-threading has apparently not dropped the prices of 2.8GHz or lower processor machines yet.  This means that consumers should wait a couple of weeks for prices to drop and then pounce.  Dell usually leads the pack in competitive pricing so expect Dell to have the best deals first.  But other manufacturers and retailers will quickly follow suit. Stay tuned to PC Deals and we'll tell you when the market turns.
Last week, Aberdeen introduced Costco's Dell Dimension 2300 offer to PC Deals.  Well, just out of curiosity, we compared Costco's configuration with the same model from Dell Online.  Buying from Dell Online (at least this week), will save you $70 off Costco's price.  But, as the past few weeks have shown, Dell Online's pricing for the 2300 is extremely volatile.  This means that some weeks the consistent Costco price may beat out Dell's offer.  If considering this configuration of the Dimension 2300, be sure to compare Costco's and Dell's current prices.
At last, a bundled offer on eMachine's T1742 appears again this week from BestBuy.  Snatch this deal up because it may not appear again.  The T1840 has returned in bundled form with a higher price tag, but that price buys you a 19" flat screen monitor and color printer all for under $700 from Office Depot.
The lack of monitors in three out of the five machines in the $600 price point makes ranking these machines difficult.  Compaq's 6310US contains a superior Athlon processor and DDR grade memory compared with the Celeron processors and SDRAM memory in the other PCs; so, the 6310US remains the winner even without a monitor.
www.circuitcity.com and www.bestbuy.com matched prices on multiple PCs this week.  But Circuit City Online has an edge if you're looking for a machine that costs over $1,000: Circuit City will send you a $100 gift card after receiving your mail-in rebates.  Expect more incentives to appear from these competitors as we near the holiday season.
Dell continues the overall trend of lowering prices from the recent high we saw.  This week Dell offers free shipping and CD-ROM upgrades.  That means on all models above the Dimension 2300 you get a CD-RW and DVD-ROM for no extra cost. 
Gateway continues to offer specials on individual machines.  Note that the 300S machine now has an upgraded 2.0GHz Celeron processor.
Sony Online offers a plethora of models not seen in retail stores at competitive prices, although many of them are higher-end.  Sony continues to focus on media capabilities, but top-of-the-line processors, etc. are not available.  You can now customize a Sony Viao PC starting at $699.  And Sony offers $100 off all models as well as one cent shipping on all products, making their deals competitive with BestBuy and Circuit City Online.  Sony Online offers a free Epson C42UX printer with any Viao desktop purchase through December 31st, making a bundled deal direct from Sony more reasonable.
Customize a Sony Viao PC and get an 80GB hard drive for the price of a 60GB one.  Sony Online also offers $50 offer a 120GB or 160GB hard drive.  Ground shipping for a customized model is free.  But beware, now that Sony allows for full-scale customization watch out for automatically added extras that can add hundreds to the cost of your PC.  For example, the RZ series customizable machines costing over $1,500 include a Sony Giga Pocket Personal Video Recorder; choosing to exclude this feature saves you $220.
