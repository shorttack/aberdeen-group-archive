# The 1988 DEC DECtp Benchmark Tests: IBM 3090 DB2 Performance Controversy and Industry Analysis

## Background and Context
In 1988, Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) and IBM engaged in a highly publicized benchmark controversy that would ultimately lead to the formation of the Transaction Processing Performance Council (TPC). This battle centered around the Debit/Credit benchmark, originally developed by Jim Gray at IBM and published anonymously in 1985 as "A Measure of Transaction Processing Power" in Datamation magazine.

## The DECtp Test Controversy
### DEC's Challenge
DEC conducted comprehensive Debit/Credit benchmark tests on both DEC and IBM equipment, including IBM 3090 mainframes running DB2. These tests were announced in July 1988 and showed DEC systems achieving a substantial price/performance advantage over IBM systems.

### IBM's Response
As reported in Computerworld (October 24, 1988), "IBM cried foul in the Debit/Credit benchmark contest, claiming that its machines perform three times faster than Digital Equipment Corp. said they do."

IBM's counterattack included:
- Running their own Debit/Credit benchmarks exclusively on IBM hardware (Enterprise System/9370 Models 50, 80, and 90, and a 4381 Model 22)
- Claiming their machines performed three times faster than DEC's measurements indicated
- Announcing plans to join the Transaction Processing Performance Council (TPPC)
- Proposing their proprietary "Ramp C" benchmark as a potential industry standard

### Key Technical Differences
DEC used 1,000 terminals in their implementation; IBM used only ~100 terminals but claimed mathematical equivalence.

Tom Sawyer (Codd & Date Consulting): "It's mathematically proportional, but performance could vary widely in implementation... Any scoring in this area is controversial."

## Industry and Analyst Commentary
John Logan (Aberdeen Group VP): "This is pure FUD [fear, uncertainty and doubt]. IBM must be hurt by the DEC performance figures."

Jim Gray: DEC test on "virtually identical IBM model yielded substantially poorer results" — "additional fuel to the on-going benchmarketing wars."

## Formation of TPC
On August 10, 1988, Omri Serlin (Itom International, Los Altos CA) successfully convinced eight companies to form the Transaction Processing Performance Council (TPC). Founding members: CDC, DEC, ICL, Pyramid, Stratus, Sybase, Tandem, Wang.

Kenneth MacMorran (IBM Dallas): IBM considering joining TPC; "It's probably a good assumption that we will join."

## Computerworld Original Text (October 24, 1988)
THE NEWSWEEKLY FOR THE COMPUTER COMMUNITY
October 24, 1988 | Vol. XXII | No. 43 | 148 Pages

Benchmark battle escalating
BY STANLEY GIBSON, CW STAFF

IBM cried foul in the Debit/Credit benchmark contest last week, claiming that its machines perform three times faster than Digital Equipment Corp. said they do.

In addition, IBM said it will probably join the Transaction Processing Performance Council, a group of hardware and software vendors attempting to define transaction processing benchmarks. IBM may also submit its proprietary Ramp C benchmark to the council for adoption as a standard.

IBM's response came after DEC ran Debit/Credit tests on both DEC and IBM equipment and released performance figures in July. Those results gave DEC a substantial price/performance advantage over IBM.

Although DEC said then that it would issue a full report this month on its tests, the firm now says it will not meet that deadline because it is rerunning the tests for an external auditor and wants to see the TPPC's full definition of Debit/Credit.

IBM said it will publish by Nov. 1 an auditor's report written by Tom Sawyer, a senior consultant at the Codd and Date Consulting Group in San Jose, Calif.

"This is pure FUD [fear, certainty and doubt]. IBM must be hurt by the DEC performance figures," said John Logan, vice-president of the Aberdeen Group, a Boston-based consulting firm.

Although DEC tested both its own and IBM equipment, IBM tested only IBM machines, specifically: Enterprise System/9370 Models 50, 80 and 90 and a 4381 Model 22. IBM also used a different Debit/Credit implementation.

While DEC reportedly used an implementation of 1,000 terminals, IBM used only about 100.

"It's mathematically proportional, but performance could vary widely in implementation," Sawyer said. "Any scoring in this area is controversial. There is no way of predicting with certainty what would happen with a full implementation of terminals."

Kenneth MacMorran, manager of IBM's performance evaluation center in Dallas, agreed that the presence of an actual network could affect the performance of a system but claimed the IBM test was a valid implementation of Debit/Credit.

MacMorran said he was responsible for the benchmarks that began the flurry of activity. It started when he tested DEC equipment under IBM's proprietary Ramp C benchmark and IBM published the results. DEC responded by criticizing Ramp C as proprietary and launching an effort in support of Debit/Credit as an industry standard.

Ramp C and Debit/Credit are both valid but are fundamentally different. While Debit/Credit simulates simple transactions in an automated teller machine network, Ramp C uses a mix of transactions that is more analogous to the work done by many businesses.

On July 18th, 1988, ORACLE made history by setting performance records in every major computing environment. ORACLE set the world record for performance by running 265 transactions per second (tps) on a 3090-600E class Amdahl mainframe running the IBM MVS operating system. This breaks the old record of 240 tps set by a cluster of 16 Tandem computers.

ORACLE also set the record for DEC minicomputers by running 49 tps on a VAX 6240 running VMS. ORACLE set a UNIX record of 124 tps on a large minicomputer from Sequent as well.
