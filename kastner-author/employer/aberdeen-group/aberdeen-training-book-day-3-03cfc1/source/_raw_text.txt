How To Write A Profile:
Process Overview
• A well-written Profile will be:
– Focused, focused, and focused
• Title, Summaries, Sections, Paragraphs, Messages
• Organized to match the audience’s most likely
thought process
– Well researched
– Outlined in your head before one word is
committed to paper
• Discuss with other Aberdeen analysts to sharpen
your thoughts before starting to write
– Have no typos or misspellings when completed
– Be engaging, informative, and entertaining

Research
• Interview the suppliers’ executives
– How does the company view itself?
– What does the product/company do?
– What is new and different about today’s offering
compared to the past?
– What makes the offering and value proposition
unique?
– What does it believe its market position to be?
– What customer problems is the supplier trying
to solve?  What are the benefits?

Research (continued)
– Who are the direct competitors?
– What are the future product directions?
– How is the company structured?
– What were its origins?
– What are revenues and profitability?

Research (continued)
• Search the Web

Writing: The Introduction
• Write the “hook” — this is your first 1-3
sentences that will convince the targeted
audience to read on
– Must build on the title
– Must describe why the Profile is worth reading
— usually describes an IS issue or problem
that needs planning attention and states that
the Profile will shed light on this subject
– Should not provide research or analysis
– Should never be the Executive Summary

Writing:  The Words
• Start by writing your draft section titles — like
an outline.
• Make the section titles tell the story in their
own right with well chosen words
• Each section should describe and summarize
your research findings
– Add your research notes and the messages you
want to communicate under each section heading
– Each feature described should be followed by the
appropriate benefit that will be derived
– Put a minimum number of opinions in your first
draft or you will quickly lose focus and objectivity

[Page 19]

Slide 19, Chapter 3
Writing:  The Words (continued)
• Starting with core product/company capabilities, begin
writing each section
– Leave Executive Summary and Aberdeen Conclusions
until last
– Each paragraph should explain the wording of the
section head
– Make sentences as simple as possible
• One thought per paragraph
• Try and keep each paragraph to only 3-4 sentences
• DO NOT START EDUCATING YOUR AUDIENCE WITH
BACKGROUND MATERIALS THAT ARE OFF THE TRACK
OF THE SECTION HEADER.  IF YOU FEEL YOU NEED
EDUCATIONAL SECTIONS, PUT THEM IN SEPARATELY
AND AT THE BACK
• A one-sentence situational description is okay

Writing:  The Style
• Use simple, declarative sentences
– The use of parenthetical phrases within a
sentence can quickly get you in trouble
– Better to use multiple sentences
• Use descriptive terms and adjectives as
much as possible
– Build imagery — look for places to put
adjectives to help the reader clearly and
directly understand your messages and
opinions.  Be creative — build new words

Writing:  The Style (continued)
– Hyphenated phrases, such as “IBM’s one-size-
fits-all widget”, are encouraged
– Make the Profile entertaining and engaging for
the reader

[Page 23]
Confidential
Aberdeen Group, Inc.
Slide 23, Chapter 3
Writing:  The Style (continued)
• The term “Aberdeen believes …” is reserved for
Viewpoints
– Profiles should be more product/company
descriptive and analytical while Viewpoints
express a strongly held opinion and
recommendation of actions to end-users
– Avoid writing to “you”
• It is very difficult to consistently write a Profile using the
second person
• It is impossible to know who “you” is — is “you” the
CEO, CFO, VP MIS, programmer, casual desktop user, or
whomever?

[Page 24]
Confidential
Aberdeen Group, Inc.
Slide 24, Chapter 3
Writing:  The Style (continued)
• Pronouns should be used sparingly
– “It”, “they”, “their” — can be confusing as to
what is being referenced
– “He” and “She” are often inappropriate gender
connotations
• Enterprises are “it” — not “they”
• Avoid slang that non-US audiences will not
understand (this does not mean be boring, it
merely means Japanese reading the Profile on
the Web will not understand “wicked good” —
so be considerate)

[Page 25]
Confidential
Aberdeen Group, Inc.
Slide 25, Chapter 3
Writing:  The Style (continued)
• Do not use contractions such as “it’s”,
“don’t” and “can’t”
– It looks lazy — does not read well
– Many abbreviations, such as O/S for operating
system, create confusion for readers
• Avoid hackneyed expressions — they are
sophomoric
– For example, “This product is as fast as
greased lightening” would be a no-no

[Page 26]
Confidential
Aberdeen Group, Inc.
Slide 26, Chapter 3
Writing:  The Style (continued)
• Write as though you were speaking to
somebody — if you do not use a word or
phrase in every day conversation, do not
write it
• Examples:
– “thus”
– “the two companies interacted”
– “heretofore”

[Page 27]
Confidential
Aberdeen Group, Inc.
Slide 27, Chapter 3
Writing:  The Style (continued)
• Try to use initial capitals on acronyms of
more than four letters
– Risc/Unix is easier on the eyes than
RISC/UNIX
• Avoid “user” to mean an implementer
– Aberdeen refers to the end-user, the
consumer, of information technology
• Never assume a reader knows acronyms
– Always spell on first usage — Relational
Database Management System (Rdbms)

[Page 28]
Confidential
Aberdeen Group, Inc.
Slide 28, Chapter 3
Writing: The Style (continued)
• Put parenthetical comments within em
dashes — not parentheses
– “The company’s strategy — emphasizing
volume over quality — is a mistake in
Aberdeen’s opinion.”
• Use the em dash for highlighting key
phrases
– “The product’s key strength is
performance — not ease of use.”
• Hyphenate adjectival pairs
– An “end-user tool”

The 6 Deadly Early Mistakes
and Their Antidotes
• DON’T try and explain everything,
including the history of IT, in a 6-8 page
Profile
• DO assume your reader has been in
charge of an IT operation but simply does
not have the time or resources to
research or analyze the specific area you
are focusing on

[Page 40]
Confidential
Aberdeen Group, Inc.
Slide 40, Chapter 3
The 6 Deadly Early Mistakes And Their
Antidotes (continued)
• DON’T lose focus and therefore lose your
reader’s ability to follow your reasoning
• DO Simple sentences; one idea per
paragraph; each paragraph supports the
section; each section supports the title.
Run-on sentences are, by definition,
unfocused

[Page 41]
Confidential
Aberdeen Group, Inc.
Slide 41, Chapter 3
The 6 Deadly Early Mistakes And Their
Antidotes (continued)
• DON’T substitute your own opinion of
what you would do versus laying out the
facts and options for IS decision makers.
• DO stay slightly aloof from the fray
– While there are multiple sides to every issue,
it is the Aberdeen analyst’s role to honestly
communicate their research and analysis to
IS decision makers

[Page 42]
Confidential
Aberdeen Group, Inc.
Slide 42, Chapter 3
The 6 Deadly Early Mistakes And Their
Antidotes (continued)
• DON’T write in an artificial or stiff way
• DO read your Profile out loud — if you
sound asinine, change the words until
they flow smoothly and comfortably

[Page 43]
Confidential

Slide 43, Chapter 3
The 6 Deadly Mistakes And Their
Antidotes (continued)
• DON’T keep the Profile effort to yourself and
then stall or rework the same aspects of it for
what seems to be forever
• DO get others involved!!
– Take a 1/2 hour to discuss the issues involved
with anybody that might have relevant insight
– It is more productive for a manager to spend an

right.”

– Answer client’s specific questions about a potential
