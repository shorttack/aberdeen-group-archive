# Describe the dates, vendors, and analog equipment of the previous radios, now replaced

The previous Boston Police Department radio system was an analog network that had been in place since the mid-1970s, operated through the Boston Area Police Emergency Radio Network (BAPERN) and maintained by the Greater Boston Police Council (GBPC). The analog system used UHF T-band frequencies, which were initially coordinated by the GBPC after a formal petition to the FCC in the early 1970s. The system's official startup occurred around 1974, after a two- to three-year planning and procurement period.[^2_1][^2_2]

### Dates

- The analog BAPERN system build-out began in mid-1974, with full service for area police departments by late 1974.[^2_2]
- The City of Boston and partner agencies used the analog system continuously until the conversion to digital encrypted radios on August 9, 2025.[^2_3][^2_4][^2_5]


### Vendors

- The BAPERN network was built, managed, and maintained by the Greater Boston Police Council, Inc. (GBPC), which represents more than 120 agencies.[^2_1][^2_2]
- The system’s primary radio equipment manufacturer historically was Motorola, with occasional upgrades and studies recommending Motorola hardware for both legacy and expanded UHF and 800 MHz operations.[^2_6][^2_7]


### Analog Equipment

- The core technology was a UHF T-band radio system, with base stations, repeaters, and mobile units operating on multiple frequency pairs licensed to BAPERN and member agencies.[^2_7][^2_1]
- Early equipment featured Motorola first-generation radios and consoles, using frequencies like 471.2125 MHz, 471.2375 MHz, and similar, with subsequent proposals for additional channels and coverage upgrades throughout the late 1970s and into the 1980s.[^2_7]
- The analog system supported interagency interoperability, district-level dispatch, and city-wide incident command for more than four decades before being replaced by a digital encrypted system in August 2025.[^2_5][^2_8][^2_1]

This timeline and equipment background highlight the transition from a Motorola-driven, GBPC-managed regional analog radio system (since about 1974) to the new digital encrypted technology deployed in 2025.

# Drill down on history and facts of “The system’s official startup occurred around 1974, after a two- to three-year planning and procurement period.[pcsdma +1]”

Based on the detailed historical documentation from the Greater Boston Police Council and related sources, here are the comprehensive facts and timeline regarding the "two- to three-year planning and procurement period" that led to BAPERN's 1974 startup:

## Historical Context and Genesis (Late 1960s)

The planning period began in response to significant civil unrest during **1968-1972**, described as "a period of great civil unrest nationwide, the focus being namely America's involvement in Vietnam". Boston was particularly affected due to its "large number of colleges and universities," making it "a hotbed of dissent". In **late 1969**, there were "very loud protests in the Boston/Cambridge area, some of which produced violence and destruction".[^3_1][^3_2][^3_3]

## Formation and Initial Planning (1971)

The critical breakthrough occurred in **1971** when the informal group of police chiefs "brainstormed and, looking at the state of land mobile radio, quickly realized that there were just insufficient frequencies in the high band allocation to solve their issues". This led them to petition the FCC for a block of frequencies in the ultra-high bands (UHF T Band).[^3_1]

The FCC had originally allocated T-Band spectrum (470-512 MHz) for land mobile communications operations in major urban areas under **Docket No. 18261 in 1971**. Boston was one of the 11 metropolitan areas designated to receive this spectrum allocation.[^3_4]

## Formal Organization and Planning Phase (1971-1974)

Simultaneously with the FCC petition, "the group formalized and registered as a 501 C (3) non-profit corporation" and established the Greater Boston Police Council. They decided to name their radio collaborative the "Boston Area Police Emergency Radio Network (BAPERN)".[^3_1]

The planning phase involved:

- **Dues assessment** on a per capita basis for member agencies[^3_1]
- **Annual assessments** for remote lines (telephone lines to carry radio signals from base stations to transmission towers)[^3_1]
- **System design** for five initial frequencies: two area-wide channels covering all agencies and three district channels for more localized needs[^3_1]
- **Equipment procurement** primarily featuring Motorola radio equipment[^3_5]


## System Deployment and Startup (Mid to Late 1974)

"After a two- to three-year period of planning, design, and funding, the network started to come online on a department-by-department basis around mid- to late 1974". The initial deployment included approximately **40 departments** encompassing agencies such as "Quincy, Milton, Dedham, Waltham, Medford, Revere, and others," in addition to the core founding departments.[^3_1]

This timeline confirms that the planning and procurement period extended from **1971 to 1974**, representing exactly the "two- to three-year period" referenced in the GBPC historical documentation.[^3_6][^3_1]

# In Boston, the new radios were tied to a new 911 dispatching computer system developed by Arthur D Little Inc. And funded by LEAA federal support. Detail the history of this 911 system and successors.

Boston’s advanced 911 dispatch system—the prototype of its kind nationally—originated in the early 1970s when Arthur D. Little Inc. was contracted, with federal Law Enforcement Assistance Administration (LEAA) support, to design a computer-aided dispatch (CAD) system for the Boston Police Department. This pioneering system tightly integrated with new police radios, forming the backbone of modern emergency response in the city.[^4_1]

### Origins and Arthur D. Little’s Role

- In 1970, Arthur D. Little produced initial technical specifications for a command and control system for the Boston Police, including automated dispatching and real-time incident tracking.[^4_1]
- LEAA grants funded this initial development, reflecting a broader federal push to modernize urban public safety communications.[^4_1]


### System Features and Firsts

- The system marked one of the first operational use cases in the U.S. for computer-aided dispatch, where CAD terminals helped automate call intake, assignment, and resource tracking for police response—eliminating manual paper logs.
- It was among the earliest examples linking 911 telephone systems, police radios, and computer-based dispatch into a unified technology stack.[^4_2][^4_3]


### Federal and National Significance

- Boston’s CAD deployment was used as a demonstration project to encourage other major U.S. cities to adopt similar digital dispatch systems, fueling the growth of 911 as a nationwide standard in the late 1970s and 1980s.[^4_3][^4_2]


### System Modernization and Successors

- Mid-1990s: Boston upgraded to a new generation CAD/911 system in 1994, serving until 2014.[^4_4]
- 2014: Boston launched a \$17 million dispatch modernization (using an Intergraph CAD platform) integrating real-time GPS, ShotSpotter gunfire detection, and seamless service for police, fire, and EMS. This replaced manual entry and text databases with a fully geospatial, drag-and-drop interface.[^4_4]
- 2024-2025: The city began testing a new CAD system set to replace its decades-old predecessor, supporting next-generation 911 features, enhanced regional interoperability, and improved integration with the new radio technology and digital mapping.[^4_5]

Boston remains a national leader in public safety communications, with its Arthur D. Little-designed, LEAA-funded system setting the pattern for computer-aided dispatch and emergency 911 infrastructure in the U.S..[^4_5][^4_4][^4_1]

# Technical specs and capabilities of the original ADL dispatch system

The original Arthur D. Little (ADL) police dispatch system installed in Boston in the early 1970s was considered pioneering for its time, combining several innovative technical components for public safety operations.[^5_1][^5_2][^5_3]

### Technical Specifications

- **Central Mainframe Computer:** The ADL system relied on a central mainframe, likely an IBM or UNIVAC model of that era, to handle all CAD (computer-aided dispatch) functions and record management.[^5_3][^5_1]
- **Terminals and Operator Consoles:** Dispatchers accessed the mainframe via “intelligent” CRT terminals, allowing them to enter incident details, dispatch units, and update call statuses in real time.[^5_1]
- **911 Call Handling:** The system integrated automatic telephone call logging from 911, linking every incoming call with a case record and digitally timestamping response actions.[^5_1]
- **Radio Integration:** Unit dispatch information was “pushed” both by voice radio and via an early digital data messaging system to specially equipped police vehicles.[^5_3]
- **Geographic File Lookup:** The central computer was programmed with a geographic database of beats and sectors; when a call was entered, the computer could recommend the nearest available units and display their status/location to the dispatcher.[^5_3]
- **Incident Recommendation and Assignment:** The software automatically calculated which patrol cars were closest, based on up-to-the-second unit check-ins (manually updated over radio), for fastest response.[^5_3]
- **Automatic Time Logging:** Every transaction—call intake, dispatch, arrival, scene clearance—was logged by the system, supporting post-incident reporting and response-time measurement.[^5_1]
- **Display Screens in Vehicles:** Some patrol cars had early mobile data terminals (“small television screens”) that received alerts and instructions from dispatch, representing one of the first field uses of such equipment in U.S. policing.[^5_3]


### Core Capabilities

- **Unit Status Tracking:** Dispatch could instantly query and see the current assignment/status of every car.
- **Fifteen-Second Dispatch Cycle:** Time between 911 intake and digital dispatch of nearest cars averaged just 15 seconds in documented trials.[^5_3]
- **Automated Resource Suggestion:** System recommended dispatch of units not only by beat assignment but also by proximity and availability, rather than relying solely on dispatcher memory.[^5_3]
- **Record Generation:** Comprehensive records for accountability, analysis, and management; enabled the introduction of analytical policing in Boston decades ahead of most other cities.[^5_1]

These features made the ADL system a model for later generations of CAD and 911 technology—integrating telephony, radio, automation, and electronic data management at a time when most U.S. cities still used manual logbooks and paper call slips.[^5_2][^5_1][^5_3]

# Detail “2014: Boston launched a \$17 million dispatch modernization”

In June 2014, Boston launched a \$17 million overhaul of its 911 and public safety dispatch system, replacing two-decade-old technology to boost emergency response and officer safety. The system, built by Intergraph, went live across police, fire, and EMS agencies, providing a real-time, map-based view of the city and all active units.[^6_1]

### Key Features and Innovations

- **Real-Time Geospatial Mapping:** The system continuously maps every police car, ambulance, and fire engine, as well as each 911 call and ShotSpotter gunfire detector location, allowing dispatchers to visually assign units with simple drag-and-drop functionality.[^6_1]
- **Faster, Smarter Dispatching:** Automation allowed quicker identification and deployment of the nearest available resources. Instead of manually tracking unit locations in text databases or by memory, dispatchers had instant situational awareness to support faster, more informed decisions.[^6_1]
- **Unified Platform:** The system served all public safety agencies—police, fire, and EMS—allowing seamless incident coordination and resource sharing citywide.[^6_1]
- **Improved Response Times and Safety:** Streamlined workflows enabled fire companies and ambulances to be notified within seconds, shaving critical time off emergency responses.[^6_1]
- **Advanced Analytics:** The platform allowed officials to index and analyze calls by time, location, or weather conditions, supporting trends analysis for optimal resource deployment.[^6_1]


### Funding and Implementation Details

- **Budget:** Originally budgeted at \$17.1 million with \$11.7 million spent at launch time (records management enhancements were still pending).[^6_1]
- **Training and Preparation:** All dispatchers, first responders, and call-takers underwent extensive hands-on training to ensure smooth transition and avoid technical glitches experienced in other cities.[^6_1]


### Next Generation Readiness

- The Intergraph system set Boston up for “Next Generation 911” capabilities—including support for texts, videos, and expanded mobile communications, building the foundation for future upgrades.[^6_1]

This modernization represented one of the largest investments in Boston’s public safety technology in recent decades, directly improving emergency management citywide.[^6_1]

### Abstract

Kastner's role at Arthur D. Little Systems (ADLS) on the federally-funded LEAA-grant 911 computer-aided dispatch (CAD) systems built in the 1970s. ADLS designed a fault-tolerant dual-Data-General-Eclipse minicomputer dispatch architecture first deployed in Boston, then migrated to Philadelphia, Minneapolis, and Aurora CO. As vendor support manager, Kastner led hardware configuration, procurement, burn-in, and installation for all four cities. He separately led ADL's design of the St. Petersburg FL 911 dispatch on the city's IBM 370/135 with CICS, recommending and modifying the Hampton Roads VA dispatch system for transfer.
