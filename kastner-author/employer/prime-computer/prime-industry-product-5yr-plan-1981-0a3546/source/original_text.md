EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

INTRODUCTION

This plan was prepared by the Market Planning and Management
Department of Marketing Development. The plan defines the market
environment and product requirements for the next five years based on
the perceived needs of the vertical markets Prime sells into. The
plan describes where our computers will be used; what users will be
doing with them; and what products are needed to meet Prime's market
growth objectives. The plan recommends specific product features that
Market Planning believes are necessary to our success. It is our
initial input into the ongoing cycle of long term product development.

WHY AN INDUSTRY PRODUCT REQUIREMENTS PLAN?

Prime is a marketing-driven company. Market Planning is chartered to
develop the plans and programs necessary to penetrate growing and
profitable markets. During the past year, Market Managers have
prepared market plans which define the nature, attractiveness and
marketing strategies for those vertical markets which are critical to
Prime's ongoing and future success. Market and sales programs based
on those plans are being implemented now. Marketing programs alone
cannot sustain the Company. Prime needs products (hardware, software
and services) which meet the needs of the market place. The market
plans address those products and product features required to
implement specific marketing strategies in specific vertical markets.
The market plans, however, do not give the Company a clear picture of
the product requirements common to all of Prime's vertical market
targets. The plan you are reading is the combination and synthesis of
the product requirements Prime needs to meet its corporate growth
strategies in the first half of the 1980's.

The premise we used in preparing this plan is grounded in common
sense,

"The set of product requirements common to all markets is where
most of Prime's product development attention and investment
should be placed".

**page-4**
WHAT IS COVERED IN THE PLAN?

The plan is Market Planning's initial input to the product development
cycle. It contains the following:

o The market opportunities in each planned vertical market;
o The market strategies in each planned vertical market;
o Short term products needed to survive and/or meet 1981-82 revenue
forecasts by market;
o An analysis of what kinds of computing Prime computers will be
doing, by vertical market and price band.
o Gross level system metrics by kind of computing and by price band;
o Detailed product requirements and product feature recommendations
for each of seven kinds of computing (computational, graphics,
timesharing/program development, office automation, transaction
processing, query, small business).

The information and recommendations in the plan are primarily of value
to Marketing Development and to those involved in managing and
participating in the long term product strategy and product
development process.

ASSUMPTIONS

The plan assumes the following:

o Marketing and Development is aligned with corporate strategies;
o Prime can make or buy the products needed;
o Product feature prioritization is an iterative process of which
this plan is the first iteration.
o Market Planning lacks the expertise and charter to define complete
functional product specifications.
o Development timing issues are beyond the plan's scope. Market
timing issues are included.
o Development resource limitations are ignored because the
limitations change, and resource requirements change. Market
Planning is defining what is needed, not necessarily what is
feasible.

o Programmable parallel channel interface product like the HPC-4

o Improving floating point precision and consistency.

o COBOL functionality at or near full ANSI intermediate level.

o FORTRAN 77 compile and run-time performance.

o Hyperchannel interface product.

HOW PRIME SYSTEMS WILL BE USED (Section 3.)... The multitude of vertical market applications were categorized by
functional use. Even though most Prime computers are used in a multi-
functional environment, the individual major functions were isolated.
The functions are computation, timesharing, including program
development, graphics, including CAD, office automation, transaction
processing, query and small business. Two sets of tables were
developed: one set of seven functional use tables that shows by five
price bands how important the function is to a particular vertical
market; a second set of five price band tables show a matrix of
functional use by vertical market.

The tables do not lend themselves to written summarization. Anyone
interested in projections on what our computers will be doing
(functional system use), where they will be doing it (vertical
market), and in which price/performance category (price band), should
consult Section 3.

GROSS SYSTEM CHARACTERISTICS (Section 4.)

Section 4 contains 35 charts representing all the combinations of
functional use and price band. Each chart contains the disk capacity,
communications ports, relative CPU performance and representative
vertical market applications. Both the typical requirements and the
absolute range of requirements are shown.

In summary, the charts show that our current product price/performance
is acceptable or better in most vertical market segments. In order to
penetrate further, both higher and lower price/performance systems
than we currently offer are justified by market needs.

TRANSACTION PROCESSING FUNCTIONAL USE (Section 5.)

Transaction Processing (TP) is an important requisite to marketing in
the commercial segment. It involves near realtime processing of
discrete, pre-programmed units of business procedures entered by
clericals with no real knowledge of computers.

The TP operating environment is distinctly different from Prime's
timesharing heritage. The key TP product attributes are response
time, I/O performance, audit and control, availability, and complex
networking. Also, the lack of qualified on-line commercial
programmers and analysts explains a backlog of applications at most
companies. This creates an opportunity for Prime to market a
productivity story to the EDP department management of F1000-type
companies.

**page-7**
The Fortune 1000-type company is a prospect with opportunities for
long term, multiple system business. We can expect a slow but
progressive revenue growth from them as pilots become multiple
departmental systems. We do not foresee significant corporate
revenue streams from TP at Fortune 1000-type firms before 1984.

The stand-alone TP system sold to small and medium companies will
require applications packages and support such that we recommend a
channel of distribution strategy using system builders and dealers.

Prime is now developing TP products which will provide a solid base
towards meeting the product requirements of the TP functional area.

QUERY FUNCTIONAL USE (Section 6.)

The query functional use encompasses online information search and
retrieval, report generation, and most commercial programs run in
batch. Although query is usually associated with commercial products,
virtually every Prime system does some query work at some time..
Therefore, products for query provide at least some benefits to every
Prime system customer.

The best characterization of the query environment is timesharing
with a heavy input/output (I/O) content. This puts a premium on I/O
system throughput. The ad hoc query user is not computer
knowledgeable. He is apt to be more concerned with the ease of use and
functional breadth of the query interface to the computer than how
fast the answer comes back. Programmers traditionally view query
programs particularly report generation programs, as intellectually
unrewarding. Automatic report program generation tools will increase
programmer productivity and decrease the cost of developing user
software.

Significant product requirements for query are:

o Data management efficiency, ease of use, and functionality.
o Communications support for data on networks, and for large local
  networks of terminals and other devices.
o Utilities for fast loading/unloading of large data files.
o Programmer productivity tools for report writing.
o Higher capacity disks and printers.
o $10K-$30K personal computer.
o Data security.

As rapid technological innovation continues, data processing will
include more information processing. Ho natter how the specifics
evolve, query will play a major role in the future of information
processing.

COuPUTA^IOH FCHCTIOWAL USE (Section 7.)... The computation functional use covers the 'number crunching'
applications of science, industry, research and academia. Computation
usually involves applying complex algorithms in programs to
observation data. Some of the computation-intensive applications
running on Prime today include printed circuit board routing, refinery
piping, finite element modeling and actuarial analysis. Our
computational product capabilities have fueled Prime's growth success
for several years. It is a credit to our product set and marketing
skills that we usually only see one competitor when computational
timesharing is called for, albeit that competitor is DEC'S VAZ.

As the cost per computation drops, the market demand for computation
increases. The computation area is price elastic. Furthermore, the
problems computational computers are being asked to solve are becoming
so complex or otherwise insolvable that we believe there will always
be a market for computers which model, simulate, and analyze. Prime's
marketing strategy in computational markets is to have strong
computational capabilities within the framework of a compatible,
friendly, easy to use, general purpose product line. We recommend
continuing our avoidance of single user, 'hot box' niches where our
multi-functionality is not require.

The major product requirements for the computation functional use are:

o Virtual memory throughput in a multi-user environment
o Interconnectivity to common computation-oriented peripherals .like
  graphics workstations, array processors, and micro-computers.
o Extensive program development tools in an easy to use environment.
o Higher capacity disks and tapes.
o Increased data throughput over networks.
o . Interface to mainframes via Hyperchannel.
o Connectivity to a broad range of Prime and non-Prime terminals.

COMPUTER-ASSISTED DESIGN AND MANUFACTURING (Section 8.)

CAD/CAM is part of a larger functional use called graphics. The
graphics function includes CAD/CAM, design graphics, mapping and image
processing. Graphics in general and CAD/CAM in particular are viewed
as 'attack' market segments.

Graphics system users are usually white collar or professional workers
who are using computers to improve productivity. Graphics is used to
display information and to design products. In this case, products
range from building designs to automobiles, display advertising, and
movies. Both the display and design segments are growing at rates
around 50% per year.

Today, the graphics user has a wide range of products to choose from.
Prime offers price benefits over mainframes, functionality and
communications benefits over stand-alone workstations, and performance
benefits over networked micros. We must continue to innovate in the
graphics area in order to stave off a wide range of competition.

The software algorithms needed to manipulate designs of solids or
patterns for structural or electronic design are increasing the load
on the central system. This trend will continue to drive us towards
more performance.

Until Prime's Medusa and other CAD products establish our market
presence, we are under constant comparison to the stand-alone design
vendors like Applicon and Computervision. If Prime could offer a
$50,000 (hardware cost) system for a single workstation, with full
Prime functionality and expandability, our competitive position would
be strengthened.

The key graphics functional use product requirements are:

o Faster input/output throughput
o Programmable parallel interfaces
o Longer distances between terminals and the CPU
o A $50,000 single user graphics system (plus software)
o A low cost character terminal
o A low cost color graphics printer
o Improved documentation, training, and support services.

The plan does not address the future software feature requirements of
Medusa, Prime's structural CAD product.

TIMESHARING FUNCTIONAL USE (Section C.)

Timesharing and program development are functional areas where Prime
has a long history of product development. Our multi-functional,
multi-user operating system, PRIMOS, has hardware and microcode
assists to optimize throughput. Prime has the finest multi-user
timesharing system in the world. Almost all Prime Computer users come
in contact with our timesharing features at some time. We expect this
to be less true in the future as some TP, OA and CAD users are
isolated from PRIMOS, but timesharing will continue to be an important
product functional area.

From a business standpoint, sales to strictly timesharing customers
are a decreasing percentage of Prime's business since service bureaus
and education are mature, nearly saturated markets. While the long
term timesharing business picture is not bright, we are under
competitive and market pressure to broaden the price/performance range
of our product line. The new timesharing markets include energy,
government and finance. The marketplace focus on the Prime 850 is an
indication of the potential for very large timesharing systems at
mini-computer prices. At the low end, we recommend a personal
computer product compatible with the rest of the product line as a
counter to personal micro-computers, and as an important part of an
evolving strategy for departmental information systems.... The competitive pressures come mainly from DEC's VAX product line.
DEC will remain the competitive benchmark indefinitely, and we see the
competition increasing in intensity as DEC broadens its product line
and matures its software. Many of the product requirements we have
noted are counters to DEC.

The key timesharing functional use product requirements are:

o System-wide throughput improvements
o Ease of use and consistency in command environment
o Program development tools
o Data, system and program security improvements
o Connectivity to Prime and non-Prime devices, especially local area
  terminal networks
o Data transfer throughput improvements in Primenet
o $10,000-$30,000 personal computer.

OFFICE AUTOMATION FUNCTIONAL AREA (Section 10.)

The office automation OA functional area includes the collection,
collation, and dissemination of information by clericals, managers,
professionals and executives. It is one of the fastest growing market
segments, but a lot of the growth is in replacing typewriters with
limited intelligence word processing devices. Prime's office
automation product strategy has been to bring our existing product
strengths in compatible, multi-functional systems with good query and
timesharing capabilities together with the 'office application'
elements of host-based word processing, management communications and
advanced text management. OA-is the first Prime application product.
It has been followed by the MEDUSA CAD product and, next year,
transaction processing.

The OA product has been plagued with several problems: stability, low
word processing functionality, lack of networking and ease of use. At
this time, Marketing is evaluating new positioning strategies in order
to focus on high leverage prospects with our product strengths. In
addition, marketing programs are underway to marry third party
software packages together with OA. This will greatly strengthen our
product offering to managers and professionals with decision support,
query and presentation graphics display software.

The proliferation of commodity-priced low margin word processing
workstations that cannot communicate with each other presents an
intriguing opportunity. Prime's excellent communications capabilities
can be enhanced so that Prime provides a computer which translates one
word processor's communications to what is understandable by another
vendor's word processor, facilitating the movement of documents in the
many organizations with mixed vendor's equipment. We recommend that
Prime pursue this niche.

The key product requirements for the office automation functional area
are:

o Terminal, data management and network throughput
o Connectivity for Prime and non-Prime terminals
o Communications capabilities
o Ease of use features
o Tailorable for foreign languages
o Industry-standard word processing
o OA and EDP gateways and interfaces
o Import/Export utilities.

SMALL BUSINESS FUNCTIONAL AREA (Section 11.)

The small business area is the area covered by Prime's dealer
program. The small business user is typified by requirements for low
cost, easy to use, turnkey systems with application software,
installation, customizing and training provided by the vendor. The
system usually is at the core of the business' operations, so
availability and field service support are important issues.

Market Planning was chartered to incorporate the dealer program into
its market planning and management in late June. A products
requirement section will be written and presented after the dealer
program has been studied and applied to vertical market plans.

1.0 PRODUCT STRATEGY
1.1 Corporate Strategy

The products Prime Computer Inc., builds and sells are based on a
long term, long range corporate strategy. Prime's marketing
thrust is based on the following product line characteristics:

o General purpose computers with broad-based market appeal
o Exceptional price/performance
o Compatibility and commonality of configuration
o Used (today) by sophisticated end users
o Used (tomorrow) in wide ranging environments from offices to
factory floors
o Easy to use and friendly to users
o Multi-user and multi-functional
o Coexistence with mainframes in distributed processing
environments
o Avoid vertical integration of rapidly changing, high technology
products.

Given the corporate strategy and some of the obvious computer
market trends, Prime's future product strategy anticipates trends
towards:

o A broader range of central systems capabilities
o Increased range of reliability, availability and support
products and services
o End-user 'system' transparency
o Enhanced programmer and professional productivity tools
o Increasing use of our ring resource technology in both stand-
alone and distributed environments
o A leadership commercial system family with data management and
communications as key components.
o Extensions into information processing like FAX, PBX, voice
etc.
o Riding the technology curves towards increased functionality at
lower prices
o Transferring solution software investments to our computers
o Packaging issues like electrical consumption and cabinetry
volume and area.... Four rapidly emerging markets give Prime the opportunity to
establish an industry-leadership position within these markets.
They are:

o Local area networks of terminals and other devices
o Distributed processing including personal and departmental
systems
o Office automation
o Computer-aided design and manufacturing (CAD/CAM).

## Page 19 — Section 2.1: Marketing Strategies

This section describes the 1981 and early 1982 marketing strategies for each market.

### Construction Engineering

- Focus sales prospecting on users of large structural analysis programs and on the largest construction firms (our success in this market has been with small and medium firms).
- Obtain industry software applications such as MSC/NASTRAN, PATRAN, ICES/STRUDL and ADINA. All of these programs are computation intensive and they require large virtual program sizes.
- Sales tools will stress our product and reference strength in the engineering sciences.

### Utilities

- Use a long term, National Accounts sales strategy.
- Use system builders and joint marketing to handle technical segment which is our current base. Transmission simulation, power monitoring and routing require mostly computational capabilities. An APC-4-like device is needed (minimally) to handle microprocessor-based data acquisition.
- Horizontally diversify into commercial applications in such areas as inventory control, maintenance scheduling, and financial planning. This broadens our addressable market and minimizes business risk should we lose our present technical market niche.

### Manufacturing Engineering

- Continue to add application software solution sources so that the prospect is faced with choosing software and Prime products become a foregone conclusion.
- Vertically, focus on the high growth segments in electronic CAD and mechanical CAD.
- Introduce CAD products in 1981 that will capitalize on the CAD market opportunity.
- Develop and foster relationships with university and government programs which lead to the development of new algorithms in software. Several Prime customers got started from university spinoffs with highly specialized packages.

## Page 64 — Section 5: Transaction Processing

From an information processing viewpoint, transaction processing (TP) is the execution of a preprogrammed unit of business procedures at the demand of a user. Typically, this involves the input of the type of request, data tailored to the type of request, and the processing of the request. A general ledger journal entry is an example of the kind of business procedure that TP can automate.

The TP functional use area has basic commercial data processing product requirements. Without a TP product set, Prime would be severely limited in its marketing position to the commercial market. TP is not all of commercial information processing. Query, discussed in Section 6, is a necessary complement to TP.

The TP operating environment is distinctly different from Prime's traditional emphasis on timesharing variants. The key attributes to Prime's success with TP are response time, I/O performance, audit and control and recovery. All in a networked environment. Secondly, the dearth of qualified business programmers and analysts and the backlog of applications at most installations has created an opportunity to improve programmer productivity through the use of automated application development tools.

Most of the uniqueness Prime can bring to the TP market is in software. TP systems will have a high software content, approaching 50%, which will yield excellent margins because the marketing cost for software is trivial.

There are two distinct types of transaction processing product prospects:

1. The Fortune 1000-type business or government agency. They are involved through a central TP organization in departmental, division and organization-wide TP plans involving large hardware and software investments.
2. A small or medium organization seeking a single stand-alone TP engine for their business.

**Key product requirements in the transaction processing functional area:**

- Response time
- Data management throughput and network functionality
- Application development tools
- Availability
- Communications functionality
- Breadth of terminal and device connectivity
- Training, maintenance and support services.

---

## Page 65 — Transaction Processing (continued)

The second type of TP prospect is a small or medium organization seeking a single stand-alone TP engine for their business. These organizations will also want an integrated set of general business and vertical market software packages. In short, the second type matches the profile of system builder and dealer customers. We recommend a channel of distribution product packaging strategy for this class of user.

The differences between the TP environment and Prime's traditional customer:

- The TP user is a clerical.
- The TP programmer knows COBOL and not much else.
- The TP application is part of the organizational and procedural guts of the business: availability is crucial.
- The repetitive nature of the transactions and the potential large number of users make response time the critical performance issue.
- Each transaction, arriving at random, may generate large data access activity, either locally or over a network.

Market Planning is aware of the Prime TP products now being built in the U.K. This section was written based on perceptions of the TP market and the experience of Market Planning in the TP market.

- Graceful degradation and failure.

- Process to process communications.

### 5.7.4 Journaled Updates

## Page 85–86 — Section 5.13: Typical TP Application Scenario

The purpose of this section is to describe a typical use of a TP system. The following scenario depicts a typical manufacturing order entry transaction. To the computer, dozens of actions are required. To the Terminal Operator (TO), it's just one transaction.

1. Terminal Operator (TO) opens the morning mail and finds a mail order. After timestamping the order, TO turns to his terminal.
2. TO selects the Order Processing menu function. ((Load and execute order program step one; after validating that TO is authorized to do orders)).
3. TO selects mail order option, as opposed to telephone. ((Load mail order program, display order options screen)).
4. TO has no customer number on order, so alpha lookup is necessary. Enters customer name. ((Customer file lookup on customers whose names approximate the entered name. Display all approximations with customer numbers and addresses)). TO selects the right customer by sight verifying customer's order address.
5. ((Automatic credit check of customer's status. Involves file checks against accounts receivable, outstanding orders, and the customer's credit limit.))
6. ((Extract terms from customer record. Display as default)). TO accepts default customer terms or overrides for this order.
7. ((Extract Bill to address from customer record. Display it.)) TO enters up to seven lines of Ship To Address data.
8. ((Extract customer's preferred method of shipping and display it.)) TO accepts default or overrides.
9. ((Consolidate the order information to this point and display in the upper half of the CRT screen. Lower half is a partial screen for order line items)).
10. TO enters quantity and part number. ((Search product file for part number. Is the quantity on hand? If not, backorder. Where is the quantity located? What is the most economical shipping point?))
11. ((Display standard list price.)) TO overrides price or accepts standard list.
12. ((Extend price times quantity. Reduce customer's available credit. Calculate sales tax.))
13. TO keeps entering quantities and parts until the order is filled. When TO accepts the order, it becomes committed. ((Take each part record and change allocated to committed quantity. Schedule shipment and/or manufacturing. Update onhand inventory and order backlog. Rewrite customer record with reduced line of credit. Write the whole order to the order file.))

_Note: The order entry of parts numbers and quantities can be done very quickly by the TO. Response time here is very important. Unfortunately, the computer processing of each line item involves several I/Os and computations too, all of which tend to inhibit fast responses._

## Page 101 — Section 7: Computation

Heavy computational loads have traditionally been performed on large mainframes. Prime has played a moderate role in "number crunching" environments. Typically, we have done the front end data manipulation, shipped the data to the mainframe and done post-processing data analysis. Price performance improvements across the industry in the past three years have made local computation on superminicomputers an attractive alternative to mainframes.

The continuing drop in the cost of computation has created a growing demand for computation: the demand is price elastic. Furthermore, the problems computational computers are being asked to solve are becoming so complex and otherwise insolvable that industry and government are demanding more computational capacity. There will always be a market for computers which model, simulate and analyze.

Competitively, Prime has avoided the pure computational environment. SEL and Harris are examples of 'hot boxes' which are sold to do mostly computation in dedicated (single user) environments. Prime is most competitive in environments where timeshared computation, other data processing, communications or a mix of activities are involved. Prime's marketing strategy in computational markets is to have strong computational capabilities within a compatible, friendly, general purpose environment.

### Section 9: Timesharing/Program Development

Prime Computer has a great deal of experience in building products for timesharing use. A large percentage of our historical business has been timesharing, and Market Planning estimates that 20–25% of our present system sales are for timesharing and program development uses. This percentage is expected to slowly decline to around the 15% level over the next four years.

The rush towards personal computing — that is, local terminal, storage and processing power — has been extensively discussed in the trade press. The crux of the product differences between Prime's host-based timesharing system and a stand-alone personal computer is 'resource power'. An Apple II computer can support 10MB of disk, a single user process, and 48K of memory. A Prime 250 can support 2000MB of disk, 64 processes, and a megabyte of memory. There is only a tenfold difference in price.

Market Planning recommends that the company seriously explore the product band between dumb terminals and stand-alone micro-processor based systems. Market Planning believes there is a natural product fit and an opportunity market for personal computers with integral networking and Prime host access. These personal computers would be packaged as professional, managerial and engineering workstations in local area networks with full access to all the resources of a Prime or other host.

Internal Prime Computer Inc. five-year Industry Product Requirements Plan dated July 15, 1981 (COMPANY CONFIDENTIAL), authored by Peter Kastner of Prime's Market Planning and Management Department of Marketing Development with input from Prime market managers (the 'and Others' coauthors). The plan synthesizes vertical-market plans for Construction Engineering, Power Utilities, Manufacturing Engineering, Manufacturing Production, Banking, Government, and Energy (Petroleum) into a single statement of common product requirements for Prime's first half-of-the-1980s strategy.
