# HP Pre-Summit Analyst Phone Call — Kastner Feedback

> Archived from: HP-Analyst-Phone-Call-6.doc
> Original publication date: 2003-09-01
> Author: Peter S. Kastner (Aberdeen Group, Chief Research Officer)

---

## Original Document Text

HP is getting ready to do their world-wide summit in another couple weeks so it wanted to get feedback from core analysts as to how different aspects of HP are doing right now so it can tune their presentations and the things it focus on at the summit.

Q:  If we look at HP as the Enterprise Group, the Imaging and Printing Group, Personal Computing Group, and the Services Group, what would be your focus at Aberdeen?

Peter:  My primary would be PSG and IPG but, as chief research officer, I am the person who focuses on HP as a company.

Q:  If you look at HP at the corporate level, it has tried to affect a transition over the last several months from a very inward-focused company, working through the Compaq merger, and are now trying to drive an outbound message of their business value to both the analyst and to the market.  If you think of what’s been communicated to you as an analyst what kind of core business is HP presenting to the market right now?

Peter:  I think there have been some very simplistic corporate messages, and those have not been particularly communicated, or drilled down, at the AR or analyst facing events that I’ve been a part of.  I have briefings all the time from various HP people and it’s never tied back to any particular corporate themes that I can recall.

Q:  One of the things that HP has been trying to push is the notion of HP as the low total-cost-of-ownership provider.  Have you been hearing this idea, and secondly is it believable? 

Peter:  No and no.

Q:  How about a message of reduced complexity?  And is that believable?

Peter:  Yes.  It is believable in the consumer side, but to a lesser extent in the IPG side.  There are no proof points on the ESG side.  And on the services side, there is only a marginal connection to the business drivers around creating and maintaining the global value chain.  The adaptive enterprise looks to be too IT facing and too little business executive management facing.

Q:  That’s actually the next area I wanted to talk about, HP’s “Adaptive Enterprise” initiative.  So you’re saying that right now it appears that it’s too IT facing and too little executive…

Peter:  …value chain benefit oriented.  That’s comment one.  Comment two is that adaptive enterprise is an ESG and Services initiative.  You haven’t tied in PSG and IPG.

Q:  How critical is the “Adaptive Enterprise” to HP’s future?

Peter:  Analysts are at the point where they want some beef.  There’s been too much vision and not enough “what can I do and how can I do it?”

Q:  If you look at HP’s “Adaptive Enterprise” and contrast that to IBM’s “On Demand,” do you see one or the other organizations as winning the mindshare war at this time?

Peter:  No.  It’s also kind of up in the air as to just what the “computer utility” and “capacity on demand” means.  Some of my deep analysis of what IBM is doing on the services and software side would indicate that it may well be going in a direction that won’t meet the current or near future needs of a lot of companies.  The On Demand strategy is too web services-oriented and not enough infrastructure metric-oriented.

Q:  You said that the “Adaptive Enterprise” is really an ESG and a Services initiative.  Would you say that Services are a critical and integral part of an Adaptive Enterprise initiative?

Peter:  Yes, because adaptive enterprise essentially says “Rip and Replace.”

Q:  Do you think that in the services group, HP has the Services necessary in this point in time to deliver on an AE promise?

Peter: For an individual project, yes?  Fortune 500 company transformation?  HP doesn’t have the capacity.

Q:  If you look outside of AE, do you see any other major initiatives going on at HP at this time?

Peter:  Yes, sometimes they blur together.  I do see that PSG and IPG are working a little bit better together.  “Better together” being one of your themes.  

Overall, the company wears so many different hats.  Carly has a whole different set of messages depending on who the audience is – and that’s of necessity.  If you’re talking consumer products you have one thing to say, if you’re talking to IT executives you have a second thing to say, if you’re talking to enterprise executives about the overall HP proposition, that’s a third set of messages.

Q:  Do you see that HP, over the last several months, has tried to integrate their brand and their message across the enterprise, SMB and consumer segments?  

Peter:  Sure, I see the Wall Street Journal ads just like everyone else.  But if I walk down the aisle in Best Buy, I see the Compaq brand and the HP brand with equal amounts of shelf space.  If I go to the HP website, I find Compaq pages interspersed with HP pages. And that’s Compaq, the former corporation, not just Compaq the brand.

Q:  If you look at HP as providing products and service to the enterprise, SMB and the consumer, do you see them as stronger or weaker in any of those markets?

Peter:  No.  The fact that ESG has new executive management after continued lack of profitability would suggest that things are not going right there.  There’s a new PSG, SMB market vice president.  PSG is barely profitable, so continuing efforts are needed there.  And IPG is certainly very profitable and executing pretty well, although I think that HP could definitely be doing more in the high-end printing area.  And I would say that the company is doing best in the consumer space, although not profitably.

Q:  How about in the mobility space?  Do you see HP as having a focus or an initiative around that, and do you see them as a viable player in that market?

Peter:  You mean HP, the company that was very late to market with Centrino-based notebooks?  Certainly, the company has products in this area, but no I don’t believe it has earned its stripes in Mobility as a lifestyle.

Q:  How about the area of digital imaging.  Do you see HP as focusing efforts on that?

Peter:  Yes, quite successfully.  HP is a leader in that space.

Q:  Are you familiar that in the enterprise market, HP has been pushing the message “Demand More?”

Peter:  Yes.  It’s in the ads.

Q:  What do you think HP is telling them to demand more of?

Peter:  Their IT.

Q: If I gave you four labels that could be attached to HP, I’d like you to tell me which one most appropriately describes the company as a whole today.  The four labels are: 1) a technology components company, 2) a technology solutions company, 3) a business solutions company, or 4) a services company.

Peter:  I think HP is a technology components company that is moving to technology solutions, the least believable is that it’s a business solutions company.  

Q:  If you think about your interactions with HP –not interactions with HP clients- but interactions with the company,  Has HP demonstrated to you that their customers have achieved business value from their relationship with HP? 

Peter:  No.  It’s not a subject in the briefings that I’ve had.

Q:  How about when you talk with HP’s customers of clients, do any of them talk about HP as creating business value for them?

Peter:  Yes, absolutely.  But HP’s problem today is that too much of its business comes from its installed base.  That would imply that HP is having troubles in generating new customers and value and related competitive issues would have to play a role in that, particularly at the business solution and technology solution level.  Boxes are boxes.  And HP doesn’t want to compete on a box level – nor should it want to.

Q:  If you think of HP at the enterprise level, and look at HP’s core delivery capabilities, how would you consider the companies capability to measure and maintain a link between business and IT?

Peter:  It would be spotty.  In some cases HP has done very good work, but I don’t view that as either a core competency or, more importantly, something that the company tries to do with every customer trying to build a measurable value chain that ties IT and its value back to the business benefits that are delivered by the IT.

Q:  How about HP’s ability to architect and integrate heterogeneous IT environments? 

Peter:  Adequate, but not world leading.  Simply for the reason that HP has modest skills in integrating with IBM mainframes which remain a measurable portion of big company IT infrastructures.  Clearly HP has a lot of expertise in Unix and a growing market share in Linux.  The company is underplaying the Microsoft market.

Q:  If we look in the SMB market segment, HP has been pushing a message entitled “Get More.”  Are you familiar with this message?

Peter:  Yes.

Q:  What is HP telling SMB clients to get more of?

Peter:  HP is telling their clients to get more from a relationship with HP, to demand more, and ultimately to use more.

Q:  In the SMB space, how would you rate HP’s portfolio of products that “work seamlessly together.”

Peter:  Across all geographies, all product lines, all solution sets and vertical markets?  That’s just no believable.  However, in the SMB space in IPG HP is doing some very good customer delivery and satisfaction type of things, but I’m not prepared for the global hug and a kiss.

Q: How about in the SMB segment HP’s ability to provide comprehensive services and support?

Peter:  It depends on the geography.  But it gets an okay grade.  However more can be done with partners when HP is not the delivering organization. 

Q:  Along those lines, how strong or broad to you think HP’s partner network is that serves the SMB market?

Peter:  It could be better.  Two points: 1) HP is not going to market with a combination of enterprise PC and imaging products.  It’s still 4 different stovepipes at the divisional level; and 2)  while there are specific initiatives around application solutions such as SAP, it get a B-.  That’s a very big opportunity for the company that is being underserved.

Q:  If you look at HP’s partner network in the SMB space, does HP do much to foster channel partner loyalty?

Peter:  It tries to buy channel partner loyalty, and that’s probably not enough.

Q:  Dropping down into the consumer space, can you share with me your thoughts on HP’s current capability around digital imaging and photography.  Do you think HP is recognized as a leader in that space?

Peter:  Yes and yes.  High grades there.

Q:  Do you think HP is going to suffer serious competition from the more traditional camera makers like Minolta, Olympus, Cannon and such?

Peter:  Well, HP is not a full-spectrum camera company.  HP doesn’t sell a camera with interchangeable lenses for instance.  But for consumers it has focused their offerings pretty well.  And on the whole printing and scanning side, HP is definitely a leader.

Q:  How about in the digital media moving into the digital media and entertainment space?

Peter:  High grades there.  We wrote it up and it’s on our website.

Q:  How about with the home PC space?

Peter:  HP has not adequately differentiated the Compaq and the HP brands in the retail channel.  Therefore you have two essentially competing brands so marketing dollars are not as effective as they might be, especially at a time when Dell continues to ruthlessly focuses on their own go-to-market proposition.

Q:  If you look at HP in the consumer segment, being focused on connectivity and end-to-end processing, the “radical simplicity” concept of easily bringing a image from camera to the printer, as an example.  Do you think that HP is the leader in the area of connectivity and end-to-end process simplicity for consumers?

Peter:  Not yet.  We’ve done research that HP was involved with for the Internet Home Alliance that would suggest that a lot more customer-faced interaction is necessary to get that across.  But, that at the Big Bang launch, HP has a program with Microsoft to do in-store demos, so over time and in the US, you’ll get consumers to connect the dots better.

Also, HP is dropping the ball by waiting for the home networked video market to evolve, so instead of being a potential leader, it’s going to let other companies set the standards.

Q:  Who do you see as the companies setting those standards?

Sony for one, but other point companies like Magic.

Q:  In the consumer space, how do you rate HP’s service and support?

It’s only mediocre.  And that certainly also echoed by the big PC magazine survey as well.  Basically HP’s PCs break too often.
