Project eLiza Positioning and Messaging Workshop 
Project and Report Overview
IBM is developing a strategy that focuses on a self-managing and self-healing architecture.  The benefit and impact of the technologies behind this strategy will be tremendous and will broaden IBM's appeal to both current and potential new customers.  In order to maximize this positive impact, IBM needs to bring a succinct, clear message and position to the market.  
As one component of the development and clarification of this positioning and messaging, IBM engaged Aberdeen Group to conduct a Positioning and Messaging Strategy Workshop.  This workshop was held in Somers on May 16, 2001.  In this workshop, Aberdeen Group listened to how IBM is extending Project eLiza messaging beyond what was delivered at the IBM E-server Analyst Symposium earlier this spring.  Aberdeen provided interactive feedback in the form of key high-level messages that:
Fit within an overall vision of computing that resonates with key decision makers; and
Cut across three major classes of stakeholders or audiences: line of business executives, Information Technology (IT) management and staff, and end-users of computing services within and without the enterprise.
This report builds on and amplifies Aberdeen’s feedback at the meeting, provides a structure for the messaging recommendations, and incorporates IBM feedback and meeting discussion.
Report Assumptions
Certain assumptions and assertions about IBM market strengths and fundamental server strategies underlie any more detailed discussion of positioning and messaging.  This section serves to make such assumptions explicit.  It also serves as a reminder of points that may often be taken for granted within IBM but not by others.  Communicating — with proof points — these assumptions is a component of broader positioning and messaging.
IBM is a leader in systems management today and eLiza positioning will reflect starting from a position of strength rather than from a standing start.
eLiza is NOT equivalent to or a synonym for e-servers.  eLiza is a server management strategy — not a server strategy.
eLiza is not IBM server-specific.  eLiza will manage customer environments — including solutions from both IBM and others.  IBM’s goal is to have eLiza be best supported and first supported on IBM e-servers — but not only supported.
eLiza self-management is transparent to developers.  Third-party software will not require modification using a whole new set of API’s in order to take advantage of eLiza’s capabilities.
eLiza is products — not just a concept or an internal IBM engineering rallying point.  
A High-level Computing Vision
At the highest level, IBM needs to paint a picture of the role of IT in business that hooks in senior (CxO-level) customer management.  The following five observations about the role of computers in today’s businesses — especially but not limited to e-Businesses — point to the need for business executives to consider their IT strategy and architecture as a fundamental component of their overall business strategy.
Computers are running the business in a fundamental way — and without (working) computers a business will largely cease to operate.  This point is most true in a pure e-Business context — such as amazon.com — but applies in other contexts as well (e.g., airline reservations).  
The real-time enterprise has arrived — enabled by compute tools and decision support.  For example, achieving the long-time promise of just-in-time manufacturing requires advanced end-to-end automated supply chain systems.  The successful implementers of technology for real-time enterprises gain competitive advantage through faster inventory turns and more nimble decision-making. 
Manual backup procedures are increasingly unable to deal with computer failures.  Even those businesses that do not consider themselves e-Businesses — such as grocery chains — find that manual backups are largely ineffective and, consequently, failures of their computerized systems have a direct and catastrophic effect on customer service levels.  Thus, the computer system is the business system.
The computer system is the total instantiation of the business process.  The IT architecture implicitly defines the time, place, and manner that employees interact with each other and with customers or suppliers, the level of access to different types of information within and without the enterprise, and the service goals of the organization.  The implication is that business processes, by definition, demand management.
In five to ten years, one of the big paybacks will be computers managing computers and running business processes automatically.  People will be taken out of the loop and employees will focus on exceptions rather than routine, repetitive tasks.  Lower labor costs, better control, and faster responsiveness (i.e., real-time) will drive this trend.
Expanding further on the last point, there are significant opportunities for automation even just within the domain of exception handling.  Knowledge workers often follow a process that goes something like: discover exception; query the cause of the exception; make a decision; transact to change things; and report/collaborate.   The self-management of computer systems goes beyond simple exception reporting that only makes the discovery of exceptions easier.  Rather, self-management properly extends to performing all process steps — subject to pre-set policies.  
The goal is not to send a wireless page to the system administrator that something is wrong — the level of operational innovation envisioned by most wireless system administration today.  Rather, the goal is to use pre-set policies that send wireless pages such as:
There was a problem and it has been fixed. 
There is a problem.  Approve the implementation of a default procedure.
There is a problem for which you have the opportunity to make a decision that effectuates a change.  Would you like to take one or more actions?
Or — don’t bother with the wireless page at all because pre-set policies have established an autonomous course of action for which no priority approval or notification is required.

Additional Findings, Implications, and Recommendations 
In addition to the three messages for three classes of stakeholders described in the previous section, Aberdeen makes the following findings and recommendations regarding eLiza, its positioning, and its messaging.
Set clear parameters around eLiza including the establishment of specific, quantifiable “stretch” goals (e.g., reducing management costs by 90%), milestones, and proof points.  Lay out the program in IBM-statement-of-direction style.
There is a compelling need to brand eLiza.  In addition to a clear identity at a non-technical level, branding implies a logo (e.g., eLiza-enabled).  Think about what Budweiser did for iguanas (and Budweiser…).
A key part of the process is defining what and when eLiza is.  eLiza is ultimately “things” as well as a concept.  eLiza does not exist today — although IBM has leading management products today.  At some point, a first phase of eLiza must come into being.  Part of the process is defining the parameters of this first physical instantiation of eLiza and its scope vis-à-vis existing IBM products.
The working groups are a means to an end.  Strategy and overall planning should drive the creation and structure of working groups — not the other way around.  The bottom line is the work that needs to be done — not the manner in which the work is parceled out to working groups with specific names.  
A sub-text to the eLiza theme should be “this is not business as usual.”  This sub-text is a CEO/CIO-level part of the pitch and must be matched by dramatic proof points — such as so reducing the number of management keystrokes that one operator can manage 10 times more servers.  Avoid positioning “eLiza as great technology” (even though it is).
The eLiza message to developers should emphasize the masking of complexity — i.e., eLiza is not about developers mastering a thousand more API’s.  A significant eLiza function — from the developer point of view — is to act as a “super operating system” that transparently provides a reliable foundation for high-level software services.
eLiza is about both e-Business and other types of business.  The assertion that e-Business is just a more difficult aspect of the general case — i.e., if you can do e-Business, you can do anything — may be largely true but it will get lost in translation on the way to customers.  Customers with more traditional businesses may just tune out if they hear an e-Business message.
Service providers can generally be thought of as being today where enterprises are going — from an IT architecture perspective — but there are twists.  Enterprises are typically more heterogeneous with a much greater legacy integration component.  Service providers have unique issues especially in the need to physically separate customer workloads to a degree and in a manner acceptable to their customers — whether or not other approaches are “better” in the abstract.
IBM needs to articulate proof points for the specific areas of eLiza where the company has the strongest leadership position.
Proof points are also a key to selling CxOs on the ROI (return on investment) improvements inherent in an eLiza managed enterprise.
Make sure the “eLiza is heterogeneous” message is not confused with the “eLiza is based on standards” message.  The heterogeneous message will have great pull with executives while standards are a ho-hum.
An umbrella messaging opportunity exists with other IBM initiatives: Pervasive Computing + Real-time Computing (discussed above) demands Managed Computing (i.e., eLiza).
For 30 years, we haven’t allowed the science of MIS to optimize the enterprise.  “We’ve always done it this way” remains pervasive.  Put another way, automating a mess yields an automated mess.  Moving to more efficient, highly automated enterprises mandates doing new things rather than optimizing bad processes.  IBM can add key value here through the intersection of eLiza and best practices from services — self-management of best practices.
