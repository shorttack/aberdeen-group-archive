1994 Worldwide Commercial Multiuser RISC/Unix Market $10B (Source: Aberdeen Group, February 1995)

IBM Hardware Line-up:
RS/6000 Servers:
- Second largest supplier of multiuser RISC/UNIX systems -- 19% market share
- 52% revenue increase '93/94
- 1994 revenues = $2 billion
- Transitioning from uniprocessor Power2 (RIOS) to SMP PowerPC 601 servers
- IBM-first strategy: frequently offer DB2/6000 at considerable cost savings with platform
- Value Proposition: IBM's professional services orgs help customers implement RISC/UNIX-based open client-server computing

SP2:
- Up to 512 Power2 processors each running AIX 4.x
- 1994 revenues = $200 million
- 370 SP1 and 2 systems installed; 70 SP2s for commercial applications
- Positioned as high-end of RS/6000 line and DB/2 offload for ES/9000
- Major commercial applications: Complex Decision Support, LAN server consolidation, OLTP
- 380 ISV promises to port RDBMSs and applications
- Futures: SMP Nodes and RIOS2 upgrades
- Value Proposition: IBM's entry into very large scale, open systems computing

IBM Strengths: Size (global support, range of products, talent pool), customer base and sales knowledge, services (planning/implementation/operations/outsourcing/financing), third-party support (ISVs by 1000s, PCM/peripheral suppliers, professional service orgs e.g. Andersen Consulting)

IBM Challenges: PowerPC 600 will not be leading the industry for many years. Re-energizing field org. Gaining trust of leading-edge buyers. Learning to work with ISVs as allies. IBM's chief competition for IS executive allegiance is not other hardware suppliers (HP) but software solution suppliers (Oracle, SAP, Microsoft).

Points-You-Should-Know: IBM RS/6000: Customers buy for support and historical relationship. Platform-of-choice for VARs whose customers can't afford AS/400. RDBMS ISVs fear IBM will switch customers to low-priced DB2/6000. Expect growth in 1995 as datacenter managers experiment with Tier-2 surround strategies. RS/6000 with DB2 is neither fastest nor least expensive. Whole story includes NetView, Data Propagator, DataHub.

HP Hardware Line-up:
HP 9000: Multi-user RISC/UNIX industry leader -- 45% share; 60% revenue growth last 3 quarters; 1994 revenues = $4.5 billion; leading platform for Unix in terms of technology and ISV support.
HP 3000: Legacy MPE/IX system on HP PA-RISC hardware.

HP Strengths: Trusted supplier for transition to high-performance UNIX. Supported by ISVs and professional services. Global support. Technology leadership (RISC processors, complete Unix, OpenView). PSO knows Unix thoroughly.

HP Challenges: Lean org means opportunities slip by. Making transformation from hot-box to incumbent supplier. PA-7200 not fastest chip. No very high-end RISC/UNIX offering (clusters, MPP). Being squeezed by Intel/NT at low end. After 3 years as Top Gun, HP is getting arrogant, sloppy.

Points-You-Should-Know: HP is mainframe alternative leader. HP's most common hardware competitors in new deals: IBM and Sun. HP CSO has separate sales force from CPO causing confusion. Consistently ranked most respected. Should be respected but not feared.

Digital Hardware Line-up:
Digital ALPHAserver XX00: Multi-user RISC/UNIX 64-bit -- 3% market share; 150% revenue growth 1994; ~$2.5 billion with all add-ons; Value: Faster processors for vast memory/disk.
Digital VAX: Legacy VMS -> OpenVMS; VAX revenues declining 35-50% per annum; <10% of company revenues; Hold base while selling evolution to OpenVMS and ALPHA AXP.

Digital Strengths: Alpha performance + aggressive pricing = best price/performance in industry. Digital's UNIX (OSF/1) meets more standards specs than any other Unix. Will deliver enterprise-class open platforms with Microsoft NT on Alpha. Software frameworks for enterprise-wide open computing from desktop to datacenter.

Digital Challenges: Not yet out of restructuring woods -- customers concerned about who will sell/support them. Has yet to establish critical mass for long-term viability. Alpha/NT looks rosy but lacks 1995 revenues to sustain.

Points-You-Should-Know: Diligence and resolve regaining respect. Excellent engineering. Large In-Memory Databases (LIMD) could revolutionize enterprise OLTP and DSS. Digital and Oracle increasing strategic commitments -- Oracle acquires RDB and installed base; Oracle is the LIMD database.

Sun Hardware Line-up:
SPARCcenter 2000 and SPARCserver 1000: Multi-user RISC/UNIX - 10% market share; ~$1.1B revenues (~20% of Sun); SPARCcenter 2-20 processors; SPARCserver 2-8 processors; challenging IBM for #2 with ISVs; Value Proposition: open systems price leader for high-risk, high-reward solutions.

Sun Strengths: Originator and leader in open systems client-server marketplace. Large number of software applications. Innovator in networked solutions. Positive VAR/distributor relationships for lower costs. Strong ISV relationships.

Sun Challenges: Limited direct field support. Very few references for SPARCcenter 2000/SPARCserver 1000 in business-critical OLTP. OS transitions historically problematic; Solaris problems in 1994. Real-world scalability very poor on all Sun workstations/servers. Sun not a major player in new standards groups; OpenLook GUI may be excluded.

AT&T Global Information Solutions (i.e. NCR):
- 1994 total revenues ~$4.2 billion; flat for several years; 1% profit (1994)
- Financial backing of AT&T ($75B revenues) but could be spun out
- Strategy: open systems from desktop to MPP; Globalyst (PC to departmental) and Teradata (decision support)
- All servers based on Unix and Intel 486/Pentium -- no RISC server
- Unix System V.4 MP OS with RAS enhancements
- Low-end also runs OS/2, SCO Unix, NetWare, Windows NT; PCI bus; full peripherals including RAID; LifeKeeper HA clustering

AT&T GIS Strengths: Excellent server breadth; good/leading TPC price/performance; AT&T backing; bullet-proof Unix; Intel multiprocessor champion ($1.2B 1994); retail/banking market strength.

AT&T GIS Challenges: Leader in Intel/Unix but losing to RISC providers and Compaq. Intel depending on AT&T GIS for P6-based enterprise servers. Lacks fire to gain market share. Loyal customer base. Failure to properly manage Teradata caused backlash and opened DSS opportunities.
