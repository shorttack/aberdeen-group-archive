Client-Server Architecture Best Practices
- As many as 4 tiers of computing: Desktop, branch/region, enterprise
- Separate tiers for OLTP and DSS: Avoid the "query from hell" impact on OLTP
- Need for central management; Need for data migration and transformation

[Slide 8]
Symmetric Multiprocessing
- Sweet spot in the market: scalability; higher profit margins for hardware & software; highly competitive
- SMP is alive and well: Was dead at 16 processors; New hardware and software give SMP new life; Will still be maturing in 5 years

[Slide 9]
Clustering
- Very mature on Unix
- Typically used for high availability, particularly in manufacturing and telecommunications
- Windows NT clustering will emerge in 1996, thrive in 1997 (Compaq + Tandem; DEC)
- Best applications with Online XPS: High-availability transaction processing; Warehouse scalability after topping out SMP

[Slide 10]
Parallel-Scalable (i.e., MPP)
- The heights of scalability -- Needed for large warehouses
- Driven by IBM with PowerParallel SP (e.g. SP2)
- AT&T GIS is focused on making profits while retaining customer base -- a problem partner in '96
- High end OLTP; Server consolidation
- An attack market for Informix with XPS

Systems & Platforms
- 1996 is the year of the application server

CSS Battle Lines

[Slide 16]
Database-centric or Application-centric
CSS Supplier Architecture Orientations
Database-centric (mainly VARs): An application designed for one database; Performance "optimized" for one database; Gateway access to other databases
Application-centric (mainly ISVs): for a heterogeneous database environment; "optimized" for select number of databases; Native ANSI SQL drivers and Gateways
Source: AberdeenGroup, 1995

[Slide 17]
Toolsets are Making a Difference
Home-Grown: Many suppliers have built their own; Perceived lack of available functionality; Majority are CASE-based (i.e., ABAP from SAP)
Industry Standard: A growing percentage of newer suppliers; Majority are C, Visual C++ and Visual Basic combos; Few Gupta, Uniface, PowerBuilder; Fewer use RDBMS supplied toolsets

[Slide 18]
Channel Mix
Customers want direct -- Suppliers can't afford it
Direct: Expensive; High SGA-Impact; Major System Suppliers are pulling back
Indirect: Outward burden-shifting; Low & Mid Customers buy-through channels; Resellers with highly-variable (outdated?) expertise

[Slide 19]
Who Gets the Midrange Market? - Unix
- Dominant OS for RISC and open systems suppliers
- Major variants include HP-UX, IBM's AIX, SVR4, Solaris
- Enormous historical burden from the academic community. Only in 1990s has Unix emerged as practical and reliable for commercial applications
- From buyer's standpoint, every Unix is different, often subtly

[Slide 20]
Who Gets the Midrange Market? - Windows NT Server
- Fast-growing OS-of-choice for PC LANs, workgroup servers
- Has the look and feel of Windows; Runs almost all Windows programs
- Hardened, security, multiprocessors
- Versions for Intel (90%), Alpha, MIPS, PowerPC
- In 1995, Windows NT scales to handle department/divisional loads
- NT price/performance is giving Unix suppliers a real scare

[Slide 24]
CSS Market Characteristics
Company Size & Revenue Dictate Requirements:
- $25 Million and less: $500 to $5,000 per module; PC LAN-based; Primarily VARs
- $250 Million and less: $10,000 to $75,000 per module; Fortune 1000 -- time-share surrounded by client-server; Mostly direct
- $251 Million and up: $50,000 to $150,000 per module; Fortune 500 -- legacy leveraged by client-server; Direct

Fortune 1000: Human Resources 1-3 months; Finance & Accounting 3-12 months; Manufacturing & Distribution 9-24 months
Small Business: Human Resources 0 months; Finance & Accounting 1-2 months; Manufacturing & Distribution 2-6 months
