## **Executive Summary** 

his report analyzes IT budget and spending trends. Many organizations manage their IT infrastructure as a cost center, separating their IT investment from inT vestments in production capabilities. As a result, the fiscal discipline that the organization uses to manage its investment in production capacity, which could also enhance the productivity of the IT infrastructure, is rarely applied, and the consequent benefits are rarely realized. Most organizations have effective investment and cost mechanisms in place for facilities directly affecting production, but in very few cases are these mechanisms applied to the organization’s computing resource. 

## **Key Business Value Findings** 

Surprisingly, while most organizations focus on controlling IT infrastructure cost, few organizations have an effective cost control regimen for their IT resource. They curtail investment, reduce staff, expect productivity increases, presume successful consolidation, or explore outsourcing or off-shoring, and yet do little to effectively understand either their IT infrastructure’s current cost structure or the end user’s actual requirements and priorities for IT support. 

## **Implications & Analysis** 

As a result, enterprises generally sub-optimize their IT investment. Existing capabilities are under-utilized, cost trade-offs unwittingly sacrifice important user requirements, and when investments are made they miss the mark. Such factors leave Aberdeen to conservatively estimate that _**industry is wasting between 15 and 25 percent of its IT investment.**_ 

## **Recommendations for Action** 

- Adopt service-level objectives as a mechanism for IT cost recovery and funding. 

- Benchmark IT efficiency to develop a better understanding of the IT cost structure. 

- Explore strategic outsourcing to off-load inefficient or low-value-add IT operations. 

- Adopt consistent, firm-wide, business relevant financial metrics to gauge IT costs and value. 

- Centralize IT spending under joint governance led by end user organizations. 

- Consolidate data centers; reduce footprints through partitioning, grid, or utility computing technology; consolidate workloads to reduce systems management, security, and operational overhead; and reduce the number of instances of applications or data items.

## _Chapter One:_ **Issue at Hand** 

- Cost pressures and user demand for new function can be simultaneously resolved with consistent business-relevant, firm-wide financial metrics for IT performance, linked to service-level-based cost recovery 

- • Consolidation (footprint, image, workload, and data) can substantially improve IT efficiency 

• Outsourcing makes sense in a context of well-understood IT costs and end user priorities 

his year saw continued cost pressures on IT budgets. Few organizations report IT spending increases, while many foresee cuts. IT spending as a percent of revenue, T while varying dramatically by industry sector, is generally flat, with spending on information security growing as a proportion of total IT spend – only because other areas are experiencing cuts. 

In response, many companies are considering a range of measures to control cost. Often outsourcing may provide efficient handling of some cost elements. But outsourcing in itself can mask problems rather than solve them. Consolidation can reduce cost, assuming that applications environments being consolidated are the right ones, and are properly aligned to begin with. 

Cost pressures can represent more than financial challenges. Effective CIOs use cost controls to enable transformation, to accelerate consolidation, and to re-align IT with critical business strategic initiatives. 

Cost pressure on IT is not new. The CIO of 

one global manufacturing firm remarked that in his 20+ years in the industry he could not recall a time when users were generally content with existing services and IT budgets were unconstrained. The core issue is to ensure that IT resources are managed to meet business requirements efficiently (cost) and effectively (alignment). Forces that tend to drive costs out of control (to be investigated in further work) include organizational overhead, hardware and software terms and conditions, changing workload mix, user reprioritization, vendor enticements, and muddled strategies. In the face of these forces, outsourcing can be a tempting escape route. Yet even outsourcing can fail to achieve its goals without proper oversight and planning. 

The highest pressure CIOs face overall is users requiring new function (Figure 1). This result, an average of responses across all groups, is the right place to start to understand IT budget challenges. However, there are profound differences between classes of users and how that pressure is recognized and managed. Too often organizations react to requests for new function as disruptions to the existing plan. Too often organizations believe that if their operational budget plan is accepted the IT organization is aligned with the rest of the business. Too often these pressures are seen as conflicting and unmanageable. And too often the IT organization is not aligned. Overall, strategic alignment is the fifth priority item.

## **While IT organizations recognize that business requirements must come first, their highest priority actions are aligned not with meeting customer requirements but with controlling cost** 

The highest priority pressure enterprises report is demand for new capabilities. However responding to demand for new capabilities requires a robust and flexible IT infrastructure, along with continuing involvement with the business strategic planning processes. This should suggest that the IT project office or some analogous function should be also a high priority, since it is the component of the IT organization responsible for maintaining a presence in the strategic planning activities. However the IT project office is placed behind three activities, all cost-focused. 

While it is possible to interpret these data to suggest that the IT organization is giving lip service to the pressure of customer requirements, a more careful reading of the data, supplemented by interviews with selected survey respondents, suggests that the disconnect is a serious and on-going dysfunctional response to that pressure. That is, the business expects the IT organization to manage its internal response to business pressures in the same way that other operational areas do; however the reality is that most IT organizations do not have the process maturity to deploy a comprehensive and effective response to the twin needs of the business: satisfy customers and control costs. 

What is more striking is the shift in priorities among different populations as the data are sorted by best-in-class vs. the rest. In best-in-class firms, the actions, capabilities, and enablers are more closely aligned with responding to customer demand for new or enhanced functions. The difference is the degree of alignment within the IT organization: best-inclass IT organizations are distinctly effective in investing proportionately to support customer needs (Figure 3).

Best-in-class firms prioritize the pressures they face quite differently from the rest. While most identify customer requirements as the largest pressure, for best-in-class firms that is in third place, behind sustaining IT alignment and watching expenses. This is not because Best in Class ignore customer requirements, but because they understand that by sustaining IT alignment they will be sure to meet the most crucial customer requirements as a normal part of doing business. Those instances where customer requirements arise - that are not aligned with overall business strategies will be rare. Also, the Best in Class see inadequate expertise (regarding IT budgeting) as a much lower priority concern than the rest. This again is not because best in class don’t recognize this as a problem; rather, it is directly because best-in-class firms have solved that problem. 

The lowest priority pressure is the erosion in value of aging technology. This is a key element of the IT life cycle, however little overt attention is paid to it. This fits oddly with the highest priority capability – centralized IT asset management. It is as though bits and pieces of effective cost control are assembled haphazardly alongside necessary elements of effective business support. 

The differences between best-in-class firms and the rest appear across all industry sectors and all sizes of enterprise. This suggests that IT leadership is generally approaching the challenges in a piecemeal and uncoordinated fashion. There are significant opportunities to improve.

## **Outsourcing and off-shoring may not resolve cost or customer issues** 

Outsourcing is a tempting alternative if an IT organization seems to be unable to control its costs effectively. By determining which elements of the IT suite of capabilities are not strategic, the conventional wisdom goes, the IT organization can avail itself of the optimal cost service provider for those non-crucial services while focusing scarce resources on the most sensitive activities. This should allow the firm to build a barrier to entry by rapidly deploying crucial capabilities. 

In some cases this situation is realized. But in other cases, the additional cost of oversight (within the outsourced organization) and the requirement for profitability (in the outsourcing organization) mean that the actual total cost is greater than the optimal cost for providing IT services within one organization. There is an optimal size for each instance of IT service delivery, but (as the recent J P Morgan Chase/IBM situation revealed) it may be within the reach of large organizations. 

In many cases outsourcing is attractive not because it is lower in cost than the current IT operation, but because the cost of the current IT operation is not known or understood by the enterprise. A fixed price, even if higher than the current expense, may be preferable to simply not knowing. 

Similarly, outsourcing sometimes promises better customer satisfaction than the current IT organization can achieve. However, the reality is too often that the IT organization does not know what its own customers actually want and need to fulfill their mission. Again, the cost of achieving a measurable customer satisfaction level, even if greater, may be preferable to not actually knowing if the end users are satisfied. In this discussion, we are using customer satisfaction in the formal sense of measurable conformance to requirements. By not knowing how satisfied customers are, the IT organization may be hard-pressed to explain how well aligned it is with its customers.

## **Laggard Steps to Success** 

_1. Initiate consistent, regular, timely, firm-wide, customer-relevant measurement of IT spending and value_ 

Eliminating local or IT-focused measurements is not the goal. IT still needs to measure internal parameters such as bandwidth utilization or storage capacity to learn when a resource is low. Internal IT-centric measures should _not_ be used for calibrating customer service levels or gauging customer satisfaction and alignment, or for cost recovery. Effective measures must be developed with the end user.

_3. Deploy cost recovery schemes based on customer satisfaction_ 

The level of customer satisfaction should determine the rate at which funding is granted to the IT organization. At this point, the firm needs to consider whether the IT organization should become a profit center. As a cost center, funded from a flat allocation, there is no true business incentive to remain aligned or even focused on customer requirements. As a profit center, the IT organization can over-recover costs for the following purposes: investment in IT infrastructure projects too broad for any user department to fund, possible rebates to user organizations that do not receive required service-level-based goals, and separation of general overhead activities (regulatory compliance initiatives, project office activities, information security spend, disaster recovery planning) that are not attributable to any one line of business.

## **Industry Norm Steps to Success** 

## _1. Consolidate IT activities_ 

Consolidation has four levels: footprint, image, workload, and data/applications. Footprint consolidation means bringing multiple machines into one location, saving on the cost of physical facilities. Image consolidation means displacing multiple smaller machines and running the workloads on partitions in a single larger machine. The benefit comes from having to manage fewer physical machines. Workload consolidation means aggregating multiple workloads into one larger image. The benefit comes from having fewer instances of systems management tools and operational interfaces. The cost is making sure that the workloads are compatible. This can involve renaming files and programs, and developing consistent operational procedures. Finally, applications and data consolidation means reducing the variety and number of instances of a particular application or data item. The cost involves winnowing out multiple redundant data items and selecting one application vendor to run the business. The benefit is radically simplified maintenance, operational simplification, and responsiveness to changing business conditions (flexibility or more generally alignment). 

## _2. Adopt standards-based IT budget processes_ 

Any exceptions from the standard evaluation and purchasing methodology must be carefully scrutinized. If this critical component of the IT lifecycle can be brought under control, consistent and effective measurements will follow across the rest of the lifecycle. As long as subordinate organizations can locally optimize their purchase decisions, the larger organization will suffer complexity, inconsistent data, and structural uncertainty regarding its true budget effectiveness. 

_3. Deploy service-level based cost recovery_ 

The elements of alignment are two: develop customer-centric, business-relevant metrics, and then sell those services at the price the customer is willing to pay. Improvements in service should be funded by the end users, since they are in the best position to value those services. By disintermediating the management hierarchy, the It organization working with the customer base can best jointly define, price, and deliver the required level of support. 

## **Best in Class Next Steps** 

_1. Mange the IT organization as a company within a company_ 

While few CIOs see themselves as subject to competition in a conventional sense, a broader definition demonstrates that IT organizations are subject to competitive forces, in the sense of Michael Porter’s Five Forces established in Competitive Strategy (Porter, 1998). End users may solve problems for themselves; vendors can reach through the IT organization to offer solutions directly to end users; substitutes such as ASPs and MSSPs can displace conventionally delivered IT services; as can alternative solutions such as appliances. By viewing these broader sources of competitive force clearly, the CIO can effectively manage their tendency to drive the IT organization out of alignment with the firm’s wider business goals.

These terms are defined as follows: _Pressures_ _**—**_ external forces that impact an organization’s market position, competitiveness, or business operations (e.g., economic, political and regulatory, technology, changing customer preferences, competitive) _Actions_ _**—**_ the strategic approaches that an organization takes in response to industry pressures (e.g., align the corporate business model to leverage industry opportunities, such as product/service strategy, target markets, financial strategy, go-to-market, and sales strategy) _Capabilities_ _**—**_ the business process competencies required to execute corporate strategy (e.g., skilled people, brand, market positioning, viable products/services, ecosystem partners, financing) _Enablers_ — the key functionality of technology solutions required to support the organization’s enabling business practices (e.g., development platform, applications, network connectivity, user interface, training and support, partner interfaces, data cleansing, and management)
