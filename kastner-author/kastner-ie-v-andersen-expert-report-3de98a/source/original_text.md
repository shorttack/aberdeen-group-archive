Executive Summary 
The litigation between Intelligent Electronics (IE) and Andersen Consulting is a complex 
matter that should be viewed from the standpoints of the market place, the capabilities of 
IE’s management and information technology (IT) staff, the capabilities brought by 
Andersen Consulting, and the maturity of various IT technologies in the mid-1990s.  After 
carefully considering these different standpoints based on my experience and the ample 
evidence in the case record, I have developed a theory of the case that is amplified in this 
report. 
Theory of the Case 
Andersen was asked by IE to proceed with the utmost speed. 
From the late-1980s to the mid-1990s, IE was a major participant in the personal 
computer (PC) distribution industry, an industry undergoing fundamental structural 
changes.  These changes that started in the 1994 time period ended IE’s ability to operate 
as an aggregator of PC equipment orders to a reseller network of largely captive 
franchisees.  Rather suddenly, IE had to compete for its reseller business based on service, 
speed of delivery, inventory availability, and numerous other “customer-centric” factors at 
which IE had not previously had to excel.  IE management believed that the only way to 
prosper by the late 1990s would be to “get big fast” — to triple in size from over $3 billion 
in 1993 to $10 billion in revenues in 1999.  At the heart of IE’s ability to transform itself 
would be state-of-the-industry IT systems that would be a competitive advantage to IE.  
But IE had under-invested in IT infrastructure during the early 1990s, so its systems were 
behind the competition.  This business scenario forced IE to ask its selected IT system 
integrator, Andersen Consulting, to proceed with all possible speed to build the IT 
infrastructure that would enable IE to compete successfully on service and availability as 
well as price.  Time was of the essence.

Page 11
IE wanted to embark upon leading-edge application deployments as part of its market 
positioning to its customers, suppliers, and shareholders. 
IE’s reseller customers were also largely in the PC distribution business.  These buyers 
were technology-savvy and liked new uses for the products that they sold to end-users.  
Therefore, IE had a strong motive to market its new IT systems vision to its customers, to 
“eat our own dog food” in the vernacular.  IE also had a reason to gain favor with its 
suppliers, large IT companies such as Apple Computer, IBM, Compaq, and Hewlett-
Packard.  Contracts with these suppliers were negotiable annually, and there were literally 
millions of extra marketing and distribution program dollars available to favored 
distribution-channel partners.  IE, for example, was distributor for IBM’s Eduquest 
program to the education market.  Therefore, IE had a strong incentive to impress its 
suppliers with its internal systems technology and IE’s ability to strongly support the 
resellers in the channel.  Lastly, IE had obligations to its stockholders to invest in leading-
edge IT infrastructure that would allow it to be a low-cost PC distributor.  Importantly, IE 
had limited cash resources and high cash requirements.  Now that a high value of IE's 
stock was critical in being able to negotiate favorable stock-based mergers with other 
industry players as one option for rapidly achieving $10 billion in size.  I believe advanced 
IT systems became critically important to IE’s management for more than just the mere 
transaction and record-keeping capabilities of the technology.  IE was looking for an IT 
competitive weapon to enhance its position in the market. 
IE did not have the capabilities and core competencies required to either manage IT 
operations or to deploy new applications. 
Although IE wanted and needed new information technology, in the end, the company’s 
management and staff just did not succeed at executing IT projects.  The record shows 
major failures by IE to implement IT projects successfully before Andersen arrived and 
after Andersen departed.  In fact, Andersen helped deliver two of a painfully small number 
of IT successes during 1993-1997.  In spite of the IT-related experience of IE’s 
management and Board, the company never really recovered from under-investments in 
the 1991-1993 period, nor did it have the IT staff numbers or depth needed to implement 

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 12
management’s get-big-fast-with-IT strategy.  Exacerbating the situation for IE was a 
decentralized management team with widely divergent views of where and how the 
company’s IT objectives should be achieved.  The team did not work cohesively, and 
Andersen was unable to overcome divided internal IE management to achieve its 
engagement objectives. 
IE made a strategic business error in attempting to move into the reseller business as 
opposed to becoming a more broad-based distributor. 
IE grew from 1982 to the mid-1990s primarily by supporting an ever increasing number 
of PC resellers, becoming by 1993 the “aggregator” for the complete product lines of the 
major PC companies such as Apple, Compaq, Hewlett-Packard, and IBM.  When “open 
sourcing” became the norm in 1994 and 1995, IE lost the ability to maintain captive 
resellers.  IE chose to react to this fundamental business change by acquiring more 
resellers and moving closer to the ultimate PC end-user and buyer while maintaining a 
narrow base of products to resell.  In retrospect, the successful survivors of the PC 
distribution industry shakeout of the late 1990s were those who became adept at broad-
based product distribution, the so-called full-line distributors.  IE chose the wrong path at 
the wrong time, and did not have the resources in the end to recover.  Note that none of 
this point has anything to do with an information technology implementation project as 
Andersen embarked upon with IE. 
Andersen performed its responsibilities in a reasonable and competent manner. 
When all the disputes are analyzed, my conclusion is that Andersen acted reasonably and 
competently during the IT strategy project and performing its tasks during the 
implementation phase program called IE/2000.  Given the time-is-of-the-essence focus 
from the first point above, Andersen commenced multiple IE/2000 projects in parallel.  IE 
was not prepared to dedicate the necessary management and IT staff needed to keep the 
projects moving at full speed, so Andersen began “backfilling” its own personnel to 
substitute for the unavailable (or not even hired) IE personnel.  And individual business 
process owners at IE began demanding additional functionality beyond what was 

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 13
originally planned in the IT strategy.  This increased the program’s scope without 
consequential change to planned budgets or schedules.  While Andersen did not follow the 
letter of its own internal project management methodologies, the evidence is that Andersen 
worked diligently and competently to fulfill the needs of its client, IE.  And Andersen 
provided voluminous project management documentation and used one of the industry’s 
first customer quality management programs to IE’s benefit. 
IE was the owner and overall systems integrator of the IE/2000 program. 
The Consulting Systems Agreement (CSA) between the parties calls for a time and 
materials (T&M) basis for Andersen’s compensation, with IE acting as the prime systems 
integrator for the IE/2000 program.  Prior to approval of the CSA in early 1995, 
Andersen had performed services for six months on a month-to-month T&M basis, 
meaning that IE could terminate Andersen at any month-end.  In a T&M contract, it is 
computer industry practice for the contractor to follow the instructions and priorities of its 
client.  In my experience, such situations are common in the industry, and in particular 
where an IT implementation is only one part of a larger business transformation.  In IE’s 
case, it was a major paperless warehouse initiative in Denver and Memphis that would 
use pieces of the IE/2000 program.  Given this situation, it makes sense that IE would be 
in overall control of its business initiatives such as building new warehouses, with 
Andersen following IE’s lead.  Moreover, Andersen gave IE the option for T&M, fixed 
price, and “gain sharing” payment schemes when the IT strategy report was presented, 
along with corresponding management responsibility requirements.  IE chose the T&M 
payment arrangement that also required it to own management control. 
I believe the evidence supports a theory of the case based on the above six factors.  Using 
this theory, I have examined thirteen major assertions in the case, detailed my findings as 
an expert, and reached opinions and conclusions in each of these areas.

Findings and Conclusions Regarding Assertions by Plaintiff 
Assertion: Andersen should have recommended upgrading MSD instead suggesting the 
interim solution, Option 2. 
MSD, IE’s highly modified and largely undocumented order management system based 
on a software package from MTI, was in a poor state of construction and disrepair.  It 
would have required an enormous business risk to build a new, industry-leading IT 
system for IE on such a poor foundation.  The reasons against going forward with MSD 
were thoroughly discussed with IE management in the IT Assessment section of the IT 
strategy report in March and May 1994.  I have concluded that the options presented to 
IE management were appropriate, and that under the circumstances Option 2 was the 
right option to pursue. 
Assertion: Andersen should have resigned the account 
Andersen delivered considerable value to IE at all times during the time of its work with 
IE.  Given the dramatic shift in IE’s PC distribution market, aggressive actions were 
required.  IE was a client in a high-risk business, undergoing substantial transformation.  
The easy action would have been for Andersen to resign.  The decision to stay and 
continue in spite of numerous obstacles is to me an indication that Andersen Consulting is 
a dedicated, well-run services firm.  Andersen only stopped work after IE stopped paying 
the bills.   
Assertion: Andersen should have provided a more defined scope for its engagement with 
IE than it did. 
The strategy approved by IE management was for IE to transform its business at the 
enterprise level and at the business process level.  The reason is that the functional 
orientation that IE had employed in the past was no longer affordable (labor costs were 
too high) or customer-centric.  Even though time to market was critical, the IT 
professionals could not start programming until IE itself agreed on how these new 
business processes would work and what the organizational impact would be — that is, 
until the scope was defined.  In addition, Andersen was asked to and did perform services 

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 15
in areas far outside the scope as laid out in the IT strategy report.  But under the terms of 
the time and materials contract, such out-of-scope activities would have been expected to 
be added to the project. 
Assertion: Andersen should have performed more quality assessments at the outset 
Andersen has one of the oldest and broadest Client Quality Management Assessment 
(CQMA) processes in the systems-integration consulting industry.  Using this process, 
Andersen employed senior partners with no IE project responsibilities to examine the day-
to-day operations of the project from a management and technical standpoint.  Before the 
IT strategy and implementation projects, Andersen could have done a project risk 
assessment, an internal process designed to surface potential costs and risks on fixed price 
contracts.  Because of the time and materials nature of the agreement with IE, I do not 
believe such risk assessment was necessary. 
Assertion: Andersen staffed the project with inexperienced or inappropriate individuals 
If this assertion were true, then IE had the remedy in the CSA to ask for replacements.  IE 
did not do so.   
I do believe that there would have been some benefit to having a third partner involved in 
the project and located in Exton, PA to work with senior IE management due to the 
Denver-Exton geography differences, the expanded scope of the project, and the need, in 
hindsight, for better, senior-level IE management alignment and support. 
Assertion: Andersen was not an expert about the future of the HP 3000 as it portrayed 
itself to IE 
As discussed in point one above, the MSD software was not a sound basis for significant 
further software enhancements, and new applications running on an AS/400 were 
planned.  Moreover, the HP 3000 computer on which MSD ran, while a viable hardware 
platform going forward, was being abandoned by independent software vendors (ISVs).  
Without ISV support, the HP 3000 faced a long road to obsolescence, and yet IE very 
much wanted a state-of-the-industry IT system.  Therefore, what Andersen knew about 

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 16
HP’s plans and when they knew about these plans is irrelevant.  The HP 3000 was not a 
strategic IT-industry platform to HP in 1994 and beyond in spite of HP’s continued 
support for existing customers.  Andersen had adequate skills for the minimal HP 3000 
work that it was required to perform. 
Assertion: The AS/400 proposed by Andersen was obsolete 
In 1994, IBM was in the midst of a well-publicized transformation of its highly successful 
AS/400 platform from a stable host platform to supporting client-server architecture with 
much improved performance.  Unlike the HP 3000, in 1999 the AS/400 continues to gain 
market share.  It has a well-regarded client-server and Internet e-commerce capability.  It 
has survived several generations of challenges by competitors.  There are now 
approximately 500,000 AS/400s installed around the world, and the AS/400 is widely used 
in warehousing and distribution, the core components of IE’s business.  I believe that the 
AS/400 selection was appropriate. 
Assertion: The capabilities of ASF were over-promoted 
The Application Software Factory (ASF) combined sophisticated methodologies and 
integrated software tools to automatically turn design definitions into working computer 
code, thus eliminating an enormous amount of programming and program testing time.  
Andersen also brought ASF-designed applications from a previous engagement to IE for 
use as a base level for the CDF/2 project.  I believe that the assertions made during the 
litigation that Andersen promised to bring the CDF/2 application from another 
engagement overstate the likely case.  The reality is that Andersen brought the application 
design components from another engagement to the IE/2000 program.   
The ASF performed its role successfully.  The CDF/1 application was produced by the 
ASF and installed at the Denver warehouse.  Subsequently, IE modified the design and 
installed the modified application at the Memphis warehouse.

Page 17
Assertion: CDF/1 and CDF/2 were throwaway applications.  IE should have been advised 
to go directly to the target architecture. 
Option 2, the chosen option, called for CDF/1 and CDF/2 to be quickly implemented 
without a client-server interface, then subsequently upgraded with client-server 
functionality in projects CDF/3 and CDF/4.  In Option 3, a viable option proposed 
neutrally by Andersen, the implementation would have taken longer but achieved full 
client-server functionality in one step.  Given a time to market need, I believe Option 2 
was appropriate.  Moreover, there is no reason that IE’s investment in the CDF/1 and 
CDF/2 computers and applications would have to scrapped; the application logic could 
continue to reside on an AS/400 server in a client-server architecture for CDF/3 and 
CDF/4. 
 Assertion: The Willbern Group report to IE’s Board of Directors in February 1995 was 
competently conducted and accurate 
IE selected the Willbern Group, a one-employee company, because of its experience in 
documenting “runaway” software development projects.  Mr. Willbern and a contract 
assistant spent two weeks researching the status of the program, then hurriedly prepared 
and presented a presentation to IE’s Board of Directors.  If Willbern’s goal was to shock 
the board with news of internal dissension, then he succeeded.  But Willbern’s 
methodology relied on what he heard walking around and questioning IE/2000 project 
staff, then publishing barely substantiated allegations.  He apparently did not consider the 
trade-offs made prior to February 1995 or the delicate, strategic, business-transformation 
situation that IE as a company was in.  As to the accuracy of the report, there is truth that 
the project needed more control, particularly over scope.  He also caused the project team 
to waste valuable time and resources in plowing old ground, specifically the software 
package examination in March 1995. 
 Assertion: AS/400 performance was going to be inadequate for IE’s business needs. 
Andersen performance consultant McCarthy concluded that the AS/400 had the capacity 
to operate CDF/1 and CDF/2 with the future volumes projections provided by IE.  His 

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 18
conclusions were based on extensive performance benchmark simulations.  I concur with 
McCarthy’s conclusion.  Importantly, the counter to this assertion is that CDF/1 was, in 
fact, installed at IE’s two warehouses with no evidence of performance problems.   
 Assertion: Andersen planned to improperly use the application code resulting from CDF 
projects 1-4 to its own benefit at the conclusion of the IE/2000 project. 
The CSA allows both parties to use the applications developed during the IE/2000 
program without further payments.  The assertion is that Andersen would unfairly benefit 
from enhancements to the ASF for use in a client-server environment.  These 
enhancements, which IE needed and which were being designed for the CDF/3 and 
CDF/4 project phases, were never completed because IE cancelled work on the CDF/3 and 
CDF/4 task orders early in the projects.  Therefore, Andersen never obtained the asserted 
benefit.  Moreover, Andersen had at least two, well-funded efforts to develop client-server 
development tools independent of the ASF and IE.  Andersen just did not need the client-
server version of ASF to run its business. 
 
 
 
Assertion: Option 2 and its implications were not adequately explained to IE management 
Andersen and the IT strategy team from IE presented three viable options to IE 
management in March and to the Board in May 1994.  Option 2 offered the fastest time to 
initial implementation, but cost only modestly more than Option 1 (modifying a software 
package) and Option 3 (going directly to client-server).  I have concluded that the three 
options presented were the correct three to put forward as viable candidates.  IE had 
several IT-experienced executives on the Board and on the management team that 
reviewed the IT strategy report, and IT was at the heart of IE’s business.  These executives 
were, in my opinion, knowledgeable enough to make a sound decision based on the 
information contained in the IT strategy report and with their detailed, insider knowledge 

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 19
of IE’s actual business capabilities and strategies.  Moreover, I have reached the 
conclusion based on the evidence that Option 2 was the best option for IE to choose given 
the theory of the case I have described above.

Key Case Issues 
I believe that the summary of the case can be condensed to the following six points, based 
upon the evidence I have reviewed. 
1. Andersen was asked by IE to proceed with the utmost speed. 
IE believed that reaching a size of $10 billion as quickly as possible was a business 
necessity.  Andersen was engaged by IE in December 1993 to objectively describe its 
options for updating IE’s Reseller Network Division’s IT applications and infrastructure.  
Andersen was told that this was of the highest priority (Hascall-39), and needed the 
existing IT applications to be replaced as quickly as possible for three key reasons1. 
A. IE believed that its current production system, a software application named MSD 
from a company called MTI and other business-critical applications running on 
an HP 3000 would not be able to meet IE’s production needs.  Therefore, IE had 
to change its core production system as soon as possible or risk being unable to 
conduct business. 
B. The industry IE was in, distributing PCs and their peripheral equipment to a set 
of captive resellers, was undergoing a structural change.  Through a new 
distribution convention called “open sourcing”2, IE’s captive resellers would be 
free to buy their products from any distributor at prices comparable to those from 
IE.  As a result, IE would have to be more competitive in the future than in the 
past.  And one of the key areas of vulnerability that IE had was poor business 
processes to support its resellers due to the lack of functionality of its existing IT 
applications.  If the IT infrastructure was not improved rapidly, IE faced the 
threat of losing reseller business to competitors with greater operational efficiency 
— competitors who could support the now independent resellers business processes

While I would observe that the technology options that were considered but not presented

Introduction 
This report consists of a description of the transformation of the PC distribution business 
from the early-to-mid-1990s, an analysis of the impact of Open Sourcing on the industry 
in the mid-1990s, a discussion of how the major PC wholesalers changed their strategies 
over time, and an overall summary of research findings.  This research project was 
undertaken at the request of Peter Kastner, Executive Vice President of Aberdeen Group, 
Inc., for the purpose of assembling background information on the subject of PC 
distribution industry dynamics during the period of 1992 through 1996.  Research was 
drawn from publicly available information that has been analyzed based upon the 
author’s experiences during that time period.   
The author of this research note is Tom Dwyer, Aberdeen Group Research Director.  Mr. 
Dwyer has over twenty-five years of experience in the computer industry.  He has held 
senior management positions in product marketing, channel development and expansion,

Summary Findings 
Intense competition, extremely low margins, rapid technological change, and small 
margins for error characterized the PC distribution industry in the early-to-mid-1990s.  
Many distribution companies were either acquired or merged with others to obtain the 
revenue volumes necessary to be viable.  PC suppliers were continuously changing their 
distribution programs and component suppliers were offering ever-broadening numbers

and types of new products that users wanted and resellers were expected to integrate into

Industry Overview 
The PC distribution industry consists of companies that help suppliers more efficiently 
deliver products to end-user customers.  PC distribution companies are broadly segmented 
into two categories — wholesalers and resellers.  Suppliers sell to wholesalers; wholesalers 
sell to and support resellers; and resellers sell to and support end-user customers.  
Collectively, these PC distribution companies are often referred to as the “channel”.  Each 
company in the channel adds value in some significant way to deliver to the end-user 
customer the product and support services required in an economically efficient manner.  
The wholesale distribution companies benefit the suppliers by: 1) making large volume 
purchases, which lower the supplier’s selling, transaction, inventory carrying and delivery 
costs; and 2) recruiting, training, selling to, maintaining and otherwise supporting a large 
and diverse set of resellers. 
The wholesale distribution companies benefit their reseller customers by: 
1. Making it easy to obtain products from multiple different suppliers in quantities as 
small as one unit; 
2. Providing fast delivery of ordered products; 
3. Configuring multiple components to meet specific customer requirements; 
4. Delivering products directly to the end-user customer (if requested) so the reseller does 
not have to maintain inventory; 
5. Handling returned products; 
6. Providing services, such as marketing, sales training, repair, financing, etc. that lower 
the reseller’s costs.   

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 89
The reseller benefits the end-user customer by delivering customized products and services 
in such a manner as to best meet the individual end-user customer’s unique business 
needs and requirements. 
So while the wholesaler focuses on PC product acquisition, availability, pricing, delivery, 
service, etc., the reseller focuses on meeting each end-user customer’s specific business 
and PC product requirements. 
The wholesaler category includes full-line distributors, industrial distributors, specialty 
distributors, and regional distributors.  These different types of wholesalers are 
differentiated by the number and variety of products they carry, the type of reseller 
customer they serve, their market focus and their geographic coverage. 
The reseller category includes corporate resellers, value-added resellers, systems 
integrators, direct marketers, independent dealers, owner-operated chains, franchise 
chains, and computer retailers.  These different types of resellers are defined and 
distinguished by the end-user markets they serve, and by the type and amount of value 
they add to the basic products.  Examples of end-user markets include large corporate 
accounts; small and medium-sized businesses; educational institutions; government 
agencies; health care providers; home users, etc.  Examples of added value include 
installation and configuration, daily operations management and support, network 
installation and configuration, application systems development, and management of the 
PC asset across a customer’s dispersed sites. 
A hybrid type of company that attempts to span the gap between wholesale distributors 
and resellers, called the Master Reseller, or Aggregator, appeared in the mid-1980s when 
a number of retail chains and franchises merged and provided dedicated service to PC 
suppliers.  Master resellers are functionally similar to full-line distributors but focus on 
selling only a relatively few product lines — typically high-volume, brand-name hardware 
systems — to a network of franchised dealers and affiliates.  Whereas full-line distributors 
may carry over 100,000 products, master resellers typically carry under 2,000 products.  
Whereas full-line distributors sell standard and discrete products, master resellers perform 
a high level of product customization and configuration and sell bundled products.

are contractually affiliated with, but also purchase complementary products required to 
fill out their product line that they need in relatively small volumes from full-line 
distributors.  Intelligent Electronics would be classified as a Master Reseller/Aggregator.

PC Distribution Industry Characteristics in the early-to-mid-1990s 
1. In 1991 there were four companies that were considered to be pure aggregators: 
ComputerLand, Inacom, Intelligent Electronics, and MicroAge.  The four largest full-
line distributors at that time were Ingram Micro, Merisel, Computer 2000 Group AG, 
and Tech Data.   
2. Starting in 1990 the rapid growth of the PC products wholesale distribution industry 
exceeded that of the PC industry as PC suppliers turned their distribution operations 
over to intermediaries as quickly as possible and the demand for component 
peripherals (such as modems) and software from independent suppliers increased.   
3. Faced with the pressures of declining product prices and the increasing costs of selling 
direct to a large and diverse group of resellers, suppliers increasingly relied upon 

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 97
wholesale distribution channels for a greater proportion of their sales — including 
eventual sales to and placement within large corporate users.  To minimize costs and 
focus on their core capabilities in manufacturing, product development, and 
marketing, many suppliers began outsourcing an increasing portion of certain 
functions, such as distribution, service, technical support, and final assembly, to the 
wholesale distribution channel. 
4. Resellers began depending even more on the resources and capabilities of their 
wholesaler or master reseller.  Increasing product complexity, shorter product life 
cycles, and an increasing number of PC products and components caused resellers to 
depend on wholesalers for more of their product, marketing, and technical support 
needs.  In addition, resellers relied to an increasing extent on wholesalers for inventory 
management and credit to avoid stocking large inventories and maintaining credit 
lines to finance their working capital needs. 
5. Starting in 1991 the competition in the wholesale distribution industry intensified 
between full-line distributors and master resellers when Apple, Compaq and IBM 
authorized full-line distributors to carry their products and sell to small and medium 
VARs.  Resellers had previously been forced to work almost exclusively with master 
resellers to obtain these products — but now they had a choice of wholesaler types.  
Competition among wholesalers was based on price, product availability, product 
brand, product breadth, credit availability, and speed and accuracy of delivery.  
Resellers were continuously looking for ways to lower their costs and increase their 
revenues.  This translated into additional requirements on the full-line distributors 
and master resellers to offer product customization, product technical support, and 
other value-added services such as help desk support and product evaluation reports.   
6. In 1992, declining prices for PCs and components were caused by competition from 
clone manufacturers and mail-order suppliers, falling microprocessor prices, and 
aggressive pricing competition among name-brand suppliers.  In 1993, wholesalers 
witnessed the previous 1990 profit margins of 7% decline to 4% and expected them to 
further decline to 3%. 

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 98
7. Wholesale distribution was and is capital intensive — it requires significant working 
capital to finance accounts receivable and product inventory.  The wholesaler must 
offer resellers payment options with attractive credit terms, ranging from 30-to-60 
days, and must also purchase sufficient supplier inventory to provide fast delivery 
times.  Beginning in 1992 wholesalers obtained financing programs from commercial 
credit companies and the financing arms of suppliers to reduce their credit exposure 
and working capital requirements.  Any negative changes to financing agreements had 
an immediate impact on the wholesaler — increasing its working capital 
requirements, decreasing operating margin and hindering its ability to win business. 
8. Beginning in 1993 and continuing through 1996 wholesalers were faced with product 
shortages from the suppliers, which caused the resellers to lose sales opportunities.  
Open sourcing further increased the competition for this scarce supply by enabling 
resellers to search for the desired products from any wholesaler.  Suppliers instituted 
product allocation policies using cash payment incentives to grant increased 
allocations.  Wholesalers with strong cash positions were in the best position to receive 
increased allocations.  The resellers being served by these wholesalers were therefore in 
the best competitive position to obtain the products they needed. 
9. The combination of product shortages and open sourcing intensified the competition 
between master resellers and full-line distributors for resellers’ business.  While prior 
to open sourcing there were examples of resellers leaving one master reseller network 
to join a competitor, this kind of activity increased substantially in 1994 and 1995. 
10. The decline in product prices and the increased competition from full-line distributors 
in 1992 through 1994 prompted master resellers to look for alternative sources of 
revenue.  The search for higher margin revenue sources included adding new product 
lines, and expanding the master reseller role to become a service provider.  Master 
resellers often began carrying new products with longer sales cycles, which delayed the 
revenue benefit beyond the master reseller’s original expectation.  As master resellers 
became service providers, they alienated some of their resellers who questioned this 

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 99
new strategy — prompting them to look at other distributors that weren’t competing 
against them. 
11. Expanding into international markets was another tactic for increasing revenues that 
many PC wholesalers pursued in the early-1990s.  Markets outside the United States, 
which represented over half of the PC industry's global sales, were characterized by a 
more fragmented wholesale distribution channel than in the United States.  Fueling 
this change in business tactics were the wholesalers’ traditional value-chain partners 
— suppliers and resellers — pursuing global growth.  The suppliers and resellers 
wanted their wholesalers to be with them by providing international sales and support 
capabilities.  In addition, the non-U.S. PC products industry was less mature and 
growing more rapidly than the U.S. market.  The conclusion many PC wholesalers 
came to was that international growth opportunities were significant. 
12. The combination of declining margins and increased competition during the early-to-
mid-1990s also prompted master resellers to employ a number of innovative business 
practices to obtain relief from the downward pressure on margins and to drive more 
sales volume.  Such practices included lowering franchisee royalty fees for high 
volume commitments, renegotiating deals with freight carriers, re-engineering 
business processes, upgrading to more effective information systems, consolidating 
warehouses, and revising reseller-pricing policies. 
13. Wholesalers had to make major investments in their supply chain re-engineering 
processes and improve their information technology infrastructure to reduce operating 
costs and enhance reseller services.  Wholesalers sought to realize operating 
efficiencies for themselves and their reseller customer by providing an efficient 
distribution system focusing on ease of order placement, speed of delivery, facilitation 
of product returns and reduction of freight costs.  Individual wholesalers made these 
investments with varying degrees of short-term success. 
14. A series of PC industry consolidations at all levels started in 1992 and has continued 
to the present.  For example, resellers merged with other resellers to create economies

of scale through increased revenues and to attempt to gain subsequent competitive 
advantages.  As another example, wholesalers converted previous franchisee 
investments into acquisitions in order to prevent those resellers (formerly 
independent) from buying from competitive wholesalers.  Master resellers 
experimented with new lines of business — some became involved with retail; others 
developed expertise as service providers.   
Open Sourcing and Second Sourcing: When, Why and How 
Historically, branded computer systems from large suppliers such as Apple, Compaq, 
Hewlett-Packard, and IBM were sold in the United States under the single sourcing model 
— resellers were required to purchase products exclusively from only one wholesaler — 
either a master reseller or a full-line distributor.   
In 1993, competitive pressures led some of the major computer suppliers, such as Compaq 
and IBM, to authorize second sourcing, in which resellers could purchase their products 
from a source other than the resellers primary master reseller or full-line distributor.  
Under this program the second source was subject to certain, more restrictive terms and 
conditions (such as higher prices or the elimination of floor planning subsidies) than the 
primary source. 
In 1994, PC computer manufacturers such as Compaq and IBM authorized Open 
Sourcing — a channel policy under which resellers could purchase the supplier's product 
from any source on equal terms and conditions.  Open sourcing minimized the distinction 
between master resellers and full-line distributors, because it allowed both full-line 
distributors and competing master resellers to sell to a master reseller’s formerly captive 
reseller customers.  Master resellers lost their exclusive reseller arrangements.  Suppliers’ 
institution of second sourcing and open sourcing distribution policies put the largest and 
most efficient distributors of PC products, which provided the highest value through 
superior service and pricing, in the best position to compete for reseller customers.   

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 101
Open sourcing caused a free-for-all-battle for market share among master resellers and 
full-line distributors.  While there were many ways to fight this battle, the one that was 
invariably used was price — each wholesaler had to have the ability to successfully 
operate on even smaller margins than in the past. 
Open sourcing enabled pure full-line distributors to develop or acquire master reseller 
divisions.  This created a new category of wholesaler called a Master Distributor—the 
combination of a full-line distributor and a master reseller.  Master resellers responded to 
the encroachment of the full-line distributors by changing their business models as well.  
Some became corporate resellers as they added higher margin services.  Master resellers 
also extended their distributor role by expanding their product lines. 
In conclusion, open sourcing caused major industry consolidations and changes to 
previous business models.  Open sourcing increased the competitive pressure on 
wholesalers, blurred the lines between full-line distributors and resellers, and jeopardized 
the viability of the master reseller role as a distinct category of the distribution industry.

Summary Findings 
Six of the eight firms described in this report have centralized organizations that are 
responsible for developing, maintaining, conducting, and promoting QA and related 
corporate standards (i.e., best practices) and processes.  These organizations typically act 
as independent reviewers to periodically carry out QA reviews of active projects.  The other 
two of the eight firms profiled firms also have centralized organizations whose roles are to 
develop and promote QA and other standards, but members of these groups do not actively 
participate in project reviews.  Rather, senior partners within the relevant practice areas 
carry out such reviews.  These partners are not members of engagement teams.  Among all 
eight firms, the actual responsibility for ensuring adherence to the firm’s quality 
standards and practices rests with the service delivery teams and goes up the practice area 
management, or line of business management reporting chain of command. 
• 
As a rule, independent QA organizations do not become involved with a potential 
client during the engagement opportunity assessment and proposal stages, although 
some firms do have guidelines that specify when such involvement can or must 
occur.  Such guidelines are generally based on pre-defined criteria such as risk; 
revenue potential; client gain/loss potential; match between engagement goals and 
agreed-upon deliverables and service supplier capabilities and expertise; inclusion of 
new or untested technologies; and a new client-relationship opportunity.  None of the 
suppliers interviewed for this report share pre-engagement risk assessments with 
potential clients.  Typically, pre-engagement risk assessments are used by 
professional service organizations to determine whether or not they can meet client 
expectations and deliverables while maximizing their own project profitability. 
• 
Suppliers use internal QA reviews and related activities as self-improvement 
mechanisms — “What are we doing that we can do better.”  They are also used to 
ensure that engagement managers conduct projects in such a manner as to maximize 
profitability and that they make effective use of internal resources.  For this reason, 
most QA reviews are, by design, exercises in self-criticism.  However, to encourage 
openness, the majority of firms do not use QA reviews as a means for determining 

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 108
employee performance.  Most interviewees stressed the critical need for openness, 
and stated that identifying and focusing on problems identified through the QA 
process in a non-confrontational manner is the best way to ensure successful projects 
and quality deliverables.  
• 
While clients are typically interviewed during QA reviews, the purpose of such 
interactions is both to determine the state of the working relationship between 
supplier and client team members and to understand the client’s views on the 
effectiveness of project activities and processes.  Therefore, the aim of such interviews 
is to gauge the level of client satisfaction in terms of how a project is proceeding, not 
the quality of the deliverables.  Measures of the latter are based on acceptance tests, 
joint project reviews, and ultimately, client-satisfaction surveys.  The real measure of 
the effectiveness of internal QA reviews is whether or not recommendations resulting 
from them are carried out in a timely fashion.  This is typically done through follow-
up reviews scheduled in accordance with the severity of the problems identified. 
• 
The firms researched tend not to mandate internal QA reviews for each and every 
engagement.  In this sense, Andersen’s requirement that all projects undergo at least 
one such review is above industry norms.  Typically, the extent and frequency of 
internal QA involvement in engagements is based on risk, revenue opportunity, and 
the other criteria described above.  
• 
As a rule, suppliers do not share internal QA reports with clients except in edited or 
summary form.  Of the eight leading firms researched this report, only Deloitte, 
Keane, and CSC share complete reports with clients as a general practice.  EDS and 
PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC), on the other hand, make a practice of not sharing 
QA findings with clients unless specifically requested to do so, and then the firms 
deliver only summary reports.  Furthermore, it is PwC’s policy to destroy QA reports 
a year after projects are completed.  It is worth noting that none of the individuals 
interviewed for this report could recall a single instance where internal QA 
documents were used in legal actions.

industry norms in terms of centralized coordination and the use of mandated

Ernst and Young LLP (E&Y) 
E&Y’s Quality Advisor program was started in 1992.  The program was an outgrowth of 
an internal restructuring process and was triggered by a few “unhappy client” 
engagements that were blamed, in part, on growth and integration problems stemming 
from the merger of Ernst and Whinney and Arthur Young that created E&Y in 1989.  The 
focus of the program is to create a collaborative partnership with major clients by 
reducing client risk, thereby ensuring a healthy, long-term relationship between the client 
and the firm. 
E&Y’s Director of Risk Management in the U.S., who coordinates program activities with 
the heads of the firm’s practice areas, manages the Quality Advisor program.  The 
manager of the practice area under which the engagement falls assigns a Quality Advisor 
to all engagements with revenues above $1M.  The Quality Advisor reports to the practice 
manager and is typically at the partner level or higher and is a recognized expert in his or 
her practice area.  The role is a line function and does not report to either engagement 
managers or client relationship partners. 
E&Y’s program methodology was developed in-house and is not based on any particular 
set of external  guidelines.  Quality Advisors are briefed about an engagement during the 
pre-proposal and start-up stages.  Quality Advisors review project plans, resource 
assignments, planned deliverables, and milestone schedules.  Throughout the course of an 
engagement, a Quality Advisor will interview client personnel and E&Y team members.  
He or she will then conduct quarterly reviews in which both client and E&Y project team 
members participate.  The reviews can last up to two or three days and can be quite 
intensive ⎯ including reviews of reports from software testers and integration specialists.

Neither engagement managers nor relationship managers participate in the quarterly

KPMG Consulting 
In 1996 KPMG moved from a post-engagement quality review model to a more proactive 
QA model.  The move coincided with the company’s reorganization of its consulting 
practices.  It was also based on the recognition that as KPMG took on larger, more 
complex engagements on a national level, the company had to keep better track of quality 
and risk issues on an ongoing basis.   
The current Quality Assurance Program (QAP) is under the direction of the National 
Risk Management Partner for Consulting and is staffed by three senior partners with 
decades of collective consulting experience.  As KPMG is organized by industry practices, 
the practice leaders own the ultimate responsibility for all engagements in their respective 
areas.  Thus, the QAP team members serve as a “second set of eyes” for these individuals. 
The team meets by phone or in person on a monthly basis to review a list of open 
engagements, identify those that qualify for review, and select and schedule those that 
qualify.  Any engagement with a contract value of $1 million or higher is assigned to a 
QAP team member.  Other engagement selection criteria include complexity, association 
with a new KPMG services, and size.  In addition, an engagement may be selected for a 
QAP review at the request of its industry practice leader.   
A QAP reviewer typically does not meet with the engagement team until the engagement is 
under way, but will always schedule a meeting within the first three months of its start.  
Prior to the meeting, the QAP representative will request background material, such as 
contract or statement of work, project plans, budget, etc., from the engagement team.  The 
reviewer will then meet with the engagement team and conduct interviews with between 
two-to-four key client personnel.  KPMG stresses that reviewers meet with client personnel, 
not to defend or explain, but to obtain the client’s views on how the firm has performed, 
how the personnel assigned to the project are performing, and identify activities that they 
can do better.   
Specific areas that a reviewer will study include engagement management (e.g., budgeting, 
resource utilization, etc.), quality (of actual deliverables as well as client perception of the 

Report of Peter S. Kastner, Intelligent Electronics v. Andersen Consulting 
Page 113
deliverables), and financial management (e.g., where is the project relative to the budget).  
During the review process, the QAP team member will meet with the engagement team to 
discuss findings and make recommendations.  A typical review will last four-to-five days, 
including report preparation. 
Engagements are assigned red, yellow, or green risk flags.  A red flag is taken as an 
indication to the industry practice leader that there are conditions that require his or her 
direct, immediate attention.  Yellow flagged engagements contain items that require direct 
action by the delivery team.  Green flag reports list the reviewer’s comments and 
suggestions.  Report copies, regardless of how they are flagged, are sent to the engagement 
team, the industry practice leader, and the KPMG executive team.   
The QAP team reviewer will conduct monthly, follow-up reviews for any project that 
receives a red flag.  This process will continue for as long as critical issues remain.  Yellow 
flagged engagements may undergo additional reviews by the QAP team member but, as a 
rule, follow-up activities are the responsibility of the appropriate industry practice leader. 
KPMG follows an internal, situational release-based model for application development 
and systems integration projects.  Project plans and schedules include acceptance tests 
and the company will work with clients on release schedules to decide whether or not a 
particular risk or the absence of certain functionality should or should not cause a 
schedule change.  KPMG is in the process of a phased rollout of a knowledge management 
tool to consolidate engagement histories and best practices with industry practice specific 
templates.

National Partner assigned to oversee all economic and market condition risks associated

beginning of every engagement or project, the leader or responsible partner completes a 
formalized Risk Questionnaire.  Only those projects receiving a pre-defined, weighted

PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) 
Within PwC, responsibility for QA and risk management functions resides with the P&L 
business units.  At the same time, each of the firm’s three major geographic regions ⎯ the 
Americas; Europe, the Middle East, and Africa; and Asia/Pacific ⎯ has an organization 
responsible for overall QA and risk management standards and practices.  The functions 
of the regional QA organizations are in the areas of review and advisory services, best 
practices development, and the coordination of internal practices to ensure consistency of 
QA and risk management at both the regional and corporate levels.  
The history of PwC’s regional QA organizations goes back to the pre-merger days of Price 
Waterhouse, Inc.  Providing a centralized repository of information and expertise, the 
practice of having separate QA oversight functions became formalized in 1988.   
Today, each PwC regional QA group is made up of a combination of permanent and 
rotating staff that tend to be senior consultants.  Members report directly to business unit 
heads and, through dotted-line organization structures, to the regional QA heads.  
Depending on the financial scope and, in some cases, the complexity of a particular 
engagement, QA staff can get involved in projects as early as the request for proposal 
(RFP) review and response stage.  They are responsible for recognizing and 
understanding risk issues and potential quality barriers up front in order to ensure that 
engagements are organized to address them.  Engagement teams are responsible for 
following QA processes and the ultimate quality of deliverables.  QA teams are responsible 
for ensuring that engagement teams put such processes in place and for determining 
whether or not they are being followed. 
Regardless of when QA staff are assigned to engagements, reviews are supposed to occur 
on a quarterly basis.  The process starts with a review of relevant documentation ⎯ 
proposals, contracts, project plans, deliverables, and issues logs.  These last are kept by

As a rule, QA reviews seldom identify issues that have not already been noted by the

during the QA review have typically been proposed and implemented and/or rejected by 
the engagement teams.  The primary purpose of the QA reviews is to ensure that internal 
best-practice processes are being adhered to and that the teams are following the tasks that 
they themselves have planned.    
Following a QA review, a report is submitted to the engagement team and to higher 
management levels, including any layers between the team and the business unit or P&L 
head.  Copies are also submitted to the regional QA and Risk Management partners.  As a 
rule, clients do not receive copies of reports unless they specifically request them.  
Individual reports are archived until subsequent reports are issued, at which time 
previous reports are destroyed.  Final QA reports are destroyed one year following 
completion of the engagement to which they apply.  However, reports can be scrubbed of 
specific client information and used as sources for the development of best practices and 
to update corporate practices.  The subsequent best practices then become incorporated 
into the firm’s methodologies, which are maintained by PwC centers of excellence.   
Finally, as part of the typical PwC individual review process, engagement leaders are 
measured against whether or not they follow standard QA practices and points are 
assigned or taken away based on that measure.

Andersen’s corporate-wide Quality Management System (QMS) covers all aspects of 
quality management— from internal best practices and methodologies to external client 
satisfaction measurement.  Client Quality Management (CQM) is the component of QMS 
that applies directly to ensuring that client engagements are focused on what the firm 
refers to as its overall commitment to Quality client services — Quality Service, Value 
Delivery, and Client Success (QVS). 
The firm’s Managing Partner for Quality, assisted by a Quality Leadership Council and 
Area/Region Quality Management Partners, is responsible for developing overall quality 
guidelines and practices and for ensuring that each Practice Unit’s Quality Management 
(QM) efforts are aligned with the firm’s quality objectives and in compliance with 
company policies.  As the title implies, Area/Region Quality Management Partners are 
responsible for coordinating QM activities at the area/region level.  They work closely with 
Quality Management Partners at the Practice Unit level and with Area/Region Managing 
Partners to ensure that quality activities at both the Area/Region and Practice Unit levels 
are aligned with the firm’s best practices and objectives.   
At the same time, it is common for Practice Units to expand on corporate standards and 
practices and add new best-practice standards as appropriate to the business focus.  The 
firm’s culture also encourages the development of best practices and bottom-up 
enhancements to corporate standards.  Andersen’s internal QA standards and practices 
were developed internally and are updated and refreshed from both internal and external 
sources, such as ISO 9000. 
Responsibility for ensuring that Andersen QVS goals and objectives are met resides within 
each of the firm’s Practice Units and rests with the Consulting Management Partner — 
Practice Unit (CMP).  This person is assisted by Quality Management Assessment 
Partners (QMA partners) who are responsible for ensuring that engagement proposals, 
commitments, and deliverables adhere to Andersen QM policies and principles.  As 
described below, QMA partners conduct Client Quality Management Assessments

responsibilities, accountabilities, and authorities for managing the plan, in addition to 
procedures for gathering and reporting measures and metrics against engagement quality 
goals, quality training curriculum, and ongoing activities associated with the development 
of and adherence to engagement quality deliverables and objectives.
