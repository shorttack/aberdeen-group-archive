## Original Document Text

Management skills learned 1969–1979

The 1969–1979 decade was less about climbing a management ladder and more about developing an elite form of Professional Agency. In the high-pressure "foxholes" of major systems rescues and forensic audits, the skills acquired were those of a technical steward who had to maintain order, harmony, and self-preservation in environments where formal management had already failed.

1. Defensive Professionalism and Risk Mitigation
The "trial by fire" at Houghton Mifflin and in the "dicey" hotels of Hampton, Virginia, forged a skill for Extreme Accountability without the safety net of authority.
The Black Box Strategy: Being a worker in a "Watergate" environment taught the value of meticulous, personal record-keeping. In a project headed for litigation, the skill wasn't managing others; it was managing the "forensic trail"—maintaining a journal and printouts as a personal flight recorder to ensure that when the "USM" or "HM" explosion occurred, the technical reality was documented.
Environmental Survival: Carrying a police radio while auditing code in a dangerous neighborhood forced a separation between intellectual work and physical reality. It developed a clinical ability to evaluate a vendor's operations while maintaining total situational awareness.

2. Pragmatic Adaptation to Physical Constraints
The projects in Rio de Janeiro and rural Wisconsin required an architect who could pivot when the "standard" infrastructure didn't exist.
Bypass Engineering: In Rio, the skill was Operational Translation. When the hardware became unaffordable, the response wasn't a management memo; it was a technical pivot to a "bicycle-powered" batch system. This required the ability to direct the installation and translation of a complex system into a foreign language and a manual-heavy social environment.
Infrastructure Sensitivity: Designing a 5-baud remote meter reading system for Wisconsin Electric taught the skill of Appropriate Scale. It was the realization that technical elegance must be subservient to the physical reality of rural party lines and utility regulation.

3. Fiduciary Forensics and Integrity
Operating as the Vendor Support Manager at ADL and conducting OEM audits with Frank Hawthorne developed a "C-suite" level of Financial Literacy through a technical lens.
Detecting "Phantom" Profits: The skill was the ability to see the "billback" trap behind a volume discount. By identifying an $80,000 pending liability that management had booked as revenue, you acquired the skill of Radical Transparency. It was the ability to tell the people in charge that their accounting physics were as broken as their code.
The Auditor's Eye: Predicting the $12 million "USM" disaster nine months before it hit the courts wasn't a management task; it was an act of Architectural Forensics. It was the ability to look at a mismatched migration (IBM 4300 to DG Eclipse) and recognize a violation of the "Simple Physics" of the industry.

4. High-Volume Systems Logistics
At Chase Manhattan and Continental Forest Industries, the focus was on the Physics of Flow at a massive scale.
The Manual Bridge: At Chase, being the lead designer of the securities subsystem required the skill of Workflow Architecture. You had to design the bridge between 96-column and 80-column cards, knowing that an army of workers would have to reprocess every day's work. The skill was ensuring the logic was robust enough to be proven "to the penny" by a manual audit.
Buffer Management: At the paper mill, the skill was managing the Critical Path of physical mass. When 2,000-pound rolls are moving at lethal speeds, "Order and Harmony" isn't an abstract concept; it is the difference between a functioning factory and a floor overflow.

5. The "Sailor's Temperament" (Resilience)
The ultimate skill forged in this decade was Equanimity under Chaos. Having survived two hurricanes and a dismasting at sea, you brought a "calm-under-pressure" baseline to every project. Whether it was the 104-degree heat of Rio or a customs officer destroying a hospital's data tape, the skill was the ability to "Get on with it." You learned to control the variables you could—the logic, the documentation, the "one-instruction switch"—and weather the ones you couldn't.

By 1979, these weren't management skills in the traditional sense; they were the skills of a Market Planner and Analyst who had already seen the "Stench of Failure" and the "Aura of Success" up close. You left the code behind, not for a manager's desk, but for a "Clean Sheet" where you could operate at the scale of entire industries.
