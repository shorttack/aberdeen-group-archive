Preface 
Customers engage content delivery network (CDN) service providers for two pur-
poses: to increase the speed of end-user access to Web pages and to cut the costs 
of Web site delivery. The number of CDNs peaked in 2001, when Aberdeen tracked 
more than 40 vendors. The stringent market challenges of the last three years 
pared the field to fewer than a dozen. 
However, usage of CDNs rose dramatically during that same period. Today, most 
corporations, governments, and educational institutions rely on CDNs to increase 
the availability and reliability of public-facing Web sites and to economically cir-
cumvent the unknowns and inefficiencies of the open Internet. CDNs are standard 
procedure for delivering most types of Web-accessed content, from static images 
and streaming media to whole sites and downloadable software. 
The CDN suppliers left standing are all the stronger for surviving the dot-bomb tri-
als. They are rapidly developing new services to move beyond basic image, page, 
and media delivery. The objective is to enable customers to deliver applications 
over the Internet as reliably as they deliver static and streaming content. Leverag-
ing their core competence of building and maintaining Internet overlay applica-
tions to meet these demands, CDNs are combining existing services with new 
technologies to offer comprehensive application delivery solutions.

Customers Attest to Mirror Image’s Value 
Mirror Image has gathered a fleet of loyal customers over the past six years. Aber-

Fastclick, Inc. 
Much like the content delivery industry, online advertising was hit hard during the 
economic downturn of 2000 through 2002. Founded and funded just months be-
fore the market dropped, Fastclick survived owing to (a) good financial manage-
ment and (b) an advanced technology platform designed to accommodate growth 
and manage expenses for Internet advertising services. In late 2003, Fastclick was 
delivering more than 5 billion impressions per month using two services: 
• Ad Network, which connects 14,000 buyers and sellers of online ad in-
ventory 
• AdServer, a high-performance engine that hosts and serves Internet ad-
vertising and includes Web-based tools for customers to correlate pricing 
models with advertising campaigns and build positive ROIs 
Steve Chang, the company’s chief technology officer, reported that there are three 
technology demands for serving the high volume: hardware, bandwidth, and secu-
rity. As the company scaled up in mid-2002, Chang recognized that these three 
demands would become bottlenecks to ad-serving performance and company 
profitability. Scaling at least one of the three was required to meet the growing 
need, but doing so would eat into the profit margins. Building out all three areas 
was completely infeasible given the budget constraints. 
Chang decided to use a CDN service provider to “reduce bandwidth and infra-
structure costs.” “With a CDN, we needed fewer servers, less room, less power, 
and less floor space to meet our performance thresholds,” he said. Chang chose 
Mirror Image because “price was a huge issue,” and Mirror Image was willing to 
work with Fastclick to build a pricing model that “took into account [its] growth 
trajectory over time.” Mirror Image was also willing to “go the extra mile” and form 
a tight business and technology relationship.

An International Toy Retailer 
Aberdeen interviewed the director of Internet operations at a major international 
toy retailer (who requested anonymity), a longtime Mirror Image customer. The 
director has been with the company for 25 years and has overseen Web operations 
for the last six years. He oversees the company’s infrastructure, hosting, and Web 
application design and operation. 
From the launch of its online site, the company’s goal for its Web operations was 
to make customers “more comfortable with our products, which leads to a more 
easy transition to shopping.” The e-commerce elements of the site are “as close to 
a brick-and-mortar store as possible,” with the products organized similar to a 
physical store and using the same layout as the company’s catalogs. A priority was 
also set for the company’s online operations to develop a reputation that mirrored 
the high quality associated with its worldwide brand. 
Though e-commerce is certainly part of the site, much of the site is not sales re-
lated. Consumers are encouraged to sign up for free services that tie online games, 
puzzles, and activities to physical products. The current enrollment is “in the mil-
lions of members.” The company continually creates new interactive components, 
movie clips, storyboards, and downloads to encourage children and parents to use 
the company’s products as creatively and regularly as possible.

The company hosts the Web applications at two data centers, one in North America

New Services from Tested Building Blocks 
Responding to customers’ needs, Mirror Image has aggregated its technology and 
service elements into its “Smart Content” services platform. This platform lever-
ages elements of the CAP architecture and marries them with an advanced XML-
based rules engine and enhanced report tools, such as those developed for the toy 
retailer. 
Each customer will be able to program its own content rules into the engine, 
based on geotargeting, time of day, place in rotation, “wild card” image replace-
ments, and other parameters. These rules are distributed with the content 
throughout the global network. End-users are served from a local data center, us-
ing the XML rules and the locally cached content. 
Mirror Image expects IT, Web operations, 
and other technical groups to benefit from 
the Smart Content platform in two ways. 
First, the new services allow greater visibil-
ity into actual CDN usage to confirm the 
benefits of speed, efficiency, and accuracy. 
Second, the reporting and user interfaces 
will allow nontechnical groups to manage 
the flow of content in, out, and around the 
CDN. Once IT or Web operations install the 
distribution rules, the customer’s marketing 
group, e-commerce team, or other non-IT 
staff can insert content directly into Mirror 
Image’s network without involving IT or 
Web operations. 
As a result, IT and Web operations staff will be able to remove themselves from be-
ing the key masters of the content delivery services. Customers such as Fastclick’s 
Steve Chang will have more time to focus on nondelivery issues. The director of 
Internet operations at the toy retailer will be able to develop XML rules that cus-
tomize content to local audiences and let the marketing department own that cus-
tomization process. 
Aberdeen Conclusions 
For many years, CDN and caching suppliers have promised to separate managing 
the content in the network from managing the network itself. Mirror Image cus-
tomers attest to the need for greater visibility into where their specific content is 
accessed in the supplier’s network. Whereas CDNs can increase flexibility, improve 
response times for end-users, and lower the scalability costs for Web operations, 
customers of CDNs need verifiable data before they will shift more content and 
application delivery responsibility to the CDNs.
