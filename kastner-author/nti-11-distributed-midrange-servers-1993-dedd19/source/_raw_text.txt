As enterprises are rebuilding their IS infrastructures with a combination of
midrange servers and PC LANs (local area networks), senior executives are
researching how midrange servers can be the basis for successfully
accomplishing their goals. In this chapter of the New Technology Impact

Systems with Midrange Servers using the latest technology advances.
The critical reason midrange servers are being used as the foundation for
next-generation enterprise IS systems is really quite simple ‚ÄĒ they provide
the comprehensive functionality at reasonable prices that enterprises need
for business-critical multiuser applications.
Aberdeen will first describe the key new enterprise requirements for
multiuser information systems and then the operating characteristics of
today‚Äôs midrange servers. Aberdeen will then highlight how midrange
servers match the operational requirements of enterprises. Midrange
servers provide IS executives with multiple options when designing
distributed architectures. Therefore, Aberdeen will describe the topologies
most frequently employed and why.

technical and product perspective, noting how they can meet executives
requirements.

Many IS executives remember midrange servers as small but cost-effective
machines best used to support workgroups of under 50 users for
timesharing applications. However, midrange server technology has
changed dramatically in the last 2-3 years. Most notably, I/O bandwidth,
processor speed, and systems and communications software have advanced
to the point that midrange servers now exceed IBM ES/9000 mainframes
in technological capability and systems reliability.
Suppliers have scaled their midrange servers to the point where they are
economical to use both in distributed locations where there is just one
employee (such as a brokerage office in Sri Lanka) and to support over a
1,000 online concurrent users.
To leverage the benefits of 1990‚Äôs technology, midrange servers are built
using state-of-the-science microprocessors that surpass the performance of
handcrafted mainframe CPUs.
To ensure high-end growth capabilities to handle applications requiring
even greater throughput than can now be handled by mainframes,
advanced midrange servers provide growth paths through symmetric
multiprocessing (SMP) with near-linear performance increases.

functionality in all three of these critical areas than either mainframes or

The objective of the next generation of distributed systems is to better
support line managers and departmental staffs in carrying out their daily
responsibilities. This is a business operations end-user-centric view of the
world as opposed to the mainframe-centric view that sees the central
computing resource as an end-unto-itself or the PC-centric view that sees
the single-user as king.
Midrange servers provide the functionality to expedite interaction between
users within specific departments and the rest of the enterprise. This
capability is a requirement for enterprises seeking to reorganize from the
traditional silo or hierarchical model to the faster moving horizontal
structure.
The reason Aberdeen refers to midrange servers as opposed to midrange
computers is that RDBMS software is an integrable part of the server.
RDBMS technology, including the actual database engine and supporting
application development tools and applications, provide benefits beyond
merely fast CPU processing. The RDBMS provides: speed of application
development, implementation and enhancement, departmental and
individual user customization with minimal IS resource requirements, and
application portability among different hardware platforms.
In the future, Aberdeen expects to see enterprises implementing client¬≠
server applications based upon their RDBMS-of-choice as opposed to any
one manufacturer‚Äôs particular midrange server platform.

The result is that IS executives comprehensively evaluate midrange server

Aberdeen Notes:
Based on research with enterprises that have successfully implemented
production distributed systems with midrange servers, Aberdeen believes
that the three-tier plus architecture has been the most successful.
Enterprises choose the most appropriate single-user platform based upon
functionality and price for the application and connect it to a midrange
server that may be distributed by geography and/or application. Naturally
the multiple midrange servers will be networked together to provide
efficient data exchange and system redundancy.
Central IS maintains the production systems to consolidate data from the
multiple midrange system. While this production system has traditionally
been a mainframe, it can easily be a high-end server with superior-to-
mainframe I/O. Very importantly, this production system allows IS to
backup the distributed midrange servers and, if desired, even the connected
PCs.
Finally, the analytically system is typically updated each night to allow
managers the next day to review the status of the business and make
tactical decisions.

use high speed disk arrays today that are simply not available for
mainframes. The result is high-end midrange systems have faster wall
clock time results for even batch operations than older mainframes.

enterprise needs today and at the same time represent the best way to
protect an enterprise‚Äôs investment in multiuser computing for the future.

Many enterprises are following an evolutionary approach to distributed

midrange servers with an advanced RDBMS that can effectively support

technologies over time.
The surround approach typically assumes that new applications will be
developed on midrange servers. But at the same time, it allows the
enterprise to capture positive returns on mainframe applications that have
been released to production within the last several years and extend the
operational life of current mainframe databases.

systems ‚ÄĒ that is files are retrieved from the server, worked with on a PC,
and then returned with any modifications to the PC LAN server. Only
occasionally does the PC LAN server database verify that the record meets
any criteria for acceptance back into the PC LAN server. Such a lack of
data integrity and security is inappropriate for any OLTP application and
most decision support applications. However, the use of a midrange server
with a robust RDBMS overcomes these major problems.
By connecting the departmental midrange server to the central IS
production server, IS can now provide both departmental and PC backup
services as well as version control. This allows the department to function
on its objectives while minimizing the overhead requirements to directly
support computing systems dedicated to its specific application functions.
Most importantly, the midrange server provides departmental PC LANs
with an application and data gateway to the rest of the enterprise.
Authorized and verified data can be quickly transferred to the department
in a format that can easily be accessed by individual PCs and only
auditable data may be transferred to other systems throughout the
enterprise.

Aberdeen Notes:
Taking a look into the future, Aberdeen sees three classes of midrange
servers being the chief hardware building blocks for multiuser production
computing in larger enterprises.
Datacenter application servers will be located in what is today the central
datacenter. Each server will be dedicated to a major online application.
This minimizes software license pricing, provides the optimum response
time to end users, and allows the application server to grow as application
throughput requirements increase. Such datacenter application servers will
be linked via high speed fiber optic.
To provide a linkage with remote and distributed locations, midrange
servers acting as gateways will be used. These systems will be optimized
for high availability and will run the next-generation of transaction
monitors to ensure the efficient flow of data to applications while
maintaining data integrity. Changes in applications, data flows, and user
security can be managed through such gateways. These gateways may be
connected to the rest of the enterprise through a combination of LANs and
WANs (wide area networks).
Finally, distributed production servers will be placed at both remote sites
and in locations where physical security is of extreme importance.
