Aberdeen Notes:
Managing Change in Open Systems: A Technology Status Report provides
a straightforward review of the evolution of the computer industry’s vision
of Open Systems. The material includes a realistic discussion of the role of
Unix, as well as describing the changing nature of applications portability
and internetworking.
Aberdeen identifies the five significant component technologies that
suppliers are offering as building blocks for the next generation of
distributed, downsized information systems. These critical technology
planning areas are hardware (including major microprocessors), systems
software, application development toolsets, networking, and changing
architectures. The video will highlight the position of major suppliers in
each of these areas and will review the contributions of important
standards organizations.
Managing Change in Open Systems also provides an analysis of current and
emerging user requirements in a concise four point presentation. These are
the real issues driving the revolutionary trend toward components-based
information processing. Finally, the video seminar wrap-up contains action
items for the information technology professional and business executives
concerned with leveraging Open Systems for their own, unique enterprise
requirements.

Aberdeen Notes:
During the mid-1980s when the term Open Systems began to be widely used,
achieving "openness" was typically associated with two relatively simple
concepts: applications portability and communications interoperability.
Popular hardware suppliers during that period offered products based upon
proprietary architectures. Unfortunately, this meant applications that ran on
one system were often NOT available on other systems of a different design.
In order to avoid the constraints created by becoming locked-in with a
proprietary supplier, customers began to focus on Unix as the universal
operating system. Collectively we reasoned that if Unix were available on
multiple hardware platform types and sizes, then applications software written
for Unix would become easily portable from machine to machine. While Unix
has become increasingly popular, applications portability turned out to be too
narrow a vision of the future.
Today the industry has a broader perspective on Open Systems. It includes
powerful microprocessor-based PCs, workstations and servers, LANs and wide-
area-networks, relational databases, software toolsets, internetworking
equipment, increasingly standardized communications protocols, and a variety
of other technology components - perceived as interchangeable parts and
building blocks for next-generation systems.
Open Systems also poses important business as well as technology selection
issues for the 1990’s executive. As the information processing industry has
migrated from highly-centralized, proprietary systems to components-based
computing that is distributed throughout the organization, responsibility for
integrating these components has quietly shifted from the supplier to the
customer, causing enormous upheaval for both. Open Systems has meant new
multi-billion dollar opportunities for a number of young companies - and
severe headcount downsizing and bankruptcy for other traditional major
suppliers. For the user community, Open Systems now means access to a
dizzying array of powerful new technologies. It also mandates personnel
retraining, budget redistribution, and completely new business relationships
with the supplier community.

Aberdeen Notes:
The Information Systems organization must monitor and evaluate products,
suppliers, and trends in five important technology planning areas.
Key issues for the Information Systems Architecture include hardware
topologies: how to downsize or rebalance centralized mainframe systems,
and how to build distributed, client-server applications. Another important
component of the architecture is the integration of an organization’s
software systems, its cross-functional applications and databases.
Hardware systems include terminals, PCs, advanced workstations, servers
and multi-user systems plus a wide variety of peripherals. Aberdeen
emphasizes that these hardware platforms are increasingly built from
microprocessor building blocks.
Systems software includes not only operating systems, but database
management systems and other utility software designed to link distributed
computing environments together.
Application Development includes software toolsets, CASE (computer-
aided software engineering), 3GLs such as COBOL and C, Al-derived
languages such as LISP, and object-oriented environments with C++.
Networking includes LANs, wide-area network access, voice-data
integration, and inter-LAN connectivity. LANs and internetworking
continue to be the industry’s fastest growing and most challenging area.

Aberdeen Notes:
Many organizations have effectively structured their information systems
around an architectural concept that Aberdeen calls Three Tier Plus.
In this environment, the data center is still a focal point for traditional
production applications, especially those in the financial management
portfolio. Trends in the industry to downsize and build distributed systems
are clearly taking a toll on revenues and profits in this mainframe segment.
However, Aberdeen field research also indicates that many organizations
are building new strategic applications around the data center and
simultaneously rebalancing the mainframe - rather than simply throwing
out the mainframe in the first stages of a measured transition to Open
Systems.
New applications in the organization are being created on three types of
platforms outside the datacenter. These include the PC, workstation and
LAN workgroup environment for decision support, the midrange
departmental server and multiuser platform market driven by departmental
applications, replicated corporate systems and relational databases, and
analytical systems based largely upon loosely-coupled, massively parallel
hardware architectures. It is this last analytical category that represents the
"Plus" in Aberdeen’s architectural design.

A handful of microprocessor architectures have become the building blocks
for a majority of 1990’s hardware systems. In 1980, the designs listed
above represented less than 3% of worldwide hardware revenues. In 1992,
these microprocessors account for more than 65% of worldwide hardware
revenues -- growing to 75% or 80% by 1995. Intel, of course, dominates
unit shipments with its 80x86 architecture and PC base. Aberdeen expects
that Intel will ship nearly 30 million of these devices in 1993 alone!
Hewlett-Packard, IBM, and Sun Microsystems have achieved a significant
performance and market position with their respective reduced instruction
set (RISC) architectures. Digital Equipment has introduced Alpha RISC
during 1992 and hopes to regain momentum with this design during the
balance of the 1990s. Motorola dominated the technical workstation and
embedded systems market in the 1980s with 680XX. Its marketing
programs for 88xxx RISC have, however, failed to generate as much
enthusiasm. Motorola is seen by Aberdeen, therefore, as focusing
increasing attention on its partnership with IBM for Power Architecture.
MIPS Computer Systems was acquired by Silicon Graphics in 1992 after
the demise of the Advanced Computing Environment and loss of partner
Digital Equipment. While MIPS has stabilized and is delivering the 64-bit
R4000, the company is not playing as wide an industry role. Likewise,
Fairchild’s Clipper and the AMD 29000 are being effectively used as
specialized parts for specific market opportunities.

the user must consider IBM OS/2, the forthcoming IBM/Apple Power PC,
USL’s Destiny, Sun Solaris, Hewlett-Packard HP-UX with Empower, and
Santa Cruz Operation’s Open Desktop, in addition to Windows/NT.
Longer term, Aberdeen sees a move to object-oriented systems. In this

this important, next-generation opportunity.

Aberdeen Notes:
A critical element in the success of a PC computing implementation is the
local area network or PC LAN.
Through an excellent combination of technology, packaging, pricing, and
vibrant distribution channels, Novell has achieved dominance in the PC
LAN market. By building an enviable indirect channel partners program,
the company has been successful in companies across a wide spectrum of
revenue size classes and vertical markets.
Banyan is best known in larger organizations where its Vines-based
directory services and complex network feature support can be leveraged.
In order to expand its market opportunities and presence, Banyan has
identified ways to integrate its value-added technology with Novell servers.
Despite its intense marketing programs and well-regarded technology,
Microsoft’s LAN Manager has not dominated the market through
independent channels. Only through partners like Digital Equipment with
Pathworks and IBM (whose PC LAN product was originally based on a
LAN Manager-operating system partnership) has Microsoft struggled to
achieve 26% share.
Recognizing the importance of this market, Microsoft continues to press
for increased penetration. The company recently announced Windows-for-
Workgroups and has also tightly integrated network functionality into
Windows/NT. Likewise, Novell has partnered with Unix Systems
Laboratories, (USL) to form Univel so that it can broaden its reach and
viability. Banyan will come under increasing market pressure if it cannot
find a partner, especially as the competitors add more sophisticated
distributed computing functionality to their product offerings.

Aberdeen cannot emphasize strongly enough that the success of any Open
Systems initiative is tied largely to the effectiveness of the enterprise’s
software development strategy. It is critical to understand that traditional
applications will need to be completely rewritten in order to take best
advantage of new hardware platforms and architectures such as distributed

RDBMS (relational database management systems) are not only the
mainstream choice for data management in a single department application

RDBMSs are also the glue of next generation distributed systems with
features like triggers, procedure recognition, and two-phase commit.

□ COAX
□ TWISTED PAIR
□ FIBER
Aberdeen Notes:
When planning for networks, the information systems group has options
and choices on three fundamental levels. The first is wiring alternatives.
Coaxial cable was used for a long period of time as the primary means of
connecting terminals and computers. It was also the first mainstream
wiring approach used for local area networks. Although coax (both the
thin and thick varieties) can be characterized as supporting secure, high-
volume data transfers, it is also expensive, heavy, and not particularly easy
to install or maintain.
The industry is moving away from coax to twisted-pair copper and fiber
optic wiring implementations. Twisted-pair comes in various types and
grades. The simplest to understand is basic voice-grade telephone wire.
The LAN wiring scheme 10-Base T uses this unshielded copper cable. It
is an attractive option because twisted-pair is already installed in the
building - or represents an easy and inexpensive dual connection
(telephone and data line) installation. IBM offers shielded twisted pair to
ensure the security of the wiring plant.
Thus far, fiber optic cable has largely been installed as a backbone network
wiring media. Increasingly, however, as fiber has become less expensive
and easier to work with during installation, its high speed, high bandwidth
characteristics have become popular with customers for new application
areas such as multimedia or technical computing LANs.
The industry is pursuing through supplier consortiums and standards
committees ways to increase transfer speeds on less expensive media.
Aberdeen believes that this trend will soon make it possible to achieve
high volume throughput on superior grade copper, while using fiber for the
especially complex application environments and for network backbones.

Aberdeen Notes:
Once the wiring system is in place, computer systems communicate with
one another by using one or more network access methods.
Ethernet has become a standard in the PC and workstation LAN markets.
Ethernet is based upon a broadcast architecture. This means that any
computer can send a message across the LAN at any time, and that all
other computers are always listening - and waiting to capture a message
addressed specifically to them. If two computers transmit simultaneously, a
collision occurs on the LAN. This collision is corrected by the Ethernet
protocol. Both computers then wait and retransmit at a later, random
interval. Ethernet offers transmission speeds of 10 megabits per second.
Token Ring, originally developed by Proteon, Inc., was brought to market
by IBM and is prevalent in IBM-based accounts. This access method uses
a token (a unique combination of bits) which is passed from workstation to
workstation. The device that has the token at any single point in time is
permitted to transmit data over the LAN. All workstations between the
sender and the ultimate destination decode enough of the message to
determine whether to accept the message or simply pass it along. The
receiving workstation releases the token to be used by the next device in
line. Token Ring supports 4 and 16 megabits per second speeds and is
typically found on IBM’s shielded twisted-pair.

Aberdeen Notes:
FDDI (fiber distributed data interface) is a third, established LAN access
methodology -- and a high speed outgrowth of work in token ring
technology. FDDI runs at 100 megabits per second. FDDI was developed
for fiber optic media, but work is well along with CDDI (C is for copper),
an attempt to offer FDDI transmission speeds on twisted-pair copper.
Likewise, if fiber media is installed in a facility, it is possible to run
Ethernet on it. While this results in slower speeds, it also is much less
expensive to use Ethernet adapters than FDDI adapters.
ATM (asynchronous transfer mode) is an emerging network access
technology originally developed as a high speed interconnect among the
telephone carriers. ATM employs switching to achieve two things:
dedicated connections between or among devices and extremely high data
transfer rates. ATM is increasingly of interest to workstation and LAN
suppliers that believe it will accelerate the acceptance of complex, high-
volume applications such as graphics intensive multimedia or video­
conferencing. ATM is being championed by a supplier consortium called
the ATM Forum.

□ HUBS

Aberdeen Notes:
In order to interconnect distributed computing systems and LANs, a whole
new group of suppliers has been introduced to the communications market
over the last five years. These suppliers of inter-LAN components, often
called internetworking equipment, provide products designed to meet
various end-user requirements.
Hubs act as a wiring center, traffic cop and concentrator for local area
networks. Wiring media (typically twisted-pair copper) runs from the hub
to each connected device. Often a fiber optic link connects two hubs,
perhaps on two different floors of an office building.
Bridges allow a workstation on one LAN to communicate with a device on
another local area network. Bridges are modestly intelligent devices and
simply pass messages from one loop to the next without interpretation.
Routers, on the other hand, intelligently sort, prioritize and direct messages
from one device to the other. They are also capable of translating
messaging protocols in heterogeneous LAN environments.
Switches are inter-LAN devices that establish direct and dedicated
connections between computers in different LAN environments. The
dedicated, switched connection improves performance when there are many
devices degrading throughput or when there is a high volume of data
transfer.

Aberdeen Notes:
Standards organizations play a vital role in Open Systems. One of the first
important groups was the International Standards Organization (ISO). It
created the Open Systems Interconnect (OSI) seven layer networking
model. OSI standards are widely accepted as an industry communications
reference model. However, Aberdeen notes that most suppliers still
predominantly offer their own implementations of OSI rather than selling
OSI-specific products at a low generic-brand price point.
The Open Software Foundation was created by Digital Equipment,
Hewlett-Packard, and IBM as a response to the mid-1980s perceived threat
of an alliance between AT&T and Sun Microsystems. It was felt that if
these two organizations were to control the Unix operating system, future
opportunities for the other suppliers would be at risk. OSF developed the
MOTIF graphical user interface. The group is also responsible for an open
operating system called OSF/1, the distributed computing environment
(DCE), and the distributed management environment (DME).

As a response to OSF, AT&T and Sun founded Unix International. Unix
International was chartered to provide guidance to Unix Systems

Unix. Since those early days, AT&T has spun off USL and it has been

all equity interests in Sun Microsystems.
The operating systems wars seemed to have cooled for now, as most
suppliers have either begun or plotted a strategy to secure, multiprocessing
versions of either Unix or OSF/1. For all the players in OSF and UI, the
nature of the competition has shifted from debates about operating system
kernels to the increasingly important issue of how to build and manage a
distributed computing environment.
X/Open is a consortium of suppliers and users that has attempted to create
a model for these next generation computing concepts called the Common
Application Environment (CAE). The group has published several releases
of the X/Open Portability Guide that sets out the rules for conforming to
its vision. The group was originally founded in Europe and has thus far
had limited impact on the United States, even though it boasts many U.S.
corporations as members. Aberdeen believes that X/Open has the
potential to play a wider role in North America, especially if OSF and
Unix International fail to maintain membership or lose credibility and
marketing momentum.

Aberdeen Notes:
Aberdeen field research and analysis provide insight into the changing
nature of our industry’s real vision for Open Systems. In our opinion, this
is what Open Systems really means.
1. The end-user can now be given access to an almost infinite variety of
custom computing solutions at the desktop. From a large variety of
hardware configurations (memoiy, peripherals, processing power, etc.) to
an enormous portfolio of powerful systems and applications software, the
industry has nearly kept pace with the users’ insatiable appetite for
everything, anytime, anywhere.
2. Once local processing requirements have been satisfied, the end-user
typically wants to reach out across a network for one of two additional
things: incremental processing power and/or more and different types of
information. Either of these items can be positioned anywhere inside (or
outside) the enterprise from the PC LAN network server to a remote,
lights-out data collection node on the Arctic Circle.
3. The user and the Information Technology management team are
increasingly unforgiving if new technologies - some of which we haven’t
even discovered yet - cannot be seamlessly integrated into the Open
Systems architecture without throwing away all our existing systems.
Customers want access to scalable solutions and new whiz-bang
functionality without the need to discard what they currently have in place.
4. 
Users want all of the above at a reasonable cost. Competition is
fierce. Components are available from multiple sources. Commodity
pricing is increasingly the standard rather than an exception to the rule.

Aberdeen Notes:
For the executive who needs a memory-jogging catch phrase to capture the
essence of this discussion on Managing Change in Open Systems, Aberdeen
recommends an ACTION plan. The six letters in this word can be
translated into key ideas in the following way.
A is for Architecture. The Information Systems architectures of the 1990s
demonstrate a trend toward open, distributed, object-oriented, client-server
systems.
C is for Components. New information technology consists of components
such as microprocessors, RDBMSs, plug-and-play peripherals, network
protocols, and software operating systems. Components of these building
blocks are often interchangeable. This provides the customer with
flexibility and high performance computing. Interchangeable components
are a hallmark of Open Systems.
T is for Timing. The MIS executive must understand rates of change. In
order to implement projects successful, the acquisition of a given
technology must occur in time to improve information systems effectiveness
and efficiency - but not before the technology is ready for prime time.

Aberdeen Notes:
I is for Integration. The price for access to the flexibility and power of
Open Systems technology components is that the customer must assume
increasing responsibility for integrating these technologies into a complete
solution. For example, traditional proprietary systems came with the
implicit supplier warranty that all components would work together:
terminals, operating systems, hardware platforms, data management, and
even applications. Now the customer is increasingly responsible for the
integration of these modules - typically created by multiple suppliers.
Unless the customer has found a trusted business partner in its hardware
supplier, professional services firm, or integrator -- Caveat emptor!
O is for Organization. The impact of Open Systems is placing great
demands on the organization. These changes mandate staff retraining,
new business relationships with suppliers as well as new kinds of
responsibilities for MIS within the enterprise. Open Systems and new
technology options also tend to flatten the user organization and suggest
enormous possibilities for business-process reengineering.
N is for New Economics. Margins for traditional products are shrinking,
forcing suppliers to cut costs and people. Distribution channels are
changing as the direct sales trends toward the indirect and innovative
channels program. There is greater demand for professional services than
ever. The economics of the computer industry are changing dramatically,
forcing those who fail to react quickly and effectively to financial losses or
bankruptcy and oblivion.

### Abstract

This Aberdeen Group workbook provides a comprehensive status report on Open Systems as of January 1993, covering the transition from proprietary to open, distributed computing architectures. It assesses five key technology components — hardware microprocessors, systems software, application development toolsets, networking, and emerging architectures — and profiles major vendor positions and standards organizations. The study culminates in an ACTION framework for IS executives managing the organizational and economic challenges of open systems adoption.
