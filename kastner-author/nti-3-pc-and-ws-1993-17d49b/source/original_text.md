Aberdeen Notes:
PCs and Workstations: Vying for the 1990’s Desktop will cover the issues
surrounding the next generation of desktop systems.
As enterprises wrestle with the issues involved in migrating from a
mainframe-centric computing environment to the new client-server
information architecture, the decision concerning which desktop systems to
implement throughout the enterprise is taking on even greater importance
than before.
At this time, PCs and workstations are vying to be enterprise buyers’ choice
for the next generation of desktop systems. Intel-powered PCs claim to be
the system of choice in terms of low price and support of all major
operating systems — Microsoft’s Windows family including MS-DOS and
NT, IBM’s OS/2, and several variants of Unix including USL’s System 5.4.2
(Destiny); Apple claims that it has all the features — and more — in the
Macintosh that Intel/Windows-based PCs are attempting to achieve.
Yet workstations powered by Rise processors and running the Unix
operating system were originally designed with the functionality — excellent
graphics, multi-tasking operating system, high performance processors,
built-in networking — that IS executives are demanding in their next
generation desktop systems. While price has been the primary objection to
workstations for commercial applications, the industry saw Sun
Microsystems break the $4,000 barrier in 1992 and the other workstation
vendors vow to match this price in 1993.

Terminals still represent the majority of clerical user data input/output
devices because they are inexpensive and most multiuser ISV software for
applications such as accounting and manufacturing resource planning do
not use the potential of an intelligent desktop system.
PCs are the winners for stand alone business applications such as word
processing, spreadsheets, and personal database as well as technical/casual
applications such as two dimensional technical drafting due to the
availability of applications, relatively low-cost, and wide spread user
literacy.
And due to fast floating point and graphics performance, built-in
networking and essentially unlimited program size, workstations are the
users best bet for technical/power applications.
But as interconnected workgroups have become more important, PCs and
workstations are vying for the desktop.
And finally, to lower the users’ cost per seat, there is a dramatic increase in
the use of both PCs and workstations as servers in place of traditional
multiuser systems. In this case, the PC or workstation acting as a server
can have an attached X-station, a lightly configured PC, or even a terminal
attached to lower the total workgroup price.

Aberdeen Notes:
Even with billions invested in terminals and pre-1992 PCs, IS executives
are under increasing pressure to replace these desktop systems in order to
improve operational effectiveness. This change will represent capital
expenditures over the next several years that will account for up to 50% of
the capital expenditures of many enterprises.
GUI’s are a requirement in the 1990s for two major reasons. First, a GUI
can pay for itself many times over by decreasing training time in high
turnover positions where an employee is typically taking information from
a customer or prospect and then responding to the directions of an online
transaction processing (OLTP) system. Second, a GUI can coach casual
users, including enterprise decision makers, through applications that they
only access occasionally.
To allow for the effective implementation of cooperative processing
software and to increase individual knowledge users’ efficiency, the current
generation of MS-DOS PCs needs to be upgraded to include multi-tasking
capability.
Very importantly, desktop systems, with their relatively cheap MIPS, simply
must do more of the enterprise processing than they do today to off load
the more expensive multiuser — mainframe and midrange — systems.

The decisions made about the correct desktop systems will affect the
success of the enterprise throughout the rest of the decade. IS decision
makers know that this is a once-in-a-generation decision upon which future
enterprise-wide information processing capabilities will be based. As a
result, IS executives are delaying the enterprise-wide implementation of any
one technology until they feel comfortable it will meet their critical
requirements.
When making the major 3-5 year capital investment that the next­
generation of desktop systems will represent, buyers must be sure that
these systems will be client-server ready.
To lower IS administrative overhead costs, the systems must be able to be
administered through open systems-based network management tools.
The desktop systems must be available for a low price to allow the
enterprise to allow each employee access to one at work and to ensure that
a compatible system is affordable for home use.

Aberdeen Notes:
There seem to be as many definitions of client-server computing as there
are suppliers of computer hardware and software. However, most users
define the goal of client-server computing to be the ability to actually take
advantage of the relatively low-cost MIPS embodied in desktop systems.
This topology is best embodied in cooperative processing variant of client­
server computing.
However, the most common type of desktop-based client-server computing
that is actually implemented today is remote presentation — the desktop
system, typically a PC in this instance, merely provides the user with a
pretty picture of the application and data in the form of a graphical user
interface (GUI).
More sophisticated client-server applications running today use the desktop
system to access multiple remote databases simultaneously. For these
applications, the workstation is a more common enterprise choice.
Due to developer design issues and time lags involved in implementing
client-server applications, IS decision makers want to make sure that their
next generation of desktop systems will have the capability to run all the
variants of client-server shown above.

Experienced IS managers have found that with a desktop-based computing
topology, systems administration costs actually increase — not decrease as
hoped for. And the problem is increased exponentially with the number of
desktop systems and the variety of applications.
IS is demanding that desktop systems be able to be completely managed
remotely; this includes remote backup, application distribution and update,
new user additions/retired user deletions, and hardware/software error
detection and diagnostics.
Finally, the network must be able to support different hardware products,
including printers, from different manufacturers and even platforms with
different operating systems. And it should be flexible enough to accept
intermittent attachment from laptops, vest pocket, and mobile computers.

Aberdeen Notes:
Like kids playing the childhood game I-Dare-You, the users and
independent software vendors are each looking to the other to see who
really intends to wholeheartedly support which future desktop system.
While independent software vendors (ISVs) will commonly state support
for a system, product delivery may never materialize if enough users do not
support it. Or the technology of a new operating environment may be
beyond the skill levels of an already over burdened ISV’s development
staff.
On the other hand, a few large, key customers may demand that an ISV
supplier support a new desktop system only to discover six months latter
that this was not the beginning of a user trend but only an anomaly. And
then, even our leading-but-over-the-edge customer will typically retrench.
The next generation desktop system must be fully and competently
supported by traditional multiuser application ISVs for client-server
computing, traditional end-user mass-market PC ISVs and specialty ISVs
that develop low-volume products for technical professionals.

Aberdeen Notes:
At first glance, the requirements for both stability and flexibility seem
inconsistent. Yet with the rapid product cycles in the desktop, users are
demanding stability of the base system while having the flexibility to
smoothly expand its functionality when there is a substantial advantage to
do so. And with 12-18 month product cycles, this seems to be a
requirement every 12-18 months even though the nucleus desktop hardware
will remain essentially unchanged for 3-5 years.
The faster processing speeds of RISC processors, the ability to
reimplement RISC chips in shorter design cycles to take advantage of the
latest technology and their lower-level design points would seem to make
them a natural for the desktop. But the historical CICS designs of both
Intel and Motorola are the winners in maintaining compatibility with
installed boxes and keeping the prices of their volume shipments to a
minimum.
The hardware components used in next-generation of desktop systems must
be both stable and have the industry support required to allow
customization and upgrading through board add-ins.
And most importantly, the operating environment must be both stable and
yet flexible enough to allow new features to be added. This is extremely
difficult to do — historically most operating systems have take five years to
mature to the point where the majority of users felt they were production
ready.

IS executives want application portability and scalability — write an

and with only one set of source code to maintain. This was the initial
promise of Digital’s VMS operating system in the early 1980s and IBM’s

computing topologies, it becomes a requirement that the next-generation
desktop be compatible with senior multiuser systems.
The workstation suppliers are taking advantage of the current technology
of their Unix operating systems by scaling their workstation and server
versions of Unix into lower cost desktops systems.
At the same time, Microsoft, as the leading desktop operating system
supplier, recognizes this and is positioning Windows/NT as the common
operating environment.

Microsoft’s strategy is to provide compatible operating system for the

NetWare network operating system with compatible operating systems for
both Intel-based PCs and workstations.

marketplace with its Unix-for-Intel products, Santa Cruz Operation has still
found its greatest success in providing a multiuser operating environment,
in conjunction with Informix and other RDBMS suppliers, for small
business groups. We do not expect to see SCO/Unix participate in a major
way in the next generation of desktop systems — there are simply too many
better alternatives.

### Abstract

This Aberdeen Group workbook analyzes the competitive landscape between Intel-powered PCs and RISC/Unix workstations for enterprise desktop dominance in the 1993-1996 timeframe. It defines workgroup computing requirements, compares platforms across client-server capability, networking, cost, application software, and multiuser compatibility, and evaluates supplier strategies from IBM, Apple, Compaq, Dell, Sun, HP, and operating system vendors. Aberdeen recommends workstations for technical/power users, low-end Unix workstations as NT contingency, Macs for functionality, and PCs for all other users through 1993.
