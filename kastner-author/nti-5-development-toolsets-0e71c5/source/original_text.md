Aberdeen Notes:
There can be no disagreement that, despite all the trade press headlines
about hardware price-performance improvements, the biggest challenges
facing information systems departments continue to be software
development and programmer productivity. The industry has made steady
progress in introducing new tools, including 4GLS, sophisticated data
management systems, and more recently point-and-click graphical user
interface (GUI) builders. However, the majority of installations still are
constrained by the fact that programmers trained solely in COBOL
maintenance or proprietary systems maintenance simply are not prepared
for the rapid prototyping and distributed systems production development
of the 1990s.
As an industry, both software suppliers and MIS departments must improve
software quality, improve the turnaround of new applications and bug fixes
to existing programs, and reduce costs of development and maintenance.
For software suppliers this improvement will translate into reduced time to
market and improved profitability. It will also mean being able to port
efficiently and more rapidly to a wider variety of hardware platforms.
From the perspective of both the software developer supplier and MIS
department, only the acquisition and effective use of automated toolsets
will enable us to break the software development bottleneck so that we can
really leverage the benefits of new hardware and networking technologies
available today.

Aberdeen Notes:
Aberdeen notes five important field research findings.
Before the industry can realistically expect to achieve its vision of
distributed, client-server systems, software development techniques and
technology will need to improve. Although many new and powerful
products have been introduced over the last five years, building on the base
of 4GLs from the early 1980s, it is fair to say that development expertise in
using them is still constraining overall industry growth.
Second, beyond the use of tools, Aberdeen believes that the
implementation of structured design and modular programming techniques
will position the enterprise for easier documentation, more code reuse, and
a smooth transition to object-oriented systems.
Third, there is more emphasis than ever on heavy end-user involvement in
application prototyping. Users want - and must be given - an opportunity
to participate in an iterative software development process. Gone are the
days when a requirements analysis were submitted, to be followed by a
rigid, out-of-date, 3GL test system months later. Now we must provide a
rough outline and test, change, modify, test again and then withdraw to
develop the robust production version.

Aberdeen Notes:
Our fourth finding is that the complex nature of distributed, client-server
applications that fit the requirements of a high competitive 1990s business
environment simply cannot be built using manual programming techniques.
While it is useful to exit certain portions of the application to invoke 3GLs
for "heavy lifting," only automated toolsets can construct the code modules
and manage the multiple variables of a sophisticated distributed, GUI-
based computing architecture.
Finally, we have discovered from our interviews that attempts to build an
"enterprise scale" repository have largely turned into failed initiatives. This
does not mean that individual toolsets, databases, and application
development environments do not need or effectively use their own
repositories. It does mean that waiting to deploy a system until all the
elements and entity relationships of a large project in a multi-national
corporation are in place is a recipe for disaster.

PROGRAMS FOR SOFTWARE TECHNOLOGIES
Aberdeen Notes:
Because internal programming experience is often limited to COBOL and
traditional 4GLs, the evaluation of new toolset technology is sometimes
challenging. Simply differentiating among various products features and
functions can be confusing. Nevertheless, because costs associated with
maintenance and new application programming represent such a large
percentage of the MIS or line-of-business departmental budget — successful
evaluation and experimentation are a key to success.
For this reason, Aberdeen recommends that IS departments separate the
RDBMS from the tools decision - it isn’t always necessary to select the
RDBMS supplier’s toolkit. It can sometimes be supplemented or replaced
by other products. Keep in mind that the 3GL, especially C and C+ +, still
represent a viable component of the overall application development
strategy.
While the customer should prepare as best as they can for object-oriented
development, in 1993 it is still a bit early for the mainstream MIS
department to standardize on any one supplier or approach to OOPS. We
recommend that customers watch carefully developments of the Object
Management Group, one that is focused on creating meaningful standards
in this area.
Finally, we have discovered that the most successful software development
activities occur in IS groups where senior executives fund a continuing
stream of staff training and product evaluation opportunities.

Aberdeen Notes:
Optimum software development strategies concentrate on three major
areas. The first is consideration of the current applications and systems
software portfolio. There are opportunities to enhance and redeploy these
legacies systems -- although the industry’s earlier vision that existing
applications would simply be submitted to a black-box translator for
reengineering have largely failed to meet expectations. Most redeployment
occurs as porting and rehosting applications on new, high-performance
microprocessor-based servers. Reengineering has come to mean
rearchitecting the application in conjunction with a complete review of
fundamental business processes, or business process reengineering.
More typical of new development projects is the creation of strategic,
mission-critical applications on high-performance servers with 4GLs,
toolsets, and RDBMS platforms.
To accomplish either of these objectives requires a continuous cycle of
planning, training, and experimentation -- with full participation of the
professional IS staff and the end-user customer.

Computer-aided software engineering (CASE) is considered by Aberdeen
to be one segment of the software development tools market, rather than
an overreaching, umbrella term used to describe the entire area. When we
think of CASE products, we include product from companies such as
Andersen Consulting (Foundation), Knowledgeware, Texas Instruments
(IEF), and Intersolv (formerly Index Technology and Sage Software)
These products focus on full life cycle from design/analysis to code
generation and documentation for large scale, mainframe systems
applications and have not been used as often as rapid prototyping products
for distributed, PC and PC LAN-based deployment environments.

Aberdeen Notes:
Aberdeen sees a continuing decline for Fortran in commercial applications.
Even in the language’s traditional technical/engineering stronghold there is
more emphasis on C and C+ +.
Likewise, ADA seems confined to the federal government sector where its
strengths in large project management and mandates by the defense
department have created an installed base.
COBOL continues as the language of choice for large-scale mainframe
environments and has been sustained by various CASE products. Many
data centers have gone to maintenance mode on COBOL applications --
and denied funds for additional mainframe-based application development.
Even MicroFocus COBOL for microprocessor-based systems is under
pressure from the C language.
C and C+ + are clearly the languages of choice for new application
development - especially in the independent software vendor (ISV)
community. Aberdeen, however, does not recommend or expect that the
majority of programmers in commercial MIS organizations will write all
new applications in C. Rather, we envision a mostly-tools, some C and
C+ + multi-component development strategy emerging over the next two
years. In the ISV community, we expect continued emphasis on C and a
gradual migration to object orientation.

Aberdeen Notes:
There are four key components of emerging strategic applications. At the
interface layer are presentation services. Increasingly, applications require
a graphical user interface. Major GUI alternatives include Windows,
Macintosh, Motif, and OpenLook. ISV developers are having difficulty
with the expense associated with supporting all of these options.
Therefore, automated toolkits are being developed to create the GUI at
runtime. Some of these will be discussed in the following pages.
The business logic of the application is most often created today with a
4GL - with exits to 3GLs such as C for optimization. For example, a
programmer might develop an application using Oracle Forms (4GL) and
then exit to call customized C subroutines when optimized performance
became a requirement or if the complexity of the application outran the
functionality of the Forms product.
At the lowest level, the procedural portion of the application will require
access to data, either in the local file environment or in a host-based
distributed environment. The industry standard for this exchange has
become Structured Query Language (SQL). One major challenge is that
much of the enterprise’s core data elements are still in a hierarchical rather
than relational mainframe file structure. Thus despite progress with SQL
services, it continues to be difficult to access legacy systems data. Bridges
to IBM’s IMS files, for example, are required.

Aberdeen Notes:
MIS buyers are confused by the huge variety of client-server toolsets. The
bottom line for MIS is to acquire toolsets capable of scaling to enterprise
applications and which have proven to have the robustness needed to
protect data in mission-critical applications.
There is no universally satisfactory client, so most organizations are dealing
with multiple client platforms running, typically, Windows, OS/2,
Macintosh, and Unix. There is a huge ongoing benefit to tools which
minimize the effort needed to port and support multiple platforms.
There are many different classes of client-server tools. Specialty tools such
as end-user report writers should be used for what they are best at, not
stretched to fit all applications.
Today’s pilot applications targeted at small workgroups should consider
how the toolsets will scale to production-scale where hundreds and even
thousands of clients will be present.
Lastly, some toolsets recognize the software lifecycle of design, develop,
deploy, and enhance. We expect more product functionality aimed at
supporting multi-developer projects.

□ POSITION OF OBJECT MANAGEMENT GROUP
Aberdeen Notes:
In Aberdeen’s opinion, object-oriented programming is an approach to
software development that will likely revolutionize the industry by the end
of the 1990’s. Object-oriented software is not, however, a market as much
as a method or software design strategy.
Unlike procedural programs, that move from the first to last line of
instructions and refer to separate data files, objects contain both data and
instructions. In this sense, each object is a self-contained program. What
is important is not how the object works internally, but rather how and
whether it can interact with other objects. From the simplest and most
superficial perspective, it is possible to consider an object-oriented program
similar to a program with a maximum number of subroutines. Each of the
subroutines executes a certain task when called into service by the master
program.
The Object Management Group, headquartered outside Boston, is a
consortium of suppliers and large MIS organizations dedicated to building
distributed systems based on objects. It is working to standardize the types
of service requests that objects should universally provide to one another,
as well as how objects behave in distributed systems.

Aberdeen Notes:
The opportunity to leverage a variety of new software development toolsets
into gains in programmer productivity is clearly available to MIS
departments in 1993. There are several important ways to insure success.
First, it is important to understand the features and functions of each of
the new categories of products, in order to establish realistic expectations
for the development organization and end-users about what each class of
product can and cannot do.
Second, it is important to become well-informed about the specific
strengths and weaknesses of individual offerings. Where do various
suppliers overlap in functionality and where do specific supplier lead their
competitors? Only by participating in seminars, conferences, and product
briefings - as well as by experimenting with the leading technologies - can
the MIS group stay abreast of the rapidly changing landscape.
Third, it is important to identify the types of tools and the specific suppliers
whose product strengths match the vision and needs of your enterprise and
its information systems architecture.
Finally, and we cannot emphasize this enough, plan and budget for
increased staff training and development.

### Abstract

This Aberdeen Group workbook surveys the rapidly changing software development toolset landscape in 1993, presenting key field research findings from CIO interviews and ISV briefings. It identifies best-in-class tools across 3GLs, 4GLs, RDBMS-integrated tools, GUI builders, and CASE, and defines the three-layer architecture — presentation, business logic, data access — of strategic applications. The study evaluates client-server development readiness, positions emerging GUI tools from Powersoft, Gupta, and others, and previews object-oriented technology as a late-1990s mainstream prospect.
