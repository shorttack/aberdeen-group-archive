Aberdeen Notes:
There is no issue in enterprise computing that is debated so passionately as
the fate of the mainframe. In this chapter of Aberdeen’s New Technology
Impact Series, we will objectively and realistically evaluate The Role of the
Mainframe.
To kick-off this chapter, Aberdeen will explain why IBM is losing its
dominance in corporate computing by its unwillingness or inability to
transform the ES/9000 into the computing platform-of-choice for the
future. We will then compare IBM to how Unisys is evolving its A-Series
mainframe to meet the needs of MIS — and growing in a supposedly dying
mainframe marketplace. Next, we will characterize how mainframe and
midrange hardware is blending to provide users with systems that are best
described as simply multiuser. And then we will explore the most
important mainframe technology — applications and application
development software. The subsequent issue is personnel related — how
MIS staff will evolve as the economics of mainframe computing change.
Finally, Aberdeen will clarify the IS-executive issues raised by the choice of
either evolving current mainframe applications to other platforms versus
developing applications anew using the green field approach.

Aberdeen Notes:
The paradox in the world of IBM mainframes is that data centers are
continuing to add MIPS capacity at a rate of 30% per year yet the
mainframe suppliers are all losing money. For example, IBM claims that it
shipped more mainframe MIPS in 1992 than in any other year — but 1992
was its least profitable year. And, IS executives considered mainframe
CPUs to be purely commodity MIPS — forcing prices so low that IBM and
Amdahl have thrown away their price books as irrelevant.
Even more daunting is the senior management decree in the majority of
user organizations that no more application development will be done on
the mainframe — mainframe applications are to be kept merely in
maintenance mode.
From the supplier perspective, mainframe business is less profitable than
even the PC-clone business. For example, Amdahl’s gross margins are
running at 30% compared to 45% for PC-clone manufactures. And
mainframe suppliers IBM, Fujitsu, Hitachi and Bull are all blaming their
poor 1992 financial results and requirements to lay-off employees on the
sickness of the mainframe business.
And yet, with all this chaos, almost every Fortune 500-sized enterprise
relies on mainframes to run its business. Something has to change.

Aberdeen Notes:
Simply put, the IBM mainframe architecture does not address key business
economic and operational issues in today’s world.
The most critical issue is that users must wait 18-to-36 months for major
new applications. Even trivial report changes require 6-to-9 months,
depending upon the importance of the requester.
IS spending is now approximately 50% of all capital spending in many
enterprises. Yet capital spending is typically done with the intention of
obtaining a return. However, today, mainframes are viewed as a cost
center with negative returns.
But the final and fatal flaw of the IBM mainframe may be that it does not
allow line managers the authority and capability to run their operations
using IS resources proactively. And internal mainframe MIS staffs possess
neither the time or propensity to aggressively respond to enterprise­
business issues.
In any struggle between these two groups, operational line management
and MIS staff, senior line management will eventually prevail.

Aberdeen Notes:
The future for the mainframe business at IBM is not promising, with
management playing Russian-roulette games at headquarters. Aberdeen
believes that it is unlikely new senior executives will commit to re­
invigorating the mainframe business. As the new CEO has said, he is most
interested in stock price-performance. Aberdeen believes that this
portends a disinvestment out of mainframes.
The installed base of IBM mainframes continues to decline 7% per year, as
datacenters are consolidated. To make up for the short fall in new
software licenses, both IBM and the most prominent independent software
vendors (ISVs) will continue to increase annual maintenance prices. This
creates a destructive cycle of increasingly fewer systems and a higher
supplier software tax on those remaining.
Finally, the stock market has punished IBM for its mistakes in the
mainframe world by driving its market valuation down to the level of more
customer relevant suppliers.

Aberdeen Notes:
Aberdeen’s hard-nosed assessment of IBM’s mainframe business comes
down to the bottom line that IBM simply did not advance the ES/9000
technology as it either could or should have to meet the requirements of
the 90’s. MVS remained a closed operating system; mainframe TCM
processor technology fell behind industry standard microprocessor
technology; IBM’s DB2 relational data base technology trails by several
generations independent software competitors focusing on the midrange;
IBM has not upgraded mainframe disk drive systems to RAID technology.
Aberdeen believes that IBM and other IBM-mainframe-oriented suppliers
will attempt to harvest the mainframe business to maintain profit margins.
Computer Associates’, the largest independent mainframe-oriented
software supplier, pricing models exemplify this trend.
Based upon our user interviews Aberdeen projects that IBM mainframes
will be gradually phased out of enterprises over the next decade — and will
probably be irrelevant within the next 2-to-4 years. IBM could have
reversed this consequence if it had recognized its dwindling market power.
And IBM’s mainframe division (or whatever entity might emerge) still
might try to make a comeback when faced with massive cost-cutting.

that allows users to plug in low-cost, industry-standard peripherals. The
result has been that A-Series users can maintain their investment in
applications and trained personnel while evolving into a downsized Unix
and PC topology.

Aberdeen Notes:
Advanced users are simply using the term multiuser to describe the senior
system in their enterprise computing topology. At this time, advanced
midrange systems are functionally more powerful than 95% of all installed
mainframes for all applications — including batch processing.
The high-end RISC processor technology found in many midrange systems
now has the power of the largest mainframe TCM-based processors. And
the next generation RISC processors will handily out perform mainframes.
For this reason, IBM has announced that it will be porting its MVS
operating system to its RISC-based RS/6000 platforms.
And while many have postulated that there would be a role for the
mainframe as a data repository because of the mainframe’s superior
input/output (I/O) bandwidth, new midrange systems from suppliers such
as Hewlett-Packard and NCR are actually providing greater I/O then
IBM’s peak performance of 9.5 MB/SEC with ESCOM. And disk arrays
are standard on midrange systems while just being introduced on
mainframes.
The result is that midrange computers can even more efficiently perform
the intensive batch applications that were formally the sole province of
mainframes. And the hardware technology superiority claimed for
mainframes over high-end midrange systems is insignificant for running
user applications.

Aberdeen Notes:
More important than hardware is the software required to make the
computing platform easily do what users want.
Recognizing this market reality, IBM attempted to put new life in the
mainframe application marketplace with the introduction of AD/Cycle in
1990. However AD/Cycle has become a fiasco since IBM could not
provide the promised services. The result was the decline of mainframe-
oriented independent software vendors who were depending upon
AD/Cycle to complement their products.
As the marketplace moves to relational data base management systems
(RDBMS), IBM has attempted to migrate users to DB2. Unfortunately,
DB2 simply does not have the functionality contained in competitive
midrange RDBMSs from suppliers such as Oracle, Ingres and Sybase. And
IS executives who realize the capabilities of their RDBMS are key to
application success have avoided using DB2 and IBM mainframes.
More money is spent on PCs than on mainframe computers. To leverage
this investment, IS managers wish to move to client-server computing.
IBM mainframe software shows no such clear cut path despite the promises
made when IBM introduced SAA (Systems Application Architecture) in
1987.

Aberdeen Notes:
While the price of mainframe hardware is declining and while many users
essentially pay zero for their 5+ year old hardware, the software licensing
economics are unrealistic in today’s world. Annual license fees keep going
up with no corresponding increase in productivity.
Even more devastating to the economics of running a mainframe shop is
the effort required to maintain existing applications. Capped resource
availability is limiting the ability to develop and implement new
applications critical to the survival of the enterprise. And when we refer to
resources, we especially point to trained, qualified and enterprise-
experienced personnel. It is unrealistic to use an enterprises’s best IS
people to maintain aging mainframe applications that no longer match the
business realities of the enterprise.
It should be little wonder that a cost-cutting CFO targets mainframe
software as a excellent source of hidden revenues. Aberdeen believes that
lowering mainframe systems and applications software annual maintenance
charges is the senior management reformation that will accelerate the
departure of the mainframe from corporate computing topologies.
Simply put, the mainframe is being viewed by senior financial management
as a liability, not an asset.

Aberdeen Notes:
There is one counter-veiling factor to keep the mainframe alive for the
next several years. And that is simply the fact that the majority of data
center professionals are IBM-mainframe trained. The lack of personnel
experienced in both midrange and PC LAN computing will permit
mainframe inertia to exist past when both the economics and technology
are no longer competitive.
Many mainframe veterans are not able to make the transition to 1990’s
technologies at all. Even the best and the brightest must typically attend
educational boot-camps where they have the opportunity to be completely
immersed in the new technologies with their peers. Interestingly enough,
those mainframe-trained executives that do make the transition are usually
the most enthusiastic and effective at applying the new generation of
computers and communications technologies within their enterprises.
Not only are technical skills required in making the transition but also
creative business skills. Remember that the data center contains the
repository of the enterprise’s business procedures. And also that
mainframe-trained staff have not thought of alternative methods of
conducting the business with different technologies. It takes a rare and
special breed of leaders to make this transition — and not every enterprise
has such individuals.

The last applications that will be migrated from the mainframe will be

replaced by more cost-effective alternatives. An excellent proof point is
the large number of mainframe-oriented independent software vendors
rewriting their applications for non-mainfirame computing environments.

Aberdeen Notes:
Finally, it is clear that existing mainframe applications will need to be
completely rewritten to meet the new requirements of true client-server
computing. One of the major objectives of client-server computing is to
maximize the use of relatively cheap desktop computer processing power
and minimize the use of comparatively more expensive multiuser systems.
The client desktop will take responsibility for communications, report
generation, and data validation and manipulation. These functions are now
embedded in mainframe Cobol programs. Therefore, legacy mainframe
applications will need to be rewritten to allow enterprises to move to the
next generation of computing topology.

Aberdeen Notes:
A real dilemma facing senior management and IS executives is how to
transform their current mainframe applications. This is a multi-million
dollar decision in any enterprise now running a mainframe.
The fear of senior line management is that the mainframe is an obstacle in
achieving business objectives that are critical to the success, and possibly
even survival, of the enterprise. Therefore, their hearts often say
revolution is the best approach even though their normal management
prudence would prefer an evolutionary approach. The result is frequent
decrees from on-high stating that the corporate strategy is to evolve off the
mainframe in an unrealistically short time.
On the other hand, MIS management does not believe that its counterparts
appreciate the enormousness of change involved. All the enterprises’
business procedures will have to be re-deployed and new technologies
adopted. And of course, the incremental monies available to finance these
efforts will be limited. As a result, MIS executives tend to want to stay
with the evolutionary approach which is within their capability to manage
while avoiding chaos.

EVOLUTIONARY APPROACHES
□ SURROUND - USE MAINFRAME AS DATA

□ REHOST/CONVERT
□ REWRITE
□ LOWERS MIS COSTS BUT OFTEN AT CAPITAL

Aberdeen Notes:
The most realistic evolutionary approach is to surround the mainframe with
a combination of midrange and PC LAN systems. In this topology, the
mainframe eventually becomes a mere data repository. Many datacenters
that committed early on to IBM’s DB2 for new, mission-critical
applications have found that they have had to resort to surrounding the
mainframe in order to meet their performance and application-update
requirements.
For those mainframe datacenters that do not want to change both
hardware and software at the same time, a convenient evolutionary
approach is to re-host or convert their applications to powerful midrange
systems when possible. An example of this is mainframe applications
written in Software AG’s Natural being re-hosted on RISC/Unix midrange
systems that are compatible with Natural.
Another evolutionary approach is to merely re-write the current mainframe
application to run on less expensive midrange systems. Aberdeen believes
that this is the least positive approach because it does not allow IS to take o
advantage of the opportunity to upgrade the enterprises’ business processes
and create links with complementary applications. It merely lowers the IS
cost of running the same application.
Finally, many IS shops claim that they cannot afford to move off the
mainframe because of their massive investment in 3270 terminals or IBM-
compatible disk drives. While there are technical solutions to this obstacle,
this is a financial issue that can be overcome by aggressive leasing
programs, especially in this age of low interest rates.

Aberdeen Notes:
More and more executives are deciding that the mainframe is merely a
placeholder in their enterprises’ computing topology. And these executives
are pursuing what is commonly termed as the green field approach.
The green field approach begins by re-examining the business processes,
determining both if they are correct for the future and what should be
modified. IS executives then assess which applications are appropriate for
supporting efficient business processes. It is interesting to note that many
IS managers report that up to 80% of their mainframe applications — and
these are applications that they are spending good money supporting — are
now irrelevant to the way their enterprises do business.
After working with non-IS senior management to build a consensus of how
the business should run based upon the green field approach, IS then
develops the appropriate new information architecture to be implemented
over the next 2-3 years. The major gating factor to the green field
approach is the availability of qualified internal personnel. As a result,
nearly all green field approaches require the help of outside professional
service organizations.

Bluntly stated, unless IBM makes major changes to the way it runs its
mainframe business in the same manner as Unisys, Aberdeen envisions the
ES/9000 architecture fading from the enterprise topology. In other words,
the role of the traditional IBM defined mainframe is merely maintenance
in the short term and irrelevant in the long term.
It will be a major IS management endeavor to retrain current mainframe
technologists to support the migration to new systems. Yet Aberdeen
believes that it is worth the effort because the most valuable asset an IS
department can have is an energized individual who has gone through
more than one enterprise-wide application cycle.
At this time, most mainframe evolutionary strategies have the primary
objective of lowering IS costs. While this represents goodness, it still will
not address the greater problem of lowering total enterprise overhead
(SG&A) expenses and the ability for operational managers to make better
decisions supported by IS access to real time information.
Green field strategies are very attractive to senior managers who are
frustrated with what they believe is IS’ lack of responsiveness to the
enterprise’s needs. And a greater number of IS bosses are sharing this
same vision and blaming the basic architecture of the mainframe for being
a serious constraint to obtaining a greater competitive advantage.
Therefore, as IS and senior operational executives approach business issues
from the green field perspective, there is an inherent assumption that the
mainframe will not be part of the future.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group objectively evaluates the declining role of the IBM ES/9000 mainframe in enterprise computing, contrasting it with Unisys A-Series growth. The study analyzes the paradox of increasing mainframe MIPS adoption alongside financial losses, software economics failures, and the blending of mainframe and midrange hardware capabilities. Aberdeen concludes that IBM mainframes will gradually be phased out and recommends either evolutionary (surround, rehost, rewrite) or green-field replacement strategies for IS executives.
