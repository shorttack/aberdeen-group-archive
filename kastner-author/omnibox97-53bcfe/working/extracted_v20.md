## **Introduction** 

OmniBox, Inc. was formed in 1987 to develop an infrastructure for delivering digital, interactive content to consumers in the home.  The company holds three patents for software distribution, digital compression and transactional processing — key enabling process technologies for the development of and participation in a potential 100-million U.S. household electronic commerce market.  Based on these proprietary technologies, OmniBox has developed a unique service and delivery infrastructure that is attractive to advertisers and consumers; one that will accelerate the ability of cable and satellite TV operators to generate new revenue streams from home-based electronic commerce.  With unique technology enabling an exceptionally low cost structure, the OmniBox™ Interactive Broadcasting Network* is well positioned to become an early leader in the emerging market for _interactive television plus electronic commerce_ . 

Having successfully developed and field-tested the key components of its network infrastructure, OmniBox, Inc. seeks $7 million in funding to support the national roll out, marketing and sales of the OmniBox Interactive Broadcasting Network in 1997.

## **Executive Summary** 

If the growth of the Web market has been explosive, the growth of OmniBox’s market — the convergence of interactive television and electronic commerce providing a new class of home-based entertainment and services — could be even greater.  The simple reason is demographic: while computers can be found in over 30 percent of U.S. homes (Statistical Research, Inc., 1995), every household that cares to has one TV -- if not more.  Industry analysts therefore agree that television will play a major role in home electronic commerce. Most also predict that the growth of the TV-Web market, soon evolving to interactive TV, will depend more on cable and telco operators’ ability to roll out services than on educating and convincing consumers.  Intel estimated that in 1994, 26 million homes already had both a PC and cable.

## **The Target Market: Interactive Television Plus Electronic Commerce** 

A 1996 report by Frost & Sullivan projects the market for interactive television and associated advertising to reach $1 billion by 2002.  Three factors are driving this market — first, the intense communications-industry competition to ‘own’ the communications gateway into consumer’s homes; second, a consumer demand for interactive home electronic services; and third, new technology that can affordably deliver these services to the home in new ways. 

The race is on among cable, satellite and telephone operators to build what is known as the _Full-Service Network_ , an infrastructure that integrates cable, telephone and computer technologies so that subscribers can access everything from a Sharper Image catalogue to computer games and food delivery services.  Time Warner has rolled out its interactive, digital multi-media network on a limited basis to 4,000 subscribers in Orlando, Florida; while U.S. West and Bell South are testing similar networks of their own.  But industry analysts and operators alike agree that the time and cost of building a fully integrated, two-way infrastructure has delayed what all believe is the ultimate convergence of today’s separate TV, telephone and computer communications networks.  Thus, the trend among suppliers is to deploy their homebased electronic commerce offerings via the Internet based on the infrastructure that currently exists.

## **The Target Prospect: Cable and Satellite TV Operators** 

A 1995 survey by Statistical Research, Inc. found that 25 percent of today’s households could support an interactive delivery system requiring a co-located TV, phone line and PC, while over 80 percent could use a system requiring only a co-located TV and phone line, and 75 percent could receive interactive TV delivered over a cable network.  These household market proportions help explain why the OmniBox Network infrastructure is optimized to provide interactive electronic commerce and services for the 11,000 cable operators serving 60 million subscriber households across the U.S.  With decades of entertainment industry experience among its senior management, OmniBox knows what this target prospect needs. 

Cable operators face the challenges of providing additional digital channel capacity to deliver the range of services that subscribers demand; of handling the millions of transactions that interactive television will generate; and of defending their subscriber base from telco, Internet service provider (ISP) and direct broadcast satellite (DBS) operators while they find the capital outlays necessary to increase their systems’ capacity and  interactive intelligence. Reducing and in some cases eliminating the cable operator’s biggest barrier to interactive television -- the capital cost of rewiring cable trunk lines, switching networks, video servers and set-top receivers -- is OmniBox’s key market appeal. 

OmniBox enables cable operators with older coaxial plants and analog set-top boxes to add revenue-generating channels with no rewiring from the head-end to the digital set-top box or home PC.  The most obvious applications for these added digital channels are extensions of existing home markets — video on demand, interactive retailing and information retrieval (downloads), video gaming, financial services and similar applications for which the TV rather than the PC will likely be the information appliance of choice. 

The OmniBox Network will also appeal to: 

- Satellite, wireless and telco operators, who can inject OmniBox or local content into their programming mix and derive revenues from transactions split between OmniBox and the operator; 

- Global 1000 corporations, for whom the OmniBox Network can serve as a software and multimedia-content distribution mechanism (for example, to broadcast real-time video news

## _Early OmniBox Adopters_ 

In 1996 OmniBox’s market validation activities resulted in several signed and/or nearsigned contracts and dozens more letters of intent that provided a finer-grained view of the company’s  near-term customer prospects: 

_**Direct Broadcast Satellite Operators**_ DBS operators offer early revenue potential for OmniBox because the set-top boxes are already there. ## **The OmniBox Value Proposition** 

- OmniBox has clear market appeals and competitive advantages in the following areas: 

- _**Lowest cost solution**_ -- For cable operators, the combination of no head-end rewiring, an economical $10,000-per-video stream encoding cost, and set-top financing represents the most affordable solution available. 

- _**Immediate digital capability with minimum capitalization**_ -- Operators can achieve a minimum of 10 new digital channels for each analog channel replaced and can effect this replacement incrementally, continuing to broadcast both analog and digital programming until the conversion is complete. 

- _**Near-term realization of interactive TV**_ – OmniBox systems can be installed and implemented _now_ .  Smaller operators can begin building new revenue streams from interactive TV right away -- as opposed to too late or not at all. 

- _**Exceptional price-performance**_ -- While OmniBox’s per-channel cost is 30 percent lower than competing solutions, performance in terms of picture quality and motion compensation is equal to MPEG I and superior to MPEG II. 

- _**Local operator control**_ -- Unlike a digital full-service network such as Time Warner’s, the OmniBox Network allows operators to control the digital encoding and broadcasting of their programming in real time, at the local level.  Thus, operators can use existing equipment to inject local ad content into video streams and to change that content — for example, a new sale price on advertised items — in real time. 

- _**Access to Internet and online services**_ -- Since subscribers can access on-line services such as the Internet through the OmniBox Network, operators have an opportunity to participate in an additional new revenue stream by becoming an Internet Service Provider.  In rural locations, this could prove very attractive to consumers who otherwise face long-distance phone calls to their ISPs. 

- _**Participation in all revenue sources without administrative overhead**_ OmniBox’s patented transaction processing system allows operators to receive a fee from subscriber transactions without having to administer consumer transaction requests, switching and billing systems and order processing. 

- _**Technological flexibility**_ OmniBox Network content can be equally enjoyed with a light-weight Internet client (e.g., a Browser on a PC or on a Network Computer) or broadcast to its own cable hardware or to alternate set-top hardware from Primestar, Phillips and others. OmniBox’s technology is also compatible with the telephone lines, satellite, and microwave/wireless transmission vehicles, and could be available for delivery in those vehicles shortly after launch in the cable market.

The three phases and their estimated timetables are: 

_Phase One, Q2/1997_

## _Transactional Processing System_ 

This patented system allows consumers to download virtually anything -- entertainment, tickets, software, financial services -- into a set-top box or PC for browsing and purchase at their leisure.  The system transmits the order via telephone modem or other communications channel to the financial institution, which clears the credit and executes the order.  Based on experience with its transaction processing partner Bank One, OmniBox’s approach should prove attractive to the majority of financial institutions because it provides the high levels of security and privacy of the U.S. -bank electronic funds transfer system. It also speeds payment clearance, minimizing “float”, and offers a simpler, more robust two-network architecture.  Cable industryfunded market research also indicates that subscribers will react favorably to the simplicity and security of this ordering process, resulting in increased pay-per-view transactions. 

## _Electronic Software Distribution_ 

OmniBox’s patented method for downloading software (music, games, video) for recording by the viewer allows TV or PC subscribers to access multiple entertainment channels and preview them before making a purchase decision. The compression and store-and-forward capabilities of this system create an opportunity for OmniBox to pioneer the mass-market distribution of computer software, audio CDs, and even short videos. Although untested, this market has potential in the $100 million-and-up market range by the year 2000 according to Aberdeen Group.  The two key enablers will be $300 (retail) CD-ROM recorders (estimated in 1998) and greater digital bandwidth into the home (1996 and after). 

## _Digital Video Compression and Decompression_ 

OmniBox has exclusive use of a digital video compression technology that is compatible with yet costs far less to install than the better known MPEG-II while delivering superior picture quality.  Additionally, the encryption performed within this proprietary compression algorithm  solves a major problem of broadcast operators especially the incidence of stolen signals for pay-per-view programming, which in some cases can be as high as 60 percent. The proprietary technology enables broadcast operators to obtain a minimum of 10 high-quality digital video channels for each analog channel at 30 percent lower cost.  While integrated analog/digital systems have been explored by others, OmniBox was the first to successfully demonstrate such a system in collaboration with Time Warner/Manhattan in 1995, and offers the only commercially available solution based on this technology.
