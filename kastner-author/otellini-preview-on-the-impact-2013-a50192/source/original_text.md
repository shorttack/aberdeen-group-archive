On the Impact of Paul Otellini’s CEO Years at Intel
Posted  on  April  19,  2013  by  Peter  S  Kastner
Intel’s  CEO  Paul  Otellini  is  retiring  next  month.  His  40-­year  impact  on  Intel  now
ending,  it’s  a  timely  opportunity  to  look  at  where  the  IT  industry  has  changed  and
where  it’s  likely  headed.
Facing Challenges in Technology, Growth, and Competition
In  2004,  Intel  was  #46  on  the  Fortune  100  list,  and  had  ramped  production  to  1
million  Pentium  4′s  a  week  (today  over  a  million  processors  a  day).  The  year  ended
with  revenues  of  $34.2  billion,  up  13.5  percent  from  2003  and  higher  than  the
previous  record  of  $33.7  billion  set  in  2000,  the  Internet  Bubble  peak.
The  immediate  challenges  in  late  2004  fell  into  three  areas:  technology,  growth,
and  competition:
1.   Intel  processor  architecture  had  pushed  more  transistors  clocking  faster  to
gain  greater  performance  from  a  single  processor  core,  requiring  higher
voltages,  and  generating  more  heat.  The  solution  was  to  use  the  benefits  of
Moore’s  Law  to  put  more  cores  on  each  chip  and  run  them  at  controllable
voltages.  The  multi-­core  Intel  PC  was  born  in  2006  in  the  Core  2  Duo.
2.   Competitor  AMD  was  ascendant,  and  a  growing  menace.    Two  weeks  before
taking  over  the  corner  office,  Otellini  received  a  market  research  report
saying  AMD  had  over  52%  market  share  at  U.S.  retail,  and  Intel  had  fallen  to
#2.  Clearly,  Intel  needed  to  win  with  better  products.
3.   The  PC  market  was  80%  desktops  and  20%  notebooks  in  2004  with  the  North
America  and  Europe  markets  already  mature.  Intel  had  chip-­making  plants
(aka  fabs)  coming  online  that  were  scaled  to  a  continuing  20%-­plus  volume
growth  rate.  Intel  needed  new  markets,  fast,  to  keep  up  its  profit  margins
while  paying  for  the  ever-­increasing  cost  of  new  fabs.
How Things Changed By Making Right-Hand
Turns
To  Intel  insiders,  a  right-­hand  turn  gets  you  out  of  the  path  that’s  not  working
without  reversing  course.  Otellini  made  many  right-­hand  turns  throughout  his
tenure,  changing  the  Intel  culture,  setting  higher  expectations,  and  moving  in  many
new  directions  to  take  the  company  and  the  industry  forward.

Baseline Technology and Architecture Initiatives
Process:  Intel’s  process  technology  in  2004  was  at  90nm.  Today,  the  company  is
shipping  sample  units  to  customers  for  2014  products  at  14nm,  an  improvement  of
five  processing  nodes.
Design  for  Manufacturing:  To  reliably  achieve  a  new  process  node  and
architecture  every  two  years,  Intel  introduced  the  tick-­tock  model,  where  odd  years
deliver  a  new  architecture  and  even  years  deliver  a  new,  smaller  process  node.  The
engineering  and  manufacturing  fab  teams  work  together  to  design  microprocessors
that  can  be  manufactured  in  high  volume  with  few  defects.  Other  key
accomplishments  include  High-­K  Metal  Gate  transistors  at  45nm,  32  nm  products,
3D  tri-­gate  transistors  at  22nm,  and  a  50%  reduction  in  wafer  production  time.
Multi-­core  technology:  Today,  even  $200  smartphones  have  multiple  cores.
Intel’s  tack  is  to  use  the  ubiquitous  development  tool  ecosystem  for  Intel
Architecture  (IA)  as  a  lever  for  computing  across  small  and  tiny  (Atom),  average
(Core  and  Xeon),  and  high-­performance  (PHI)  workloads.  Embracing  64-­bit
computing,  the  company  introduced  processors  that  take  advantage  of  today’s  large
memories,  and  are  critical  to  data-­center  workloads.  There  is  a  continuum  across
computing  needs  all  supported  by  a  common  IA.
Performance  per  Watt:  Otellini  led  Intel’s  transformational  technology
initiative  to  deliver  10X  more  power-­efficient  processors.  Lower  processor  power
requirements  are  allowing  innovative  form  factors  in  all-­in-­one  desktops  and  are  a
home  run  in  the  data  center.  The  power-­efficiency  initiative  comes  to  maturity  with
the  launch  of  the  4th  generation  of  Core  processors,  codename  Haswell,  later  this
quarter.
Major Product and Market Initiatives
Desktop  PC:  drove  the  industry  for  twenty-­five  years  until  notebooks  and
smartphones  surpassed  the  PC  in  unit  volume  about  five  years  ago.  It’s  a  mature
business  and  the  designs  became  increasingly  forgettable.  Bringing  the  desktop
market  back  into  the  excitement  spotlight  is  clearly  an  opportunity  for  the  PC
industry.  Intel’s  NUC,  a  very  small  form-­factor  for  a  home  entertainment  system,
and  all-­in-­ones  are  examples  of  innovation  that  I  expect  to  see.
Notebooks:  Intel  spent  billions  over  the  last  decade  building  the  technology  and
market  for  notebook  computers.  Demand  soared  from  about  20%  of  units  to  over
65%  today.  The  energy-­efficiency  technology  initiatives  discussed  above  are
specifically  aimed  at  getting  high  performance  when  needed  while  maintaining  an
all-­day  battery  life.  The  Ultrabook  initiative  changed  the  design  framework  and
created  much  more  enticing  notebooks.  I  expect  better  graphics  and  even  better
performance-­per-­watt  in  this  year’s  Haswell  models.
Data  Center:  Intel  has  proved  highly  attractive  to  IT  buyers,  displacing
proprietary  brands  and  platforms.  Energy  efficiency  is  also  a  huge  winner  in  the
data  center,  where  large  users  measure  electricity  consumption  in  megawatts.
Multi-­core  Xeon  processors  are  able  to  take  on  almost  any  commercial  workload  at
the  lowest  cost  of  ownership.
Intel  inside  now  applies  to  a  wide  variety  of  storage  and  communications  products
within  the  data  center,  the  result  of  partnerships  with  companies  such  as  EMC,
NetApp,  and  Cisco.  The  Xeon/Phi  combination  is  already  in  seven  of  the  top  10  global

supercomputers.
Mobile:  The  early  Atom  processors  introduced  in  late  2008  were  designed  for  low-­
cost  netbooks  and  nettops,  not  phones  and  tablets.  Mobile  was  a  market  where  the
company  had  to  reorganize,  dig  in,  and  catch  up.  The  energy-­efficiency  that  benefits
Haswell,  the  communications  silicon  from  the  2010  Infineon  acquisition,  and  the
forthcoming  14nm  process  in  2014  will  finally  allow  the  company  to  stand  toe-­to-­
toe  with  Qualcomm,  nVidia,  and  Samsung  using  the  Atom  brand.
Software:  The  company  acquired  Wind  River  Systems,  a  specialist  in  real-­time
software  in  2009,  and  McAfee  in  2010.  These  added  to  Intel’s  own  developer  tools
business.  Software  services  business  accelerates  customer  time  to  market  with  new,
Intel-­based  products.  The  company  stepped  up  efforts  in  consumer  device  software,
optimizing  the  operating  systems  for  Google  (Android),  Microsoft  (Windows),  and
Samsung  (Tizen).
Intelligent  Systems:  Specialized  Atom  systems  on  a  chip  (SoCs)  with  Wind  River
software  and  Infineon  mobile  communications  radios  are  increasingly  being
designed  into  medical  devices,  factory  machines,  automobiles,  and  new  product
categories  such  as  digital  signage.
New  Markets:  Otellini  made  several  right-­hand  turns,  shifting  the  company’s
energy  focus  from  the  mature  markets  of  North  America  and  Europe  to  emerging
geographies,  notably  the  BRIC  countries  —  Brazil,  Russia,  India,  and  China.    That
formula  drove  marketing  efforts  which  accounted  for  a  significant  fraction  of
revenue  growth  over  the  past  five  years.
Financial and Corporate Performance
Intel  has  delivered  solid  financial  results  under  Otellini.  With  ever  more  efficient
fabs,  the  company  has  improved  gross  margins.  Free  cash  flow  supports  a  dividend
above  4%,  a  stock  buyback  program,  and  a  hefty  capital  expense  program  targeted
at  building  more  efficient  fabs.
The  changes  in  financial  results  are  summarized  in  the  table  below,  showing  the
year  before  Otellini  took  over  as  CEO  through  the  end  of  2012.

The Paul Otellini Legacy
1.   From  a  finance  standpoint,  Intel  is  a  highly-­respected  value  investment,
paying  a  4%+  dividend  and  buying  back  billions  of  dollars  in  stock  from  its
steady  cash  flow.  It  is  championed  by  value  investors.  The  company  regularly
meets  predictions  to  Wall  Street.  However,  the  growth-­stock  premium  the
company  had  in  the  Internet  years  is  gone.
2.   From  a  management  and  governance  standpoint,  the  company  shows  many
touches  of  an  MBA.  Business  processes  are  more  streamlined,  the  company  is
somewhat  more  agile,  budgets  and  schedules  are  made  and  kept.  The  company
has  deepened  its  pool  of  managers  and  trained  them  better.  Execution
excellence  is  expected.  The  company  gets  good  grades  for  responsibility.
3.   The  Tick-­Tock  model  is  a  business  school  case  study:  churning  out  ground-­
breaking  transistor  technology,  processors,  and  high-­quality  leading-­edge
manufacturing  at  a  predictable,  steady  pace  of  engineering  to  volume
manufacturing.  This  repeatable  process  is  Intel’s  crown  jewel.  It  is  a  national
asset.
4.   Intel  has  dramatically  expanded  its  product  and  geographic  footprint,
developing  go-­to-­market  strategies  that  fit  local  cultures  and  markets  using  a
wide  spectrum  of  computing  on  IA-­based  products.
5.   Intel’s  PC  and  data  center  products  are  arguably  leaders  in  technology,  and
the  company  has  gained  market  share.
6.   The  mobile  phone  and  tablet  initiative  is  three  years  into  a  six-­year  plan.
Recent  products  like  the  Xolo  smartphone  for  Africa  show  technology
promise  as  well  as  a  willingness  to  build  market  experiences  outside  the  jaded
and  mature  U.S.  market.
7.   There  is  no  clear  future  for  any  single  mobile  platform.  Tablets  will  not
replace  or  substitute  for  notebooks  and  desktops.  Many  convertible
notebook/tablet  products  are  arriving  as  the  industry  mixes  tablet  and
notebook  DNA  to  see  what  the  market  wants.  Haswell,  inexpensive  touch
panels,  and  Windows  8.1  are  an  exciting  mix  for  holiday  2013  sales.
8.   The  company  has  disclosed  $35  billion  in  capital  expenses  for  new  chip-­
making  plants  and  equipment,  creating  manufacturing  flexibility,  foundry
opportunities,  and  demonstrating  a  commitment  to  keep  at  the  forefront  of
chip-­making  technology.
9.   Plans  proceed  for  advanced  SoCs  that  incorporate  processor,  chipset,  radios,
and  some  memory,  which  will  reduce  the  real  estate,  cost,  and  complexity
needed  to  build  a  smartphone  or  tablet,  ATM  or  digital  sign.
10.   The  emerging  trend  towards  Software  Defined  Networking  (SDN)  will
commoditize  more  data-­center  hardware,  opening  up  opportunities  advanced
SoCs  in  routers,  switches,  power  management,  and  other  activities.
11.   The  multi-­core  opportunity  is  hardly  over.  Expect  advances  from  many  small
cores  in  consumer  devices  to  even  larger  supercomputers.
12.   AMD  is  a  lesser  competitive  threat.  The  extensive  performance-­per-­watt
efforts  are  showing  fruit  against  ARM-­based  competition.
13.   With  billions  more  in  cash  flow  compared  to  a  decade  ago,  Intel  is  financially
strong.
Looking  back  and  looking  forward,  this  has  been  a  decade  of  great  technology
change,  with  Intel  leading  in  many  areas  and  making  right  turns  in  others.  Paul
Otellini  is  well  regarded  by  the  industry  and  his  peers  for  Intel’s
accomplishments.  History  and  not  analysts  will  grade  the  legacy  of  Paul  Otellini  as
CEO  at  Intel.  I  am  comfortable  predicting  he  will  be  well  regarded.
