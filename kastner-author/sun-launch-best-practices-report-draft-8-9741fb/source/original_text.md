Best Practices in Hardware and Software Industry Product Launch Process
Preface
In June and July 2002, Aberdeen Group interviewed five leading technology companies regarding the process whereby they launched new hardware, software, and service products.  Each interview included questions about the following:  the company’s product launch organization and the process defined by that group; typical lead times for tasks preceding product launch; and materials and methods included in the product launch.
The companies interviewed were:
    • three computer hardware platform companies, Dell, IBM, and Unisys;
    • the 3rd largest software company (Fortune), Computer Associates; and,
    • the office equipment company, Xerox.
The original scope of the project called for as many as twenty-five interviews, and Aberdeen began by contacting all the major computer hardware and software companies and several office equipment companies, a total of twenty-eight companies.  It quickly became clear that most companies considered the product launch process to be strategic and they were concerned regarding the objectives of the research.  Aberdeen reviewed this early response with Sun, and they agreed that Aberdeen would attempt to gather data from the most relevant companies to Sun’s business, though it was unlikely that the number would exceed five to ten.
The remainder of this report will describe the findings of these interviews though individual responses will not be associated with a specific company.
Launch Process:  Organization and Methodology
Each of the five companies described a global, standardized process across all product groups — hardware, software, and services.  All of the companies allowed variations among the groups with two describing the variations as insignificant and three acknowledging that individual groups had some autonomy and the degree of variations differed from product to product.  Two companies placed responsibility for product launches and the definition of a methodology in a single group (in one case, Product and Solutions Launch reporting to VP of Integrated Business Strategy reporting to VP Marketing; in the other, Product Release Strategy reporting to VP Marketing) while the other three had no common launch organization.
The execution of a product launch always involved a matrix-managed, cross-functional team led by a launch team owner that handled the integration and facilitation of product launch activities.  The team may include product development, test, procurement, manufacturing, marketing, sales channel, fulfillment, training, customer support, finance, analyst relations, and press relations functions.  In the two companies with separate launch organizations, the team owner reported into that group.  In two companies, the team was led by the general manager for the product area, and, in one case, it was led by a launch coordinator representing sales, working closely with a marketing manager and a product specialist.
One company described a rigorous process but also described significant variations in the activities required and the specific interfaces implemented between engineering and marketing.  Another company described entire phases, such as a rigorous business case review, being necessary for some products but not others.  For example, OEM products would not require many of the process steps.
Launch communications are customized, as necessary, by sales channel.  Operational processes are customized as well to adapt to specific infrastructures or channels within geographies.  Processes will be different for partner initiatives.  Nearly everyone acknowledged some variations from product to product.  For example, significant launches may conduct a dedicated press conference and analyst briefing.  In addition to the activities required, most agreed that business units had the autonomy to vary the process with respect to details such as timeframes and last-minute adjustments.  While a rigorous, published framework is defined for the organization, it is rarely executed precisely in an identical fashion.
In the two companies with a separate launch organization, those groups owned the definition and modification of the formal process.  The other three companies were vague regarding how proposed changes to the process were handled, but it was stated that senior executives of the product groups were required to approve changes.
Lead Times
The responses to the questions regarding lead times before product launches varied from very detailed answers to vague, high-level answers.  Two companies explicitly stated that the lead times vary from product to product, while the others implied that adjustments and late exceptions often take place.  Aberdeen got the sense that while a framework and structure might be rigidly enforced, the specific timeframes would always vary somewhat.  Some tasks, such as a launch readiness review, might be “required” to occur 90 days in advance, while other tasks might be completed right up to the launch itself.
One company submitted a detailed, electronic response which is attached as Appendix A-1.  Another company gave reasonably detailed verbal answers which are presented in Appendix A-2.  The others gave responses that are summarized in Appendix A-3.
Materials and Methods
All respondents indicated that their companies were using all of the methods listed — hardcopy paper, Web-based e-learning, Website, classroom, electronic documents, Intranet database, and collaboration software — with an increasing emphasis on Webinars/Webcast technology.  It was also noted that work is done differently in different regions.
Appendix B-1 and B-2 are precise electronic responses.  Appendix B-3 and B-4 reflect verbally delivered responses.  A fifth company replied “all of the above.”
Conclusions
While all companies had a well-defined, detailed process for product launches, there was also accommodation of adjustments for specific circumstances.
As timeframes tighten and pressures for new products increase, there seems to be an increase in the variations from standard process and the autonomy of individual product groups and business units.  For this reason, it is often difficult to differentiate between the process as defined and the process as implemented.
