The Business Value in IT Outsourcing  
Hypothesis 
 
IT outsourcing has been around long enough for enterprises to taste the fruit and judge for 
themselves. Key drivers to IT outsourcing include high internal costs, staff skills quality and 
instability, infrastructure under-utilization, and challenges in technology adoption or regulatory 
compliance.  Yet many enterprises have not yet done a serious make-or-buy decision. Why not?  
On the other hand, many who have signed IT outsourcing contracts have serious buyer’s remorse. 
They complain about misjudging their ability and competency to manage the outsourcer, poor 
choices in metrics and SLAs, confusing low cost with value delivered, inflexible or outdated 
contract language, and other C-level headaches. Are these unhappy outsourcing customers on the 
way to court or are there ways to get the relationship on track?  
To both those who outsource and those who do not, Aberdeen will be asking how enterprises are 
maximizing total business value from an outsourcing decision, to be published as a benchmark 
report in late June 2006.  
 
Why Are Many Companies Ignoring Outsourcing? 
 
Since we began quantitative tracking of IT planning issues in 2004, Aberdeen has observed five 
major reasons why enterprises choose to outsource some or all of their IT: 
• 
Focusing on core competencies is the decision that IT excellence is best achieved by an 
outsider, allowing management more time for non-IT business decision-making; 
• 
Saving money is what most expect to achieve, although palming off a dysfunctional IT 
organization’s problems on a naïve outsourcer is a pipe dream; 
• 
IT resource constraints are a common complaint, and include lack of talent, costly talent, 
staff turnover, and having the wrong IT skill-sets in a rapidly changing company;  
• 
Time-to-market is a concern in highly competitive industries where IT agility is critical — 
outsourcer size, skills, and agility are often cited; and 
• 
Culture wars happen when line-of-business management gets fed up with IT intransigence 
and demands change, including the nuclear “blow it up” outsourcing option as a way to start 
anew. 
The range of outsourcing options available to companies of all sizes is extensive, and includes 
simple application outsourcing (e.g., e-mail) all the way to full-blown business process 
offshoring of major processes (e.g., general ledger or accounts payable). In spite of these options, 
many enterprises tell us they have never done a serious make-or-buy outsourcing decision on any 
segment of their IT requirements.

Research Brief 
The first research question Aberdeen is determined to answer is what is inhibiting enterprises 
from taking a serious look at how operational transformation through IT outsourcing might 
benefit the organization? 
Getting to IT Outsourcing Success: Is Failure a Prerequisite?  
 
IT outsourcing takes skills that many companies apparently do not have. This should not be 
altogether surprising given the complexity of IT infrastructure, the nebulous IT performance 
metrics in place at many enterprises, the lack of a clear vision as to exactly what “good IT 
practices and results” ought to be, and the nature of the outsource contract process.  
We cannot name a single company that has outsourced IT functions and not learned some 
lessons — often very painful lessons not to be repeated under any circumstances. Our study will 
probe those lessons learned with a maturity model aimed at learning from the failures and 
mistakes of outsourcing practitioners. 
Outsourcing with Baseline Mis-Alignment 
The first class of IT outsourcing customers we intend to measure are those who are clearly 
misaligned. This can occur when the scope is poorly understood, when inaccurate internal 
metrics are used to drive pricing and SLA terms, and when the business changes over time away 
from the outsourcing contract terms. These companies are significantly out of alignment with 
what they intended and contracted to do. We suspect this population represents up to 30% of IT 
outsourcing engagements. Moreover, these customers may not realize that they are seriously 
misaligned.

metrics. We will probe satisfaction levels and gather data on challenges to successful

Finding the Optimal Outsourcing Partnership 
Our hypothesis is that 5-10% of multi-million dollar IT outsourcing agreements are driving all of 
the anticipated business value to the buyer’s business. Our planned research will identify such 
companies and use a combination of survey and interview techniques to extract the metrics and 
techniques used by these enterprises to drive business value success in a true partnership with the 
outsourcer. We will answer the question, “how are companies successfully outsourcing and 
capturing the business value planned for the outsourcing contract?” 
Summary  
 
Aberdeen is embarking on a benchmark of IT outsourcing because prior research indicates a high 
level of dissatisfaction with outsourcing in general. We are also continually deepening our 
knowledge of the IT organizational maturity, with over 2,000 companies surveyed on IT in the 
past two years. From these data, we know that there is a wide spectrum of IT maturity levels 
across numerous metrics.  
The Aberdeen report, The Business Value in IT Outsourcing, will benchmark all size companies 
in the Global 5000 across all industries. The report will summarize the findings on why IT 
outsourcing projects become sub-optimal, how companies are aligning their outsourcing 
contracts and operations to maximize measurable business value, with recommendations for how 
outsourcing companies can improve their performance and outsourcing maturity.  In short, how 
companies can get and keep on track to business value.

Kastner, VP and Research Director, Enterprise Integration.
