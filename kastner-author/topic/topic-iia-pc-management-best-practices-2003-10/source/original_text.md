Chapter 1
Executive Summary
Introduction
This report provides guidance to management and auditors for addressing the business and technical controls related to personal computers (PCs), and for understanding the PCs’ overall impacts on the business control environment.  Auditors with a basic understanding of PC management can consult with executive, financial, technology, and operational management on:
    • maintaining effective PC and network security,
    • assessing the true costs of the networked PC environment,
    • managing software and other PC components, and 
    • maximizing PC contributions to business efficiency and security.
This report also provides guidance for assessing and documenting PC controls in light of requirements in the Sarbanes-Oxley Act and other legislation and regulations.
The Auditor’s Role
Information technology has become an essential foundation for an enormous variety of business processes.  The underlying technologies, systems, and applications are constantly evolving.  Business models are also changing as companies strive to take full advantage of the Internet while mitigating the inherent risks of connecting to a universally accessible network.  The role, expertise, and methods of the auditor must keep pace with these advances.  New methodologies must be adopted for integrating the costs, risks, and value of the technology infrastructure into the larger audit framework, and for identifying new business opportunities enabled by technology.
In today’s business environment, effective technology policy and efficient infrastructure management are fundamental business assets.  Appropriate procedures and a suitable technology framework are essential ingredients if an organization is to remain effective, competitive, and valuable to its stakeholders.  Slight changes in IT operations relating to hardware and software updates, virus and worm defenses, and system and data backup can save organizations millions of dollars—and potentially save the business.  An auditor who understands the fundamental concepts behind technology costs, risks, and value can play a fundamental role in helping to align technology investment with overall business goals.
The Importance of the PC
Amidst the complexity and cost of data centers and core business applications, it is easy to overlook the importance of the ubiquitous and relatively inexpensive PC.  Yet desktops and today’s increasingly mobile notebooks are involved in an enormous variety of business processes.  A large percentage of employees in most businesses could not fulfill their job function without a working PC.  A simple way to gauge its importance is to multiply the number of PCs by the $8,000 to $9,000 that represents the total cost of ownership per system in a typical enterprise environment.
Of course in today’s networked world, it is also necessary to consider the risk associated with failed, infected, or hacked PCs which can result lost productivity, lost business opportunity, damaged credibility, and extensive liability if third-party data or systems are negatively impacted.  In our increasingly security-conscious world, these issues are growing in importance and must be addressed by all responsible organizations.
As will be demonstrated in this report, best practices for PC fleet management can reduce total costs by 20 to 25 percent for a typical company—while improving the security, performance, reliability, and agility of the infrastructure, and the enterprise.  A knowledgeable auditor can play a key role in bringing about these changes.
About this Report
This report is based on research and collaboration with leading experts in the business issues surround the use and management of networked PCs.  There were four major phases of research and preparation:
    1. An extensive review of literature and guidance;
    2. An online survey accomplished via The Institute of Internal Auditors’ GAIN Flash Survey process (Global Audit Information Network);
    3. A Technology Forum that convened leading experts in the field of technology, security, audit, and corporate governance; and
    4. Extensive review and revision of the report by a strong cadre of volunteers and partner organizations.
To provide an appropriate framework for the issues discussed in this report, survey questions were organized around the COSO “Internal Control Integrated Framework.”  Parts of the report are also designed to address the new COSO “Enterprise Risk Management” report exposure draft that was circulated when this report was being prepared.
In presenting the results of these efforts, this report looks at six key issues that are of fundamental importance to most organizations.  The core findings for each of these issues is presented below, and discussed in greater detail in the corresponding chapters.
Business Risk and Insurance
The business benefits of global connectivity via the Internet are undeniable—and so are the risks.  Internet-connected PCs are subject to frequent attacks from viruses, worms, hackers, and other digital threats, and are also subject to internal misuse from authorized employees.  The connection of back-end systems to networked PCs adds to the risk with the potential for compromising large quantities of business data or third-party information.  No longer can management or other system users afford to be naïve about PC practices as these will not only impact their own organization, but will also impact their business partners, customers, other users of the Internet, and even critical environmental, economic, and defense infrastructures.
Traditional insurance policies were not designed to cover these risks, and most now carry explicit exclusions for network-related security events.  Network insurance policies have been established to cover this gap.  These policies are typically modular in nature, enabling companies to construct customized coverage based on their specific risk profile.  However, coverage is qualified based on the level of internal controls established for the PC environment.  In fact, without comprehensive and documented controls, it may be impossible to get coverage at all; or businesses may find that detailed exclusions prevent them from collecting when a crisis strikes.

A risk management program is therefore necessary to quantify “value at risk;” identify vulnerabilities; implement appropriate internal controls based on an organization’s risk appetite; and define insurance needs to cover residual risk.  We recommend tThe Systems Assurance and Control (SAC) model provides, which is an extension of the COSO “Internal Control Integrated Framework.” to address information and security management within the business context.  COSO establishes a well-tested, comprehensive framework, while SAC integrates essential concepts related to the use and control of information technology.
Comprehensive and documented internal controls lie at the heart of effective PC fleet management.  Policies should extend from generalities at the highest organization levels, down to the specific technologies and processes deployed in and around the PC infrastructure.  Enterprise standards are essential to limit complexity and support automated tools and efficient processes for strategic planning, asset management, deployments and upgrades, software licensing and maintenance, and core security functions such as firewalls, virus protection, and event logging.
Wireless Value and Business Risk
Wireless mobility is adding to the value of the PC infrastructure—and the importance of effective security and risk management.  Access anywhere and any time has become a creed for many businesses, and wireless technology is clearly the next wave of advancement.  The value should not be ignored, yet neither should the risks.  In the survey results used for this reportproject, only 21 percent of respondents had performed a cost analysis for wireless, and only 30 percent have addressed wireless as part of the policy/procedures for PCs..   Providing their businesses with a strong system of internal controls to enable rapid but controlled deployment of this new technology should be a priority for many businessesorganizations. 
See Chapter 2 for more information.
Compliance and Regulatory Issues
In a networked business environment, PC management requirements will be impacted by virtually all legislation and regulation that addresses the integrity, reliability, or privacy of business information.  The Sarbanes-Oxley Act, HIPAA, the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act, and California Law SB 1386 are just a few examples of the kinds of developments that are changing baseline requirements around the world.
Understanding the regulatory and litigative environment is a fundamental component of business risk assessment and responsible management.  However, businesses organizations that focus only on regulatory compliance will miss out on the substantial business value of strong and effective internal controls.  In the end, strategies and methods become “best practices” because they make good business sense.  In some cases it may take a great deal of information gathering and analysis to justify the costs associated with best practices.  Other times it may be sufficient to note certain practices are mandated.
For businessesOrganizations that are driven by compliance can , we recommend they take advantage of this motivation to look more broadly at their policies and operations.  As an organizations moves toward a comprehensive system of internal controls based on COSO or a similar framework, the value of best practices becomes increasingly more quantifiable.  The business recognizesQuantified benefits may include substantial competitive advantages, —and regulatory  compliance becomes a relatively simple matter of documenting existing processes.
See Chapter 3 for more information.
Information Security and Appropriate Use
“In the modern world, everything that business or government does with their information technology becomes part of the global information infrastructure.  We must build infrastructure to a very high standard. Attaching weak components to the infrastructure puts your organization as well as your neighbors at risk. Responsible citizens will contribute only sound components to that cooperative infrastructure.
“A sound information security program ensures that an organization can rely on its critical business information. Information security is a fundamental and unavoidable cost of doing business and, when provided in a timely and effective manner, protects the health of the organization.  Indeed, information security enables competitive advantage and new business opportunities.”
—	Information Security Management and Assurance: A Call to Action for Corporate Governance, The Institute of Internal Auditors, April 2000
In the survey leading up tofor this research portproject, 93 percent of respondents reported their organizations had policies governing information security and acceptable use for their PC infrastructure.  However, only 33 percent considered their policies and practices to be tightly controlled.  Responses indicated numerous areas where prevailing practice falls far short of effective practice, including virus protection, for which less than 30 percent believed their organization employed best practices.  This indicates a significant opportunity to improve security by improving the monitoring and enforcement of control requirements, and by implementing the practices described in this report.
Theis need for better virus protection management came into particularly sharp focus when the Blaster worm and SoBig virus were launched during the time this report was being preparedation of this report.  These attacks impacted organizations across virtually all industries and the government sector.  In some cases entire networks were shut down and PCs were inoperable.  Other digital attacks — such as Nimda, Code Red, and the Slammer virus — have had comparable impacts, each one resulting in billions of dollars in global loss due to downtime and cleanup costs.
As many companies have learned during these attacks, just having up to date virus protection does NOT by itself provide an adequate level of protection.  A comprehensive approach to managing infrastructure controls must be in place to protect the business from digital threats of all kinds, including not only viruses and worms, but also hackers and malicious insiders.  Internal control and security elements such as firewalls, virus protection, intrusion protection, software maintenance and patching, event logging, encryption, authentication, and end-user awareness programs should be carefully controlled and integrated into a larger security framework.
However, even the best applications of these controls cannot totally ensure a virus or other threat will not be brought into the environment via a control weakness in an individual machine or a newly discovered vulnerability.  Cyber threats can negatively impact the reliability of information and processing, so the networked PC must be regarded as a significant element in the overall system of internal controls.  Moreover, business strategy must anticipate threat scenarios based on experience and emerging patterns, and continually enhance protection by deploying automated software updates and other protective measures as well as developing and testing response techniques before an attack is launched.
Ultimately, the ability of an business organization to deploy, integrate, and manage an appropriate security framework depends on organizational support and funding.  This makes it important to understand security requirements within the larger context of business goals and risk management.  Organizations that take the extra steps to quantify the total cost and value of PC security will be able to justify essential enhancements based on the return on security investment (ROSI) for each project.  Internal auditors can play a crucial role in this endeavor, and help to bridge the conceptual gap that often exists between business and IT decision makers.
See Chapter 4 for more information.
Operations and Business Continuity
As the foundation for individual productivity—and a key component in many core business processes—the PC infrastructure should be considered recognized as a crucial element in business continuity programs.  In any large enterprise environment, individual PC failure and restoration occurs on a daily basis.  A single failure may have limited impact, or it may notthe consequences may be serious.  It is therefore important to understand and prioritizethe relative importance of business functions and processes as well as their dependence onthey relate to individual PCs, and to install tools and processes that establish priorities and enable appropriate recovery times.
This Business continuity involves integrating recovery policies with help desk functions, data backup solutions, software restoration, and hardware repair or replacement processes.  Automated tools for centralized data backup and software image restoration have become recommended recognized best practices, because they not only accelerate recovery, but can also reduce total costs for IT support.  Of course, it is less than optimal to tack advanced recovery tools and methods onto a chaotic and poorly managed PC environment.  All pPractices that lead to a more standardized, stable, and reliable PC infrastructure ultimately reduce the cost and complexity of PC recovery, and improve enterprise productivity.
Individual PC recovery is relatively simple.  Recovering a network of virus-infected systems is far more complex, and requires effective and coordinated action across the enterprise.  It is essential to have comprehensive recovery plans in place, supported by well-trained employees that know what to do and when.  The impact of a major digital attack can be substantial, and preventative action is essential.  However, since no network is 100% safe, efficient and coordinated recovery efforts are equally important.
See Chapter 5 for more information.
Software Management and Currency
The environment in which the PC operates is dynamic and will continue to be characterized by significant change and growth.  Innovation, spurred by competition and demand, continuously improves hardware and software functionality, performance, usability, and security.  Every organization must manage its processes to continually support ongoing changes, updates, and the introduction of new hardware and software.  Few, if any, are in a position to scrap their existing technologies and start over from scratch, so diversity of systems adds to the challenges surrounding PC maintenance.
One of the most important software currency issues is security patch management.  There is an intense focus on security today, and an active hacker community is ready to capitalize on detected vulnerabilities.  The result is an endless series of security patches from leading software vendors.  These patches must be evaluated, tested, and deployed to appropriate systems based on a clear understanding of costs and associated risks.  This is a significant challenge, yet one which no responsible management team can ignore.
In managing change, businesses must be aware of the risks associated with older productsPC systems.  Software vendors do not provide patches for unsupported systems, but hackers continue to discover vulnerabilities and launch attacks against them.  As noted by Aberdeen Group’s Peter Kastner, the keynote speaker at The IIA’s July 2003 Technology Research Forum, there are more than 40 million desktop computers running outdated operating systems.  Microsoft no longer supports Windows 95, and in 2003 will stop supporting Windows 98 and NT 4.0.  Organizations running those older operating systems will no longer receive services such as software bug fixes, security patches, or new software versions.  According to Kastner and other industry analysts, this will make these systems difficult or impossible to secure.
Interoperability also plays a major role in managing change.  Even though a business function may not need the improved performance or functionality of a new operating system or application, an upgrade may be necessary to maintain compatibility with other software components.  In most cases, the best answer is to follow industry developments and carefully manage change to provide a steady migration toward new solutions, with minimal disruption to business or IT practices.
Enterprise PC standards are an essential component of effective change management.  A chaotic environment with hundreds of different hardware and software configurations is difficult to manage, and nearly impossible to transition in a cost-effective manner.  Of course, standards alone are not a solution.  Adopted standards must be appropriate to the organization’s environment, technology posture, and culture; and effective mechanisms must be in place to ensure and monitor compliance.  Successful solutions are those that automate the management and compliance processes rather than relying on human intervention.
See Chapter 6 for more information.
Total Cost of Ownership and Finance
Auditors traditionally have not dealt with the total cost of ownership (TCO) issue, but now recognize it as an opportunity to add substantial value to the organization.  It is equally important to understand the close connection between business processes and the underlying technology infrastructure.  This can enable auditors to quantify the value of specific IT investments and optimizations, so costs can be effectively balanced against risks and benefits.
The good news is that comprehensive best practices for PC management are a win-win opportunity.  They not only mitigate enterprise risk and improve productivity, but can also substantially reduce total costs.  There is a strong tendency for businesses—especially under difficult economic conditions—to retain PCs as long as they serve their main function.  After all, it is hard to argue with the immediate cost savings of delayed acquisition.  Yet, as already mentioned, 70 to 80 percent of the total cost of PC operation is unrelated to acquisition and deployment costs, and older PCs are inherently more costly to manage and secure.
Here again, there is no one-size-fits-all solution, but a 3-year upgrade cycle for desktops and a 2-year cycle for laptops seems to deliver best overall value for most organizations, especially when instituted as part of a comprehensive best practices program that includes standardized hardware and software configurations and automated tools for core management functions.
Enterprise standards are fundamental to simplifying the PC environment and reducing total costs, yet it is usually not possible to maintain just one standard PC version, or image.  Instead, each organization must balance the number of configurations against productivity requirements and optimal refresh cycles.  This is usually accomplished with a phased approach—replacing machines and software in groups on a planned schedule.  This group approach to upgrades helps ensure a high degree of uniformity, and can be one of the most important factors in controlling TCO.
The research for this report indicates the majority of companies have not yet implemented these best practices.  Due to cost pressures, many tend to look at the short term and do not have regular refresh cycles for systems and software.  More than 50 percent replace PC’s “as needed” or on a cycle greater than four years.  32 percent replace operating systems “as needed,” and 54 percent do not have criteria established for OS upgrades.  There is clearly an opportunity for businesses to join the leaders in this endeavor.  With careful planning and migration, best practices can deliver a reasonable return on investment during implementation, and lasting value once deployed.

The Business Case for Sound PC Practices
What is a “Best Practice?”  It is not what the majority of organizations are doing, but rather a set of integrated processes that have been carefully designed and undertaken in the light of quality management, legal compliance, and efficiency.  Best practices become best practices because they deliver, on average, the best total business value in comparison with implementation costs.  Perhaps most importantly, best practices incorporate internal controls that allow businesses to quantify the value of the practices themselves, and move toward customized operational practices that deliver increasing value.
As will be discussed throughout this report, best practices for PC management impact an enormous variety of key business issues, from risk management and insurance, to legal and regulatory compliance, to business agility, to enterprise productivity, and the total cost of operations.  The PC has been around for many years, and its impacts are pervasive and increasing.  Though the e-business hype has gone the way of the dot-com boom, the movement toward Internet-based solutions continues, with associated improvements in workflow efficiency and cost reduction—and increased dependency on the reliability and security of the PC infrastructure.  With these developments, PC management is no longer an administrative function with limited impact on the organization.  The PC infrastructure has become a strategic business asset, and should be managed accordingly.

Chapter 2
Business Risk and Insurance
Introduction
Effective controls at the PC level are an essential component of overall business management.  In fact, the lack of effective controls has been the cause of total network outages for many organizations, with associated lost business opportunities.  The best solutions incorporate many of the best practices discussed throughout this report.  However, every organization will have its own business risk strategy and will therefore adapt these best practices as needed, and structure its system of internal controls accordingly.
There is no such thing as a completely secure PC or network, so insurance continues to play a key role by transferring residual risk once appropriate internal controls have been established.  This is an area that has undergone—and continues to undergo—much change.  Threats and vulnerabilities are constantly evolving, which alters the type and amount of coverage needed.  Insurance providers are also modifying their policy provisions to ensure they do not cover more risk than they are willing to accept. 
To manage risk in this volatile environment, organizations should closely assess their current coverage to be sure it fits their risk profile and business requirements.  They should also be aware that internal controls can have a significant impact on insurance rates, including the ability to purchase obtain coverage at all.  The stakes are high, and IT organizations should have formal processes in place to regularly monitor, evaluate, and enforce internal controls to ensure compliance with internal objectives, external legislation, regulations, agreements, and insurance coverage provisions.
Risk Model
The business risk model must be comprehensive and closely tailored to match each organization’s dynamic internal and external environment, including its unique mission, strategies and objectives, and the market forces and other factors that impact its success.  Control structures for operations, reporting, compliance, and safeguarding should be designed to move the organization toward its desired results, reduce inherent risk, and manage residual risk.  Of course, these factors depend on each organization’s risk appetite, which will vary with the regulatory environment in their particular industry, and with stakeholder expectations and the importance of protecting results and reputation.  These concepts are described in detail by the Systems Assurance and Control (SAC) Model, which is outlined in Appendix F.
Jay Stott, the Vice President of Information Technology Audit at Fidelity Investments, is the principal author of the SAC Model.  He delivered a presentation at the Technology Audit Forum for this research project in which he addressed factors related to planning and managing the PC fleet.  He described how PC related risks are influenced by a variety of factors, including: a highly dynamic environment, difficulty in determining total cost of operations ownership (TCO), and the fact that PCs are typically managed as personal systems and not as enterprise assets.  Stott emphasized that a significant part of the problem has been caused by the fact that PCs are so inexpensive.  This has resulted in their widespread deployment, typically with inadequate attention to internal controls.  A comprehensive understanding of the total costs and risks associate with the PC infrastructure is essential to drive businesses organizations toward the kinds of formal strategies, policies, and best practices that are appropriate for such a large and distributed enterprise asset.
Many organizations currently use the COSO “Internal Control Integrated Framework” as a control model.  This COSO is generally regarded as an appropriate model for documenting the reliability of internal controls for compliance with the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOA).  However, although it addresses business control activities, it COSO has little to say about the details of supporting technologies.  The SAC Model expands this framework to include the automated support of business controls, as well as controls for individual technology components and processes.
The SAC Model focuses on the key concepts of availability, capability, functionality, protectability, and accountability, which are widely recognized as business control attributes that are particularly pertinent to IT.  These terms are often referred to as business assurance objectives, since they imply a broader range of issues than are traditionally dealt with in a business control framework.  In essence, the SAC Model helps to ensure that management provides the appropriate infrastructure, resources, and organizational commitment to meet these business assurance objectives using the basic resources that are available: people, technology, processes, investment, and communication.
Policies, standardsStandards, and pProcesses
Effective controls depend on a comprehensive framework of policies, including policy statements, standards, and processes.  This framework must extend across many different levels—from the broad policies set by executives and the board to the detailed technical implementation of policies, such as those enforced by an organization’s firewalls or its authentication and authorization solutions.  PC policies and standards address hardware and software requirements as well as permissible use of the systems.  They state whether people can load their own personal software or if the systems are limited to purely business use.  They define whether people can use their own equipment in lieu of company equipment in some situations, such as when remotely accessing email or other business services from home computers.  To provide a meaningful foundation for controls, the policy and standards framework must cover the broad spectrum of PC management issues—extending far beyond the designation of standard platforms and configurations.
Note: Many of the items described below are discussed in more detail later in this report.  The following is intended only to cover the basic issues that should be considered in establishing appropriate policies, standards, and processes for regulating the PC control framework.
Roles and responsibilitiesRoles and Responsibilities
An organization’s people must clearly understand their responsibilities for building, maintaining, monitoring, and using the PC environment.  This is critical because indirect costs for PC management are typically one of the highest components of TCO.  It can be very expensive for users to attempt to learn new software and resolve problems on their own.  It is not uncommon for a small department to have an unofficial “PC guru” who spends considerable time and effort supporting other people.  These ad hoc approaches to PC management contain many hidden costs, and it is important to establish and manage roles and responsibilities effectively.
Planning and bBudgeting
PC hardware and software are treated as assets, and replacement cycles must be planned and budgeted accordingly.  This includes deciding when and how many PCs to deploy and when, and tracking both the hardware and software costs of upgrades.  Some organizations have thousands or tens of thousands of servers and PCs.  The cost of an individual PC is not high.  A good business machine currently runs about $700.  However, it costs approximately $8,000 to $9,000 to support a PC and user over the useful life of the system.  These costs require careful scrutiny, and planning, and management.
Acquisition, deployment, and disposition
Acquisition, deployment, and disposition are three critical aspects of managing and controlling PCs.  Policies should clearly assign authority for equipment purchasing, and standards should identify preferred and allowable systems to buy.  The connection of devices into the network should also be carefully controlled, to prevent unauthorized or insecure access to network resources.  Acquisition and deployment policies should be established to identify who gets what kind of equipment, and how to redeploy systems to new users, if and when appropriate.
Disposition applies not only to the physical removal of a PC, but also to the removal of sensitive data and software prior to disposal.  There was considerable press coverage about data left on machines from failed Internet ventures after the dot- com implosion.  People were buying machines over the Internet that were full of company data that had not been purged.  This is a critical issue, and policies should include proper procedures for removing sensitive information whenever a system is disposed of or redeployed.
The Importance of Disposition Controls
In 2002, an employee in a Canadian company removed a hard drive from a PC.  The PC was being removed from the fleet, but before its data washad not yet been purged.  When the company noticed the drive was missing, they began an investigation and notified several companies that had data on the drive.  The drive was subsequently recovered from the employee, but not before one company notified its customers that their information was potentially compromised.  To avoid such risks, companies must implement strong physical and logical access control throughout the disposal process.
Physical sSecurity
Equipment theft is common, so standards should specify how PCs are physically secured.  Cable locks can be a good investment, even in supposedly secure locations, since it is not uncommon for a thief to tailgate an employee through a door to steal a PC.  The loss of equipment can be trivial compared to the value and sensitivity of information that may be lost or disclosed.
A standard after hours walk around by security personnel to monitor PC security is another useful strategy.  Are systems locked down?  Are they left logged into the network or applications?  Are passwords written on “sticky notes” attached to the monitor?  Security staff can take digital photographs and publish them as ‘bad examples.’
Asset Mmanagement
Our research indicates many organizations manage their PC assets poorly.  Effective asset management begins by knowing who is responsible for each device on the network.  Then, as problems arise, there is a responsible party to contact.  It is also important to know each system’s hardware and software configuration, its physical location, the identity of all authorized users, the details of access management and authorization, and a PC service contact.  Much of this information will change over the life of the system, so accurate tracking can be difficult.  Nevertheless, it is essential for efficient fleet management, and to enable timely remediation when problems arise.
License usageLicense Usage
Most enterprise organizations have agreements that stipulate software vendor payments based on actual installation and/or usage.  They specify whether and how many copies can be made, as well as vendor expectations for how the organization will track and document compliance.  Good license management is important to avoid both overpayment and underpayment, which can result in vendor penalties.
Application sSupport
Application support is expensive, whether it involves end-user help and training or application patching and troubleshooting.  Both tasks involve personnel with specialized expertise, and can include some degree of lost end-user productivity.  These costs and activities should be closely monitored and managed.
Operating System Build, Maintenance, Aand Patching
PC operating systems are subject to frequent vendor updates to repair interoperability issues and to “patch” newly discovered security vulnerabilities.  With the frequency and extent of harm attributed to cyber incidents from viruses, worms, and hacking, it is surprising how few organizations have active and thorough policies and processes for software patch management.  Of the survey respondents, only 58 percent automatically deliver software patches to the PC environment.  85 percent deliver patches as needed rather than on a fixed schedule.
Patch management is required for PCs as well as servers.  The process is complex, and requires a strong and active program that identifies needed patches (not all are critical), distributes them, and audits the environment on a regular basis to be sure they are properly installed.  (See Chapter 7, Software Management and Currency, for more information.)
Logging
Event logging is supported in today’s PC operating systems and should be enabled at an appropriate level on all systems to establish effective audit trails.  However, logging must be carefully tuned, to avoid excessive information gathering that could conceivably fill up an entire hard drive with nothing but logging events.  It is also advisable to establish automated tools for collecting logged information in a central repository where it can be stored and analyzed.  Logs from individual systems are useful, but combined data can be used to detect patterns and trends that impact costs and risks across the infrastructure.
Logical aAccess
Logical access to systems, applications, and information should be fully defined by policy.  In a Microsoft Windows environment, the key issue is “administrator rights;” in a Unix environment it is “super-user rights.”  Administrators and super-users can access and change anything on a PC—often over the network.  In a well controlled environment, this will not be a long list of people, and it is imperative to carefully control and track who they are.  A common strategy among hackers is to attempt to establish administrative rights on any computer they access, so they can set up accounts and passwords, extend their penetration across the network, and cover their tracks.
Firewalls
Firewalls monitor and filter incoming and outgoing traffic between systems and/or networks, and are an integral component of PC security.  Traditionally, firewalls have been used at the network perimeter to control access into the corporate enterprise network.  However, increasing numbers of PCs—including notebooks and home computers—are active outside network boundaries, and are not protected by the network firewall.  In general, these systems should be protected with software-based firewalls that are designed for PCs.  Administering software firewalls on a variety of portable PCs can be challenging, since these applications require frequent updating and patching.  They also tend to present users with messages that can be confusing and even intimidating.  Firewall policies and standards should therefore be carefully constructed to balance the requirements of security and ease-of-use.
Remote  Aaccess
A variety of control techniques can be used to manage remote access to various network resources, and to the network itself.  Depending on the sensitivity of the information, a simple user ID and password may be sufficient.  When appropriate, one-time-use passwords can provide a higher level of security and access restriction.  Virtual private networks (VPN) are often used to encrypt data that is exchanged over the Internet or wireless networks.  Be aware that VPN security only applies to network access and to transmitted data.  The remote user and machine must also employ appropriate security techniques, or the device or session could be hijacked, which would allow a rogue device to communicate over a trusted connection.  The rapid proliferation of mobile PCs and other remote access devices continues to raise new security issues, so controls should be regularly reviewed.
Network sSegmentation
Network switches can be used to segment large networks, which prevents users and devices on one segment from accessing resources on another segment unless actively configured by an administrator to do so.  This strategy can improve both security and performance.  Typically, servers are not attached to the same network segment as a user desktop, and developers are on yet another segment.  (Developers tend to experiment with their systems, and can be a likely gateway for viruses into the network.)  Since every PC does not need to talk to every server, this provides a degree of isolation, which can prevent virus proliferation and/or limit impact.  In the case of NIMDA, for example, it was discovered that 25 infected PCs could spew out enough email to clog a network of 40,000 connected PCs.  However, if those 25 PCs were on a single network segment, only that segment would be impacted.  Business could continue as usual on all other segments.  It is therefore important to consider security issues when establishing policies and standards for network segmentation.
Network Scanning Aand Host Based Monitoring
PCs can be scanned over the network to determine their hardware and software configuration, including the installation of security patches, firewall updates, and virus signatures.  There are also host-based solutions that perform a similar function by installing software agents on each system.  These agents, which then report to a central management application.  Either solution can provide an in-depth review of each device on the network to identify vulnerabilities.  In many cases both are advisable—a host-based system to control the environment, and network scanning to provide an independent audit of security preparedness.
Cyber  iIncident rResponse
Even with the best security controls in place, the issue is not ‘if’ you will be attacked, but rather ‘when.’  It is therefore essential to establish policies and procedures that enable a quick and coordinated response.  A comprehensive plan should include processes, roles, responsibilities, and sufficient training so people know what to do when a crisis hits.
Encryption
In today’s connected organization, data is everywhere, from back-end databases, to mobile PCs and PDAs, to CDs, to key chains with flash USB devices.  Encryption can help to protect that information from theft during both storage and transit.  It can protect against the loss of core business assets and the exposure of sensitive third-party information.
However, clear enterprise encryption standards are essential.  Without enforced standards, encrypted data can become inaccessible if an employee is unable or unwilling to unlock the data.  Consider having a master key somewhere in your encryption scheme, so if something happens to an individual you have the ability to recover their data and protect your company.
Back up and rRecovery
For most companiesorganizations, the vast majority of data in desktop and laptop computers does not get backed up—at least not on a regular basis.  Hard drives do fail and systems can be lost.  Appropriate policies, processes, and tools can protect information and reduce employee downtime.
Enterprise Risk Assessment
Our research indicates over 80 percent of organizations include the PC in their overall enterprise risk assessment.  Moreover, approximately 50 percent differentiate PC risk based on the applications and business processes performed by the system.  For example, if a failed PC is an essential link in an assembly line processes, it could cause significant revenue loss unless a rapid response plan or backup machine is in place.
When an individual PC is connected to the enterprise network, it plays a much larger role in enterprise risk considerations, since it can impact other operations through the introduction of worms and viruses or as an access point for hacking from inside or outside the organization.  These risks must be managed by providing effective controls at each individual PC, and integrating them with the larger control framework.
Insurance and Network Risk
Many organizations are under the impression that their traditional insurance policies cover network security and cyber-related risks,. but Tthis is rarely the case.  Most standard insurance policies specifically exclude network related incidents.  A failure to understand these exclusions could lead to devastating consequences for many businesses, such as loss of revenue and reputation damage—with no insurance coverage.
However, mMany insurers do offer policies directly covering network risks.  According to Tim Burke, Senior Consultant, Aon Technology and Professional Risks Group, “Insurance coverage related to the networked PC environment is still evolving and has gone through significant change in the past two years.”  Aon defines Nnetwork rRisk as “the risks associated with electronic processes and interactions arising from any business activity” such as:
    • Loss of/Damage to Intangible Assets
    • Loss of Income/Business Interruption
    • Electronic Content Liability
    • Network Errors and Omissions
    • Breach of Security/Privacy/Confidentiality
    • Release of a Virus, Trojan Horse, or Worm
The control environment at the desktop and throughout the network is key to understanding these risks and evaluating insurance coverage.  An insurance claim can be negated by the lack of due diligence in ensuring controls are adequately enforced (see the sidebar, The Critical Importance of Due Diligence).
The Critical Importance of Due Diligence
Tim Burke, Senior Consultant, Aon Technology and Professional Risks Group, discussed the case of a retailer who sustained a $10M loss due to a hacking incident involving a former employee.  The company had a $25M First Party network risk insurance policy and submitted a claim to the insurer for the loss.  The investigator discovered the software license for the targeted application had not been renewed, and the software vendor had not supported the application for 60 days preceding the hacker intrusion.  As is typical of network insurance policies, that company’s policy contained an exclusion for software issues resulting from “expiration, cancellation, or withdrawal of support….”
This is just one example of an increasingly common loss.  Insurance policies should be carefully reviewed, and thorough controls should be in place to enassure that due diligence is practiced in addressing their provisions.
First Party Risk
In network insurance policies, first party risk is comparable to traditional property insurance.  It involves the actual interruption of your network and the associated loss of revenue.  Part of that loss includes contingent business interruption, which covers your trading partners if they were affected by your downtime and their revenue stream is interrupted.  First party risk may also include extra expenses, when properly quantified, such as the actual time and resources consumed in rebuilding your system.  Many carriers do not provide this coverage because they cannot quantify the risk; or they may include $25 million limits, to protect against huge losses from aggregated attacks.  In reality, however, most larger financial institutions probably need a billion dollars or more in network risk insurance.
First party risk may also address intangible property, including theft, damage, or cost to restore these assets.  Policies may also cover the failure to safeguard electronic representations of proprietary algorithms, trade secrets, and other intellectual property.
Third Party Liability
Third party liability when related to information and networks is also known as cyber-liability, and is an area of potential litigation.  It is considered part of network risk because there is a first party aspect to it.  If, for example, you are dependent upon an application service provider, the provided applications are part of your network, and the inability to access them means downtime for your business.  If you are the application service provider, on the other hand, you need to protect yourself against litigation for your failure to perform.  Another example is breach of security and confidentiality, such as failure to comply with privacy legislation.  You may also need third-party liability insurance in case PCs in your network are hijacked and used to institute denial of service attacks on other organizations.

Enterprise Call to Action
Enterprise risk management, as well as the overall risk assessment performed by Internal Audit, should specifically include risks that arise in the networked PC environment.  These risks are evolving rapidly, as new threats, technologies and business models emerge.  PC controls must evolve in tandem.  This requires close collaboration among business leaders, IT decision makers, security experts and financial analysts.  Internal auditors can play an important role in promoting and managing this cooperation.
Enterprise risk in a networked PC environment can be substantial, and the scope and rigor of controls should be proportional to that risk.  To be effective, PC controls must be comprehensive and detailed.  They should cover not only PC hardware and software configurations, but also the people, processes, and technologies related to PC and network security, use, administration, and maintenance.  To be effective, they must also span all organizational levels, from the general policies issued by boards, to the details of implementation that depend on individual technicians and PC users.
Since even the best-controlled PC environment is not risk free, insurance should be an integral part of the overall risk management process. Most importantly, management should understand that traditional insurance policies do not cover network risk, and that network policies are rapidly evolving.  Given the frequency and impact of cyber-related outages, it is critical to track these changes and adapt accordingly.

Legislation, Regulation, and Compliance and Regulatory
Introduction
Introduction

Government and non-profit regulatory bodies worldwide are playing an increasing role in establishing baseline security requirements for business information technology.  Even regulations that do not explicitly focus on security may have substantial consequences for IT organizationsmanagement.  Examples include the International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS), Basel II Accord, the Corporate Law Economic Reform Program (CLERP), the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOA) and the Health Insurance Portability & Accountability Act (HIPAA).  These and similar other laws and regulations focus on issues such as privacy, financial responsibility, and the reliability of reported information.  All of these issues have become deeply dependent on underlying computing systems, and will therefore have repercussions on the way businesses use and manage their PC infrastructure.
The PC plays a complex role in many core issues, including security, privacy, information and control reliability, risk management, governance, and even critical infrastructure protection and national defense.  Within the overall system of business controls, the PC is both a component subject to control and a tool with which many controls are applied and managed.  It is a unique device that defies simple classification.  A business PC is a(n):
    • physical asset, 
    • processing device, 
    • repository for software and information assets, 
    • means to access information stored on other devices, 
    • communication tool, 
    • potential liability, as it can harbor both beneficial and detrimental software and data, 
    • point of vulnerability, whereby attackers may introduce harm to the network or system, 
    • point of entry for gaining access to other systems and network components, 
    • access and egress point for sensitive enterprise or private information, 
    • invaluable tool supporting enterprise productivity, 
    • and much more.
In short, the PC infrastructure presents many issues for management to consider with respect to legal and regulatory compliance in particular, and internal controls in general. 
This chapter focuses briefly on today’s legal and regulatory environment, then moves on to discuss the overriding value of strong internal controls, and the value of the COSO model for establishing those controls.  It then describes some of the key issues regarding internal controls for the PC infrastructure.


The Regulatory Environment

Good internal control is no longer just a best practice… It’s the Law!  
—PricewaterhouseCoopers

There is a global move toward tighter regulatory requirementslaws and regulations for internal controls associated with financial reporting.  One of the most far-reaching examples is the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (see below), not because it is the most stringent, but because it cuts across vertical industries and impacts any company that is publicly listed in the U.S.  Similar regulations arelegislation is emerging in many jurisdictions throughout the world. 
Privacy legislation is also evolving rapidly.  In today’s networked business environment, back-end data systems are often highly consolidated and are accessible by applications that are, in turn, accessible from the Internet.  This is a great boon for accelerating transactions and reducing the cost of doing business.  However, it also raises significant new security and liability concerns, and tight controls are necessary to protect individuals, industries and economies.  Ultimately, any company wishing to take part in the global economy and most national economies will have to demonstrate an appropriate level of internal controls.
Both sets types of regulationslegislation—financial reporting and privacy—have implications for the way companies deploy, manage, and monitor their PC infrastructure.  The vast majority of business information is collected, accessed, shared, and sometimes stored on networked PCs, and these systems are often distributed across large geographical regions.  The integrity and security of these networked systems—and the communications between them—are fundamental to the integrity and security of the vast majority of critical business information and processes. 
It would be impossible to discuss, or even reference, all the legislation and regulations that impact PC use and management.  We therefore focus on just a few, with the understanding that they are representative of a growing regulatory framework that is affecting businesses organizations throughout the world. 

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act

“The Fortune 1000 will spend $2.5B in initial Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOA) compliance work, including hefty IT investments, as 85% of companies prepare to make systems changes to support SOA requirements.”
—	Prioritizing IT Investments for Sarbanes-Oxley Compliance, Lindsey Sodano and John Hagerty, AMR Research, June 2003.

SOA Section 302 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOA) requires chief executive officers and chief financial officers of all publicly traded companies in the U.S. to personally certify in each financial report that it does not contain any untrue statements or omit any facts that are material to a true representation of the company’s financial condition.  They must also acknowledge personal responsibility for establishing and maintaining disclosure controls and procedures, and for disclosing to their auditors and audit committee all significant deficiencies in internal controls that could adversely affect their ability to record, process, summarize, and report financial data.  Finally, they must disclose any fraud that involves management or other employees who have a significant role in their internal controls.
Section 404 extends these requirements to the annual reports of these companies.  Each report must formally acknowledge the responsibility of management for establishing and maintaining adequate procedures and internal control structures for financial reporting; and provide a full report and assessment of those structures, as of the end of the most recent fiscal year.  The organization’s public accounting firm must also attest to the accuracy and validity of the assessment.  SOA Section 409 makes the need for reporting immediate, and the need for monitoring continuous.
Privacy Legislation
Privacy is well covered in other IIA and IIA Research Foundation reports, and is included here to emphasize the relevance of this issue to PC management.  Examples of significant legislation in this area include:
    • Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act - Financial Privacy—Gramm-Leach-Bliley requires financial institutions to safeguard customers’ records and information against unauthorized access.  It applies to “financial institutions,” and includes not only banks, securities firms, and insurance companies, but also companies providing many other types of financial products and services to consumers.
    • HIPAA - The Health Insurance Portability & Accountability Act of 1996—It is the responsibility of organizations entrusted with health information to protect that information against deliberate or inadvertent misuse or disclosure.  Since much of the relevant data is transmitted through or maintained on PCs, the health care industry must provide additional measures to ensure patient data is adequately secured.
    • California Law SB 1386—This legislation, which took effect on July 1, 2003, creates new disclosure requirements for any company or government agency that does business with California residents.  It requires timely disclosure of any security breaches involving the exposure of unencrypted personal information.  It also preserves a private right of action for damages and injunctive relief against any offending company.  Because of this, it is quite possible that class action suits could result from non-compliance with this law.
The Overriding Value of Internal Controls
One of the most important aspects of the regulatory environment is that it establishes a framework that is often used by courts to define “due diligence” during civil and criminal litigation.  It is therefore important that businesses monitor and evaluate regulatory issues so they can properly understand their risk profile with respect to potential litigation.   
This is clearly an important issue, yet it remains secondary to a larger truth.  Regulations typically strive to move businesses toward a baseline implementation of what is commonly accepted as best practices for internal control.  As such, the regulatory environment invariably lags behind the implementations of leading companies.  Moreover, best practices for internal controls stand on their own merit.  They deliver value to the bottom line by improving efficiency and decreasing risk across a wide range of internal and external variables.
As a result, bBusinesses that focus only on regulatory compliance are targeting the lowest common denominator, and putting themselves at a competitive disadvantage.  On the other hand, companies that focus on best practices for internal controls drive greater value into their business.  Regulatory compliance becomes a matter of properly documenting existing controls, rather than implementing major business change.
These advantages are even more compelling in today’s dynamic regulatory environment.  Strong internal controls position a business to respond more quickly and easily to emerging requirements, whatever they might may be.  They also help a business to understand and evaluate regulatory requirements within the context of their larger business, financial, and risk environment. 
The overriding value of effective internal controls is reflected in the many companies and government agencies that are optimizing their internal control structures, even though they are not regulated by Sarbanes-Oxley.  The global competitive and litigative environment is sufficient motivation for most leading organizations to embrace sound business management and control practices and adopt a culture of best practices and continuous improvement.  Of course, there are also companies that are choosing to remain privately held entities specifically to avoid the overhead of Sarbanes-Oxley compliance.  Though we hesitate to make broad generalizations, we believe that, in general, most Such a strategy is not likely to serve an organizations are not well in the long runserved by that strategy.
COSO Control Model
The COSO1 “Internal Control Integrated Framework” defines internal control as a process that is effected by an entity’s people and that is designed to provide reasonable assurance regarding the achievement of objectives in: effectiveness and efficiency of operations, reliability of financial reporting, and compliance with applicable laws and regulations.
The COSO framework identifies five interrelated components of internal control:
    89. Control Environment – People (and their individual attributes including integrity, ethical values, and competence), and the environment in which they operate.
    90. Risk Assessment – Awareness of the risks facing the organization; established objectives, integrated with business activities, so the organization operates in concert; and established mechanisms to identify, analyze, and manage related risks.
    91. Control Activities – Control policies and procedures established and executed to ensure risks are effectively managed and the organization’s objectives are achieved.
    92. Information and Communications – Systems that enable the organization’s people to capture and exchange the information needed to conduct, manage, and control its operations.
    93. Monitoring – Continuous appraisal to allow the control system to react dynamically, changing as conditions warrant.
The COSO internal control components are depicted as a cube (Figure 3.1) relating the internal controls (rows) to the key areas of focus: Operations, Financial Reporting, and Compliance (columns) for all the organization’s units and activities (third dimension).  As the framework indicates, internal control is not a serial process, in which each component affects the next; but a multidirectional iterative process in which any component can influence any other.
The iterative nature of controls has long been recognized in IT where the reliability of application or business process controls is dependent on the reliability of controls in the overall IT environment.  For example, business controls over financial information depend, in part, on IT controls that prevent and detect unauthorized access to financial systems and data.  For example, a PC that has been corrupted by a virus or hacker could remain a trusted component of the network with routine access to financial data and systems.  This could lead to the disclosure or corruption of financial data; or to the corruption of program code and/or the insertion of rogue programs that could do further damage.  Adequate controls at the system and application level are therefore essential to ensure that higher-level business processes are properly controlled. 

Note: The ISO 17799 standard is another useful framework for technology controls that can be used in support of best practices and regulatory compliance.  It focuses primarily on providing a security base line for evaluating the PC environment.

Monitoring and Enforcement of PC Controls
In a well controlled IT environment, intrusion controls and monitoring should detect unauthorized activity.  However, weaknesses in software management and/or monitoring systems could allow such attacks to go undetected.  Assuming the attacker gains access to trusted system administration features, he/she may establish accounts and passwords that broaden access and enable the hacker to destroy evidence of unauthorized activity.
The combination of controls needed to prevent and detect such activity includes, but is not limited to: password management, anti-virus measures, restriction of administrator privileges on the PC and network, system change management, monitoring of unusual systems activities, and management review and analysis of financial information and transactions. 
The monitoring of activities is critical for effective control.  Given the complexity of the environment, automated monitoring is typically required to enable a timely response to any break down in control activities.  For example, if software patches are not up to date on a networked PC, automated configuration monitoring tools will provide a timely warning for quick remediation.
In the survey conducted for this report, 92 percent of respondents indicated they have controls in place to enforce corporate standards.  However, only 8 percent of those respondents indicated that controls were tightly enforced.  When asked about areas where best practices are followed, many cited compliance with software license agreements.  No other area specific to regulatory compliance was mentioned.
The Role of the Chief Information Officer (CIO)
The CIO has traditionally maintained custodial responsibility for the organization’s financial records and other sensitive business information.  The CIO is also typically responsible for custody of the hardware and software resources that process, communicate, and store that information and generate reports.
Typical characteristics and responsibilities of the CIO include:
    • Maintaining an effective control environment for the security of systems, networks, information, communications capabilities, and interfaces with outside parties.
    • Organizing and coordinating efforts for technology risk management and compliance with laws, regulations, agreements, policies, and procedures.
    • Establishing an appropriate technology risk task force with representation across the enterprise.
    • Commissioning or conducting a technology risk assessment and acting on all findings.
    • Monitoring the ongoing management of risks related to technology.
    • Reviewing new products for security and privacy implications.
    • Supporting/delivering employee information security and privacy training.
    • Conducting regular security audits, and exposing any internal practices that are not consistent with security policy.
    • Monitoring ongoing technology threats to the organization and maintaining effective protection and recovery capabilities.
    • Reporting to top management on security and controls.
Clearly the CIO is responsible for a large portion of the organization’s system of internal controls, and must provide assurance to the CEO and CFO to enable them to comply with regulatory requirements.
Maturity of PC Regulatory Practices
In developing an efficient plan for migrating toward optimized internal controls, the concept of maturity can be useful.  The maturity of internal controls can be viewed as a progression through five stages:
    1. Unreliable—The environment is unpredictable and control activities are not implemented.
    2. Informal—Control activities are in place, but are not adequately documented.
    3. Standardized—Control activities are in place and documented.
    4. Monitored—Controls are standardized and periodically tested for effective design and operation. 
    5. Optimized—Controls are fully integrated, monitored in real-time and continuously improved.
This maturity scale can be useful in evaluating an organization’s current internal controls, and in developing a comprehensive strategy for improving practices and techniques across the organization.
The Auditor’s Role in PC Control Structures
Internal audit organizations should include review of PC controls as part of every audit, and should ensure that PC control issues are communicated to end users and integrated into the overall control process.  The framework for audit assurance is made up of a set of audit activities and methodologies that can be used to facilitate, influence, and assess an organization’s security program. 
In addition to core security audits, the internal audit group can engage in a variety of facilitation activities, such as Control Self Assessment surveys that target the PC environment.  Questions in these surveys can be designed to improve overall awareness of critical PC security issues for a variety of target groups.  They also provide internal audit teams with useful information for understanding and shaping the security culture in their organization.
Many other types of reviews and approaches can be considered to encompass various components of the audit program.  Some potential audit projects include:
    • Enterprise PC Policy Assessment
    • Security Program Development Facilitation
    • Employee Security Policy and Data Protection Assessment
    • Enterprise and Network Security Capability Assessment
    • Network Risk, Security, and Architecture Assessment
    • Review of Enterprise  Intrusion Monitoring Capabilities
    • Review of Enterprise Data Classification, Retention, Archiving, and Disposition Activities.
Levels of Assessment for PC Controls
A PC control assessment can be designed to focus on broad organizational concerns or narrow security issues pertaining to specific applications or business processes.  In the first case it is called a breadth assessment, in the second a depth assessment.  Both are necessary to develop a full understanding of an organization’s internal controls.  Both can also be broken down into smaller units so the auditor can provide assurance around a specific component. 
Breadth Assessments
Breadth assessments provide an opportunity to develop a broad perspective and analysis across a number of related functions, processes, activities, and systems.  This type of assessment typically identifies all the activities and PC control points related to a common set of management functions.  An example would be an analysis of the control environment for gathering, consolidating and leveraging customer data.  Customer data is typically gathered from many sources and activities, and a wide variety of PCs and individuals are involved.  Data may be accessed through different applications, both online and offline.  It may also be stored in different places, and aggregated in one or more back-end marketing databases.  Information may be extracted from these databases into a master customer database that is maintained by an entirely different function. 
In this example, there are numerous data risks and potential control points where the auditor might provide assessment and risk analysis.  Areas such as policy, privacy statements, Web applications, telephone applications, back-end databases, transfers of data, and uploads from remote locations should all be considered, along with any other factors pertinent to the particular environment.  An assessment survey is often a good starting point, since it provides a relatively quick method for gathering information from the many parties involved.
Breadth assessments are appropriate to high-level risk assessment.  The number of activities and systems that are included preclude detailed reviews of process, application, security, and information technology.  However, they do provide a broad perspective on the types of risk the organization faces, along with useful information for constructing depth assessments that focus on key areas of concern.
Depth Assessments
A depth assessment is a vertical, detailed audit that focuses on a specific business function and related processes, activities, and systems.  An approach is developed to evaluate the business control environment; security policies and practices; applications used to collect, process, or store data; infrastructure components; network and operating system configuration controls; IT processes for managing systems and software, etc.  This level of detail can help to provide a complete assessment of risks and controls, to understand the integrity of PC management within a given business unit or function.
Leveraging Limited Audit Resources
In some ways, PC management is like the Year 2000 issue.  For most organizations, it spans a large number of businesses, processes, activities, and systems.  Dedicated resources for addressing PC management issues may be constrained, or even non-existent, and the magnitude and complexity of the task can be intimidating.
Control self-assessment can provide an opportunity to leverage audit knowledge across a broad set of organizational areas.  Self-assessments may be performed in support of a corporate security group or CSO, or as a business facilitation effort.  One approach is to develop a PC audit program, and then leverage personnel in each business or department to execute the program with or without internal audit assistance.  A Web-based system is often ideal for leveraging limited resources to address high-risk issues.  This approach provides a central, easily accessible resource for deploying audit programs and evaluating and sharing results.
Annual Documentation and Substantiation
For the annual assessment, management must have procedures to demonstrate they have designed and maintained an effective system of controls.  The answersolution is documentation, documentation, and more documentation.  All processes, control objectives, and control activities should be documented, along with evidence that demonstrates their effectiveness. 
Substantiating evidence, such as PC system logs, help desk logs, etc., should be retained and accessible to auditors for a length of time in keeping with documentation requirements.  One solution is to store relevant data in easily accessible storage systems for a full year to meet this requirement; then preserve them on tape for longer periods as appropriate.  Another is to extend the duration of the audit process across the full year, so controls can be documented without storing large amounts of data for extended periods.
In addressing compliance issues and documentation, it is important to understand the goals, motivations and power of the governing authority.  In some cases, a major violation might simply result in a warning letter that points out weaknesses and stipulates a timeframe for improvement.  In others, the potential might may exist for your business to be fined or constrained from doing business as usual.  The more you understand about the larger social and business context, the better you can focus your efforts on the desired outcome. 
Enterprise Call to Action
It is important to monitor and understand regulatory requirements.  However, leading businesses organizations will not let this become the key driver for improving internal controls in their PC or other environments.  Instead, management should focus on continuously improving the areas addressed throughout this paperreport, such as: software currency (including patches), information security, virus updates, 24/7 operations, and continuous monitoring of the control environment.  If these key issues are addressed and an adequate control environment is in place, regulatory compliance will automatically also be satisfied.  Of course, some specific industry regulations, such as HIPAA, GLB, or FERC, may require some additional measures be taken.  In most cases, however, a good internal control structure will make it much easier to address these additional needs.
To ensure comprehensive controls, a framework such as COSO should be used in setting up structures and establishing documentation standards.  A periodic (annual) breadth assessment should be in placeconducted to evaluate and validate compliance with regulatory requirements.  An automated, in-depth monitoring process that includes the reporting of exceptions should be in place as part of the overall model.

Introduction
“People think this is a minor problem, all I need to do is hire someone to come in to put up a firewall, all I need is to have password access.  Wrong!  It is not a minor problem, and it is not easily fixed.  It takes constant attention and there is no silver bullet.”
—	U.S. presidential advisor Richard A. Clarke, National Coordinator for Security, Infrastructure Protection, and Counter-Terrorism, 2001.
Information Security is the number one issue for the American technology community, according to the results of the 2003 Top Technologies survey by the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants (AICPA).  It took the lead spot in the same survey in 2001, and came in third in 2002.  One reason for this ongoing concern is that security risks continue to increase in volume and complexity.  In the annual survey of U.S. corporations and government agencies conducted by the FBI and Computer Security Institute, 90 percent of respondents detected computer security breaches in the past year.
Yet quantifying security risk is difficult and solutions are complex.  Many businesses continue to rely on centralized network security solutions, such as corporate firewalls and intrusion detection systems.  These are important components of an enterprise security framework.  However, the proliferation of mobile PCs, wireless networking, and connected computing solutions continues to blur the boundaries of the enterprise network.  With these advances, the concept of a security hardened perimeter for the protection of systems and information within the organization has expanded.  Network safeguards must operate in concert with trusted computing environments based on the security of platforms, systems software, and operating systems.  Given the volume and diversity of PCs, notebooks and handheld devices in the average organization, an effective risk mitigation program must pay close attention to the client infrastructure.
Virtually every aspect of PC fleet management has an impact on information security, from platform choices and software management to upgrade cycles and end-user education.  Security also depends on the implementation and management of key security-focused technologies and processes, such as PC vulnerability scanning, software patching, virus protection, personal firewalls, user authentication, and the administration of access privileges.  Perhaps the greatest challenge is that security must be maintained individually on every PC because a single weak link can put the whole infrastructure at risk, threaten core business assets, and entail hundreds of thousands of dollars in IT cleanup costs.
Even with the plethora of media coverage of hacking, worms, and viruses in recent years, there is a general lack of understanding of the basic elements of information security, and of the return on security investments.  This chapter focuses on providing a framework for addressing information security, and outlines some of the key issues related to the PC infrastructure.  For this report, the widely accepted description of information security including integrity, confidentiality, and availability is used.  Information security is a subset of overall security which also includes physical security.
IT security is a core business concern for many organizations.  Security is mandated for certain regulated industries and closely watched for others that make up a nation’s critical infrastructures.  Security failures in critical infrastructures impact not only the organization but also other industries depending on such organizations — and potentially the national defense and economy.

Additional Security Guidance from the IIA:
Security solutions are complex.  They involve interactions across the entire organization, up and down the management structure, and across organizational boundaries to key players outside of the organization.  The February 2001 report, Information Security Governance: What Directors Need to Know2, from The IIA, addresses the governance and management perspectives on information security starting with the audit committee of the board of directors.  It poses ten questions directors should ask about security management, and provides guidance on ensuring the answers are appropriate for the organization.
Enterprise Risk and the PC Infrastructure
The PC infrastructure continues to increase in importance to information security.  One reason is the high volume of business and personal information that resides on the hard drives of PCs, notebooks, and PDAs.  Engineers, accountants, executives, and many other employee classes often store sensitive information on their systems.  In today’s connected environment, any device may be the target of a digital attack that results in the theft or corruption of that information.  Mobile devices pose somewhat greater risks, since they are often connected to the Internet from locations outside the corporate firewall.  They are also more vulnerable to theft, especially if left unattended in an airport or other public location.
What makes client devices even more critical to information security is that a compromised system can provide a gateway into the network, which can put core business assets at risk.  A digital threat, such as a virus or worm, can spread through the network from a single infected email.  There is also the danger of hacker infiltration.  A common ploy is to take advantage of known vulnerabilities in popular Web browsers to install a back door application into a users PC.  The back door then automatically initiates a connection to the hacker’s system and the hacker is notified of his success.  He can then capture the user’s keystrokes, including their user names and passwords.  With this strategy, a hacker can literally take over a user’s system gaining access to everything available to the victim.
Viruses, Worms, and Other Threats
Anyone who even occasionally reads or listens to the news knows the incidence of cyber attacks has been increasing steadily over the last several years.  The Computer Emergency Response Team Coordination Center (CERT CC) indicates the number of security incidents reported in the first half of 2003 (76,404) is very close to the total (82,094) incidents for all of 2002.  In 2002 incidents increased 56 percent over 2001, and if incidents continue at the current rate, the 2003 total will increase 86 percent over 2002.
Viruses, worms, and similar threats are well covered in available publications, and are not addressed in depth in this report.  Rather, the impacts of these threats are addressed in the context of security management.
A worm such as SoBig can turn individual PC’s into spam machines.  When a newly discovered vulnerability is exploited by such a worm, actions are required across a broad spectrum and may include such steps as those taken by America Online Inc. who blocked about 11.5 million copies in messages in the first day.  Sobig.F hit the computing world as corporations were still recovering from several worms that spread through holes in Microsoft Corp.'s Windows operating systems, including the Blaster worm.  It infected and crashed hundreds of thousands of computers in less than a week.  Most of these attacks are difficult to trace to their origin, and are becoming significantly more sophisticated as blended worm, spam, and other techniques converge.
The Welchi, or Nachi, worm infected 72,000 computers used by the U.S. Navy and Marine Corps and crippled Air Canada's reservation counters and call centers.  CSX Transportation said a virus infection had slowed its dispatching and signal systems, forcing it to halt passenger and freight train traffic, including the morning commuter train service in Washington.
The timeframe between the announcement of a vulnerability from Microsoft and the release of an exploit worm or virus continues to decrease.  Those writing the attack software are getting more sophisticated in the exploits.  They take advantage of vulnerabilities and leverage previous experience in writing new code.  Exploits that target specific websites or specific machines with an army of “zombie” machines can cause overpower system and network capacity disrupting network services and crashing individual machines.
Such attacks also continue to succeed because of the volume of vulnerable systems that remain attached to the Internet long after corrective software patches are made available.
Considerations for PC Security
Fundamental advances in PC platform security have been underway for several years, and the recent generation of platforms, operating systems, applications and management tools enable better security at lower costs than older systems.  They also interoperate more effectively with standards-based solutions for network security, which helps to simplify implementation and improve overall security.  The key is to combine existing technologies effectively to establish a comprehensive, standards-based security solution that is both efficient and extensible.
The following are key factors in establishing information security for individual PCs.  However, PC security also depends on the overall health and agility of the entire client infrastructure.  The implementation of comprehensive best practices for PC management is therefore as important to security as it is to cost reduction.  For that reason, many of the issues related below are discussed in more detail in other chapters of this report.
    • Software Currency and Patch Management: Security analysts estimate approximately 90% of digital attacks target known vulnerabilities in popular applications and operating systems.  It is therefore essential to actively monitor published vulnerabilities and distribute appropriate patches made available by software vendors.  Standard hardware and software configurations and automated software distribution tools are important components of this process.  (See the following chapter, Software Management and Currency, for more information.)
    • Anti-Virus and Personal Firewalls: These applications are essential for mobile systems that operate outside the protected network, and they add a useful layer of additional protection for systems within the network.  New threats emerge constantly, so these applications must be closely managed to ensure virus signatures and firewall profiles are up to date.
    • Configuration Monitoring: Patches and updates can be sent out over the network, but may not be properly installed in each PC.  Moreover, end-users may download vulnerable software or turn off key security functions to improve performance.  Because of these issues, hardware and software configurations should be actively monitored over the network.  It is generally recommended that vulnerability scans be performed at least weekly (daily is better), and that a routine process be established for rapidly assessing published vulnerabilities.  Assessments can include standard rating levels (crisis, high, moderate, low) with predetermined timelines for remediation (1 week, 1 month, 2 months, a quarter).  It is also important to assign accountable organizations to implement the stipulated controls.
    • Encryption: File encryption is becoming a best practice for mobile systems, so stored data is not accessible on a stolen notebook.  Network encryption via Virtual Private Networking (VPN) is also recommended for wireless systems.  This is especially important if they are being used to access the network from remote locations.  However, wireless communications can typically be monitored from up to a half mile away using relatively inexpensive and inconspicuous equipment.  For this reason, encryption is also recommended for sensitive wireless transmissions within the enterprise.
    • Performance: Older PCs may not provide adequate performance when running security software in tandem with productivity applications.  This can lead to users turning off security applications and putting the network at risk.  In general, such systems should be retired.
    • Logging: Newer operating systems, such as Windows 2000 or Windows XP, provide logging capabilities for auditing security events.  This is important for compliance and forensic investigation, and for understanding attacks to resolve vulnerabilities and limit damage.  Without logging capabilities, a hacker intrusion could go completely undetected.  Even worse, the malicious actions performed by the attacker could be attributed to the authorized user, and there would be no audit trail for proving otherwise.
    • Security Policies and User Awareness: An uninformed user can compromise network security in many ways, including faulty password management, opening a questionable email, downloading vulnerable software, or leaving an unguarded system logged onto the network or a business application.  Policies should be regularly reviewed and should cover current issues, such as software piracy, shareware, open source systems, unauthorized software, inappropriate content, remote PC connectivity, removal of company information, and the use of home computers for work purposes.  Training should take place on a regular basis and should include a sign-off to validate awareness and due diligence.
A balanced approach is recommended in constructing and enforcing policies, since the results can significantly impact employee satisfaction, information security and potential liability.  Policies and processes should also address the risk of malicious insiders accessing authorized information.  Internal checks and balances are important, and should be proportional to the value of the information and the associated risk of exposure or corruption.
    • Smart Card or Biometric User Authentication: Network IDs and passwords are important security tools, but they can be stolen or spoofed.  Depending on your organization’s risk profile, stronger authentication methods may be appropriate.  This is especially true for businesses seeking to simplify user access and reduce support costs by deploying authentication middleware, such as password management, single sign-on, or authentication management infrastructure (AMI) products.  These tools can be valuable and cost-effective.  However, they also enable access to more information with fewer security checks.  Stronger authentication can mitigate this increased risk.
The Importance of an Integrated Security Framework
PC security is one component in the larger enterprise security environment.  Network, server, physical, and operational security are also essential.  An effective solution requires careful integration among all these elements, and should be part of a comprehensive security strategy that is driven from the highest levels of the organization.  Key business decision-makers should be involved in establishing and implementing security policy.  Ideally, those who set the agenda for corporate security should be the same individuals who control the investment and take responsibility for success.
Solid security solutions are possible today, as is demonstrated by the wide support for Internet-based transactions in sensitive financial markets.  In general, businesses should take advantage of proven technologies and methods.  Innovation should not be the goal of security programs, but rather careful implementation and integration of proven technologies, backed by appropriate processes.
Consider the following actions and practices in the context of your own industry, organization, and culture.  There is no one-size-fits-all solution, but these recognized practices should help.
    • Risk Management: Reducing corporate risk can improve bottom line performance by reducing material impacts resulting from exploited vulnerabilities.  Benefits may also be reflected in reduced insurance premiums.  To align security investments with business needs, a formal approach to risk management is recommended (see Return on Investment for Security Spending, below).  Security is not an all or nothing proposition, but a series of tradeoffs, preferably based on quantitative analysis.  Each organization must establish appropriate levels of security based on their unique environment, and provide customers and/or associates with sufficent confidence that digital transactions are accurate, private and performed only with trusted parties.
    • Security Assessments and Audits: It is important to understand where and how you are vulnerable to potential threats, including both internal and external attacks.  It can be useful to enlist an outside security consultant to provide expertise.  You can also take advantage of freely available resources, such as the Center for Internet Security.  (CIS, www.CISecruity.org, provides methods and tools to improve, measure, monitor, and compare the security status of your Internet-connected systems and appliances, plus those of your business partners.  CIS members identify security threats of greatest concern, then participate in development of practical methods to reduce vulnerability to the threats.).  
There is no substitute for periodic independent and objective audits.  Make sure internal audit stays in touch with the information security organization, and follow-up on audit recommendations.
    • A Security Roadmap: A well constructed plan can allow you to continuously reduce your vulnerability and to increase the total value of your computing environment.
    • A Comprehensive Approach: Strong security requires thoughtful implementation and ongoing management.  An effective solution must address not only technology issues, but also people, processes and policies.  It should also be designed to support continuous improvement.  This typically requires:
        ◦ An accountable corporate security executive, who can enlist active support from key business decision-makers.
        ◦ Formal processes for managing, assessing, auditing, and upgrading security solutions and policies.  Technology alone is not an answer.
        ◦ A dedicated response team with formal disaster recovery and business continuity plans that are regularly reviewed and tested.  Ideally, they would be used to respond to daily events.  Formal plans are useless unless the organization knows how to implement them when a real crisis hits.
        ◦ A dedicated team for tracking security issues on a daily basis, and for keeping tabs on new regulations and technology advances.
        ◦ Sensitivity to internal politics and external trends and developments.  For example, new regulations or a particularly successful attack can open doors that might normally be closed, enabling substantial progress in a shorter time frame.
Quantifying Risk and Value
There are many reasons for implementing a strong security program, including the auditing of risk management and compliance and the protection of intellectual property and proprietary information.  These issues are important, and should be key to justifying the value of security investments.  However, they must ultimately be linked to bottom line value through return on investment analysis if security is to receive funding in relationship to its value.  IT security investments are increasingly being held to the same standard as other investments and it is incumbent upon IT management to introduce analytical rigor into their decision making process.  To justify initial efforts, it can be instructive to look at industry norms for the cost of security breaches.  However, an effective long-term strategy will require a more customized analysis of the return on investment for security spending in your particular organization.
The Cost of Security Breaches
Most businesses are understandably reluctant to share information about serious security events, since a publicized breach can impair their reputation, and may raise serious trust issues with customers and business collaborators.  It can therefore be difficult to obtain reliable financial information regarding the impact of security breaches, and estimates have varied considerably.
A recent research study by Ashish Garg, Ph.D., Jeffrey Curtis, and Hilary Halper of Ernst & Young LLP’s Business Analytics Practice sheds light on this important issue, by quantifying the impact of security incidents on stock price.  It also explores the impact on stock price of information security spending.  The results were published in “The Financial Impact of IT Security Breaches: What do Investors Think,” Information Systems Security, March/April 2003.  A series of articles in the ITAudit.org Forum also addresses the financial implications of security breaches and the return on security investments.  See Information Security Governance, ITAudit Forum, February 15, 2001 at www.ITAudit.org.
These sources indicate the actual cost of security events are actually greater than previously estimated, and a single event can cost millions of dollars.  Any meaningful reduction in risk or damage can therefore readily justify the cost of a sizable security program.  Of course, such studies provide only general metrics that may not apply to specific businesses.  However, they do provide a useful starting point, and can provide business justification for establishing a security program that can address the fundamental technical and financial issues that are unique to each organization.
Evaluating Return on Investment for Security Spending
Assessing the value of specific security investments begins by quantifying the value and vulnerability of key corporate assets—such as customer data, intellectual property, financial information, production systems, etc.—along with the potential loss that would be caused by damage or theft.  The analysis must also take into account the threat matrix in today’s connected environment, and the costs and risk mitigation potential of each investment.
Jeff Curtis, a Senior Consultant in Ernst & Young’s Economics & Business Analytics practice, has outlined a Value of Risk Avoidance methodology (VoRA) to assess the Return on Security Investments (ROSI).  VoRA provides a practical approach to assessing the types of threats and their expected frequency and impacts on employees and business assets.  It uses this information to quantify the value of risk avoidance in comparison with specific security program costs.  As noted in the previous section, security breeches will ultimately effect the stock price.  VoRA complements stock-focused analysis, by identifying and quantifying both tangible and intangible precursors to reduced market valuation.  These include:
    • Loss of productivity
    • Loss of revenue
    • IT support costs
    • Cost of system repair/replacement
    • Cost of insurance
    • Impact on brand and reputation
    • Loss of trading partner confidence
    • Increased regulatory risk
    • Legal implications
Curtis also addresses some of the key factors that impact an organizations risk profile, such as: the structure of the organization; the number and types of employees likely to be impacted; the distribution of employees and company information; and the industry regulatory environment.  A detailed evaluation of these factors can help security administrators understand and quantify the value of specific security enhancements.  By comparing with the costs of proposed projects, they can build a credible and defensible business case for new security initiatives.
When performing this evaluation, it is important to understand security variables and metrics in terms of the organization’s larger goals and issues.  Understanding the specific industry, including which tangible and intangible costs are most relevant, is important to build and maintain credibility with business and financial decision makers.
Of course, risk avoidance is not the only justification for security investments.  New technologies and methods may also produce substantial efficiency gains.  Security vendors are focused on the need for more integrated and manageable solutions.  In some cases, it may therefore be cheaper and more effective to install new solutions than to manage current systems.  These effects should also be included in the financial assessment.
Though unavoidably complex during initial use, a formal approach to ROSI can be instrumental in justifying and implementing an appropriate security program, and in tying program development to business value.  For most organizations, the benefits will increase substantially with time, as analysts identify, track and refine the key metrics in their particular environment.

Technology Innovation and Appropriate Use
Connected computing, wireless mobility, and Internet-based commerce offer significant opportunity and competitive advantage for most organizations, yet each of these technologies has also introduced new security risks.  In general, almost any technology that simplifies and improves access to valuable information will engender new security concerns.  PC security issues increased dramatically with the advent of networking and the Internet.  Yet the decision of whether to take advantage of these capabilities is a non-issue for the majority of organizations.  As with many new technologies today, the competitive value is simply too great to ignore.
The real challenge for business and IT leaders, therefore, is to deploy new technologies judiciously and to extend existing security solutions as needed based on careful analysis of risks and benefits.  Important issues include the existence and reliability of industry standards for the new technology, and the effectiveness and cost-effectiveness of existing security solutions.
Wireless mobility offers an instructive example.  The rapid growth of wireless attests to its productivity benefits, which analysts have estimated at up to 11 hours a week for a professional with a wireless notebook (as opposed to a non-wireless notebook).  Early wireless security standards proved too weak for enterprise needs.  However, existing technologies, such as virtual private networking (VPN), can be extended to wireless applications, providing time-tested methods for encryption and user authentication.
Of course, such solutions must be carefully integrated with existing enterpise security frameworks.  For example, best practices for wireless security typically include scanning for rogue systems and segmenting the wireless network from the trusted network so only authenticated users can access core business information.  Of course, wireless security standards continue to improve.  Businesses that wait will probably have more cost-effective security options available.  However, they will loose the competitive advantages of early adoption, which may be substantial—and they will still have to invest in establishing a comprehensive and well-integrated security solution.  Better security standards will undoubetdly deliver incremental value, but may not be worth the delay.
In general, businesses should monitor emerging technologies, and carefully evaluate potential value, along with the cost and risk of implementation.  There is an increasing convergence between computing and communication devices, which can increase flexibility, mobility and ease of information access.  Current examples include wireless PDAs and other wireless handheld devices, instant messaging, “ad hoc” networking, and voice over IP.  Ad hoc networking enables the direct connection between two devices, with no server in between to monitor (and safeguard) communications.  Voice over IP enables voice communications to be transmitted across standard data networks.
New and emerging technologies offer new capabilities and solutions that may improve communication and productivity.  Some are simple enough to be implemented by employees using free downloadable software or widely available consumer products.  This is a relatively common scenario—and a good reason for business and security executives to carefully evaluate new technologies, establish policies, and control their deployment within the organization.
The COSO model provides a good framework for assessing the control environment, but must be adapted by each organization to fit the culture, risk appetite, and strategy for adopting new technology.  Only those organizations that are unlikely to read a report such as this would even consider preventing adoption and use of popular new technologies as a viable solution.
Evaluating Security for New Products and Technologies 
As part of Microsoft Corporation’s increased focus on security, they introduced the following criteria for assessing product security.  It can be a useful model, and provides important insights for security and IT decision-makers.  In general, all IT products should be:
    • Secure by Design: Security should no longer be an afterthought in product design.  Look for standards-based tools and services that specifically address security issues, and that can be integrated effectively into your own security framework.  Products that are designed for security are likely to support better solutions at lower costs.
    • Secure by Default: The industry has long focused on convenience and ease-of-use at the expense of security.  One example was the enormous number of built-in services that were turned on by default in older operating systems, such as Windows NT 4.0 and Windows 98.  Many of these services were unnecessary for most PC users, and some would only be needed in a server environment.  Such activated but unused services add no value, but can substantially increase vulnerability.  Today, services that may not be required should be turned off by default, and it should require active configuration by an informed user to turn them on.
    • Secure by Deployment: This requires the deployment of comprehensive best practices as discussed in this chapter and throughout this report.  Look for products and vendors that provide clear documentation of the security concerns and potential solutions that relate directly to their offerings.
Enterprise Call to Action
Security has been described as a race without a finish.  It involves managing many diverse factors, such as perceptions and misconceptions, awareness and apathy, protection versus ease of use and access, compliance and effective practices, return on security investments, and enterprise risk management.  It involves dealing with rapidly changing rules, threats, vulnerabilities, and strategies.  Effective protection today may be negated by tomorrow’s technology advances and/or the discovery of new vulnerabilities in software, systems, and networks.
In short, there is no simple way to protect your enterprise from digital threats.  However, strong and efficient security is possible today.  For most organizations, an effective solution requires a comprehensive security policy and roadmap, a formal approach to risk management and ROI, continuous monitoring and assessment of threats and events, strong and immediate recovery capabilities, and regular security assessments.
In establishing and maintaining effective security, priority should be given to industry-standard security technologies that provide a flexible foundation for integration and growth.  Improving the efficiency of patch management processes and keeping your PC and server infrastructure up-to-date are among the most cost-effective steps toward enhancing security.  Regular PC refreshes and automated platform management tools help to ensure a consistent computing environment with the power and functionality to support increasing security and application requirements.
Organizations should also consider the automated management tools offered by leading PC manufacturers and software vendors.  As discussed elsewhere in this report, advanced tools based on industry standards can deliver significant advantages in asset management, platform security management, configurations and updates, and fault management.  Though some of these factors are not directly related to security concerns, they increase your ability to respond quickly and effectively to remediate emerging threats.
As mentioned stated previously, the COSO framework is a widely accepted model for internal control, and is recommended for addressing controls at the PC level.  Keep in mind that a single security breech can cost millions of dollars, which could cover the entire budget for a large security program. Business Continuity and the PC
Introduction
The PC has become thoroughly integrated into the majority of job functions in most large organizations.  Word processing, spreadsheets, presentation software, communications, and other mainstream applications are now essential productivity tools.  Many job functions also rely on task-specific applications that run on general-purpose PCs.  In most cases the data accessed by the PC, whether stored on the PC itself or on back-end systems, is equally as important as the PC hardware aend software.  If PC tools and information are not available, an employee’s function may be impossible to perform efficiently—or at all.  It is therefore important that appropriate tools and processes be in place to quickly and efficiently restore data and functionality for failed PCs.
Traditional contingency planning covers many different aspects of business continuity, such asincluding: recovery teams, action plans, equipment inventory, network and data center operations, transportation, forms and supplies, plan maintenance, alternate facilities, and testing.  This chapter addresses only those areas that are specifically related to PC management in a networked environment.  (For more details on the overall contingency management process, see Systems Auditability and Control (SAC) Module 10 Contingency Planning, by The IIA Research Foundation.  SAC documents a comprehensive framework for overall contingency planning.)
An effective strategy for the recovery of one or more failed PCs begins with a detailed understanding of business objectives, processes and risks.  In most cases, an individual PC is not critical to the entire enterprise.  However, if that PC is the sole link to a business-critical database or is used to control a critical assembly line, its importance may be extremely high.  Moreover, any networked machine can be the entry-point for a virus or worm that will impact all systems on the network, and possibly bring down the network itself.
Business Continuity at the PC level therefore depends on two primary issues.  The first is the recovery of individual machines.  With proper tools and planning, this can be a quick and relatively painless process.  The second is the recovery of the network and all affected machines in the event of a virus infection or other large-scale security event.  This may require substantial coordination across the enterprise to ben sure that key systems, network segments, and business processes are quickly restored and properly protected against further infection.
The Role of the PC Architecture
Architectural decisions can have a significant impact on PC operations and recoverability.  Three primary models PC architectures exist in information networks today.  The most common is the traditional fat client architecture in which each PC has its own hard drive, and is configured to host complete applications, and can store both software and data.  The mobile client architecture is similar, but presents special issues for maintenance, support, and recovery.  A third option is the thin client model.  In this case, the end-user device is primarily a user interface that accesses processing power, applications, and data from networked servers that maytypically support multiple clients.
In terms of system management and recovery, the thin client is probably the most efficient, followed by the fat client, and then the mobile PC.  Of course, productivity issues are critical in choosing the best architecture for a particular business and function, which accounts for the increasing popularity of mobile systems despite a somewhat higher total cost of ownership / operation.  The discussion below is applicable primarily to the fat client architectures and mobile PCs, the most popular solutions at this time.
Restoring Individual PCs
Almost anyone who has worked with PCs over an extended period of time has experienced a system crash due to a hard drive failure or some other software or hardware related issue.  At best, there are work delays as the problem is resolved.  At worst, there can be lost data that represents hours, weeks, or even months of lost productivity.
In the survey conducted for this research portproject, 40 percent of respondents reported that their organization backed up PC data in a central location.  An even larger percentage, 47 percent, reporting having a full image backed up for PC recovery.  (aAn image includes the operating system and all major applications and settings.).  In general, this a full image backup should be considered a best practice for PC management, especially for job functions that require recovery timeframes of less than 24 hours.  With today’s best tools, a failed PC can be re-imaged and its data reloaded within a few hours, and this process can be performed over the network from a remote location.  Of course, for hardware-related problems, additional measures may be required.  New components might may have to be installed or a replacement PC obtaineddeployed.
Most businesses organizations will differentiate recovery processes to some extent based on the importance of the individual PC.  For example, on the trading floor of a financial institution every individual may have two or three fully operable PCs due to the significant loss of revenue that could result from a trader being unable to perform.  Individuals in less critical capacities might may be expected to wait several hours for system restoration using the automated tools processes described above.  There might be oOthers who could reasonably expect to wait a week or more for a new PC to be ordered, configured, and installed.
Risk versus TCO
In evaluating policies, tools, and processes, total cost of operations should be a key consideration.  If automated image and data restoration are implemented for some systems, it will be incrementally less costly to extend those capabilities to more usersother systems.  In addition, these quick, automated processes can greatly decrease the burden on help desk and support staff, and also reduce the level of expertise required to perform recovery operations.
For most businessesorganizations, it will not be cost-effective to have a fully configured back-up PC for every individual in case of hardware failure.  Yet a number of partially imaged PCs can be kept in reserve at key locations, ensuring quick replacement machines for a very large number of users.  In conjunction with the automated image and data recovery tools already mentioned, this can reduce recovery times significantly, even for hardware-damaged systems.  An inventory of key components may also be advisable.  This enables failed hardware to be replaced more quickly, and returned to the user or to a central repository for redeployment.
Backup frequency is another important issue in centralized PC recovery solutions.  For mobile PCs that only occasionally connect to the network, it may be appropriate to perform a backup each time a connection is made.  For desktops and laptops that are frequently or always connected, timing is strictly a function of policy.  Frequent backups minimize potential data loss, but also create additional overhead and increase network bandwidth requirements.  The decision to perform backups hourly, annually, or somewhere in between should be based on a quantitative analysis of value at risk and the total cost of the solution.
Notebook Challenges
Notebook (or laptop) PCs present additional challenges for rapid recovery when individuals are away from a central location.  Automated data and image restoration is still possible, but bandwidth constraints will most likely slow the process significantly.  Today’s best tools are able to adjust to bandwidth limitations, and can smoothly continue restoration in the event a connection is lost and re-established.  Spare parts and replacement systems for travelers can be a more difficult challenge.  Some organizations have established contracts with their suppliers to drop-ship a new system to any location within 24 hours in the event of PC failure.
24/7 PC Support
Most enterprises have a centralized help desk to support users with PC related problems.  These facilities often provide various levels of support, and typically help to resolve software issues over the phone.  Help desk employees personnel typically have detailed procedures for addressing various PC problems.  The integration of help desk applications with network-based asset management and PC restoration tools can substantially accelerate average resolution times and improve the percentage of issues that can be handled remotely.  ItCentrally administered PC support can also help to provide a central nexus for tracking and analyzing PC problems and support efficiency.
Testing
The processes involved in recovering individual PCs are tested daily in a large enterprise.  Data should be collected and reviewed regularly to identify common problems or recurring user issues, and metrics should be devised for tracking and understanding related costs and service levels.  Problems should be summarized by category, and root cause analysis should be used to identify problematic vendors, models, components, applications, etc.  If recovery of an individual’s data is not possible, causes should be reviewed and action taken to avoid future occurrences, since even a single event can be costly, depending on the data and its value.
Specific and regular recovery testing should be implemented for PCs involved in all critical business functions.  These tests should be integrated into corporate and departmental recovery tests to be ensure procedures will work together under foreseeable scenarios.  Root cause analysis should be performed to resolve any recovery issues that arise during these tests.
Recovering the Network and Connected Systems
As already discussedpreviously addressed in this paperreport, a computer worm can impact the traffic on the network and impair enterprise operations.  It is generally more cost-effectiveefficient to protect against infection than to recover from it, so appropriate safeguards are essential.  (sSee Chapters 4 and 6 for more information.).  However, when an infection occurs, quick and effective action can potentially save many thousands or millions of dollars, minimizing operational downtime and reducing cleanup and recovery costs.
Network recovery can be a highly complex process, and is beyond the scope of this paperreport.  However, from a high-level perspective, processes should be in place for monitoring the network and individual systems for infection.  Dedicated personnel should track key security sites for early warnings about vulnerabilities and real or possible attacks.  ForIn most casvulnerabilities, there will be an existing software patches are available, or software and/or security vendors will quickly release appropriate updates and virus removal utilities.  Depending on circumstances, it may be possibleis highly advisable to test these updates and patches before deployment.  However, in some cases imminent risks may outweigh potential interoperability issues, and testing may not be appropriate.
Recovery teams should be ready to respond instantly.  Roles and responsibilities should be understood.  Actions plans should take into account how the network is segmented, as well as the business value associated with particular segments and connected systems.  A well-designed recovery plan will efficiently recover the network more efficiently, and prioritize system restoration based on the value of supported business functions.
The PC as a Contingency Solution
The PC can also serve as part of the solution in contingency planning.  Notebooks in the field, in particular, can serve as offline storage devices, especially if they have data that is regularly synchronized with network servers or databases.  In a number of cases, notebooks have provedn valuable for businesses recovering from power outages and server crashes.  Of course, these capabilities depend on synchronization with other data sources in the enterprise.  Careful planning can provide the enterprise with off-site data backup and relatively easy recovery for critical information.
PCs should also be considered as a valuable processing resource, especially during a disaster in which other systems may not be available.  These capabilities can be especially important with respect to departmental disaster scenarios, and contingency planners and internal auditors should be aware of the possibilities.
Enterprise Call to Action
Contingency planning is essential for both individual PC restoration and network restoration.  The following is a summary of key actions that help to ensure a rapid response and quick recovery.
    • Establish standard recovery times for various functions, based on specific problem categories and criticality.
    • Monitor performance for PC support and recovery processes, and document process for network recovery.
    • Have each PC user and business function perform a risk assessment and establish recovery time objectives for their machine(s).
    • Determine if any major changes should be made to the enterprise PC architecture (fat client, mobile client, thin client, or combination).
    • Establish clear policies and methods for PC backup and recovery.
    • Provide thorough testing of PC backup and recovery processes.
    • Determine the optimal number and locations of spare machines, based on user numbers and the criticality of business functions.

Software Management and Currency
Introduction
Effective software management is one of the most crucial aspects of a good PC fleet management strategy.  A well-managed software environment is more stable, with fewer compatibility issues, better security, fewer helpdesk calls, and faster problem resolution.
The importance of software management was underlined by Aberdeen Group in their 2003 list of best practices for PC management.  In prioritizing best practices by ROI, two out of the top three recommendations—deploying standard software images and software distribution—focused on effective software management.  Both of these processes can be substantially improved using automated, network-based tools.  Both processes also rely on establishing a consistent PC environment based on well-defined organizational standards.
The Benefits of Standardization
In general, the more diverse the software environment, the more difficult and costly it is to manage and maintain.  Most organizations will benefit from simplifying their environment by establishing a handful of PC configurations targeting specific user groups.  Giga Information Group reports that “the benefit of standardizing on four to six application configurations represents a 15 percent to 25 percent reduction in IT costs during the system lifecycle (an average of $1,600 per system).”3
Standardization lays the foundation for efficient fleet management.  With a consistent environment, many manual processes can be replaced or augmented with automated tools that enable fewer people to manage a large number of PCs.  When deploying new PCs, systems can be powered up over the network.  The operating system and applications can then be uploaded automatically—with minimal human intervention.  Software updates and security patches can also be distributed over the network.  Standardization also improves helpdesk support.  Since the PC support staff encounters fewer variations, they become familiar with common problems and can resolve them more quickly.
Of course, efforts at standardization must be carefully balanced against end-user productivity needs.  An engineer, an accountant, and a manufacturing supervisor may have very different application requirements.  Moreover, some organizations—universities are one example—are simply not in a position to standardize all the PCs that access their network.  Every organization must seek the appropriate balance between uncontrolled diversity and a single, locked-down PC configuration.  The ability to accurately find this balance is crucial to implementing an effective PC management strategy.
A standardized environment can present a different type of risk. Reducing Software Churn with Stable Platforms
Since many components of a PC’s operating software interact directly with the hardware components, different hardware configurations will often be unable to run the same software image.  Standardizing the software environment therefore requires standardizing the hardware environment.
Large organizations typically standardize on a small number of desktop and mobile hardware configurations, then develop and qualify a standard software image for each configuration.  Once an image is qualified, any change to the standard hardware configuration can “break” the image, creating serious interoperability issues.  Since a typical PC includes dozens of components from many different vendors, component upgrades are common.  This has been a major problem for large organizations with thousands of PCs, forcing them to reengineer and requalify images any time their PC vendor issues a component change toin one of their standard configurations.
To address this issue, all leading PC vendors now offer “stable platforms” that specifically target the needs of large businesses to minimize changes to qualified configurations.  Large organizations are encouraged to work closely with their PC vendor to understand the advantages and limitations of stable platforms.  With close cooperation, they can time their platform and image qualifications to coincide with the release of new stable platforms by the vendor.  This will enable them to take advantage of a given configuration for a longer period, and reduce the number of qualifications they have to perform.
The Volatility of PC Operating Software
Today’s PC operating systems are highly complex, with millions of lines of code, and an extensive assortment of services and capabilities.  The PC operating system (OS) acts as the intermediary between the PC hardware and software.  It is a critical component—and it is constantly changing.  Change comes from two key sources: major version upgrades, and ongoing patches and service packs, which are made available by vendors to address security and interoperability issues that arise in the interval between version releases.  A key question for IT organizations is how often they should upgrade their PC operating systems, and how thorough they should be in deploying patches and updates.
Compatibility with evolving applications and hardware components is one reason to deploy current operating system versions.  Businesses relying on out-of-date OS versions may run into complications when they try to upgrade other applications or services in their infrastructure.  OS upgrades may also introduce useful features that can help to solve certain challenges.  For example, Microsoft Windows 2000/XP includes enhanced support for several security functions, such as file encryption, virtual private networks (VPNs) and smart card authentication.  Using these features can be cost-effective, since they are included at no extra charge and automatically available in all systems running that particular OS.  Of course, businesses may have existing solutions, or they may find that third party applications offer essential features that are not included in the OS implementation.  Or they may simply not be interested in the enhanced feature set of a new OS version.
In general, every IT organization must evaluate OS version upgrades based on expected benefits versus the cost of migrating to the new version—including planning, hardware upgrades or replacements, software migration and follow- on support costs.  However, the importance of upgrading becomes critical when operating systems reach an age when they are no longer supported by the vendor.  Out-of-date operating systems are particularly vulnerable to digital attack, since the vendor no longer provides patches for newly discovered security vulnerabilities.  As noted by META Group, “…the complexity and breadth of an OS makes running without vendor support a high risk.”4

The Challenge of Security Patching
Security analysts estimate over 90% of digital threats attack known software vulnerabilities in PC or server platforms.  In most cases, vendors are aware of these vulnerabilities and have already developed software patches to address them.  Yet IT organizafunctions can be overwhelmed with the pure volume of updates, along with the complexity of resolving dependencies and ensuring application and platform readiness as they engineer, qualify, test and distribute these patches.
Microsoft and other software vendors have significantly increased their focus on security issues.  Yet the combination of software complexity, multiple deployed versions, and a very active hacker community ensures that new security issues will continue to emerge.  The increased industry focus on security also has a downside.  Increased scrutiny leads to the discovery of more flaws, which are published by the security community.  Hackers also have access to these published lists.  As a result, vulnerabilities are being exploited with increasing speed, forcing software vendors and businesses to react faster.  For example, the SQL Slammer worm took eight months to appear after the vulnerability was announced.  Blaster appeared after only one month.
Digital threats are also becoming more sophisticated.  Today’s blended worms, for example, are designed to attack and propagate in multiple ways.  Some can spread globally in a matter of minutes, rather than days.  They also do more damage and are more difficult to clean up.
There is no simple solution to this challenge.  Among both analysts and users, there are those who advocate the extremes of not patching at all, and those who advocate comprehensive patching to stay current at all times.  Neither of these strategies will work for most businesses.  Failing to deploy critical patches can have serious consequences.  Yet the cost of testing and deploying every patch for every application is generally prohibitive.
The recommended solution lies in establishing effective risk management procedures for analyzing vulnerabilities and determining the best course of action.  It is important to have a formal system in place, with standard processes and authorizations.  Each business must understand the value of their IT assets, and the vulnerability of their network and systems to digital attacks, including viruses, worms, Trojans, hackers, denial of service, and internal misuse or abuse of resources.  With this information, they can monitor new threats, and more quickly evaluate their risk exposure based on their unique technical and business considerations.  The risk of not deploying a patch can be weighed against the costs and risk of deploying it.  Some patches should be distributed as soon as possible, others can be grouped for later distribution, and others may be installed only as part of a service pack or software version upgrade.
Several additional steps are generally advisable:
    1. Implement regular security scanning to identify vulnerable systems and audit the security environment.  Analysts at the Gartner Group recommend IT organizations functions scan all systems at least weekly, and move toward daily scans.8  Scanning is important to make sure patches are not only distributed, but successfully installed.  ItFrequent scanning can also help to ensure end-users have not compromised network security by downloading vulnerable software from the Internet, or turning off security applications, such as virus scanning or personal firewalls.  Ideally, this scanning function should be independent of patch distribution processes, to provide an effective audit of the PC security environment.  (One method is to use an off-the-shelf, agent-based management system for patch distribution, and use OS-based tools and services for independent configuration scanning.)
    2. Standardize on a handful of client configurations.  This is one of the most effective ways to limit patching complexity, and to enable adequate testing in a reasonable time frame.  However, it should be mentioned that some analysts and users recommend the opposite.  They prefer a diverse base of PCs that make it hard for a worm or virus to find a consistent flaw across the infrastructure, and may therefore impede propagation.  However, we believe such an environment is may be prohibitively costly to manage, and may .  We also believe it fosters a diverse range of potential vulnerabilities that ultimately will make it more likely for one or more PCs to be compromised, and thus putting the network at risk.
    3. Create an isolated test environment for quickly testing new patches.  This should include a set of systems that mirror your deployed PC configurations.  You should also have a standard set of tests to ensure testing consistency.  When appropriate, the scope of the tests can be varied based on the severity and urgency of the security threat.
    4. Deploy automated tools for network-based software distribution, and explore the advantages of automated patch management applications.  Effective tools can accelerate distribution, making it easier to stay current with the most critical patches.
Industry Shift Ahead?
Because of the wide deployment of its software, Microsoft Corporation is one of the main sources for regular security updates.  The company currently delivers software patches through its Windows Update Web site and through update software in Windows XP, Windows 2000, and Windows Me.  The software does not download and install patches by default, but asks a user to select from various options, including simply receiving alerts when an update is available.  However, many users continue to ignore the warnings when Microsoft issues a “critical” security update, and large numbers of systems fail to get patched.  (This was the case with the Blaster worm, which started spreading several weeks after the patch was issued, and went on to infect hundreds of thousands of computers.)
The question for Microsoft and its customers is whether patch distribution should be fully automated, so Microsoft can install patches over the Internet without user intervention.  Currently, most companies are unwilling to let Microsoft, or any software vendor, have that level of control over their environment. Chapter 7
Total Cost of Operation Ownership and Finance
Introduction
Is it more efficient to replace PCs on a regular schedule or to keep them as long as they seem to adequately serve their function?  While the answer may seem obvious, many factors must be considered to make an informed decision.  The apparent savings of postponing new PC acquisitions must be weighed against the costs and risks of maintaining older PCs.  There are three main categories to consider: IT support costs, enterprise security, and employee productivity.  All of these factors must be balanced against the costs of acquiring and deploying new PCs, including the costs incurred in disposing of the replaced systems.
Older PCs are more prone to problems and failure than newer machines.  They typically run applications more slowly, and may lack the power to run new or upgraded software.  Older systems may also increase enterprise vulnerability to worms, viruses, hackers, and other digital threats, especially if they are running older operating systems and applications.  The importance of these variables will of course depend on each company’s specific business and IT environment, including their application requirements, the vulnerability of their assets to digital attack and the quality of their PC management processes.  It will also depend on cash flow considerations and PC financing options.
These are unquestionably complex issues, and there is no one- size- fits- all solution.  Perhaps the most essential point is that companies must look holistically at their PC environment in terms of costs, risk and value, and manage their replacement schedules accordingly.  As evidenced by our research results, overall costs can be reduced by viewing the PC as more than just a commodity, and addressing the complete vendor and service relationship as a single equation.
The remainder of this chapter explores the underlying PC cost factors in sufficient detail to allow an organization to understand and plan the most appropriate replacement cycle based on their unique business and IT requirements.  It also discusses recommended tools and processes for improving PC management and reducing total costs.
Optimizing PC Replacement Cycles
At the July 2003 Technology Research Forum, keynote speaker Peter Kastner, of the Aberdeen Group, presented research results indicating newer PCs have lower ongoing management costs and can appreciably reduce TCO.  He indicated that only 20 to 30 percent of a PC’s TCO can be attributed to acquisition and deployment costs. 9  The remaining 70 to 80 percent is consumed by operational costs, such as hardware and software maintenance, administration, and help-desk support. 10  It is therefore necessary to carefully consider the components of these costs when planning the replacement cycle.
Some of the most obvious factors in the rising costs of older PCs include extended warranty costs, higher failure rates, and the costs associated with maintaining a greater variety of operating systems across the PC fleet.  A diverse fleet of PCs and operating systems adds complexity to the costs of maintenance, support, asset management, monitoring, and software updates.  Support teams must have higher levels of expertise, and each software upgrade or patch must be tested and deployed differently for each different hardware/software configuration.
Another important cost factor is the decreasing vendor support for older products.  Have you noticed that even automobile dealers do not stock parts for vehicles more than five years old?  Maintenance can be even more problematic for older PCs.  Replacement parts may not be available or the total cost of a repair may equal a significant portion of the replacement cost.  In general, running unsupported hardware and software is not recommended, either from a security or a TCO perspective.
A regular PC refresh cycle offers the additional advantage of improving the consistency of IT spending and operations.  Acquisition, deployment, and transition costs are consistent and predictable.  Budgets can be planned more effectively, resources can be dedicated to PC transitions, and tools and processes can be optimized on an ongoing basis. 
When evaluating TCO to optimize replacement cycles, it is important to pay attention to all factors that impact total costs.  For example, a single outage of a network caused by a PC being out of compliance for software support or virus updates can have a significant impact on operational costs, and may impact productivity or revenue.  These costs can be difficult to track, and are therefore ignored by many businesses.  Yet their inclusion can be decisive in accurately understanding TCO, and in making a strong case for specific replacement cycle recommendations.  (For more information on evaluating TCO, see TCO and the Business Design of PC Practices, later in this chapter.)
Security Issues for Older PCs
The most important factor in PC security is software currency, especially for the PC operating system (see the previous chapter 6).  As long as software versions and security patches are current, older PCs may not present a significantly increased security risk.  However, older systems may not provide adequate performance running newer software, or may require significant hardware upgrades to do so.  There is also a risk that employees will turn off security features, such as virus scanning or encryption, to accelerate performance on an older PC.
These issues should be considered when planning PC upgrades.  Businesses should also explore the relative costs of software-only upgrades versus complete system replacement.  If existing systems provide acceptable performance running the new software, a software-only upgrade may be appropriate.  If hardware component upgrades are required (such as more memory or larger hard drives), it may be more cost-effective to simply replace the whole system.  To the extent possible, IT organizafunctions should work to synchronize OS upgrades and hardware replacement, since it is far more economical to do both together than each separately.
Asset Management
In our survey, many organizations reported a need for improvement in their PC “Asset Management” and “Tracking” processes.  Many individuals at the Technology Forum mentioned similar concerns, saying they did not maintain a reliable PC inventory.  Because of this, they were unable to perform an adequate TCO analysis or develop a TCO model for driving fleet management decisions.
For IT organizafunctions that have not yet deployed network-based management tools, an outsourced assessment service can provide a detailed snapshot of a networked fleet of PCs, and provide tools for analyzing the data.  This can make it easier to understand TCO issues when planning fleet management strategy or when implementing a specific project, such as a large-scale migration.  It can also help organizations assess the advantages of deploying an in-house asset management solution.
Today’s leading asset management tools track detailed information regarding configurations, software usage, and performance metrics.  They can integrate this information with user profiles, physical locations, warranties, and financial information, such as contracts and depreciation.  Many solutions also support asset management for non-computing equipment (manufacturing, office equipment, etc.).
Individuals involved in risk management should encourage the enterprise to utilize these types of tools to understand TCO and mitigate risk.  Capabilities vary widely, so each organization must evaluate tools and vendors closely with respect to their current requirements and future plans.
Consider the following capabilities in evaluating new tools:
    • Ability to perform a real-time inventory over the network.  This can help an organization monitor PC security configurations to quickly detect unpatched or misconfigured machines that could compromise enterprise security.
    • Ability to track diverse assets, including handheld and occasionally-connected devices.
    • Integration with enterprise directories, for more efficient policy-based management based on business roles and user profiles.
    • Ability to drill down quickly to detailed information.
    • Integration with other processes and applications, such as software distribution and help desk support teams.
    • Flexible tools for analyzing and presenting data to improve planning and operational efficiency across multiple processes (procurement, deployment, software distribution, etc.).
PC Disposal
Disposal is a significant episode in the PC lifecycle, and it is important to understand the associated costs and risks.  The most important issues are security and liability.  Sensitive information must be wiped from a PC’s hard drive before it is disposed of, stored, or transferred to another user.  Data removal should be handled by a knowledgeable technician using controlled procedures to ensure data cannot be recovered.  PCs also contain toxic substances, and should not be disposed of in public landfills.
For these reasons, it can be useful to outsource PC disposal to a qualified provider.  Many PC vendors offer disposal programs.  There are also specialty vendors that focus specifically on PC retirement and disposal.  Some will pay for systems that can be refurbished and resold, which can improve TCO for PCs that are retired in a timely fashion.  Most vendors also work with charitable organizations to ensure that the best possible use is made of PCs and notebooks that have outlived their usefulness in an enterprise environment.  By either optimizing internal processes or working with a qualified vendor, organizations can improve TCO and mitigate an important source of enterprise risk.
Automated and Remote PC Management
According to Aberdeen Group, best practices and automated tools for PC management can reduce the annual cost per user by about 50 percent (a typical savings of about $4,000 per user).  Yet many organizations resist deploying these tools due to the upfront costs, as well as the learning curve and process change required to achieve value.
The key is to develop a TCO model, and then use that model to understand and justify further tool and process optimizations.  It can take several years to move from an unmanaged to a well-managed PC environment, but a carefully planned migration can deliver incremental value along the way.  In the process, the PC infrastructure will not only become less costly to maintain, but will become more secure and agile as well.
In deploying automated PC management tools, organizations can choose between integrated suites and stand-alone tools that focus on specific management functions.  Stand alone tools may offer better functionality, but integrated suites can help to reduce training costs and leverage benefits across the full PC lifecycle.
Because of the advantages of integration, analysts at Giga Information Group now recommend that firms “move to a single management tool for core functions.”  They say “Consolidating client management tools and integrating support processes will reduce training costs, infrastructure requirements, and overall support costs by 30 percent or more, and ultimately increase customer (or end user) satisfaction.”11
Large enterprises may still benefit from using some best-of-breed tools for specific functions, such as deployment, software migration, patch management, etc.—and may be able to justify the cost of integrating diverse products in-house.  However, the value of specific features and functionality should be carefully weighed against the benefits of integrated, off-the-shelf solutions.
Industry Standards
The foundation for automated and remote PC management was laid several years ago, when industry leaders and standards organizations began to define and integrate standard technologies for PC platform manageability.  Standards adherence is now widespread in platforms intended for enterprise customers, but should still not be taken for granted.  A deployed base of systems that adhere to key standards helps to keep your options open as management tools evolve.  Consider each of the following:
    • DMI (Desktop Management Interface) — Provides a standard framework for managing and tracking components in PCs and servers.
    • WfM (Wired for Management) — Includes essential features for network-based management, including built in instrumentation, remote wake-up, use of the network as a boot device and power management capabilities.
    • CIM (Common Information Model) — A data model for describing overall management information in a network/enterprise environment.
    • WEBEM (Web-based Enterprise Management) — A set of management and Internet standard technologies developed to unify the management of enterprise computing environments.
    • Other PC Standards — Standards are continuously evolving for new and emerging technologies.  At this time, support for IEEE 802.X wireless standards (Wi-Fi) is particularly important for all new notebook purchases.  Enterprise customers should look for wireless products that support fully-ratified 802.X standards and have been validated by the Wi-Fi Alliance.  Be wary of PCs and related components that are based on pre-ratified standards.  They may be suitable for consumers and small businesses, but are risky for wide-scale enterprise deployment.
TCO and the Business Design of PC Practices
So far, this chapter has focused on general criteria for optimizing PC lifecycles and TCO.  These generalizations are important for understanding the key variables in PC management, but the most successful businesses organizations will ultimately implement methodologies for analyzing and tracking TCO in their specific business and IT environment.
To be effective, a TCO model must measure both the direct and indirect costs of owning desktop and notebook computers throughout the PC lifecycle.  The cost to purchase and deploy new systems is the tip of the iceberg.  Total costs should include the cost of maintaining and managing the PC infrastructure including planning, training, asset management, software updates, backup and recovery solutions, PC retirement and disposal, and helpdesk and onsite technical support.  It should also include associated costs for network and Internet support, printers, security resources (such as authentication and VPN systems and applications, anti-virus protection, etc.) and centralized storage resources.
Downtime costs should also be tracked and quantified, as much as possible practical, based on IT support costs, lost user productivity, and business process interruptions.  There is no single, best way to measure TCO for every organization.  Where necessary, industry averages can be used to gain insight into costs that are not readily measurable.  Over time, the TCO model can be refined and tied more securely to metrics that can be tracked on an ongoing basis.
Figure X (Slide 7 in Lutchen’s PPT) outlines the PC lifecycle in a typical organization, including high-level policies, planning and relationship management.  Note that the PC lifecycle must be integrated with human resource management to ensure new hires are equipped with the appropriate PC in a timely fashion based on their hire date and business role.  There is also a continuous feedback loop as PCs reach the end of their lifecycle and are replaced.
A map of this kind can be valuable for understanding the full complexity of the PC lifecycle.  Appropriate cost and performance metrics should be established for all key functions.  Once these relationships are understood and modeled, it becomes possible to track TCO, risk, and value based on specific events, such as a major software upgrade, a surge in hiring, a virus attack, or a large-scale migration to wireless notebooks.  The impact of policy and environmental changes can also be modeled, such as a change in standard replacement cycles, an upgrade in help desk services, or the deployment of an automated asset management application.  With these tools, administrators can gain vital insights into their specific organization, and can move toward an optimized PC environment based on a clear understanding of TCO benefits and project ROI.
The Financial Benefits of PC Best Practices
For a typical organization, the cost of moving toward PC management best practices is not trivial.  Alinean, a company that specializes in understanding the financial aspects of IT, estimates it would cost the average organization $500-$1,500 per PC to implement best practices in a Microsoft Windows environment (Figure X: Slide 9 from Pisello’s PPT).  If part of that implementation requires an operating system migration, there may be additional costs of about $1,000 per PC.  Alinean estimates the potential annual savings in a well-managed environment at about $1,845 per PC.
Based on this assessment, an average business can expect full payback in about 12-18 months.  Once payback is achieved, the benefits continue to accrue.  There will certainly be ongoing costs to upgrade management tools and processes, but these will be smaller, and the operational and financial transparency of a well-managed PC environment makes it much easier to plan and justify.
Beyond these direct financial considerations, organizations must also consider the impact of best practices on security, compliance, productivity, and agility.  In today’s legal and regulatory environment,  PC fleet management is increasingly essential to limit business risk.  It also helps to reduce PC downtime, improve employee performance through regular PC technology refreshes, and enable faster implementation of new applications and business processes.

Enterprise Call to Action
The value of PC fleet management best practices, including optimal PC refresh cycles, has been well documented.  However, moving toward a standardized and well-managed environment is not easy.  The better you understand and document the costs, risks, and value associated with your current environment, the more effectively you can evaluate the real benefits of infrastructure and operational upgrades—and justify the costs. Chapter 8
Conclusion
This report is a call to action for auditors and management to address the control, security, and efficiency of their networked PC infrastructure.  The importance of this infrastructure continues to increase, as core business processes are increasingly leveraged across more users via the corporate network, the Internet, and connected PCs.  Administrators are also faceing a growing array of new expectations for performance, mobility, flexibility, and reliability—and a rise in the frequency and severity of digital attacks from the Internet.  To top it off, these growing pains coincide with a tough economic climate in which the costs for system upgrades and operational enhancements are under close scrutiny.
These pressures make it all the more essential to closely evaluate the economics of the full PC lifecycle, including acquisition, deployment, support, and replacement; to understand the value at risk with respect to the PC infrastructure; and to integrate this evaluation with broader enterprise risk assessments.  The best protection and the greatest value can be derived only through comprehensive best practices, as outlined in this report, and summarized below.

Key Guidance, References, and Articles
Many articles and references were beneficial in this research.  They spanned a broad range of topics including new and emerging hardware and software technologies, effective management practices, information security, business continuity and recovery, intrusion protection and event handling, risk management, financial practices, and much more.  If fact, there is much more information available relevant to PC management practices than could reasonably be included in one report.  So this report represents a distillation of meaningful information into a manageable quantity.  Perhaps some day someone will write an encyclopedia of PC management practices.  Until then, here are some concepts and references you may find useful.
Privacy
There have been numerous privacy debacles in recent years, and many of them relate specifically to PC management practices.  Others are more relevant to web site management and PCs accessing web sites.  The following list summarizes the lessons learned from common themes in those events and presents some ideas of actions to take, particularly for an organization that is just starting to address privacy issues or develop policy.  It also includes recent examples that have been cited in the popular and technical press.
    • Say What You Do and Do What You Say — Ensure that all privacy policy statements posted on a Web site are supported by actual practices.  Conduct periodic reviews of policies and practices to ensure consistency across business units, and that practices comply with those policies. For larger organizations, this may mean developing an internal privacy compliance process.  In the absence of privacy laws and regulations, the U.S. Federal Trade Commission has brought actions under deceptive trade practices laws.  This is also applicable to liquidations of assets in bankruptcy or when organizations or their assets are bought and sold.  In summer 2001, concerns were raised about the bankruptcy of Egghead.com and their consumer lists.
    • Do the Right Thing — It may sound trite or altruistic, but it really is smart business to do what is right or ethical in regard to privacy, as opposed to what is legally permissible.  Numerous privacy issues have been tried in the court of public opinion where the penalty is loss of trust and possible activist reaction.  Although perhaps not illegal, public reaction to knowledge that their activities were tracked when clicking on ads can be extremely negative.
    • Patch Security Holes in Operating Systems — A number of issues arise because vendors of well-known computing platforms release their operating systems with inherent security weaknesses.  Additionally, organizations fail to apply the patches to the security holes as quickly as hackers exploit them.  Proactively monitor your operating system supplier by signing up with their automatic notification processes for any known security weaknesses and associated fixes. When the Code Red worm was active in August 2001, the operating system patches required had been widely available for months, and the industry press was full of warnings, yet hundreds of thousands of machines were compromised because they were not updated.
    • Prevent Exposed E-mail Lists — When sending e-mail to a user list, privacy has been lost by inadvertently exposing the full mailing list to all addressees.  Use standard agents to generate mailing lists and test to make sure that the software blind copies all recipients instead of printing out their full e-mail addresses.
    • Examine URL Contents — Some Web sites redirect users to affiliated sites and include personal identifying information in the URL.  Examine all links external to the Web site and use a packet sniffer to ensure the URL contents do not contain PII, especially if this is not disclosed in the privacy policy.
    • Avoid PC/User ID Tags that Can Track Machines/Users — A number of organizations have raised the hackles of consumer privacy advocates by tagging software registration wizards or online transactions with a unique ID, including a GUID Processor Serial Number, or other identifying number that can be used to profile a user.  Use a packet sniffer to ensure an ID tag is not being sent to a Web site.
    • Avoid Web Bugs or at Least Disclose Their Use — While cookies can be managed by the browser or cookie management products, Web bugs, 1x1 pixel objects loaded from a third-party Web site, profile a user in such a way that they are unaware and cannot opt out.  Examine the source of Web pages for 1x1 pixel objects loaded from a third-party Web site.  (Not familiar with Web Bugs?  Search www.ITAudit.org for explanatory information.)
    • Avoid Software that "Phones Home" — Software vendors sometimes engineer products to automatically send information back to the manufacturer.  Test the registration procedures of all purchased software diligently, including what is captured in cookies, and in URLs.  Examine software offline to determine if agents are active that can sense an Internet connection or attempt to place the computer in online mode.  Inventory all software on the Web site and ensure any data transferred off the Web site is disclosed.
    • Provide Choice — Many privacy issues have resulted from failure to provide meaningful choice in putting the individual in control of their information.  The general rule is not to use PII for any secondary purposes unless the individual has been provided a choice as to how that information will be managed on their behalf.
    • Cleanse Demonstration Software — Development teams often take a cut of a live customer database to use as test data or demo data when demonstrating to potential customers that new applications reflect reality.  It is standard practice in most organizations, whether it is an official procedure or not.  At a minimum, however, data and software quality assurance testing must include a checkpoint to remove live data, even if only being used internally.
    • Test Third-Party Software for Glitches — Follow the manual and test thoroughly.  Don't assume installations from shrink-wrap are preset with the right options.  As an example, shopping cart software that was installed incorrectly revealed customer names, addresses, and account numbers, provided you knew certain keyword searches.
    • Protect PII Even More Diligently When Change is Occurring — When making major changes on your Web site it is important to keep traditional security and firewalls, as well as content management processes, active until the final transition.  A Web site made news by exposing employee e-mail accounts and encrypted passwords during domain name migration.
    • Confirm Employees Know the Privacy Policy — Training and awareness of good privacy practices and policy is critical to ensuring compliance.  Programs should include new hires, FAQs and regular updates to prevent social engineering, and inadvertent disclosure of personal data.  A customer service technician revealed customer account information to an outside party despite policy restrictions against disclosing such data.
    • Appoint a Chief Privacy Officer — Even if not full-time, assign responsibility for privacy policy compliance to an executive of the organization highly placed enough to wield influence.  Ensure the function is adequately funded and promoted as something supported by the highest levels of the organization.

As we developed material for this project and discussed the models being used for compliance with regulatory issues, we found that almost allmany Iinternal Aaudit groups were using the COSO framework for compliance with Sarbanes-Oxley.
In 1992, the Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission (COSO) developed a model for evaluating internal controls.  This model has been adopted as the generally accepted framework for internal control and is widely recognized as the definitive standard against which organizations measure the effectiveness of their systems of internal control.  The COSO model defines internal control as "a process, effected by an entity's board of directors, management and other personnel, designed to provide reasonable assurance of the achievement of objectives in the following categories:
    • Effectiveness and efficiency of operations 
    • Reliability of financial reporting 
    • Compliance with applicable laws and regulations
In an "effective" internal control system, the five components work to support the achievement of an entity's mission, strategies and related business objectives.   A short description of each is provided below:
    • Control Environment 
        ◦ Integrity and Ethical Values 
        ◦ Commitment to Competence 
        ◦ Board of Directors and Audit Committee 
        ◦ Management's Philosophy and Operating Style 
        ◦ Organizational Structure 
        ◦ Assignment of Authority and Responsibility 
        ◦ Human Resource Policies and Procedures
    • Risk Assessment 
        ◦ Company-wide Objectives 
        ◦ Process-level Objectives 
        ◦ Risk Identification and Analysis 
        ◦ Managing Change
    • Control Activities 
        ◦ Policies and Procedures 
        ◦ Security (Application and Network) 
        ◦ Application Change Management 
        ◦ Business Continuity / Backups 
        ◦ Outsourcing
    • Information and Communication 
        ◦ Quality of Information 
        ◦ Effectiveness of Communication
    • Monitoring 
        ◦ On-going Monitoring 
        ◦ Separate Evaluations 
        ◦ Reporting Deficiencies

PC Security Tips – Provided by the National Cyber Security Alliance:
    1. Use protection software "anti-virus software" and keep it up to date.
Make sure you have anti-virus software on your computer!  Anti-virus software is designed to protect you and your computer against known viruses so you don't have to worry.  But with new viruses emerging daily, anti-virus programs need regular updates, like annual flu shots, to recognize these new viruses.  Be sure to update your anti-virus software regularly!  The more often you keep it updated, say once a week, the better.  Check with the web site of your anti-virus software company to see some sample descriptions of viruses and to get regular updates for your software.  Stop viruses in their tracks!
    2. Don't open email from unknown sources.
A simple rule of thumb is that if you don't know the person who is sending you an email, be very careful about opening the email and any file attached to it.  Should you receive a suspicious email, the best thing to do is to delete the entire message, including any attachment.  Even if you do know the person sending you the email, you should exercise caution if the message is strange and unexpected, particularly if it contains unusual hyperlinks.  Your friend may have accidentally sent you a virus.  Such was the case with the "I Love You" virus that spread to millions of people in 2001.  When in doubt, delete!
    3. Use hard-to-guess passwords.
Passwords will only keep outsiders out if they are difficult to guess!  Don't share your password, and don't use the same password in more than one place.  If someone should happen to guess one of your passwords, you don't want them to be able to use it in other places.  The golden rules of passwords are: (1) A password should have a minimum of 8 characters, be as meaningless as possible, and use uppercase letters, lowercase letters and numbers, e.g., xk28LP97.  (2) Change passwords regularly, at least every 90 days.  (3) Do not give out your password to anyone!
    4. Protect your computer from Internet intruders -- use "firewalls".
Equip your computer with a firewall!  Firewalls create a protective wall between your computer and the outside world.  They come in two forms, software firewalls that run on your personal computer and hardware firewalls that protect a number of computers at the same time.  They work by filtering out unauthorized or potentially dangerous types of data from the Internet, while still allowing other (good) data to reach your computer.  Firewalls also ensure that unauthorized persons can't gain access to your computer while you're connected to the Internet.  You can find firewall hardware and software at most computer stores nationwide.  Don't let intruders in!
    5. Don't share access to your computers with strangers.  Learn about file sharing risks.
Your computer operating system may allow other computers on a network, including the Internet, to access the hard-drive of your computer in order to "share files".  This ability to share files can be used to infect your computer with a virus or look at the files on your computer if you don't pay close attention.  So, unless you really need this ability, make sure you turn off file-sharing.  Check your operating system and your other program help files to learn how to disable file sharing.  Don't share access to your computer with strangers!
    6. Disconnect from the Internet when not in use.
Remember that the Digital Highway is a two-way road.  You send and receive information on it.  Disconnecting your computer from the Internet when you're not online lessens the chance that someone will be able to access your computer.  And if you haven't kept your anti-virus software up-to-date, or don't have a firewall in place, someone could infect your computer or use it to harm someone else on the Internet.  Be safe and disconnect!
    7. Back up your computer data.
Experienced computer users know that there are two types of people: those who have already lost data and those who are going to experience the pain of losing data in the future.  Back up small amounts of data on floppy disks and larger amounts on CDs.  If you have access to a network, save copies of your data on another computer in the network.  Most people make weekly backups of all their important data.  And make sure you have your original software start-up disks handy and available in the event your computer system files get damaged.  Be prepared!
    8. Regularly download security protection update "patches".
Most major software companies today have to release updates and patches to their software every so often.  Sometimes bugs are discovered in a program that may allow a malicious person to attack your computer.  When these bugs are discovered, the software companies, or vendors, create patches that they post on their web sites.  You need to be sure you download and install the patches!  Check your software vendors' web sites on a regular basis for new security patches or use the new automated patching features that some companies offer.  If you don't have the time to do the work yourself, download and install a utility program to do it for you.  There are available software programs that can perform this task for you.  Stay informed!
    9. Check your security on a regular basis.  When you change your clocks for daylight-savings time, reevaluate your computer security.
The programs and operating system on your computer have many valuable features that make your life easier, but can also leave you vulnerable to hackers and viruses.  You should evaluate your computer security at least twice a year—do it when you change the clocks for daylight-savings!  Look at the settings on applications that you have on your computer.  Your browser software, for example, typically has a security setting in its preferences area.  Check what settings you have and make sure you have the security level appropriate for you.  Set a high bar for yourself!
    10. Make sure your family members and/or your employees know what to do if your computer becomes infected.
It's important that everyone who uses a computer be aware of proper security practices.  People should know how to update virus protection software, how to download security patches from software vendors and how to create a proper password.  Make sure they know these tips too!

The Systems Assurance and Control (SAC) Model
Audit Assurance
Audit assurance addresses information reliability.  Auditors must look far beyond the presentation of information into the infrastructures supporting the creation, accumulation, processing, management, communication, and protection of information.  The complexity of these infrastructures, their inner workings, and their interdependencies is enormous.  Threats against the reliability and security of information, including the ethics of those controlling the information, exist across a broad array and at varying levels of detail.
The array of risks potentially impacting information security defies identification.  Logically then, one might ask: “What does an auditor’s assurance of information reliability mean?”  The answer must take into account the risks assessed, the bases for reliance on controls, the likelihood that significant problems exist that were not discovered, and/or the possibility of failure in one or more audit processes.  Increasing recognition of both the importance and vulnerability of information and its supporting infrastructures is driving the auditing profession to seek new approaches and new models as the basis for assessing risks and providing assurance.
In the continuously changing business environment a standard may be obsolete before the community to which it applies can conclude whether it is too generic or too specific.  But leading organizations that depend on their data and infrastructures understand the unacceptable costs of being unable to access information, provide services, or keep sensitive data private.  Such organizations are keenly aware of the need for a structured control environment, and take the necessary steps to maintain such an environment.
The Institute of Internal Auditors and The IIA Research Foundation have, for many years, served the auditing profession by putting into practice the motto “Progress Through Sharing.”  The essential control and audit practices in well-run organizations are often studied, documented, and presented to the auditing profession at large in the form of standards, professional guidance, and practice aids.  The Systems Assurance and Control (SAC) reports are one of the best and most enduring examples of progress through sharing in action.

The SAC Reports
For the auditing profession, knowledge of technology is a key differentiator between effectiveness and obsolescence.  To that end, the Systems Auditability and Control (SAC) reports, originally published in 1977 by The Institute of Internal Auditors (IIA), with updates in 1991 and 1994 by The IIA Research Foundation, have consistently been recognized as landmark publications providing timeless guidance and reference information in all areas where auditors must deal with technology and the related business issues.
Since the technology evolution has been dramatic in the past decade, SAC content is now being continuously updated to address e-business technologies and the myriad other issues that have emerged with widespread use of the Internet.  Systems Assurance and Control (SAC) now brings executive management, corporate governance entities, and auditors new information to understand, monitor, assess, and mitigate technology risks.  SAC will examine and assess risks that accompany each organizational component, including customers, competitors, regulators, community, and owners.
Further, the SAC reports provide a framework to guide an evaluation of business controls.  The effective practices and control approaches of industry leaders, as viewed by their auditors, are the basis for the new SAC control and assessment model.  To emphasize technology’s business impacts and electronic delivery of the new material, electronic is added to the title.  The title is further enhanced by changing “Auditability” to “Assurance,” to recognize the important perspectives of governance and the alliances — both within an organization and between business partners — needed to assure effective security, auditability, and control of information.
The SAC Model
Internal auditors must understand the business risks resulting from changes in technology.  They then must articulate responsive risk management strategies to management, and provide assurance on the availability, capability, functionality, protectability, accountability, and auditability of the systems, networks, and infrastructures involved.  (Many auditors also interpret the traditional definition of information security – including confidentiality, integrity, and availability – as equivalent to the auditor’s focus on information reliability.)
The SAC Model was developed to facilitate discussions of these issues.  As the COSO (Committee of Sponsoring Organizations for the Commission on Fraudulent Financial Reporting (Treadway Commission), see www.coso.org) “Internal Control — Integrated Framework” provided a common control language for management and auditors, the SAC Model facilitates the discussion of objectives, risks, and mitigation responses within the context of e-business.  Its purpose is to focus on how the risks resulting from rapid technology and e-business model changes can be managed, both in discussion and implementation.
The Sarbanes-Oxley legislation, that requires management reporting on the system of internal controls, and external auditor attestation regarding management’s assertions, also serves to heighten the emphasis on a reliable model for categorizing and assessing internal controls.
There are many different risk and control models, and each must be tailored to any given organization.  Topics such as the rapidly evolving regulatory environment, how much capacity is enough, and making realistic technology choices are only a few of the significant issues that can be reviewed through the lens of the SAC Model.

SAC Model Description
The COSO framework of objectives, risks, and controls (mitigating responses) is an integral part of the SAC model, since it has been successfully employed in numerous organizations.  From a global perspective, similar reports that have been issued worldwide, such as Cadbury in the UK, have the same general concepts, so this should be easily adapted in any environment.
An organization typically pursues its mission (purpose) through establishing strategies and objectives consistent with its values.  The arrow at the left side of the model indicates this process.  The organization aims to achieve desired results while enhancing or preserving its reputational standing and learning how to improve its future performance.  These outcomes are shown as the arrow at the right of the framework.
A sound control environment helps an organization stay on its intended path as it moves from mission to results.  The broad control context — effectiveness and efficiency of operations, financial and other management reporting, compliance with laws and regulations, and safeguarding of assets (COSO objectives) — is captured in the top center of the framework.  These are abbreviated for presentation as Operating, Reporting, Compliance, and Safeguarding.
Availability, Capability, Functionality, Protectability, and Accountability, in the next row, capture control attributes that are particularly pertinent for e-business activities.  They can equally be called business assurance objectives, since they are broader than the way in which some people view control.  These assurance objectives provide the “framework” through which SAC topics or modules are discussed.  For instance, Privacy concerns are addressed under Protectability and Accountability.
Availability
Information, processes, and services must be available when needed.  Specifically, the organization must be able to receive, accept, process, and support transactions in a manner acceptable to its customers.  Access via the Internet can mean availability 24/7.  To ensure availability, the auditor evaluates controls that deal with potential causes of business interruption.  These might include:
    • Physical and logical security of system resources.
    • Mechanical failure of file storage devices, servers, etc..
    • Malfunction of software or unexpected incompatibilities.
    • Inadequate capacity planning.
In the event of a problem, controls must provide for swift recovery.
Capability
Capability means end-to-end reliable and timely completion and fulfillment of all transactions.  This means the system has adequate capacity, communications, and other aspects to consistently meet needs, even at peak demand.  For systems to provide such services, monitoring of usage, service-level agreements with Internet service providers, application service providers, and others are important controls.  It is critical that system and process bottlenecks be identified and eliminated or carefully managed — the goal is to achieve and maintain an efficient and effective balance across the organization.
Efficiency of systems is an aspect of capability that leads to effective use of resources.  A key is controlling system development and acquisition methodologies to prevent cost overruns and systems that do not perform as required.  To help ensure efficiency of IT, the auditor evaluates controls that deal with causes and risks of excessive costs, characterized as waste and inefficiency.  Some of the problems might include:
    • Weaknesses in controls that result in excessive correction of errors; prevention is usually more efficient.
    • Controls that consume more resources than the benefits they deliver.
The objective is an optimal balance of control, which means acceptance of some risk.  Systems that are inefficient may foster user creation of shadow systems that work around the official system.  Such duplicate costs are clearly inefficient.  The unreliable system must be fixed before the shadow system is halted.  The objective of system development controls is to avoid such issues.  Methodologies should result in efficient and appropriate design and development of an application, and ensure that controls, auditability, and security are built into the system.
An information system that is not maintained effectively becomes unreliable.  Controls over maintenance, often called change controls, provide continuity while hardware or software changes are made, and ensure that all changes are documented, approved, and confirmed.  Maintenance controls include things like adequate user involvement in requesting, testing, and approving program changes; creating appropriate audit trails, including program change history logs; IT and user personnel approval; and sufficient documentation of program changes.  Once complete, controlled production transfer procedures reduce the risk of programmers having the ability to introduce unapproved test versions of programs into production.  (There are many other elements of change controls.  These are indicated only as examples.)
Functionality
Functionality means the system provides the facilities, responsiveness, and ease-of-use to meet user needs.  Good functionality goes well beyond the minimum transaction processing.  It should also provide for recording control information and other issues of concern to management.  Preventing problems in functionality includes considering the perspective of untrained, possibly unknown, online users.  Users can become impatient and may quit without completing a transaction or may resubmit input, causing duplicates.  To help ensure functionality, the auditor evaluates controls that monitor and provide feedback.  Some of these might include the display of progress indicators following input, positive confirmation of transactions, or monitoring user abandonment of transactions, or “hang-ups.”
Effective information is relevant to the business process, delivered by a functional system.  Relevance of information is based on system design, which requires user and management participation to reach functionality.  Problems often stem from inadequate specifications due to lack of user involvement in development, which usually means the resulting application will be ineffective.
To help ensure effectiveness, the auditor evaluates controls over timely, correct, consistent, and usable information.  The format in which information is delivered can have a substantial effect on effective communication.  The system should permit flexible display and reports that can be tailored to different audiences.
Protectability
Protectability includes protection of hardware, software, and data from unauthorized access, use, or harm.  Robust security is difficult to maintain due to the vast access possible via the Internet, the structure of which has inherent weaknesses.  Controls are needed to safeguard IT assets against loss, and identify when such loss has occurred.  Many current controls focus on reducing risks of catastrophic damage, internal fraud, or embezzlement.  To ensure protectability, the auditor evaluates general controls over IT that are often grouped as follows:
    • Data Security and Confidentiality — Access to data, an important asset, should be limited to those authorized to process or maintain specific data or records.  Protecting organizational data is the key responsibility of the information security function and its administrator(s).  The security functions may include restricting access to data through various logical access paths, based on user requirements; restricting access to program libraries and data files on a “need-to-know” basis; and providing the ability to hold users accountable for activities performed.
    • Program Security — Access to program files and libraries should be restricted to authorized personnel through use of access control and other security software.  Program updates should be monitored and controlled using library management software.  Appropriate segregation of duties should ensure that the programming function does not have unrestricted access to production programs.
    • Physical Security — Access to processors and storage devices should be limited to those (e.g., management and operations staff) requiring access to perform job functions.  Access to the host server computer room should be monitored and controlled (e.g., card access systems).  Physical control over reports containing confidential data should be implemented (e.g., distribution procedures).  Physical safeguards include fire prevention, preventive maintenance, backup of data files, and property insurance.
Many protectability objectives are designed to ensure data retains its integrity.  In other words, data is complete, accurate, and up-to-date, and cannot be changed on an unauthorized basis.  To help ensure integrity, the auditor evaluates controls over causes of erroneous data, often characterized as application controls, complemented by general controls over access, described above.  More detailed integrity control objectives include:
    • Authorized transactions are initially and completely recorded.
    • All transactions are completely and accurately entered into the system for processing.
    • Approved transactions entered are accepted by the system and processed to completion.
    • All transactions are processed only once; no duplicate transactions are processed.
    • All transactions are processed accurately, updating the correct files and records.
Procedures should minimize the opportunity for programmers and users to make unauthorized changes to production programs.  Access to system software should be controlled to avoid direct compromise of the integrity of program code, data on file, or results of processing.
Confidentiality and privacy are issues of accountability in compliance, and protectability in making it possible.  As one expert put it at one of The IIA’s Critical Infrastructure Conferences: “Without security, there is no privacy.”  Confidentiality usually refers to intellectual property, trade secrets, competitive plans, or national security.  Privacy is usually viewed in the context of personal information, including customers, employees, and stockholders, but not corporate entities.  The Internet facilitates a new level of low-cost collection.  Organizations need to maintain essential data about individuals to accomplish their missions, but some go far beyond the minimum.  Collection of information has become an industry in itself.
Accountability
Accountability identifies individual roles, actions, and responsibilities.  It includes the concepts of data ownership, identification, and authentication, all fundamental to being able to identify who or what caused a transaction.  The audit, or transaction, trail should have enough information ― and be retained long enough ― for transactions to be confirmed, if necessary.
Accountability includes the concept of non-repudiation.  This means once authenticated, a user cannot disclaim a transaction, as might happen when an online brokerage user seeks to break a trade that turned out to be a bad idea that they, nonetheless, actually caused.
It also includes issues in granting traceable access to restricted information and software functions.  This is a particular problem in IT, where systems analysts and the like resist controls over their own activities.  In some cases, monitoring of such use, while seemingly appropriate, can be turned off by the very system administrators it is designed to watch.
Organizations need to authenticate the identity of people entrusted with authority to change data files or software.  Similarly, an organization holding private information has an obligation to authenticate the identity of inquirers before disclosing information.  In such cases, accountability and privacy may appear to be in conflict.  Accountability means identifying the source of a transaction, while privacy might deny meaningful identification.  These objectives can be reconciled with care.  Accountability protects everyone, for example where a seller has a legitimate need to authenticate the identity of a buyer for credit purposes, while the holder of the credit card has a legitimate need to authenticate the seller to prevent fraudulent misrepresentation.
To support accountability, information must be sufficient, accurate, timely, and available to management to meet its responsibilities.  To help ensure reliability of information, the auditor evaluates controls over unacceptable processing and reporting.  These might include:
    • Information can be supported irrefutably.  Controls that provide support are variously known as transaction trails or audit trails.
    • Information should be timely.  It must be available when decisions are made.  This is a common criticism of financial statements issued months after the events.
    • Information must be consistent, and in accordance with applicable policies.  Errors of inappropriate processing, whether programmed or human, are common causes thwarting this effect.  Management override can be another.
Achieving Assurance Objectives
Achieving Availability, Capability, Functionality, Protectability, and Accountability requires an adequate infrastructure, resources, and organizational commitment.  Areas to make this possible include the building blocks: people, technology, processes, investment, and communication, shown in the ovals in the third band at the center of the model.
This already complex environment has to effectively respond to external forces.  Depicted as multidirectional arrows, the impact of ever-increasing interaction, interconnectivity, and system sharing with external market forces (customers, competition, regulators, community, and owners) and speed of change (velocity), compounded by the external interdependencies (providers, alliances, and agents), shows just how many forces are affecting the e-business environment.
Dynamic Environment
The context to all of this is shown by the gray oval.  The dynamic environment is what many call the control environment, although today people prefer to talk about risks and responses rather than controls.  To enable the environment to retain stability and control, Monitoring and Oversight are key elements.  Monitoring and oversight means pertinent risks, whether internal or external to the organization, are recognized and addressed on an ongoing basis and controls are confirmed to be functioning as intended.  These requirements are shown as the background, and must be effectively designed for all this to work, whatever it is called.
Auditors are moving to other areas of monitoring, such as logging, and are testing to understand where deficiencies and problems may occur.  In a world where electronic records are the order of the day, and paper is truly disappearing, auditors can no longer expect to rely on familiar tangibles such as source documents.  Today’s source can be a Web-based input form, or an electronic transmission from another system.  For example:
    • What is the business objective?  The goal of an e-business may not be clearly articulated, and those depending on it may require after-the-fact definitions and strategies.
    • What are the risks?  Technology platforms are non-homogenous and interlinked, often with outside agents — how can network security in a network of networks be assessed (the Internet and those of business allies)?
    • Where are the controls?  Traditional views may not work, since there is a need to look to the infrastructure, business processes, and middleware glue that hold it all together (where formerly one reviewed general controls and application controls).

Since the ovals in the third central band are control building blocks, the heat map illustrates how the model might be used to drill down to highlight root cause issues, discussing each according to its priority and potential consequences.

Summary
Availability, Capability, Functionality, Protectability, and Accountability are business assurance objectives.  What they mean in this context is:
    • Availability
Able to receive, accept, process, and support transactions at all times, as required (e.g., 24/7).
    • Capability
There is end-to-end reliable, timely completion and fulfillment of all transactions.
    • Functionality
System provides necessary facilities, responsiveness, and ease-of-use to meet user needs and expectations.
    • Protectability
Logical and physical security controls ensure authorized access, and deny unauthorized access, to servers, applications, and information assets.
    • Accountability
Transaction processing is accurate, complete, and non-refutable (non-repudiation concept).

Training Objectives for Auditors in eBusiness
    • Understand key information technology risk components and ways to mitigate those risks.
    • Develop knowledge of Information Systems Auditing and assurance services terms and procedures, including an awareness of advanced auditing and information processing concepts.
    • Identify sources for research of technology risk.
    • Utilize the Internet as a research and communication tool for Information Technology Risk Management and Auditing.
    • Learn the controls and key questions to ask concerning controls from an end user standpoint when you are involved in using computer systems.
    • Learn the key controls that are necessary to have a well-controlled data processing department.  Also, be able to explain why these controls are necessary, and if there are compensating controls explain why the controls may not be necessary.
    • Be able to adapt an audit checklist and audit program to various Information Systems environments.
    • Develop skills necessary to perform an Information Systems Audit and prepare an audit report.
    • Be able to describe the common features of audit software and the situations in which they would best be used.
    • Develop an in-depth understanding of specific, relevant information systems risk management topics.
    • Develop the knowledge required to sit for and pass the Certified Internal Auditor (CIA), and the Certified Information Systems Auditor (CISA) examinations.
    • Learn to monitor those areas of technology risk of most concern as identified by The IIA, AICPA, ISACA, SANS, CIS, and other leaders in auditing and information security.
    • Identify and evaluate risks in an e-business environment.
    • Understand how to adapt audit coverage in areas of advanced and emerging technologies.
    • Learn to recognize opportunities where improvements in control techniques and audit practices, such as intelligent monitors and audit analytics can significantly improve an organization’s ability to protect against threats, mitigate risks, and recover from disruptions or destruction.
