Translating Early Skills and Experiences into Marketing and Industry Analysis

The professional trajectory from 1969 to 2007 represents a continuous evolution from "Machine Architect" to "Industry Strategist," where the technical scars of the early mainframe era became the predictive tools of the analyst years.

Skills Acquired: The Forensic Foundation (1969–1979)
The first decade was defined by a high-stakes apprenticeship in the "economic physics" of computing. At PHI and Arthur D. Little, the primary skill was Micro-Efficiency and Resource Management. Because machine time was $360 an hour—far more valuable than human labor—every bit and cycle had to be justified. This fostered a mastery of I/O Optimization and Dump Reading, where finding a bug meant navigating raw hexadecimal core memory rather than high-level debuggers.
Beyond the code, this era developed the skill of Forensic Systems Auditing. Whether rescuing the Chase Manhattan "Securities Factory" or vetting police software in a dangerous Hampton hotel, the ability to look at a system and identify "thrashing" or an "Unmitigated Software Mess" (USM) nine months before failure was honed. This was complemented by Architectural Diplomacy—the ability to design "bicycle-powered" systems in Rio or rural 5-baud networks in Wisconsin that respected the physical and economic constraints of the environment.

Transition to Marketing: Scale and Deal Structure (1979–1988)
At Prime Computer, the technical mastery of Phase 1 translated directly into Market Planning and Strategic "Go/No-Go" Logic. The most useful skill here was the Capitalist's Perspective, informed by years of trading Wang stock options and riding market cycles. This allowed for a transition from fixing one engine to planning the entire factory.
The "scars" from ADL's fixed-price turnkey failures became a primary asset in Business Deal Structuring. Understanding the liability of unrecognized accruals and the "billback" traps of OEM contracts allowed for the creation of more flexible, profitable software pricing models—learning from partners like Sandy Kurzig at ASK. The technical ability to intuit Moore's Law allowed for the move toward distributed departmental minicomputers (like the Prime 750) just as the mainframe's price/performance monopoly was collapsing.

The Aberdeen Group: The "Simple Physics" of the Industry (1988–2007)
During the analyst years at Aberdeen, the most potent skill was Economic and Technical Synthesis. The "Simple Physics" mindset became a way to judge the entire industry. While peers might be caught up in vendor marketing, the engineering baseline from 1969 allowed for a clear view of which architectures would genuinely scale and which were merely "making it up as they go."
The ability to detect "USMs" at a corporate level proved invaluable. Having seen a $12 million litigation at USM/Emhart and the "Watergate" atmosphere at Houghton Mifflin, it became possible to analyze a company's health not just through its spreadsheets, but through its technical "Order and Harmony." The "Whole Industry" Perspective—knowing more than the "locals" because of the sheer breadth of cross-industry auditing—turned a background in COBOL and Assembler into a predictive tool for the global technology market.
