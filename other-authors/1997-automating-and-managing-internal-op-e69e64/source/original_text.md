Automating and Managing Internal Operations
White Paper
Executive Summary
The rush to reengineer the enterprise and ﬂatten organizational structures has
forced businesses to perform a delicate balancing act of optimizing internal
processes in light of reduced resources. To improve efﬁciencies and remain
competitive, companies are turning to information technology (IT) to automate
and reﬁne multiple internal operations. This movement towards automation has
two primary objectives: to lower the cost of operations; while simultaneously
increasing employee productivity and the enterprise’s ability to quickly respond
to customer and market demands. Processes and operations being targeted
include computer hardware, software and networking support; service level
agreement (SLA) management; defect tracking; asset management; change
management; inventory management; facilities management; purchasing
requisitions; and personnel policies; procedures and recruiting records.
Aberdeen’s research has shown that all of the above operations are related due
to the fact that each involves a common set of steps or activities. These
common steps include:
Proactive notiﬁcation of when certain thresholds are exceeded and action
is to be taken;
Monitoring the status and availability of assets, whether they be people,
equipment or services;
Recording and scheduling events and activities in a central repository;
Obtaining answers to speciﬁc technical, product or procedural questions
and resolving problems, and;
Tracking outstanding problems and questions to successful closure.
Additionally, the execution of each operation often involves access to, and
modiﬁcation of, information across the enterprise.
In response, organizations are looking to deploy a suite of related applications,
based on a common technology, to automate internal operations. An
increasingly popular term for this type of software is "Consolidated Operations
Management." Aberdeen has found that many IT organizations are using the
technology from the internal IS help desk application to automate and
consolidate their operations management.
This Executive White Paper will highlight some of the initial operations being
automated, show why the help desk is often selected as the place to begin, and
deﬁne the key success characteristics required to effectively transition to
consolidated operations management. Finally, it will show how one supplier,
Remedy Corporation is positioned to take advantage of this growing market
trend.
Internal Operations: Interwoven Activities That Add to the
Bottom Line
By optimizing internal operations, organizations not only control costs, but
more importantly, enable themselves to concentrate on their core business
activities. Employees devote less time and energy to such activities as learning
how to install and use a new software application, complying with human
resource and beneﬁt procedures, and requesting resources -- such as reserving
conference rooms or obtaining new ofﬁce equipment. Relieved of these
burdens, workers are more productive and recognize an improvement in their
ability to sell to and support customers as well as design and develop new
products. The result is a direct impact on proﬁtability.
Many companies have also found that internal operations are linked together
and that a single activity requires access to, and modiﬁcation of, data across the
enterprise. For example, to move an individual from one ofﬁce to another may
involve a change to the personnel ﬁle in human resources, a request for new
ofﬁce furniture, a reconﬁguration of the internal telephone and information
systems networks, and assignment of a new personal computer and network
address. All these activities have an associated database that must be updated.
Because consolidated operations management spans the enterprise, many users
believe the best information systems architecture for automating these
processes is a single, highly adaptable application that can serve as a universal
front end to distributed data across the organization.
Activities to Automate

Consolidated operations management includes a wide variety of activities.
However, Aberdeen’s ﬁeld research has identiﬁed the following nine elements
that are usually the ﬁrst to be considered as candidates for automation. They
are:
IT Support -- Information systems are too complex for the average end-
user to fully understand and maintain. Automated IT help desks serve a
valuable role by answering questions users may have with regards to
using a new or complicated software application and resolving the
problems that arise when a particular hardware, software or networking
component fails to function properly.
Facilities Management and Equipment Allocation -- Organizations
spend an enormous amount of time and money managing their physical
plant, maintaining supplies and ensuring that employees have the
equipment they need to perform their jobs.
Service Level Agreement (SLA) Tracking — Organizations need to
know whether their equipment is under service contract and what are the
terms and conditions of those contracts. Employees are able to
instantly know who is responsible for servicing and repairing a
malfunctioning piece of equipment. Additionally, tracking SLAs enables
management to understand what service contracts are returning a positive
return on investment so that they can quickly reallocate resource dollars to
the most economical and beneﬁcial support projects.
Asset Management — Capital equipment, from PCs to industrial
machinery, needs to be tracked and amortized so that organizations know
where a particular physical asset is and when it is time to
upgrade or purchase new equipment.
Inventory Management of Stock and Spare Parts -- Companies need to
track products, supplies and equipment as they are either consumed
internally or sold to customers.
Procurement/Ordering -- When a product or service needed by an
individual cannot be found internally, there needs to be a mechanism for
creating a purchase request, forwarding that request to the purchasing
department or directly to the vendor for fulﬁllment, and tracking the
request until the item requested is delivered.
Defect Tracking -- When a fault is found with a product or service that is
sold by a company, or a way to improve an internal practice has been
identiﬁed, there needs to be a way to record the defect or suggested
improvement and follow it through to its resolution or implementation.
Personnel Resource Management -- Just as physical goods are assets, so
too are employees and their skill sets. Several companies have
recorded worker skills in a database and used advanced applications to
dynamically match and schedule the right personnel to speciﬁc jobs or
tasks that must be performed.
Notiﬁcation of Policies and Procedures -- Organizations are able to
improve the satisfaction of their employees by providing them with
immediate access and proactive notiﬁcation of changes to internal
personnel policies, such as vacation allotment, as well as beneﬁts
information, such as 401k balances and proﬁt sharing distributions.
The Help Desk — A Natural Starting Point
As stated previously, out of the activities listed above, the one that that is
typically automated ﬁrst is the internal IT support help desk. This is due to a
variety of reasons, not the least of which is that IT professionals naturally look
to information systems to solve their problems. Those organizations that have
automated their IT support have already taken the ﬁrst step needed to
modernize the rest of their internal operations. The functionality provided by
internal help desk applications can be directly applied to other activities. Below
are examples of how ﬁve critical help desk functions can be mapped to other
business operations.
Recording and Scheduling
Internal help desks need to log problems and questions submitted by end-users
and schedule technicians needed for on-site service and facilities management.
This same technique can be used, for example, to make internal procurement
requests for ofﬁce supplies or new telephone equipment for new hires. It can
also be used to schedule a maintenance person to repair a malfunctioning
building heating system.
Monitoring Inventory Status
Just as an IT help desk is used keep track of assets such as personal computers
and software licenses, a similar system can be used by a chain of retail stores to
manage inventory levels at a distribution center. Or, a transportation ﬁrm may
use a customized application to monitor the availability of empty railroad cars.

In this fashion, companies are able to reduce the capital tied up in inventory
without having the risk of running out.
Problem Resolution
IT help desks solve problems such as "How do I log on to the new server?" or
"I forgot my password." This is done by submitting queries against a database
of anticipated and previously encountered problems and solutions. Comparable
applications can be deployed to answer questions such as "How do I get
reimbursed for medical expenses?" or "How do I process a customer’s request
for a credit line?"
Tracking Activities
IT help desks track and escalate end-users’ problems until they are successfully
resolved. This is no different from tracking defects in the products that a
company itself manufactures. For example, a car manufacturer may need to
track a transmission defect, both to notify existing customers of the problem
and to make sure that engineering corrects it in future models.
Notiﬁcation
Through links to network and systems management platforms, IT help desks
can be conﬁgured to automatically issue alerts when a particular server fails or
once certain network trafﬁc limits are exceeded. This allows immediate action
to be taken. Likewise, information technology can be used to issue warnings
and trigger a reordering of stock when inventory levels drop below a certain
point.
Because the internal IT help desk often serves as a launching pad for the
automation of multiple operations, Aberdeen believes that user organizations
need to keep future, unforeseen, uses of their help desk application in mind
with selecting an initial supplier. Selecting a solution with an adaptable
technology core can increase its value to the organization over the long term.
Success Factors
Aberdeen has identiﬁed the following six elements that are critical to consider
when selecting an application for consolidated operations management.
The ﬁrst is that the application needs to be highly — and easily — adaptable.
While many business operations are similar, each has its own peculiarities.
Tracking defects or inventory is different than tracking personal computers.
The data elements and data schemas will differ slightly for each business
process and the application needs to accommodate this reality. User
organizations need to ensure that the application can meet their speciﬁc needs.

Second, the application must have the ability to access a wide variety of data
sources and data formats. All the data that is needed often does not reside in a
single database. Inventory data may reside on a mainframe, while personnel
records may be on a Unix server running Oracle. The solution must reach
across the enterprise, regardless of platforms or databases.
Next, the system must have an intuitive user interface that can serve as a
universal front end to data across the enterprise. Aberdeen’s research has shown
that there is a direct correlation between the ease-of-use of an application and
its long-term success. Easy to use toolbars and screens that step end-users
through unfamiliar procedures are a deﬁnite advantage.
The ability to embed business rules and procedures is also a critical evaluation
criteria. For example, a company may want to specify that an alert is generated
if a new employee has been hired without being assigned an email address or if
a customer’s order has not been shipped within a certain time period.
Next, an application must allow for rapid and continuous modiﬁcation. Internal
operations are not static, but are always evolving to respond to changing
business conditions. As a result, it must be easy for system administrators to
may changes to the application on an ongoing basis.
Finally, it is important to have an open system that can be integrated with other
applications deployed across the enterprise. There is often the need to access

information and services from existing ﬁnancial, manufacturing and human
resources applications. A set of exposed application programming interfaces
(APIs) is important in this regard.
Remedy’s Action Request System: Users Extending Its Value
Aberdeen has observed that one company in an excellent position to become
the leading supplier of consolidated operations management (COM)
applications is Remedy Corporation. Based in Mountain View, CA, Remedy is
already one of the top suppliers on internal help desk applications, with over
3,000 customers to date. The company’s ﬂagship product, The Action Request
System (AR System), is an adaptable application that provides the core
capabilities required by a help desk, including call logging and tracking,
escalation and notiﬁcation, problem resolution, and reporting. It is deployed in
a wide variety of vertical industries, including automotive, computers,
chemicals/pharmaceuticals, energy/utilities, ﬁnancial services, government,
telecommunications, and educational institutions. The AR System has been
popular due to its ﬂexibility, scalability, robust set of features and its ability to
link to a wide variety of third party software, such as network and systems
management platforms. Additionally, Remedy has build a strong distribution
channel of resellers and systems integrators to ensure that customer
installations go smoothly.
In researching this Executive White Paper, Aberdeen interviewed several
Remedy customers who had deployed the AR System beyond an initial help
desk environment. Popular applications cited by users of the AR System
include:
Inventory Management;
Purchase Order Management;
Stock Reordering;
Password and Network Address Tracking;
Asset Tracking and Change Management.
Although the organizations contacted by Aberdeen were from multiple vertical
industries, all stated that the primary reason for using Remedy’s product for
these different scenarios was its ease of customization and high level of
adaptability.
As mentioned, most user organizations started by deploying the AR System to
automate and improve their help desk operations. Once the system was up and
running, two things became apparent. The ﬁrst was that the help desk
application generated a great deal of information that would be useful for other
internal operations. For example, records of the desktop platform used by each
employee is directly applicable to an inventory management or asset tracking
application. Additionally, in familiarizing themselves with the AR System
through the help desk implementation, organizations not only gained an
appreciation of the ﬂexibility of Remedy’s solution, they also created the skill
sets needed to use it in new and exciting ways.
The AR System’s success with a wide range of audiences is clearly shown in
Remedy’s ﬁnancial performance. For the ﬁrst three quarters of 1996, 53% of
Remedy’s sales were to existing customers, a great many of which used the AR
System outside of the internal help desk. Aberdeen believes that this natural
trend of using the AR System for automating multiple internal operations will
accelerate.
Remedy’s natural advantage for automating internal operations has been further
enhanced by the company’s November 1996-release of AR System Version 3.0.
Aberdeen has identiﬁed three improvements that increase the attractiveness of
the AR System. They are:
A better user interface — Remedy has added new screen widgets that aid
in data navigation and now provides complete view management
capabilities. Additionally, the company has published its User’s Guide in
HTML format for universal access across the Internet.
Easier administration — Widgets can be dynamically hidden or shown to
simplify the user interface and guide users through speciﬁc tasks. In
addition, administrators can quickly develop customized toolbars that
provide the speciﬁc functionality needed by a single user or group of
users. Also, Remedy has made it easier to embed workﬂow processes and
business rules into its application using a set of pre-deﬁned key words.
Finally, Remedy has added customized error message handling and used
hidden schemas to simplify the process of end-user access.
Improved data access -- Users can now join multiple tables into a single
screen, without writing any SQL code. And using the new Direct SQL,
companies can build links to multiple databases distributed across the
enterprise.
Aberdeen Conclusions
As organizations look to optimize their internal processes, consolidated
operations management will become increasingly critical to their
competitiveness. The dual beneﬁts of lower costs, coupled with higher

productivity and higher customer responsiveness, are compelling issues that are
attracting attention at the highest level of the enterprise.
Flexible and adaptable internal help desk applications, because they contain a
great deal of the baseline functionality — and are installed in many
organizations already — are an excellent starting point for consolidated
operations management. This is true whether the task is service level agreement
(SLA) management; defect tracking; asset management; change management;
inventory management; facilities management; purchasing requisitions; or
human resources. Remedy’s AR System is one solution that has proven itself
capable to this task. Aberdeen expects that consolidated operations
management will rapidly move up the planning agenda of senior executives and
that the AR System will become a popular vehicle for making this critical
operational objective a business reality.
