Executive Overview
Cabletron has always been a leader in LAN switch, VLAN, and network
management technologies. As early as 1990, it invested in these technologies
based on the belief that reducing network complexity would become a critical
issue for LAN technology users. The company continues to invest in a broad
range of networking solutions that are already the envy of its competitors.
Cabletron is also making a strong financial commitment to WAN technologies -
signaling its awareness that continued success depends on repositioning the
company for this market. Through a series of acquisitions, Cabletron has filled
gaps in its current product line. More importantly, it is developing a strategy
that will place it squarely in the emerging Public-Private Switched Network
(PPSN) market.
The Aberdeen Group believes significant structural changes are occurring at
Cabletron Systems. First and foremost is a new attitude toward marketing
communications. After years of not being recognized for its technical vision
and product excellence, Cabletron has decided to invest in marketing in order
to extend the company's reputation beyond the Fortune 500.
Positioning itself for a global presence, Cabletron has designed a geography
expansion program to develop direct and indirect channels outside the U.S. To
support this, the company is copying its successful Synergy Plus program,
formed for European VAR development and now scheduled for implementation
in the U.S., Canada, Latin America, and Asia/Pacific.
Perception Problems Become Management Priority
In terms of perception, few companies have suffered as much as Cabletron
Systems. Outside the Fortune 500, the company is generally regarded as just
another hub equipment manufacturer. Few know of its record as both a
technical visionary and product innovator. And to make matters worse,
Cabletron often suffers twice. When the market finally catches up to its vision,
few in the industry remember its innovative role.
The company's history is filled with such examples. Today's trade press
overflows with articles on virtual LANs (VLANs) and LAN switches, but few
know that Cabletron started work on VLANs in 1990 and released its first
switches in 1992. There have been occasions when the company's leadership
seemed downright odd - at least to those without its vision. When it decided to
make Spectrum a market leader in network management, many wondered why
the company had strayed outside its core business area. Critics did not know
that a new concept, reduced network complexity, had become a design
principle and goal at Cabletron. In 1990, few sensed the role that network
management would play in reducing operational complexity for those using
hubs, switches and VLANs.
Aberdeen believes Cabletron's perception problem is partially due to its
original target market focus, channel strategy, and marketing philosophy. For
most of its history, Cabletron sold primarily to the Fortune 500 and used its
own direct sales force to do so. In this narrowly defined market, it firmly
established its reputation for product excellence and leadership. Outside this
exclusive group, however, few knew of its many accomplishments. Since it was
so narrowly focused, Cabletron made few investments in generalized marketing
campaigns that would have enhanced its corporate image or established its
brand awareness.
As part of a major effort to take the company to the next stage of business
development - and update the market's perception of the company - Cabletron
has put in place a broad set of initiatives. It has added depth to its executive
ranks by hiring senior managers from outside the company. These newer
executives in marketing, service and support, and channels management will
have considerable influence over the way the company presents itself to the
industry. Due to their presence, Aberdeen expects Cabletron's advertising tone
will become more sophisticated and tenacious, rather than combative,
spotlighting the company's strengths rather than the competition's weaknesses.
One of these strengths is "reduced network complexity," and Cabletron plans to
weave this concept into all of its new marketing and sales messages. Going
beyond its traditional target audience of technical buyers, the company will
direct this message at higher-level decision makers. By advertising in general
business publications, the company will emphasize the strategic business
benefits of working with Cabletron and using its solutions.
The WAN Strategy: Today and Tomorrow
Aberdeen believes Cabletron Systems is in the process of repositioning the
company to be an end-to-end network equipment provider. Toward this goal,
Aberdeen sees Cabletron as engaged in a two-fold strategy. First, it is focused
on delivering the solutions demanded by today's WAN market. Second, it is
positioning itself to become a significant player in an emerging market just on
the horizon. Aberdeen calls this the Public-Private Switched Network (PPSN)
market.
Positioning for Today
Many data service and equipment markets were envisioned in the late 1980's,
but few achieved the acceptance or experienced the growth of the frame relay
and remote access equipment markets. In order to serve these segments and
establish a reputation as a WAN equipment provider, Cabletron embarked on a
dual campaign of product development and corporate acquisitions. By
acquiring Network Express and Netlink, Cabletron demonstrated its resolve to
be a complete end-to-end LAN/WAN equipment provider.
Aberdeen is impressed by the process Cabletron developed to screen candidates
for acquisition. To be considered, companies had to share its ASIC, CPU, and
software development philosophies. Through this approach, Cabletron has been
able to quickly assimilate products from the acquired companies. For example,
Network Express technology has already been integrated into the
CyberSWITCH family of remote access products.
Positioning for the Future: The PPSN Market
A major evolutionary change is occurring in the networking industry - the
movement toward what Aberdeen refers to as Public-Private Switched
Networks or PPSN. In the PPSN market, equipment from one manufacturer
will span the traditional boundary between the customer premises equipment
(CPE) market and the public carrier market. Companies that have traditionally
developed products only to the specifications of the carrier industry will enter
the CPE market with LAN equipment. In turn, LAN manufacturers will build
solutions that can be used as adjunct equipment to the Central Office (CO)
switches of local exchange and interexchange carriers. This trend can be seen in
recent industry mergers such as Lucent Technology/Agile Networks and
Cisco/Stratacom.
The characteristics of the PPSN market are still unfolding, but Aberdeen
believes the early competency requirements for success are evident. First,
successful companies will require the ability to deliver integrated product lines
that scale from the lower-end bandwidth requirements of the CPE market up to
the high-end gigabit speeds of the carriers. Second, successful companies will
need to deliver a network management platform that can span CPE and CO
environments, thereby providing an integrated network management view of
the deployed equipment. Third, successful companies will have to possess an
agile corporate culture able to react quickly to the rapid changes that will occur
in a still emerging market. Fourth, successful companies will require an
empowering strategic vision that can provide both the courage to make the
necessary investments and the fortitude to face potential failures.
Aberdeen believes Cabletron Systems possesses all the required competencies
for the first phase of PPSN market success, and therefore ranks it in the top tier
of suppliers who are positioning themselves for this market. All vendors,
however, must face the challenges of the next market phase. Cabletron must
prove it can develop the sales forces and channels to sell to the carrier and
service provider markets.
LAN Strategy: Enduring Philosophies
Cabletron's corporate history has been in close phase with all major
developments in the LAN equipment market. Starting with simple transceivers
and progressing to LAN switches and VLANs, Cabletron quickly moved ahead
to a leadership position in the industry. In 1990, at a time when most
networking companies were still debating their entry into the intelligent hub
market, Cabletron invested in SecureFast VLAN technology. At the same time,
Cabletron began development of Spectrum, its market leading network
management platform, on the belief that the management of networking
equipment would be as important as the performance of the equipment itself.
Although not immediately evident, a common engineering philosophy unites
the early investments in VLAN and network management technologies.
Cabletron has long been dedicated to the principle of reducing network
complexity, and VLANs and network management are two technologies that do
just that. For the future, Cabletron believes this principle is about to pay off.
From its perspective, the era of selling networking "boxes" is about to end.
Business requirements - specifically, the maintenance of orderly business
processes - will be as important as the features, functions, and pricing of the
solutions themselves. In this new era, three year equipment life-cycles will no
longer be tolerated because forklift upgrades cripple a network and disrupt the
normal business practices of a company. In addition, network support will
become as large an expense factor as the initial capital cost of equipment.
Cabletron believes it will succeed in this new era because its philosophy has
always been to build easily upgradable solutions that also reduce the cost of
managing and supporting a network.
Many companies tout investment protection in their product descriptions. It has
almost become a mandatory "check-off" term in marketing communications
literature. Aberdeen believes that this term not only has meaning at Cabletron,
but that the company has carried the concept to a new level. In an era of forklift
upgrades, it is truly amazing that an MMAC chassis released in 1988 can be
upgraded to technologies such as ATM, Fast Ethernet, and VLANs -
technologies that did not exist when the first chassis was shipped. None of
Cabletron's competitors had solutions durable enough to survive these
technology transitions.
At Cabletron, investment protection extends beyond preserving value in
equipment. It also means that new features and functions can be acquired
through equipment upgrades that do not mandate uprooting an entire network.
This, once again, emphasizes Cabletron's belief that business costs will be as
important a factor as equipment costs in the new era of network selling.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group Company Profile (Spring 1997) of Cabletron Systems, Inc. arguing that Cabletron's technical excellence was systematically underrecognized. Aberdeen documents Cabletron's SecureFast VLAN architecture, Spectrum network management platform, SmartSwitch product family, CyberSWITCH remote access line, and ambitious World/One global expansion program. The report introduces Aberdeen's Public-Private Switched Network (PPSN) concept and ranks Cabletron as a top-tier PPSN competitor. In reality, Cabletron peaked in 1997, reorganized as a holding company in 2000, and its successor Enterasys Networks was acquired for only $180M in 2013.
