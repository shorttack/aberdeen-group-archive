Cabletron and Ipsilon: Linking SecureFast
Architecture and IP Switching
The near forty percent reduction in the market capitalization of networking stocks
opened the door for the recent rash of industry acquisitions. Some of these deals
left analysts struggling to find the rationale behind the mergers, and customers
confused about the ultimate impact on the product lines. Cabletron and Ipsilon
put their own spin on this fever by consummating a strategic alliance in which
Cabletron made a $20 million dollar equity investment in Ipsilon. Unlike many
other deals, Aberdeen has had little trouble seeing how this alliance will benefit
both the companies and their customers.
The What of the Deal: Technology Yin and Yang
Cabletron's $20 million dollar stake in Ipsilon amounts to more than just a
strategic equity investment from one company to another. It is also a technology
sharing agreement that Aberdeen views as one of the most rational of the
industry's recent mergers and acquisitions.
Under the terms of the deal, Cabletron will integrate Ipsilon's award winning IP
Switching technology on its SecureFast SmartSwitches. GSMP (Generic Switch
Management Protocol) and IFMP (Ipsilon's Flow Management Protocol)
protocols will be supported throughout the SmartSwitch and SmartCell family.
Ipsilon, in turn, will incorporate Cabletron's award-winning SecureFast Policy
Management and Accounting services into its line of campus and wide-area
network switches via RFC 2124, Cabletron's Lightweight Flow Admission
Protocol (LFAP).
The integrated product set delivers a high-speed, switch-based network that
extends from desktop-to-desktop across both campus and wide area networks. By
deploying SecureFast SmartSwitches in the LAN and IP Switches in the campus
backbone and WAN, customers will get a highly-scalable switched infrastructure
that is robust enough to support enterprise-class applications such as Intranet and
Internet access.
Cabletron: Enabling Enterprise-Class Applications
Cabletron's SecureFast SmartSwitches deliver award winning VLAN features
such as connection-oriented accounting and security. Users, for example, can be
limited to time-of-day/day-of-week access to network as well as monitored and
billed for their use of both network resources and application programs. In
conjunction with distributed layer-3 switching, Cabletron has successfully
positioned SecureFast SmartSwitches as a leading edge solution for deploying
corporate Intranets.
Enterprise-class Intranets, however, by definition span wide-area geographical
boundaries. At first, Cabletron was limited to a reliance on relatively slow routers
to extend Intranets across wide-area organizational boundaries. Frames or cells
might travel at switch speeds in the Cabletron environment, but they would slow
to a router's pace when traversing the WAN. By combining IP routing with high-
speed ATM switching hardware, Ipsilon developed a product that offers five-times
the performance of routers at a fraction of the price. As a result of its technology
sharing agreement, Cabletron can now offer an enterprise-class network that
moves application data across wide-area network organizational boundaries at
switch speeds.
Ipsilon: Cash, Channels, and Market Access
Ipsilon gets an immediate cash infusion from this deal, but more importantly gets
long term access to Cabletron's distribution channels. This is not simply a
reference selling agreement between the suppliers. Cabletron will not only sell the
Ipsilon switches, it will also provide worldwide service and support for the
products.
In terms of rapid market access, Ipsilon acquires one more point of entry into the
Fortune 500, a market in which Cabletron has a well-established presence and a
well-earned reputation. Due to the complementary nature of the technical alliance,
Ipsilon can rest assured that its switches will be part of each enterprise solution
introduced by Cabletron into this lucrative segment.

Longer term, Ipsilon will gain a wider global presence as a result of Cabletron's
Synergy/Plus program, which is Cabletron's sales, support, and service strategy
for addressing the needs of the Global 1000.
Aberdeen Conclusions
Aberdeen is impressed by the logic of the relationship between Cabletron and
Ipsilon. Each company had developed award winning products for their respective
markets. By combining the two product lines, each vendor now contributes a
highly value-added piece to a solution set that offers customers a switched
networking environment from desktop-to-campus-to-WAN. Aberdeen is further
impressed by the fact that product is available from the suppliers now, rather than
in some unspecified time frame.
Users are now in the process of upgrading their hub and router local area
networks to high-speed frame and cell switched networks, and they will find the
option of deploying SecureFast SmartSwitches in their LANs and IP Switches in
their campus backbones and WANs extremely attractive.
From a broader perspective, this alliance creates synergy that changes the
competitive landscape. By combining Cabletron's excellence in switches with
Ipsilon's in routers, customers get a solution that competes with Cisco's Tag
Switching today. While interoperability with existing router backbones can be
maintained, scaleable switched hardware solutions can be deployed across the
enterprise without the need for a router.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group Impact Brief (April 17, 1997) analyzing the strategic alliance between Cabletron Systems and Ipsilon Networks, in which Cabletron made a $20 million equity investment in Ipsilon. Aberdeen endorses the deal as one of the most rational of the industry's M&A wave: Cabletron contributes its SecureFast SmartSwitch technology with VLAN and policy-based management; Ipsilon contributes IP Switching technology that achieves 5x router performance at a fraction of the cost. The combined solution extends switched networking from desktop-to-campus-to-WAN, competing directly with Cisco's Tag Switching. Aberdeen predicts customers upgrading from hub/router LANs will find the combined product set extremely attractive. In reality, Nokia acquired Ipsilon for $120M just 8 months later (December 1997), dissolving the alliance; IP Switching lost the standards battle to Cisco's MPLS.
