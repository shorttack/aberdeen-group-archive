Executive Summary
Customer Interaction Software (CIS) is a class of front-line
business applications used by leading organizations in all
industries to acquire and retain long-term, profitable
customers. Forward-looking operational executives are
actively seeking out the independent software vendors (ISVs)
that provide these applications and are rapidly implementing
next-generation systems based on this advanced software. CIS
covers a wide range of applications, including external
customer service, external customer support, sales force
automation, telemarketing/telesales, sales configuration,
internal help desk and quality assurance. What makes CIS so
attractive is that it has clearly demonstrated its ability to raise
revenues for those companies that use it properly. It is
increasingly apparent that the user organizations using CIS are
pulling ahead of their rivals in terms of customer satisfaction
and retention, new business development, and profitability.
This report provides the reader with a clear understanding of
the forces influencing the market coupled with Aberdeen's
insight into the future directions of Customer Interaction
Software. In addition to explaining the basic CIS modules and
important related technologies, Aberdeen also discusses how
organizations have successfully installed CIS systems and
highlights some of the pitfalls that can be avoided. Finally, the
study includes profiles on over sixty CIS suppliers, allowing
executives to quickly identify the major players and
understand their market position, as well as the unique value
each brings to the table.
This is Aberdeen's third annual report on the CIS market and
includes several important additions over our last study, which
was published in October 1997. Some of the more significant
enhancements include:
A discussion of the transition to Web-based CIS — The
Internet represents a fundamental shift in how CIS
applications are architected and deployed.
An outline of the diverging paths of customer support,
customer service and internal help desk applications —
This is an important trend for software suppliers looking
to target multiple markets.
CIS-to-ERP integration — This report contains an
overview of the rapidly emerging trend of combining CIS
applications with enterprise resource planning (ERP)
software.
New and Expanded Market Numbers — Chapter Five
provides Aberdeen's most recent data on CIS market size
and growth prospects.
More Suppliers — Aberdeen has added over one dozen
suppliers to this report.
Managing Customers with Next-Generation Software
Applications: 1997 Edition is intended for three major
audiences. The first is the business executive who needs to
understand what CIS can do and which products are best
suited to meet a particular objective. The second is the IS
executive responsible for implementing CIS applications and
integrating them into an enterprise's information systems
infrastructure. The report helps these individuals plan for the
future by outlining the architectures of various CIS offerings,
as well as the software development tools and related internal
IS skill sets needed to make the unique customizations
demanded by end-users. Finally, it is a valuable market
analysis and planning document for CIS suppliers who want to
better understand key industry dynamics and user buying
patterns in order to improve market position.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group's third annual market research report on Customer Interaction Software (CIS) — a class of front-line applications covering customer service, customer support, sales force automation, telemarketing/telesales, sales configuration, internal help desk, and quality assurance. Published in late 1997 at $895 per copy and 246 pages, the report profiles over 60 CIS software suppliers, seven systems integrators, and three systems suppliers. Key themes include: the transition to Web-based CIS architectures, diverging paths of customer support vs. customer service vs. help desk, CIS-to-ERP integration trends, and computer-telephony integration (CTI). The report names Siebel Systems, Clarify, Vantive, Remedy, Scopus, and over 50 other vendors as major market participants, and forecasts substantial market growth through the late 1990s.

### Document Assessment

| Dimension | Rating | Rationale |
|-----------|--------|-----------| 
| **Importance** | high | This report captured the CRM market at its inflection point — the moment when disparate front-office applications began coalescing into what the industry would later call CRM. Aberdeen's framework of CIS modules and its vendor landscape became foundational reference material during the most active period of CRM market consolidation (1997-2005). The report anticipated the rise of integrated CRM suites that would define Oracle's and SAP's application portfolios. |
| **Relevance** | high | The CIS/CRM framework developed in this report remains structurally valid. Modern CRM platforms (Salesforce, Microsoft Dynamics, SAP CX) still organize capabilities around the same functional pillars: sales automation, customer service, help desk/ITSM, and field service. The specific vendor profiles are dated, but the market structure analysis retains explanatory power. |
| **Prescience** | high | Aberdeen correctly foresaw Web-based CIS architectures becoming dominant, the convergence of CIS with ERP, and CTI as a critical enabler — all of which materialized. However, the specific vendor predictions were dramatically disrupted: Siebel rose to ~45% market share by 2002 then was acquired by Oracle; Vantive was bought by PeopleSoft; Clarify was sold to Nortel then Amdocs; Scopus was acquired by Siebel; Remedy went to Peregrine then BMC; and Salesforce.com (founded 1999, not in this report) eventually displaced all of them. |
