Executive Summary
Cisco is the undisputed leader in the Global 1000 enterprise, internetworking
equipment market. The company has built its enviable reputation by becoming
the dominant supplier of routing technology and by creating a brand name in
this market segment. Cisco offers a wide range of scalable router products,
provides a robust software environment called IOS and executes both its direct
and indirect channel sales and marketing programs with precision. Cisco has
sold capably into enterprise accounts where the challenge over the last five
years has been to upgrade mainframe-and-minicomputer-oriented networks so
that these might perform adequately given the surge in demand created by PCs
and PC LANs. Recently, Cisco has also taken full advantage of explosive
demand driven by ISPs and WAN service providers who were rapidly adding
capacity for the Internet.
Nevertheless, as LANs, campus backbones and WANs have grown in size,
complexity, and performance requirements, Cisco's leadership position has
been challenged by a variety of emerging technologies. The most notable
challenge, of course, has been created by the current debate about the merits of
routers versus switches. This planning issue has been raging as a critical
concern both in IS organizations charged with designing next-generation hybrid
networks and the supplier community.
The question for Cisco is always the same. Can the market leader maintain its
dominant industry position if another technology replaces routers or routing as
the preferred, low-cost, high performance networking solution? In this Profile,
Aberdeen examines how Cisco has not only maintained an impressive
leadership position in the routing market and developed its role as a
comprehensive, end-to-end enterprise network solutions provider, but also how
the company has incorporated switching into its robust core offerings.
The Internetworking Market Today
There's no doubt about it, the hottest segment of the information processing
industry today is internetworking. Mainframe-centric companies have made the
transition to three-tier, distributed architectures, and the departments of these
same organization have watched their desktop computers connect first into
LANs and then into full-fledged campus backbones, in support of new
geographically dispersed, business-critical applications. The Internet
phenomenon has added yet another element of technical complexity, demand
for new communications services and a sense of urgency to the networking
infrastructure planning agenda. Even the traditionally self-contained computing
profile of the small-to-medium-size enterprise has been drawn to networking as
access to the WAN becomes critical to emerging electronic commerce
applications. The results of this multi-dimensional growth are complex
enterprise and inter-enterprise networks based upon multiple technologies.
These hybrid, high performance networks dominate the information technology
scene today.
Cisco: At The Core of the Network
A dominant force in the computer networking equipment industry, Cisco has
built its reputation and its business by addressing issues of network complexity.
From its early beginnings as the inventor of the router, Cisco has been a major
force in setting a vision and development agenda for high performance
networks. During the last four quarters, Cisco has realized sales of $4 billion
and has taken good advantage of its $40 billion market capitalization to acquire
promising technologies.
Cisco solutions are designed and built with the complexity of the enterprise
network in mind. The company's products include routers, LAN and ATM
switches, as well as dial-up access servers and network management software.
A major theme for Cisco has been to offer solutions that have the
interoperability required in today's heterogeneous networking environment.
The Investment-Protection Thread Throughout: IOS
The core of all of Cisco's internetworking solutions is the Cisco Internetwork
Operating System, referred to as Cisco IOS. IOS has become the de facto
standard in internetworking, functioning as a unifying thread. Cisco IOS
provides cohesion to otherwise disparate networks and facilitates a scalable
migration path for growth and change. IOS software contains Cisco's value-add
internetworking intelligence. Its four cornerstones rest on scalability services,
WAN optimization services, management and security services and reliable,
adaptive routing services. IOS offers a solid internetworking foundation by
creating a single, unified infrastructure offering higher applications availability,
lower cost and improved internetwork management.
Cisco incorporates its modular IOS into its full set of network products in three
ways. It can be broken down and customized as well as dynamically installed
by end-users. Cisco's strategy is to extend IOS into multiple strategic PC
platforms, making IOS the de facto standard for PC routing. IOS can be
embedded in hardware platforms, server software or included in client desktop
operating systems such as Windows.
Planning Challenges Emerge
The addition of more nodes on the network, significant increases in processing
power, the proliferation of larger and larger data files and the increasing
adoption of multimedia applications have pushed networks to the point of
outgrowing their current architectures and available bandwidth. Furthermore,
the "old" adage that 80% of LAN traffic is confined to one local LAN segment
and 20% flows over the backbone has reversed itself. Today, almost 80% of
enterprise traffic crosses the backbone, while a mere 20% remains in any given
subnet. The result is an imbalance that demands correction.
NetFlow Switching
NetFlow Switching is designed to maximize the performance of routers by
allowing them to combine high-performance network-layer switching with the
connection-oriented application of a variety of network services. NetFlow
Switching establishes a "flow" between a source and a destination when the
first of a stream of data packets is sent through a NetFlow-enabled router. The
lookup and service application only occurs with the first packet in the flow,
instead of with every packet. Each active flow is entered in a "flow cache" in
the router, and a separate flow is established for each IP port number in packets.
NetFlow Switching is implemented in the 7000/7500 series routers.
The Role of Tag Switching
As part of its Internet scaling initiative, Cisco has introduced a new IOS
technology, Tag Switching, to its BPX/Axis ATM platforms as well as to its
7500 series of routers. Tag Switching combines the performance and traffic
management capabilities of data link layer (Layer 2) switching with the
scalability of network layer (Layer 3) routing, thus raising the integration of
Layers 2 and 3. Tag Switching enables routers at the edge of the Internet to
apply tags to traffic flows based on traditional Layer 3 routing protocols.
Devices in the core of the network can then switch traffic by using simple tags.
The Tag Switching protocol has been submitted to the IETF and has already
received the endorsement of numerous third parties.
While Tag Switching promises significant features for scaling both the Internet
and large enterprise networks, the technology will not be available for field
trials until the first half of 1997. Until then, its merits cannot be fully assessed
from an end-user standpoint.
Building for the Next Generation
Aberdeen research indicates that requirements for continually-scaling, high-
speed, next-generation networking architectures will include high port density,
and high bandwidth capabilities in the backbone and the core of the network.
Cisco's blueprint for next generation networks includes the Gigabit Switch
Router (GSR), which will complement the 7500 router, addressing different
needs and different markets. The GSR is optimized for higher bandwidth and
IP, which is the dominant backbone traffic protocol today. It will feature high
port density and high speeds, interfacing with OC3, OC-12 and up to OC-48,
as well as Gigabit Ethernet, Packet over SONET and ATM. Its multigigabit
crossbar switching fabric will sustain speeds of 80 Gigabits per second.
Aberdeen Conclusions
Today there is an increasing realization that the network is the business. Cisco
continues to dominate this increasingly competitive market, but, to the
company's credit, it has not been lulled by its success. Cisco recognizes that
there are areas of weakness in its product portfolio that need to be addressed,
especially as competition in the switching market heats up.
Tag switching holds promise for delivering that response, but it is too soon to
tell whether this approach will deliver an industry-standard level of enthusiasm
such as IOS has been able to achieve.
Aberdeen believes that Cisco fully understands the increasingly important role
that switching will play in network architecture. Only a market leader possesses
the resources to forge ahead as Cisco has brought to the table. More
importantly, Cisco has also made the management commitment necessary to
continue not just as the router vendor-of-record but as the customer's enterprise
network partner with whatever technologies are required.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group Company Profile (January 1997) of Cisco Systems examining how the undisputed enterprise networking leader evolved from router-only vendor to comprehensive end-to-end solutions provider. Documents $4B revenue, $40B market cap, >40% LAN switching market share, >80% of internet backbone traffic on Cisco routers. Analyzes Cisco IOS as the de facto internetworking standard, NetFlow Switching (per-flow statistics, QoS, 7500 series implementation), Tag Switching (MPLS precursor, submitted to IETF), StrataCom/Stratm Technology integration, and Gigabit Switch Router (GSR 12000 Series). Aberdeen predicts Cisco will maintain leadership by assimilating switching — a prediction fully validated by subsequent events.
