Commerce eXchange Answers The "Show Me
The Internet Money!" Challenge
In 1996, Internet sales of computer hardware and software comprised
approximately 37% of the emerging $520 million Internet goods market.
Aberdeen data shows this market exploding in 1997 with at least one computer
hardware vendor already at an Internet run rate of $1 million per day. At this
rate, Internet retail systems for computer hardware and software suppliers are
both strategic and mission critical.
Surprisingly, many computer-related retailers are betting their businesses on a
third-party, New York-based software solution from Interworld Technology
Ventures, Inc. In this Product Viewpoint, Aberdeen takes a second look at
Interworld's Internet commerce solutions to better understand how Internet
sales impact information systems and to explain why computer-related
retailers, with so much at stake, are using a private and, to date, little known
company to answer the CEO's challenge, "Show me the Internet Money!"
Executive Summary
What portion of a major commercial Web site should be bought? In this
Viewpoint, Aberdeen argues that the primary Internet sales channel obstacle for
high-volume sites is the lack of solution breadth. Large-scale and interactive
commercial sites are highly complex. They must be open, scalable, and
integrate multiple point products and legacy systems.
By purchasing component Internet applications and scalable infrastructure from
a single vendor with Internet business experience, managers can concentrate on
site-specific business rules, interfaces, strategic marketing and customer service
rather than experimenting with complex and untested distributed transaction
processing systems and engaging in dubious systems integration.
The same information systems executives, charged with opening the enterprise
to the emerging Internet market, are often simultaneously expected to open the
enterprise to business partners and suppliers using EDI or Extranet technology.
Unfortunately, most business-to-business solutions are not designed to manage
complex real-time customer interaction and off-the-shelf Internet retail
products process only simple credit card authorization and payment. Yet both
the complex Internet sales channel and business-to-business systems require a
sophisticated transaction engine.
Web-enabled extensions of client-server or legacy applications will not
adequately serve as Fortune 1000 Internet retail solutions because they will not
scale to potentially millions of Internet users and because the applications were
not designed to interact with consumers. Developers of Internet commerce
applications are therefore pleased to find an Internet sales solution that provides
the primary application functionality, transaction engine, high-performance
distributed transaction processing architecture, and tools for ongoing
maintenance and development.
Version 2.0 of Interworld Commerce eXchange is a comprehensive enterprise
Internet retail solution with no technology strings attached. Since day one,
Interworld chose to protect its software from any single technology weak link
by developing an abstraction layer between its applications and industry
standard databases, various Internet protocols, and object request brokers
(ORBs). To open the Internet sales channel, Commerce eXchange customers
need not bet on any specific database or object request broker (ORB) and avoid
CORBA / DCOM translation issues altogether.
The Two Faces of Electronic Commerce
Electronic commerce (EC) is a blanket term used to describe any computer-to-
computer financial transaction. It includes selling products directly to Internet
buyers and using the Internet as a means to integrate and manage business
systems and business processes. Fortune 1000 corporations often use electronic
document interchange (EDI) for access to legacy data and to provide a common
format for purchase orders, invoices, and other business transactions.
Understanding that effective Internet sales leverage existing corporate systems,
Interworld's Commerce eXchange is equipped with an EDI "driver" that enables
business-to-business communications via standard EDI templates.
Wanted: An Integrated Commerce eXchange
Commerce eXchange offers a uniquely open solution for the entire Internet
business process from shopping to fulfillment. Interworld is shipping Commerce
eXchange with the following commerce solution components:
Transaction Engine: A single transaction management system capable of
supporting complex business rules and workflow for consumer and business
transactions.
Application Portfolio: Secure and ready-to-deploy applications for opening
an Internet-based sales and distribution channel.
Architecture: Interworld's object architecture and scalable interoperating
system (Oasis) is a production-class, distributed transaction processing
platform designed to handle high transaction volumes and to communicate
with existing corporate systems via plug-in "drivers." Oasis is open to any
technology object model or object request broker (ORB).
Tools: Easy-to-use management, remote administration, and customization
tools help users fine-tune Interworld-driven sites from any location.
Microsoft / Oracle Insurance
Oracle, Broadvision, and other Internet software suppliers are promoting the
Object Management Group's common object request broker architecture
(CORBA) and TP-Monitor-like technology as the basis for building high-
performance and interoperable systems. Microsoft, however, is pushing its own
distributed common object model (DCOM) for Internet point-of-sale and retail
software. To make different object models communicate together, IS typically
implements middleware translation gateways that wrap one object architecture
in another — often with a significant performance hit.
Oasis is interoperable with both CORBA and DCOM without translation
gateways and with negligible performance degradation because it was designed
from day one with an abstraction layer between it and other object-based
architectures.
High-performance Distributed Objects
Oasis' unique remote object marshaling effectively limits the amount of
communication necessary between objects over the network, thereby decreasing
network latency and increasing performance. Network communication is limited
by migrating objects, methods, and functions across the network rather than
maintaining a dialog.
Caution: If You Build it, They May Come
For large scale commercial Internet sites, scalability is paramount. Successful
online promotional campaigns require commercial Internet platforms to scale
rapidly in order to prevent customer overflow. Aberdeen finds that managers of
large commercial sites often delay advertising for fear that a flood of traffic will
swamp the system and undermine the campaign.
Future of Commercial Interaction
Aberdeen research indicates that business-critical Internet sales are currently
restricted to markets where products can be marketed effectively with low
speed (generally a 28.8k baud modem) Internet connections. For example,
nearly all discount securities brokerages now consider the Internet to be of
immediate strategic importance. Full service firms, however, consider the
Internet sales channel to be of longer-term importance. With faster Internet
connections to the home and video conferencing software, full service
customer interaction will likely be over the Internet — not over the phone.
InterWorld Customers
Broderbund: the technology savvy company uses Interworld Digital
Delivery for electronic software distribution;
MicroWarehouse: the consumer electronics distributor uses Interworld's
electronic commerce solution to sell more than 20,000 computer products
and to distribute Microsoft's software titles;
Multiple Zones International: the direct retailer employs Commerce
eXchange to power both the PC Zone and Mac Zone Internet superstores;
Comdisco: from more than 100 worldwide locations, Comdisco is helping
8,000 leasing customers acquire, manage, and protect their high-tech
equipment;
Oncology Online: truly mission-critical medical information is shared
among clinical oncologists and hematologists;
Scholastic: the publisher provides an Internet publishing system for school
systems in the United States.
Conclusions
In 1996 computer hardware and software vendors rapidly launched strategic
Internet sales channels. 1997 will see explosive Internet sales for retail and
wholesale financial services — discount brokerages in particular — industrial
and consumer catalogs, travel services, food services, and a variety of retail
products from books to automobiles.
Interworld's Commerce eXchange is what corporations and entrepreneurs need
to answer the chief executive's demand for opening the Internet sales channel,
"Show me the money!" With production quality software already installed and
working at several successful commercial sites, the money is on the table and
Interworld's Commerce eXchange offers the breadth required for an effective
solution.
Aberdeen believes that the Interworld technology vision reflects a major trend
toward object-oriented programming and distributed transaction processing for
increasingly networked businesses and consumers.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group Product Viewpoint (Volume 10, Number 2, March 14, 1997) evaluating InterWorld Technology Ventures' Commerce eXchange 2.0 enterprise internet commerce platform. Aberdeen documents the $520M internet goods market (1996), argues that high-volume commercial sites require integrated solutions (not point products), and endorses Commerce eXchange's CORBA/DCOM-neutral Oasis architecture, comprehensive application portfolio, and B2B EDI integration. Reference customers include Broderbund, MicroWarehouse, Multiple Zones International, Comdisco, and Scholastic. InterWorld ultimately filed for bankruptcy in 2004, but Aberdeen's market framework and architectural prescriptions proved largely accurate.
