Commercial Risc/Unix 1996: HP Achieves
Over 50% Share In 28% Growth Market
Aberdeen’s seventh annual survey of the commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix
marketplace shows enterprises are continuing to invest aggressively in
Risc/Unix to build their information infrastructures. And as IS decision makers
want to make a supplier decision and then not revisit the issue for as long as
possible, they are selecting HP more often than all the other suppliers
combined.
Executive Summary
In 1996, Risc/Unix platforms ascended to a position in the information systems
hierarchy as the key platform for running new production-quality, enterprise-
wide applications. The market result was a very healthy 28% increase in
revenues to $18.2 billion — approximately $4 billion greater than last year.
The undisputed Risc/Unix market leader is Hewlett-Packard with a 52% share.
This was earned by achieving a 34% revenue growth — six points higher than
the industry average. (See Figure 1.) Aberdeen’s research shows that HP
continues to deliver products and services that best meet IS executives’
requirements for building their next-generation information infrastructures.

IBM was number two with 18% market share. It dropped one point in market
share as the high-end performance of the RS/6000 could not keep up with
enterprise-level requirements. However, the two bright spots were the growth
of the RS/6000 SP for LAN consolidation and the success of Global Services,
IBM’s systems integration division, with its ability to pull through RS/6000s.
The fastest growing supplier, third place Sun Microsystems, found a ready
market in 1996 for its web-based server and technology offerings.
1996 was a year of supplier consolidation around the Big 3 by IS buyers and
independent software vendors (ISVs). Several of the smaller suppliers began
the process of transitioning to Intel processors in anticipation of being on the
leading edge of Risc-based 64-bit Intel IA-64 processors in 1998.
Intel/NT servers made inroads into the Risc/Unix market space at the
workgroup and departmental levels. While Aberdeen did not ﬁnd any
organizations that abandoned Risc/ Unix in favor of Intel/NT, we did ﬁnd a
strong trend to acquire Intel/NT servers instead of additional Risc/Unix servers
where support — especially outside the U.S. — and cost were key purchase
criteria.
Looking into 1997, Aberdeen sees a healthy Risc/Unix marketplace as this
operating environment becomes even more widespread in the core of
enterprise- wide information infrastructures. Also of importance, IS executives
facing the Year 2000 dilemma are realizing that, where possible, placing new
client-server applications on Risc/Unix platforms is preferable to repairing
unmaintainable mainframe applications. And web-based production application
growth — ideal for Risc/Unix platforms — is beginning to explode.
Looking even farther ahead into 1998, the Risc/Unix market will be bolstered
by the introduction of servers with Intel’s IA-64 Risc processor. At this

juncture, we anticipate Unix and NT taking two parallel paths that will grow
the IS market in general. We project that high-end 64-bit Unix applications will
manage enterprise-wide production functions while 32-bit NT applications will
run workgroups and departments. And IA-64 will be the common processor
technology deployed on the majority of the most popular servers.
Solid 28% Growth in 1996
In 1996, the worldwide commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix production server
market grew to $18.2 billion — up almost $4 billion, or 28%, from its 1995
level. While the growth rate in 1996 slowed from the unsustainable 43% of
1995, the absolute increase of approximately $4 billion is about the same as last
year. This 28% growth is nonetheless signiﬁcantly higher than the 1996 global
PC sales growth of 17% or the 5% decline in mainframe revenues, reﬂecting
the relative strength of the Risc/Unix platform.
Aberdeen’s market deﬁnition of commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix production
servers encompasses revenues received by suppliers for platform technology
components (Risc-based multiuser servers, memory, attached storage, Unix
operating system and related systems software licenses, and direct installation
and maintenance service charges) used to implement production systems —
batch, OLTP (online transaction processing), internet/ intranet data access and
decision support applications.
Aberdeen believes that the market revenues would be several times larger if
one also counted the additional products and services — such as RDBMSs,
applications, networks, PCs, printers, high-end storage systems, systems
integration, outsourcing, custom application development, etc. — supplied by
both the key platform manufacturers and others to create a total Risc/Unix
production environment. Note that Aberdeen has not included
ﬁle/print/communi-cations/gateway servers or technical workstations and their
associated network servers.
The Risc/Unix market’s 28% growth continues to show the strong reliance by
enterprises on Risc/Unix platforms for running their business- critical
applications. The key alternative platforms — Intel/NT, Intel/Unix, AS/400,
and MVS mainframes — despite the best efforts of their suppliers and market
hypers, were not able to be serious challengers to Risc/Unix as the ﬁrst choice
of IS decision makers for running enterprise-wide and distributed production
applications.
Risc/Unix, ERP, and RDBMS Run Today’s Best
Managed Enterprises
Risc/Unix has proven itself through the 1990s to be a core operating
environment upon which IS professionals can build their enterprise’s ﬂexible
but safe information infrastructure. On top of this infrastructure, users have
been able to add the latest and greatest applications for their requirements —
such as enterprise resource planning applications (ERP) from SAP, Oracle,
PeopleSoft, Baan, etc. And it is these applications that add the value needed for
enterprises to meet their goals. In 1996, early adopters of these client-server
applications showed that effective deployment can increase productivity, lower
internal costs, increase customer satisfaction, and decrease cycle times.
And RDBMS suppliers, the original engine of growth for Risc/ Unix platforms,
continue to show ingenious ways of expanding their technology to solve
classical business operational problems. For example, the introduction of Data
Blades from Informix shows a pragmatic approach to combining object and
relational technologies for managing complex data types that could not be
easily handled by last year’s products. More to the point, the RDBMS
suppliers’ introduction of data warehousing technologies on Risc/Unix
platforms just a few short years ago is now showing beneﬁts for well-managed
companies.
As we go forward, the underlying strength of Risc/Unix-based information
infrastructures combined with the ﬂexibility to support new technologies that
solve real-world problems provides a fundamental and lasting value for
enterprise executives. Aberdeen believes that Risc/Unix will continue to grow
as a core infrastructure technology at the enterprise level.
Intel/NT And Risc/Unix: Two Different Server
Worlds
A key question for IS planners is where to use Intel/NT servers compared to
where Risc/Unix servers are the appropriate tool. There is much media
discussion about and supplier (both system and application) cheerleading for
each.
Aberdeen’s research has shown that Risc/ Unix remains the medium-to- large
enterprise infrastructure operating environment of choice. Its high-end

scalability and proven capabilities make it the choice for running enterprise-
wide and distributed applications that must be operating for the company to
function.
However, the suppliers of Unix have not met user requirements for ease-of-use
and low-cost workgroup conﬁgurations. Microsoft, coming from the desktop
where these are critical attributes, has focused NT Server and its BackOfﬁce
suite of products on the very characteristics where Unix is weaker. For
workgroup applications, the NT Server operating environment is "good-
enough". And in geographic areas of the world where Unix expertise is scarce,
NT is the only practical client-server operating environment.
Aberdeen’s research shows that Risc/Unix and Intel/NT are being deployed as
separate operating environments within even the same user enterprise. IS
managers are implementing each technology independent of the other — and
are not demanding transparent Unix server-to-NT server integration. Where
necessary, high-speed data transfer is accomplished with TCP/IP. Most
professional enterprise IS managers believe that the only other Unix-NT
integration service they would be willing to pay for is the ability to manage
both Unix and NT servers using the same systems and network management
framework from a single console. As history has shown, this is the same level
of integration desired for mainframe-to-open systems.
The result in 1996 was that NT has expanded the marketplace in general by
automating applications which previously were too expensive or required
scarce Unix technical skills. And it captured workgroup, departmental, and
small business customers that might have chosen a Risc/Unix platform. But,
nowhere has Aberdeen observed enterprises abandoning their Risc/Unix
platforms, even at the low end, to move to NT Server.
Hewlett-Packard: Technology Supplier To The
Stars
Hewlett-Packard (HP) continues to consistently deliver the core Risc/Unix
technologies that enterprise decision makers and independent software and
professional service partners want for building long-term information
infrastructures.
The company’s market share increased to 52% for the following reasons:
1. HP successfully delivered its 64-bit PA-8000 processor. The results of
TPC- C benchmarks indicated that this is by far the most powerful single-
processor OLTP engine in the industry;
2. Many IS decision makers have narrowed their long-term make-it-and-
forget-it server supplier choice down to HP and IBM. HP’s HP 9000
product line has demonstrated in users’ real-world production application
benchmarks greater high-end scalability than IBM’s RS/6000;
3. HP has focused its development efforts on increasing the reliability,
serviceability, availability, and manageability features of HP-UX and the
ability to integrate HP-UX with other operating environments. As a result,
IS managers that use HP for datacenter-level applications ﬁnd that
compared to alternative platforms they would rather acquire more HP
servers. The net — HP’s large 1995 market share fueled even greater
market share in 1996;
4. IS planners have seen continued protection of their past investments with
HP. Note the smooth upgrade in 1996 from HP-UX version 9 to HP-UX
version 10 and the ability to perform an in-box upgrade to the PA-8000
processor. And HP very much seems to be on track to migrate its
customers to the 64-bit HP-UX version 11 operating environment in 1997
and Intel IA-64 (Merced) servers in 1999.
The multiuser Risc/Unix market segments where HP did not compete as well in
1996 were for LAN consolidation (the IBM RS/6000 SP got top honors here)
and as a platform for a new generation of web-based technologies and
applications. In the LAN consolidation area, HP introduced its EPS (Enterprise
Parallel Server) with minimal fanfare but excellent customer reviews. And
where HP has marketed its leadership role in security as the basis for its web
offerings, Sun has better met the needs of web maestros with Java and low
prices.
IBM: Professional Services Pulling Through
RS/6000 Servers
IBM’s 20% growth in the RS/6000 line has come primarily from the acceptance
of the RS/6000 SP (formerly the SP2) — IBM’s massively parallel server — as
a LAN consolidation platform. The RS/6000 SP has become a favorite of many
datacenter managers for lowering server- support costs, especially since the
introduction of SMP nodes in mid-1996.
As it has been for too many years, IBM’s challenge lies in the core PowerPC
technology. The processor simply has not provided the performance and
scalability for high-end OLTP applications that larger enterprises require. While

AIX is a preferred Unix-derivative operating system for commercial
applications by many IS executives, IBM’s continued growth in the Risc/Unix
market is dependent upon delivering the production-quality platforms users
need for very high-end applications. This continued to be a stumbling block in
1996. IBM anticipates rectifying this situation in 1997 with the introduction of
its 64-bit PowerPC 620 processor in the RS/6000.
Very signiﬁcantly, IBM customers more-and-more seek comprehensive
computing solutions where professional services capabilities are paramount to
meet well-deﬁned functional objectives. IBM has identiﬁed 12 vertical
industries and 2 cross-functional areas for which it will replicate over 140
functional solutions for customers around the world — and many of these
require the use of Risc/Unix servers. Where the RS/6000 ﬁts the customer’s
requirements, IBM offers excellent pricing incentives. As a result, the general
purpose RS/6000 held its own for end-to-end IBM supplied solutions.
However, Aberdeen continues to ﬁnd a number of situations where the
customer contracted with IBM’s Global Services for systems integration
services but speciﬁed Risc/Unix servers from other suppliers.
Sun Microsystems: Champion of Unix For the
Internet
In 1996, Sun dramatically increased its efforts in the commercial, multiuser
Risc/Unix area — and the positive result was a 73% growth rate, higher than
any other supplier. Pioneering work in inventing and delivering web
technologies, such as Java, has paid off handsomely. Sun has established itself,
especially with the market acceptance of its Netra internet-ready Risc/Unix
server product line, as the Unix internet/intranet platform-of-choice in the
minds of most enterprise IS planners. This positioning has also paid off well in
the hot ISP (internet service provider) market.
Sun successfully made the transition to much higher performance 64-bit
UltraSparc processors in Ultra Enterprise servers in 1996. In addition, Sun is a
master at SMP (symmetric multi-processing) technology for effectively scaling
its servers to provide high-end production application support. This, combined
with the wide-spread use of Sun workstations for developing Unix applications,
increased Sun’s sales in traditional production OLTP environments. And with
Sun’s historically low prices, this has proven to be a winning story for value-
added resellers who can cost-effectively customize packaged applications for
user organizations.
The challenge Sun faces in 1997 is NT everywhere. Developers are using NT
for application development — web masters are ﬁnding Intel/NT servers
provide sufﬁcient performance and functionality for internet/intranet data
access applications. But Sun has a history of overcoming what on the surface
appear to be insurmountable obstacles.
The Fortunes of Signiﬁcant Others
In addition to the Big 3, there are numerous additional suppliers with unique
capabilities that contributed to the success of the 1996 Risc/Unix marketplace.
In order of signiﬁcance:
Siemens-Nixdorf/Pyramid: 1996 was a relatively disappointing year for SNI.
The Pyramid acquisition was intended to lay the groundwork for a sweep into
the U.S. marketplace with excellent products and Siemens’ ﬁnancial muscle.
But little was heard from SNI except for re-organizations. In addition, the core
MIPS R4400-processor technology, which SNI acquires from Silicon Graphics,
fell behind the performance leaders. SNI’s strength seems to continue to lie in
selling to internal Siemens’ divisions, German companies, and organizations,
often government, that need extensive systems integration assistance. Siemens’
Risc/Unix growth rate appears to be signiﬁcantly less than the market’s and the
company lost one market share point.
Digital Equipment Corporation: The breakneck growth of 1995 slowed down
signiﬁcantly in 1996 as the ﬁeld sales and marketing organization, especially in
Europe, could not keep up with the robustness of Digital’s Alpha-based product
line. Digital’s Unix/Risc offerings are really intended to play at the enterprise
level — but the sales organization had been structured to sell through indirect
distribution channels. In the middle of 1996, Digital made major organizational
changes to rectify this disconnect — but the damage was done. The net effect
over the 12 months in 1996 was that Digital’s growth rate approximated the
market’s and market share remained the same.
Bull: Continued commitment to core PowerPC and AIX components combined
with innovative SMP and application-integration technologies and
comprehensive systems integration services allowed Bull to grow faster than
the European market in general.
Data General: Planning for the intermediate term, Data General moved its
Unix platforms from the Motorola 8800 Risc processor to Intel PentiumPro
technology. As a result, in 1996 it lost signiﬁcant share in the Risc/Unix market

place. However, when Intel’s IA-64 Risc processor replaces PentiumPro, DG
will be back with its very robust Unix operating environment.
Tandem: This company remains the leader for users that need the highest levels
of uptime for their Unix servers. Tandem’s key Risc/Unix customers are
telecommunications providers.
ICL/Fujitsu: This Euro-Japanese company continues to move away from
Risc/Unix servers to Intel/NT. It never developed a major market share in
Risc/Unix with its strategy of OEM-ing technology from Sun. Its most
signiﬁcant Risc/Unix products are in the decision support arena.
Silicon Graphics: Capitalizing upon its strength in high-performance graphics,
SGI has positioned its server line for decision support applications that require
visual ﬂy-through capabilities and graphics-data access from the internet.
Hitachi: While a successful reseller of Hewlett-Packard HP 9000 systems in
Japan, Hitachi has introduced a line of IBM RS/6000-compatible Risc/Unix
systems — including an SP look-a-like. Hitachi, as a new entrant to this
marketplace, hopes to distinguish itself from IBM and within the marketplace
by providing what it considers to be superior systems software for robust,
production-quality commercial applications.
Other Players: Amdahl, Stratus, Motorola, and other suppliers focusing on
specialized niches — by functional area, industry, distribution channel, or
geography — offer Risc/Unix platforms. These ﬁrms have identiﬁed speciﬁc
market requirements, addressed their customers’ priorities, and continue to
support their base’s ongoing needs. However, they have not become a major
factor in the eyes of the general market or independent software community.
Intel/Unix Goes To Data Warehousing
And whatever happened to Intel/Unix servers? Aberdeen’s research shows the
Intel/Unix leaders — NCR, Sequent, DG, and ICL — are ﬁnding their greatest
successes in providing lower-priced-than-Risc data warehousing and decision
support servers. But their vulnerability is foreshadowed by losing small-to-
medium sized enterprise accounts that are now acquiring Intel/NT servers for
OLTP applications.
The Whole World Will Be Going Risc In 1998
Looking into the future, Aberdeen sees the server world dividing into two
distinct camps starting in mid-1998. While today the popular debate is Intel/NT
versus Risc/Unix for application operating environments, Intel’s projected
introduction of the IA-64 software development environment in mid- 1997
prior to the release of systems by its OEMs — which includes most of the
world’s major computer suppliers with the exception of Sun and Silicon
Graphics — in mid-1998 will change the platform landscape. Remember, the
IA-64 will be a Risc processor (or as some are describing it, a post-Risc
processor) — and the Intel environment (both NT and Unix), currently CISC-
based, will gain the performance advantages of Risc hardware and compiler
technology.
Aberdeen believes that IS planners will be selecting 64-bit Unix/IA-64-based
servers for production enterprise-level applications versus 32-bit NT/IA-64
servers for departmental and LAN applications. From the IS executive’s
perspective, the real choice will not be Risc versus CISC, or even Unix versus
NT, but more 64-bit for the high-end scalability that both enterprise-wide
client- server and network computing applications require versus 32-bit for
lower performance and cost workgroup applications.
And this implies that the Risc/Unix market will be even bigger in 1999 than it
is today as suppliers such as NCR, Sequent, ICL, Data General, Unisys — and
maybe even Compaq and other PC server suppliers that have experimented
with Unix-on-Intel — will be shipping Risc/Unix systems.
Risc/Unix Market Dynamics For 1997
Aberdeen believes that the major market force that will impact the Risc/Unix
market in 1997 will be the Year 2000 Factor.
The Year 2000 Factor has the potential of being a double-edged sword. On one
hand, some enterprise IS departments may stop implementing new applications
on Risc/Unix platforms and assign all available resources to repairing 2-digit
date code on existing systems. On the other hand, a large percentage may
decide that repairing legacy applications does not advance the enterprise’s core
IS capabilities and new applications should be implemented as soon as possible
on Risc/Unix platforms.
Aberdeen believes that by mid-1997 most IS departments will realize that due
to time constraints and risk factors, the preferred path will be implementing up-

to- date client-server applications, wherever possible, rather than taking the
repair route. New enterprise-wide applications will most likely be placed on
Risc/Unix while departmental will be on Intel/NT.
As discussed above, IS planners will begin evaluating the directions available
to them for 64-bit Unix processing in 1997. The suppliers that show
performance and industry leadership in this area will gain a major advantage
over those that do not.
Servers incorporating NUMA (non-uniform memory access) architectures will
be shipping in 1997 for some Risc/Unix platforms but mostly for Intel/Unix.
While the promise of greater multi-processor scalability has been made, early
results have been categorized as "Version 1" efforts that will be improved upon.
NUMA is a technology to watch and monitor — especially for data
warehousing applications — but probably will not make a major quantitative
impact in 1997.
New web-based applications, such as electronic commerce, net publishing, e-
mail, and collaborative computing, will continue to inject energy into the
Risc/Unix market. The advantages of web technologies are still in the early
stages of being understood and the appropriate systems implemented at the
enterprise level.
Aberdeen believes that as the software becomes more functional and scalable,
and IS professionals better understand how to use high-availability clusters for
NT, NT will capture more new sites at the expense of Unix. These sites will
tend to be at the workgroup and departmental level of large enterprises and the
headquarters’ level of small-to-medium sized organizations.
Conclusions
The Risc/Unix market is healthy and growing. As Mark Twain once said,
"Reports of my death are premature." But this market is making yet another
transformation. The ﬁrst transformation, in the late 1980’s/early 1990’s, was
from scientiﬁc/technical usage to commercial. It is now transforming to run-
the-large-enterprise with highly scalable ISV-supplied client-server
applications. And with the introduction of web-based technologies and new
RDBMS functionality, the high-end requirements for Risc/Unix platforms
continue to increase. And as enterprises face up to their very difﬁcult Year 2000
challenges, Risc/Unix servers are well- positioned to meet their requirements.
In 1996, the market consolidated with some of the smaller suppliers retreating
from Risc processors to focus more on Intel. The three major players, HP, IBM,
and Sun all experienced healthy growth as part of this consolidation.
With 52% market share in 1996, HP continues to set the market standard for
production Risc/Unix servers. IBM has found a strong position in the LAN
consolidation segment of the marketplace with the RS/6000 SP and Global
Services pulled through the general purpose models. And Sun has made great
progress in meeting the burgeoning needs of users for web-based technologies.
In conclusion, Aberdeen projects another over 20% growth year for the
Risc/Unix market in 1997 and even higher growth after systems suppliers
incorporate the Intel IA-64 in their Risc/Unix platforms.

### Abstract

Aberdeen's seventh annual survey of the commercial multiuser RISC/Unix marketplace documents 28% revenue growth to $18.2 billion in 1996. Hewlett-Packard achieved 52% market share through strong PA-8000 processor performance and customer loyalty. IBM held 18% with RS/6000 SP LAN-consolidation wins. Sun Microsystems grew 73% on Internet/web server demand and Java. The report forecasts continued RISC/Unix dominance for enterprise-level production applications through 1998-1999 while projecting Intel IA-64 as a shared future processor platform for both Unix and NT.

### Prescience Detail

**Correct Predictions:**
- Sun Microsystems' growing internet/web importance was correctly identified — Sun became dominant in internet server infrastructure before Oracle acquisition in 2010
- The NT-for-workgroups / Unix-for-enterprise bifurcation held true for roughly a decade (2000-2010)
- Year 2000 concerns driving new RISC/Unix deployments over mainframe repair proved accurate for many enterprises
- HP's leadership position was correctly assessed at the time of writing
- Web-based production applications exploding on RISC/Unix platforms proved accurate for the 1997-2002 period

**Incorrect or Partially Correct Predictions:**
- IA-64 (Itanium) systems were not available in mid-1998 as projected; first Itanium shipped in 2001
- Itanium never achieved the projected universal adoption; Intel x86-64 prevailed overwhelmingly
- The 1997 RISC/Unix market growth of >20% was likely not achieved due to Asian financial crisis and NT competition
- Data General did not return to RISC/Unix prominence with IA-64 — DG was acquired by EMC in 1999
- The prediction that the Unix/RISC workstation was to be replaced by NT proved correct faster than implied

**Long-Term Outcomes:**
- RISC/Unix market peaked approximately 2001-2003 and was steadily displaced by Linux on x86-64 servers
- HP-UX reached end of support December 31, 2025 — 28 years after this document
- Sun Microsystems was acquired by Oracle in January 2010
- Digital Equipment Corporation was acquired by Compaq in June 1998; Compaq absorbed by HP in 2002
- IBM RS/6000 evolved into IBM Power Systems and remains viable as of 2026
