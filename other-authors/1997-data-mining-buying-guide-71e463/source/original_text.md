Executive Summary
Data mining tools, in general, apply programmed formulae, or
algorithms, to large amounts of data in such a way that the
data reveal hidden patterns and relationships and uncover
correlations that were previously invisible to workers and the
business. Data mining tools, as a class, help the enterprise
understand customer behavior, predict events, and expose the
sometimes subtle linkages between events and trends. The list
of possibilities goes on. This knowledge could help devise
proﬁtable programs to retain and maximize the value of
current customers and to ﬁnd new customers.
But data mining is more about data preparation than the
sometimes wonderful possibilities of any given algorithm or
set of algorithms. To unleash the power of what Aberdeen calls
scalable, commercial-grade data mining tools, enterprises must
select, clean, and transform their data, perhaps integrate data
brought in from outside sources, and essentially establish a
recognizable environment for data mining algorithms. These
procedures typically demand - at least initially - the skills of
data mining experts. Moreover, all scalable, commercial-grade
data mining suppliers differ signiﬁcantly in the power of their
software and in the skills they can bring to an enterprise. By
misapplying a scalable, commercial-grade data mining tool, an
enterprise can squander potential and possibly millions of
dollars.
This buying guide examines the eight leading commercial-
grade data mining suppliers - ANGOSS International Ltd.,
Data Distilleries B.V., HyperParallel, Inc., IBM Corp.,
Magnify, Inc., NeoVista Software, Inc., Syllogic B.V., and
Thinking Machines Corp. As well as proﬁling each supplier
and its technologies and services, this guide compares and
ranks the suppliers according to a business-technology
framework.
Planning for Scalable, Commercial-Grade Data Mining
Data mining is a business issue; it should improve the bottom
line. A line of business, enlisting the support of information
technologists, should take the leadership role in a data mining
project, and should establish a cross-functional team to
manage the data mining process. By following the steps
discussed throughout this guide, this team will be able to
successfully initiate and justify a scalable, commercial-grade
data mining project.
The Key Technologies for Scalable, Commercial-Grade
Data Mining
The basic functions data mining algorithms perform are
prediction, clustering, and afﬁnity. Different types of
algorithms, including decision trees, neural networks, and
association rules, help perform these functions.
By parallelizing their software, suppliers of commercial-grade
data mining harness the ability of multiple CPUs to scale up
and speedup to supply the critical power to execute a compute-
intensive data mining analysis against huge databases.
However, the value of algorithms and of parallel processing all
depend upon getting the data in shape for data mining.
Approximately 75% of the work in data mining is in getting
the data to the point where data mining tools can actually start
running. A line of business must have a understanding of what
data preparations are necessary for a data mining analysis.

Vertical Focus
The vertical-industry focus of a data mining supplier is a key
selection criterion. A supplier that serves the line of business'
industry may have either built an application template or may
be able to supply business-domain knowledge that can reduce
overall costs or help pinpoint a big-payback application. If a
data mining supplier does not have experience in a speciﬁc
industry, the supplier may not be able to give the line of
business the support and beneﬁt of experience it needs. The
four industries analyzed include ﬁnancial services, retail,
telecommunications, and other vertical industries in which one
or more have a demonstrated strength.
Platforms
The server platforms on which a data mining toolset runs is a
second criterion. A data mining toolset, if possible, should run
on the customer's existing server platform. Otherwise, the
enterprise must assimilate the burden of a new platform,
including hardware costs and the cost of acquiring new skills.
Algorithms
The algorithms in a data mining toolset can have both breadth
and depth. Breadth is the ability of a data mining toolset to
perform more than one of the three basic data mining
functions: prediction, clustering, and afﬁnity. A toolset has
depth if it has more than one algorithm targeted at the same
function, say, clustering. Depth gives the data mining user
ﬂexibility.
Qualiﬁers
The fourth criterion consists of ﬁve additional qualiﬁers: life-
cycle support, architecture, ease of use, professional services,
strength of company.
Life-cycle support measures how well the data mining tools
help in all the steps of the data mining life cycle
Architecture determines the ability of the toolset to quickly
adapt to changes and to integrate with production systems; it
also inﬂuences how quickly the system accesses databases and
how adaptable the system is to speciﬁc industries.
Ease of use measures the ease or difﬁculty of using the toolset.
Professional services are a measure of the assistance that a
supplier can provide to the data mining project.
Strength of company measures the ﬁnancial strength of a data
mining supplier.
Pricing
Pricing is the ﬁfth criterion. Although the price of a data
mining toolset is only one component of the overall cost,
enterprises and lines of business need to understand the

Conclusions: The State of Data Mining
Enterprises and lines of business need to extract additional
value from their large and growing customer and product
databases. This value can take the form of increased revenues
or decreased costs, which business can derive from programs
that stimulate customers to buy more, to buy for the ﬁrst time,
or to stay loyal.
Data mining offers technology to systematize the excavation
of the value from databases. However, because of several
signiﬁcant differences in the types of tools, levels of expertise,
and ﬁnancial health among the suppliers, enterprises and lines
of business will need to carefully evaluate prospective data
mining suppliers and partners.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group's Enterprise Data Mining Buying Guide (1997) profiles eight commercial-grade data mining vendors: ANGOSS International, Data Distilleries, HyperParallel, IBM, Magnify, NeoVista Software, Syllogic, and Thinking Machines. Uses a five-dimension evaluation framework: vertical focus, platforms, algorithms, qualifiers (lifecycle support, architecture, ease of use, professional services, company strength), and pricing. Key finding: approximately 75% of data mining work involves data preparation; significant differences in vendor tool types, expertise levels, and financial health require careful evaluation.

### Prescience Detail

**Correct Predictions:**
- Data mining became the enterprise mainstream as systematically predicted; evolved into ML/AI
- Decision trees, neural networks, and association rules are still core ML algorithms today
- The 75% data preparation estimate remains accurate (called feature engineering now)
- Financial services, retail, and telecom vertical applications all proved to be major ML domains
- The customer behavior, churn prediction, and market basket analysis use cases proved extremely valuable

**Incorrect or Partially Correct Predictions:**
- Most specific vendors did not survive independently: HyperParallel (acquired by Yahoo 1996, before guide even published), Thinking Machines (bankrupt 1994), NeoVista (Accrue→JDA), Data Distilleries (SPSS→IBM)
- The significant differences in vendor viability were accurate — the market consolidated dramatically
- Parallel processing for DM proved correct conceptually but the specific platforms changed (MMP → distributed computing → GPU clusters)

**Long-Term Outcomes:**
- HyperParallel: acquired by Yahoo in 1996 (before the guide was published)
- Thinking Machines: filed Chapter 11 in August 1994 (before the guide); assets eventually sold
- NeoVista Software: acquired by Accrue Software 1999; products sold to JDA Software 2001
- Data Distilleries: acquired by SPSS 2000; SPSS acquired by IBM for $1.2B in 2009
- ANGOSS International: acquired by Peterson Partners 2013 ($8.4M); Datawatch acquired 2018 ($24.5M); now Altair product
- The data mining/ML market grew to $300B+ by the 2020s with major players being AWS, Google, Microsoft, and Nvidia
