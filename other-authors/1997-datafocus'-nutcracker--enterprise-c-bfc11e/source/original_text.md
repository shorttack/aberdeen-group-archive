DataFocus’ NuTCRACKER: Enterprise-Class

An Executive White Paper
September 1997
Executive Summary
Information Systems (IS) decision makers are using DataFocus’
NuTCRACKER in order to move core Unix-based applications to Microsoft’s
Windows NT for both workstation and server application deployments. And,
using DataFocus’ DirectLink™ technology, NuTCRACKER users are moving
Unix/Relational Database Management Systems (RDBMS) applications to NT.
IS decision makers looking to move their enterprise-class Unix applications to
NT have a few choices, including:
Starting over by rewriting Unix applications natively in Win32;
Moving Unix applications to NT using POSIX-only porting tools; or
Using NuTCRACKER to move, integrate and evolve applications on NT.
Users tell Aberdeen that rewriting applications in Win32 is too costly, lengthy
and risky and that POSIX-subsystem migration tools cannot integrate critical
applications (Oracle, Informix, Sybase, et al) or mainstream commercial
Microsoft Windows NT applications.
According to veterans, the only realistic option for IS decision makers charged
with moving enterprise-class Unix applications to NT/Intel platforms is
DataFocus’ NuTCRACKER family of products. Providing complete portability
across a wide range of enterprise-class applications from multiple ﬂavors of
Unix to Windows NT and Windows 95, NuTCRACKER is enabling IS
managers to deploy enterprise-class Unix applications on Microsoft computing
environments today.
Application programmers will tell you that DataFocus’ NuTCRACKER makes
it possible to rapidly move critical enterprise applications to NT. And, IS
decision makers will say that NuTCRACKER saved projects and careers.
Moving Unix Applications to NT
DataFocus’ users are moving critical enterprise-class Unix applications from
Sun Microsystems, Hewlett Packard, IBM, Digital, Silicon Graphics, Sequent,
NCR and Data General platforms to NT/Intel systems. Users are replacing
older legacy Unix/Risc systems for newer and less expensive NT/Intel
workstations and servers. IS decision makers tell Aberdeen that NT is a good
platform for remote sites, departmental LANs and for low-cost back-end
applications.
Unix applications successfully being re-deployed on NT with NuTCRACKER
include technical workstation applications, numerically-intensive applications
operating on servers and low to mid-range Unix/RDBMS solutions. And now,
with DataFocus’ DirectLink technology supporting Unix-based libraries for
Oracle, Informix, Sybase, Tuxedo, et cetera (see Appendix), enterprise-class
database applications are primed for redeployment on lower-cost NT-based
platforms.
Unix applications being re-deployed on NT are the result of continued upgrade
and modiﬁcation made over years of use. Ranging in size from 300,000 to more
that 16 million lines of code, IS decision makers say these Unix applications
are currently being relied upon for critical enterprise operations. And, typical of
enterprise-class applications that are upgraded over many years, IS managers
say the now complex and messy source code of these Unix applications ranges
from C and C++ to Fortran and other languages.
According to its users, NuTCRACKER is making it possible to smoothly
migrate critical Unix applications onto NT workstations and servers within a
day or two. Aberdeen has found many custom-designed enterprise-scale
applications that were originally deployed on Unix, now successfully operating
on NT. Moreover, users are currently extending these former Unix applications
by seamlessly integrating NT-based applications and utilities through
NuTCRACKER’s support for NT’s native Win32 Application Programming
Interfaces (APIs) and libraries.
Why Unix-Applications are Moving to NT

The reasons commonly cited by IS decision makers for re-deploying Unix
applications on NT include competitive market-pressure, lowered costs for
deploying applications on NT-based platforms and the ability to integrate off-
the-shelf packaged applications for NT with the core applications the enterprise
depends on.
Competitive Market Pressure
The must-be-on-NT virus has struck users and suppliers alike. Decision makers
say they are forced to ship applications on NT before competitors.
NuTCRACKER users are experiencing obvious ﬁnancial beneﬁt by deploying
the same applications on less expensive NT deployment platforms. All users are
citing lowered cost as a major factor affecting their decision to replace tired and
maintenance-hungry legacy Unix platforms with fast and inexpensive NT/Intel
platforms.
Lower Deployment Costs and Rapid Cost-Recovery
Users tell Aberdeen that the cost for porting, testing and qualifying a 1 million
line Unix application on NT using NuTCRACKER is less than three months
elapsed time. At normal burden rates in the U.S., this ranges from $75,000 to
$110,000. Thus, cost-recovery for entry-level workstations occurs after the 8th
to 11th platform is replaced. However, users of high-end Unix workstations
ranging in price from $80,000 to $150,000 are experiencing cost-recovery after
the 1st platform is replaced.
Users say that NT-based servers are $40,000 to $200,000 less expensive to
deploy than corresponding Unix servers. Users are telling Aberdeen that cost-
recovery for server deployments is occuring after the 1st, 2nd or 3rd NT server
is put in service, depending on the difference in cost between Unix and NT
platforms.

POSIX and Windows NT: DO NOT MIX
Users and ISVs say they consider using the POSIX subsystem built into NT.
But, experienced users recount that employing NT’s POSIX subsystem results
in:
A costly effort to modify Unix application source code to achieve
compliance with POSIX before porting to NT;
Applications that are isolated from NT’s Win32; and
An expensive effort to maintain the POSIX source code base.
Windows NT employs a modern client-server architecture (see Figure 2)
providing a low-level protected kernel services and three protected operating
environment subsystems; Win32, POSIX and OS/2.
Each of the protected operating environments supported by NT provides the
APIs and facilities normally used for its own implementation of ﬁle systems,
memory management, process management and inter-process communication
services that its applications depend upon. In theory, POSIX applications are
provided the operating environment services that POSIX applications expect.
But, NT requires that applications operate only under the control of one
subsystem. As a result, application developers are prevented from mixing and
matching the multiple API functions and routines from one operating
environment to another on NT. Thus, NT actually prevents application
developers from integrating Win32 functions into a POSIX subsystem-based
application.
As a result, applications ported to NT’s POSIX subsystem-based operating
environment remain forever isolated from Win32, without access to the legion
of software available for NT and without an easy upgrade path to the next
release of NT.
This problem is solved by operating applications within Win32, Microsoft’s
favored API for Windows NT. And, because DataFocus’ NuTCRACKER is
integrated with Win32, only DataFocus can provide users the ability to

integrate Win32-based tools, utilities, operating environment features and 3rd
party software with enterprise-class Unix applications for NT.

Application programmers do not write programs that comply with POSIX.
Unix application developers do not write POSIX applications, they write
applications for Solaris, HP-UX, AIX or other Unix operating environments.

A Problem NuTCRACKER Does Not Solve
While NuTCRACKER is lauded by its veteran users, there is a problem it
cannot solve, namely the conceptual misunderstanding that Unix applications
can be moved to NT without porting. Less experienced users are seduced into
believing it is possible to move Unix applications to NT through a POSIX-
ﬁlter, eliminating the need for porting and integrating application source code.
According to these users, applications are somehow magically transported to
NT without porting by pressing the POSIX-button or by simply turning a
POSIX-crank.

Unfortunately, hoping to move Unix-based applications to NT without porting
is synonymous with time-travel, a free lunch and similar otherworldly beliefs.
It is impossible to move Unix applications to NT without porting. Worse,
relying only on NT’s POSIX-subsystem for migrating Unix applications to NT
will only result in applications that cannot be extended and upgraded after the
POSIX-port is completed and the applications are operating on NT.

### Prescience Detail

**Correct Predictions:**
- Unix RISC workstations were substantially displaced by x86 platforms by the mid-2000s
- The economic case for NT over RISC/Unix servers was validated over the following decade
- The cost differential between Unix servers and NT servers proved real and important
- Win32 programmers being hard to find and expensive proved accurate
- The POSIX subsystem approach being a dead end proved correct (WSL2 replaced it)

**Incorrect or Partially Correct Predictions:**
- The statement "the Unix/RISC workstation is dead" was too early and too definitive — it took 5-10 more years
- Linux on x86 was equally or more important than Windows NT in displacing Unix RISC servers
- NuTCRACKER's long-term relevance was limited; containerization (Docker 2013) and cloud migration made porting tools less necessary
- The NT server as primary replacement for Unix proved partially correct; Linux servers grew significantly

**Long-Term Outcomes:**
- DataFocus acquired by Mortice Kern Systems (MKS) in February 1999
- MKS acquired by PTC (Parametric Technology Corporation) in May 2011
- Windows Subsystem for Linux (WSL) released 2016 makes Unix-on-Windows trivial — eliminates the porting problem entirely
- Docker containers (2013) and cloud-native development made the entire porting paradigm less relevant
- Intel Pentium/x86 architecture ultimately won; but Linux, not Windows NT, became the dominant server OS
