### Preface

Information Systems (IS) executives are telling Aberdeen that their number one target for reducing PC cost-of-ownership is desktop management. These executives are well aware that the cost to manage a PC desktop far outweighs the price of the PC platform itself — many are reporting that the average cost to manage a single, networked PC is now double to triple the PC's original purchase price.

To date, IS managers have been forced to build their PC desktop management strategies around a series of proprietary management frameworks and third-party point products. To many, this approach has proven to be not only expensive and complex, but also marginally effective as evidenced by both the high support cost per PC and inability to meet users' service level requirements.

Hewlett-Packard (HP) has made a strategic commitment to use the Desktop Management Interface (DMI) to meet IS executives' needs for an open, industry-standard approach to addressing desktop management problems. As it brings to market in March 1997 a suite of advanced desktop and systems management products and capabilities, HP will be setting itself up to go head-to-head with Compaq in the race to be #1 in enterprise PC shipments.

### Executive Summary

IS executives know that they have problems in managing their enterprise's desk-top PCs. And when IS decision makers have problems, successful suppliers work toward creating a solution. In the area of desktop management, the leading suppliers founded the Desktop Management Task Force (DMTF) as the body to create open, industry standards for desktop interfaces. DMTF has created the DMI (Desktop Management Interface) standard which hardware and software suppliers are encouraged to incorporate into their products.

HP, well known for its support of open systems and standards, has aggressively invested in making its Vectra Corporate PC products DMI-compliant and building software tools that can manage any DMI-compliant PC. The software glue between the DMI-compliant hardware and DMI-compliant management software is HP Top-TOOLS. TopTOOLS has been incorporated with all Vectra Corporate PCs, allowing IS administrators to view and manage over 350 DMI attributes. And because Top-TOOLS is DMI-compliant, it can manage other DMI-based PCs from manufacturers beside HP. In addition, TopTOOLS can be integrated with HP's OpenView management environment, providing a complete network and system view of the entire information infrastructure.

From a strategic perspective, HP will be positioning a battery of systems, desktop and network management products such as HP OpenView, TopTOOLS, numerous other desktop management applications and services, and its business partners' DMI-compliant software to compete directly with Compaq's Intelligent Manageability systems management environment. Since Intelligent Manageability only runs on Compaq products, and because independent software management vendors must be "invited" into Compaq's Desktop Management Solutions Partners Program to obtain the technical assistance required to work with Intelligent Manageability — Intelligent Manageability is, to many IS decision makers, tantamount to a proprietary solution. HP believes that its open approach to desktop management combined with an excellent support reputation and continuous price reductions will give it the opening to surpass Compaq in selling PCs to enterprise users.

HP's strategic intent is to become a major force in PC management cost reduction. Using highly manageable Vectra PCs, solid management tools like TopTOOLS and OpenView, and by providing the related service and support elements, HP expects to reduce desktop management costs by at least 20-25%, or approximately $3,000 per PC — in the very near term. Aberdeen has even higher cost reduction expectations — the combination of HP management products and services with best practice management policies practiced in end-user environments should lead to even greater cost savings — potentially reducing the total cost of PC ownership by 50% or more. While actual PC support costs and potential cost savings will vary by enterprise, HP is very persuasively making the argument that OpenView, Top-TOOLS, and its complementary DMI-compatible products offer greater desktop management functionality — and greater potential for cost-of-ownership savings — than can be found from any supplier's product set.

The HP Vectra Corporate PC and TopTOOLS will certainly gain industry attention for bringing industry standards to desktop management. PC suppliers will need to either open their systems up to DMI-compatible interfaces quickly — thus joining forces with HP — or continue on a proprietary desktop management path, and hope that their customers feel comfortable with assembling their own desktop management systems from the best-of-class products that are available in the marketplace today.

### Hewlett-Packard's DMI Products

Hewlett-Packard was the first major supplier to introduce DMI-compliant PCs — HP started to instrument its Vectra Corporate PC line with DMI attributes in 1992. As a result, customers who have implemented post-1992 Vectra PCs can take advantage of DMI to manage their existing investment in PC platforms — further extending the useful life of existing PC platforms.

From a desktop management perspective, HP has made two major announcements with respect to the Desktop Management Interface. First, HP has added additional DMI attributes and extensions to its Vectra PC line — making the Vectra Corporate PC quite possibly the most manageable PC in the industry. And second, HP has released Version 2 of its powerful DMI-enabled TopTOOLS PC management application — enabling IS administrators to use TopTOOLS in a local and remote mode to configure and set DMI attributes for desktop environments; to monitor and interpret important DMI desktop management data.

### Vectra Corporate PCs — DMI Enhancements and Extensions

Thanks to reductions in PC CPU and memory pricing, the purchase price of Vectra PC platforms declined significantly in 1996. And, while HP continues to reduce PC hardware costs, HP has also initiated a strong DMI initiative in an effort to reduce the total cost-of-PC-ownership from a desktop management perspective.

To improve PC manageability, HP has added close to three-hundred DMI manageable attributes to its Vectra PC line — and has added sixty-five unique HP DMI attribute extensions (for instance, customizable asset strings) — greatly extending the range of manageability of Vectra PC systems.

DMI attributes found on HP Vectra Corporate PC series systems enable authorized IS managers to: 1) identify desktops at the component and serial number level, 2) gain access to user information, 3) view configuration settings, and 4) examine the state of various system components such as memory or CPU. With a DMI-based management package, systems managers and administrators can gain access to previously difficult-to-obtain information such as:

- Component Identification — data regarding system manufacturer, product, version, serial number and information data. HP has instrumented the motherboard, the hard disk drive, the CD ROM, the floppy drive, the Network Interface Card (NIC) card, the Monitor, and the BIOS;
- General Information — data regarding system name, location, primary user name, primary user phone, system boot-up time and date/time;
- Operating System — information about the operating system's index, name, version, type, boot device storage type, boot device and partition indices, and operating system description;
- System BIOS — data regarding the BIOS index, manufacturer, version, ROM size, starting and ending address, loader version, release date, and primary BIOS;
- Processor — information regarding the processor index, type, family, version, speed, and group indices; and,
- Memory — information regarding both logical and physical memory including memory size, location, address, usage, capacity, and extended and expanded memory characteristics.

Worthy of note, although not directly related to DMI compliance, HP Vectras can be powered-on remotely using HP's NightDIRECTOR. NightDIRECTOR allows systems administrators to wake-up a powered-off or suspended-mode PC for maintenance or troubleshooting purposes. The core of NightDIRECTOR is Magic Packet technology, a remote power-on standard jointly developed by Advanced Micro Devices (AMD) and Hewlett-Packard. With this feature, IS administrators and support personnel can power-on and manage all network-ready Vectra, VL, VA, XM, and XA Corporate PCs — enabling IS personnel to perform PC maintenance and updates at convenient times without having to disturb busy end users.

### HP-specific DMI Extensions

In addition to the standard DMI instrumentation, HP has also developed sixty-five unique DMI-compliant extensions — making Vectra PCs more manageable and helping to improve IS manager's and administrator's efficiency. Since all of these extensions fully comply with the DMI specification, all DMI-compliant management applications can collect and edit these attributes. HP has especially enhanced the following aspects of desktop management:

- Inventory control — providing customizable asset strings of up to 80 characters as well as additional information regarding the configuration status of serial and parallel ports, and floppy and hard disk drives;
- Remote control to lock system — allowing IS administrators to selectively lock the entire system, mouse, keyboard, and power-on and reset buttons, in order to prevent end user disruptions during remote configuration procedures;
- Remote configuration — allowing the reboot and power-off of the PC from a central management console — thereby facilitating remote BIOS upgrades;
- Password and password control group — allowing for C2-level password protection;
- Advanced security — helps to prevent unauthorized data transfer through floppy disk drive controls; hard drive controls; and system port controls;
- Health Monitoring and Alerting — generating DMI alerts by monitoring hard drive information using SMART (self-monitoring and reporting technology), and POST (power-on self test) errors.

### HP's TopTOOLS Management Application

TopTOOLS is an application that allows for local and remote management of personal computers. At the local level, TopTOOLS can be used to configure and customize various attributes of a PC for DMI management. At the remote level, TopTOOLS allows IS support personnel to monitor and control PCs from a central location.

TopTOOLS includes additional management tools such as AssetTOOLS (a complete inventory management system); ConfigTOOLS (a suite of configuration and troubleshooting tools); FlashTOOL (a tool for remotely upgrading the PC BIOS on multiple systems simultaneously); SafeTOOLS (PC health monitoring and fault protection); and ProtectTOOLS (a PC security schema).

TopTOOLS can also be configured to work cooperatively within HP's OpenView Professional Suite (for managing small-to-medium sized companies having networks of 10-2,000 nodes) or with HP OpenView Network Node Manager for Windows NT (supports LANs and WANs with more than 250 nodes up to extra large enterprise networks in medium to large companies).

### Reducing PC Cost-of-Ownership Through Better PC Management

IS managers and administrators tell Aberdeen that today's desktop management packages are often incompatible with other management programs within the same information systems architecture; and that many desktop management tools are highly manual when it comes to data gathering, analysis, and distribution.

To reduce PC management costs, many IS managers believe that desktop management products need to offer:
1. Better integration of data between management environments to ease management complexity.
2. Better automation of manual tasks like software distribution and installation, or asset management.
3. Better PC manageability — especially better remote PC configuration (BIOS and driver update, power-on through the network, system lock) and better reporting at the PC platform level.

### The Importance of DMI

The Desktop Management Interface is the result of desktop management standardization efforts performed by the Desktop Management Task Force — a powerful consortium of the industry's most influential suppliers of PC hardware and software management products. The DMTF steering committee reads like a who's-who in PC computing — its leadership includes Compaq, Dell, Digital, Hewlett-Packard, IBM, Intel, Microsoft, NEC, Novell, SCO, Sun (SunSoft), and Symantec — with more than one hundred and twenty-two other PC hardware and software suppliers participating as general members.

The goal of the DMTF has been to develop and deliver a set of standards that make the management of PCs less complex, thereby leading to the reduction of total cost-of-ownership for PC desktop platforms. This framework provides a protocol- and operating system-independent data structure that enables the sharing and exchange of management data between disparate PC management programs.

Yet, the creation of a set of standards is not enough to ensure that desktop management packages can interoperate — compliance testing and certification are important and necessary to successfully establish DMI as an industry standard. In a yearly event, the DMTF sponsors a DMI compliance bake-off, gathering suppliers of DMI technologies at a common location at which DMI compliance is certified.

PC platform suppliers view DMI certification as a crucial product differentiator — by acquiring DMI certification suppliers can make a logical case that their total cost-of-owner-ship will be less than the cost of non-certified PC platforms — resulting in a comparative price advantage. Aberdeen expects that DMI certification will become an increasingly important basis for competitive differentiation between desktop platform suppliers in 1997.

### Food For Thought

From an industry-wide perspective, numerous suppliers have implemented a broad range of PC DMI attributes — but few offer the expansiveness of features that HP now offers on its Vectra Corporate PC platform. Most notably, Hewlett-Packard is the only PC supplier that can remotely upgrade multiple PC's Binary Input/Output Systems (BIOSs) simultaneously.

### Summary Observations

IS decision makers, faced with the fiscal responsibility for lowering PC computing costs, have long awaited the arrival of management standards for their PC desktop environments in order to reduce the total cost-of-desktop-ownership. These IS managers believe that the presentation of desktop management data in a common format will lead to the creation of less complex, more intuitive management software — and that simple, but more effective management software will help to reduce the overall cost of PC desktop ownership. And these IS managers believe that the Desktop Management Interface (DMI) represents that common format.

Dozens of suppliers — including all of the PC market's heavy-hitters — have committed to build and deploy DMI-based platforms and management packages. And with this level of commitment, DMI is clearly destined to become a de facto industry standard for desktop management. In 1997, Aberdeen expects systems suppliers to use the strength of their DMI-based desktop management environment (both software and hardware) as a major differentiator in corporate computing environments in the very near term. IS buyers have been anxiously awaiting the availability of DMI-based products and have already made DMI adherence a checklist item. Failure to implement means "no sale" — meanwhile, depth of implementation will win the sale.

Early on, Hewlett-Packard recognized the importance of DMI adherence as a contributing factor to the reduction of desktop management complexity and related systems management costs — and designed DMI-based products specifically aimed at reducing total cost-of-PC-ownership. HP has been especially aggressive in leading the DMI compliance charge — delivering DMI-compliant TopTOOLS and OpenView management applications, as well as adding more than 350 DMI attributes to its Vectra Corporate PC platform line — in order to simplify desktop management.

Aberdeen expects that IS decision makers who chose to embrace HP DMI-based management solutions will be well positioned to dramatically reduce their overall PC cost-of-ownership.

### Prescience Detail

**Key Prediction 1:** DMI will become a de facto industry standard for desktop management.  
**Outcome:** Partially correct. DMI did achieve widespread adoption in the late 1990s, but was superseded by CIM and WMI. DMI reached end-of-life March 31, 2005 per DMTF (https://www.dmtf.org/content/dmtf-announces-end-life-dmi). Microsoft's WMI (shipped with Windows 2000) became the dominant enterprise desktop management API.

**Key Prediction 2:** HP's DMI open approach will allow it to surpass Compaq in enterprise PC sales.  
**Outcome:** Incorrect in predicted form. HP did not overtake Compaq through the DMI competitive angle; instead, HP acquired Compaq in 2002 for $25 billion. The Vectra line was discontinued and replaced by the HP Evo (based on Compaq's platform).

**Key Prediction 3:** HP TopTOOLS + Vectra will reduce PC TCO by 50% or more.  
**Outcome:** Unverifiable at this specific level, but enterprise PC TCO did improve significantly through the late 1990s and 2000s through automation, standardization, and remote management tools — broadly aligned with Aberdeen's directional prediction.

**Key Prediction 4:** Magic Packet/NightDIRECTOR remote power-on will be valuable.  
**Outcome:** Correct. Magic Packet evolved into the Wake-on-LAN (WOL) standard, still widely supported.
