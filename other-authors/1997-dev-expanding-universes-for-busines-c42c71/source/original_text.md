### Preface

When making any business-critical technology purchase, line-of-business managers and IS executives must question whether the suppliers under scrutiny will be able to continually refine the adopted technologies to keep pace with the enterprise's emerging goals. Enterprises therefore typically assess four signs of supplier health in a given market: technological excellence, continued innovation, installed-base experience/satisfaction, and the supplier's financial performance.

This Strategy Profile examines Business Objects — the company, its tools, and its users — and finds that Business Objects continues to set standards for many would-be competitors while inspiring decision-maker confidence at the enterprise, line-of-business, and workgroup level. Factoring user response to Business Objects as a company, to its technologies and vision, and to its ability to execute its product transitions, Aberdeen concludes that Business Objects continues to demonstrate a well-established pattern of innovation and success.

### Executive Summary

Since its emergence in 1990 as a premier supplier of query and reporting software, Business Objects — both the company and its technologies — has become synonymous with the widespread trend of empowering business users to access information from corporate data stores. Among the first to deliver and prove the value of giving business users a query and reporting tool that hides the complexities of the Structured Query Language (SQL), the well-financed public company now has an installed base of 475,000 licenses, representing broad usage within 4,000 enterprises across more than 60 countries. The company's market-share leadership in query and reporting software drove revenue last year of $85.1 million. Moreover, that vote of market confidence, which is evident in both the size of Business Objects' installed base and in the number of critical data warehouse and third-party software suppliers that partner with the company, translates into ongoing business momentum and technical challenges reserved for elite top-tier software suppliers.

Rising to the challenge, Business Objects unveiled in March 1996 BusinessObjects 4.0. Underestimating the complexity of a complete architectural transition, the company had initial difficulty stabilizing the software and paid the price in a small ($300,000) third quarter 1996 loss. The company rebounded with fourth quarter 1996 profits of $1.8 million from revenues of $25.8 million.

User organizations with large BusinessObjects installations say the software is now stable and performing well. Many laud the company for its professional comportment during the transition and claim that the 32-bit BusinessObjects 4.0 was worth the wait.

Like its predecessor, BusinessObjects 4.0 uses a powerful metadata layer to relieve both IS personnel and business users from the complexities surrounding the use of SQL, the means for integrating and processing data in the rows and tables of a relational database. While storing the metadata in a relational format within a BusinessObjects optional server-side repository or on the client, BusinessObjects 4.0 arms administrators with a drag-and-drop tool that helps them quickly create the "Universes" that now encourage and support the any-direction, business-user analysis typically referred to as OLAP, or on-line analytical processing.

Although the 4.0 breakthroughs are numerous, Aberdeen believes that three — IS tools enhancements, ease of use for end users, and depth of analysis — are particularly noteworthy. The enhancements for IS recognize that extending business-user freedom to larger populations throughout the enterprise mandates increasing IS control in the areas of access and security, remote management, and resource consumption. BusinessObjects 4.0 now divides this task between a Designer Module and Supervisor Module.

Equally important, Business Objects integrates analysis capabilities into BusinessObjects 4.0, transforming the tool from its previous status as a powerful query and reporting tool into an integrated query/reporting/analysis facility.

In addition, BusinessObjects 4.0 significantly increases the types of analysis a user can perform. BusinessObjects users, for example, slice and dice data, or drill more deeply into it in search of trend-influencing factors — the types of analysis that had been previously only available in several high-end, and relatively expensive, multidimensional databases. BusinessObjects 4.0's powerful Microcube Engine combines a multidimensionally aware client-based SQL query generator and compact microcubes that are optimized for "on the fly" multidimensional reporting and analysis.

### Preparing Enterprises For The Future

This combination, Aberdeen believes, sets the stage for business users to intelligently engage with and disengage from BusinessObjects' server-side technologies and for the enterprise to engage its data without unduly draining network, PC, and database "bandwidth." This rational approach to information-access bandwidth is valid whether BusinessObjects is part of a more conventional client-server installation or an intranet. And while enterprises attest to a cost-saving value from initial, albeit scaled down, intranet usages, Business Objects is preparing for the 1997 release of an Internet product code-named Project Darwin — a product that reflects the evolution of BusinessObjects into an object-oriented, component-based toolset that is capable of delivering the client-server, interactive, power of BusinessObjects 4.0 on the WWW.

With this solid technology and business foundation, the company is proving equal to its leadership position by ushering in business user (as opposed to specialist) data mining software. Called BusinessMiner, this desktop software employs a sophisticated yet simple to use decision-tree technology.

Factoring BusinessObjects 4.0, the higher-order WWW capabilities soon to be delivered within Project Darwin, and the integration of the data mining tools, Aberdeen believes that Business Objects has positioned itself as a top-tier multi-product software company with solid financial performance, solid technologies, and a solid reputation — and thus as a solid supplier choice in a rapidly shifting decision-support marketplace.

### BusinessObjects 4.0

With the successful completion of BusinessObjects 4.0, which the company estimates as taking 150 "person years" of development and $15 million dollars of investment, the company has transformed BusinessObjects into an integrated query/reporting/analysis tool, at once completely rearchitecting (but leveraging) its predecessors and increasing its value with the addition of multidimensional features. BusinessObjects 4.0 consists of the following integrated components:

- **Reporter**, an end-user tool that helps business users to create ad hoc reports via drag-and-drop techniques, thus eliminating the need to understand SQL. The Reporter can combine data from multiple data sources as well as alert users to exception conditions;
- **Explorer**, which transparently builds compact desktop microcubes for users as they drill from summary to detail data, or in the reverse direction, and allows users from within a report to view data from numerous perspectives;
- **Document Agent Server**, a resource-saving third tier in the BusinessObject architecture that can be scheduled to automatically update and distribute reports and is effective in more conventional client-server architectures or for refreshing and distributing WWW pages;
- **Designer**, a graphical (IS) tool for building Universes, or the business representations of the database, includes routines that automatically check designs, and facilitates the use of performance-enhancing aggregate tables. Business Objects also provides Rapid Deployment Templates (RDTs) that speed up the building of Universes for popular business applications, for example, Oracle Financials, PeopleSoft and SAP; and
- **Supervisor**, a graphical administration tool for establishing repository user-access privileges (down to the row level) and for establishing the size of query-result sets and the amount of execution time permitted for individual users and workgroups.

### Multidimensional Metadata

The drag-and-drop metaphor, which simplifies business-user analysis, also helps IS personnel transform the SQL into terms, or Universes, that business users can understand. Unlike previous BusinessObjects releases, BusinessObjects 4.0 Universes can contain multidimensional perspectives, which administrators can build within the metadata itself.

As the driving force behind all query, reporting, and analysis, BusinessObjects metadata can contain:
- Renamed columns, i.e., customers;
- Formulas; e.g., sales revenue;
- Dimensions, e.g., time, city, product data, account; and
- Measures, or the numerical data, e.g., sales, profits, and gross margins.

Since the software can work with any relational schema, enterprises can introduce multidimensional analysis into the enterprise without the need for non-mainstream, proprietary multidimensional tools, or Relational OLAP tools, which typically require constructing star and/or snowflake schemas. Aberdeen believes that the BusinessObjects "schemaless" approach to multidimensionality gives enterprises the kind of flexibility that may be particularly valuable in the rapid deployment of data marts.

### The Value Of Microcubes

BusinessObjects 4.0 automatically generates a microcube when a user runs a query. The integrated query, reporting and OLAP tools work together to translate the business-language queries into SQL, then automatically build and populate individual multidimensional microcubes. This ability to construct microcubes on the fly obviates the time-consuming and resource-draining task of building cubes in advance. Moreover, the microcube architecture can significantly escalate performance, especially relevant as user populations increase.

A business user, for example, can E-mail a cube to a colleague, who can continue with the analysis as if it originated on his or her desk, thus eliminating the need to re-create the query that prompted the results and the need to alert the colleague.

### Intranet

BusinessObjects 4.0 is particularly versatile for the WWW, which over and over again has proved to be a tremendous cost saver as a vehicle for document distribution. Today, users of BusinessObjects can browse an HTML (hypertext markup language) report and hit a hyperlink to download it and start drilling. Enterprises can instruct BusinessObjects 4.0's Document Agent Server ($4,995) to automatically refresh WWW pages and schedule distribution.

### Pricing

With the introduction of BusinessObjects 4.0 and its added functionality, enterprises can now buy the Reporter (query and reporting software) at $595 and add Explorer (for integrated OLAP) at $695. Business Objects now offers a module for designers and supervisors at $1,995 each.

### Project Darwin

With Project Darwin, Business Objects will (in a phased manner) bring to the WWW all of the major decision-support functions it offers on a full client. Using BusinessObjects 4.0 as the foundation, the company is "serverizing" client code, complementing the application server with Java applets, and implementing protocols for network access. With distributed code running on the server, Project Darwin could significantly reduce maintenance and configuration management costs, and thereby drive down the total cost of ownership.

Now preparing Project Darwin for beta testing, Business Objects executives say they have been able to reuse a substantial amount (over 90 percent) of existing BusinessObjects code. Moreover, as a byproduct of the encapsulation of existing code, the company claims that enterprises with existing BusinessObjects 4.0 Universes will be able to plug-in Project Darwin and use their existing Universes.

### Data Mining

Data mining is a method of data analysis that has proven effective in helping enterprises discover unknown relationships, patterns, and associations within corporate data. Previously viewed as solely the domain of specialists armed with a variety of powerful, arcane, expensive, and difficult-to-use tools, data mining can now be part of the typical business user's analysis toolset.

Via a single button on the BusinessObjects desktop, enterprises can interact with mind-opening BusinessMiner software, decision-tree-based technology that the company has licensed from its Open Data Mining Initiative partner ISoft. Business Objects has other Data Mining Initiative partners including Angoss Software (KnowledgeSEEKER), Data Mind (DataMind), IBM (Intelligent Miner), Silicon Graphics (MINESET), and SPSS for statistical analysis.

BusinessMiner uses a combination of rule induction, statistics, and a tree-like flow chart to expose the patterns within the enterprise's data, essentially ranking the factors (given a specific input) that most influence an output. This type of knowledge is available at $995 standalone or $495 when purchased with BusinessObjects 4.0.

Aberdeen believes that BusinessMiner will, first, augment the decision-support desktop and the analysis that users perform with it, and, second, pressure suppliers, users, and consultants of high-end data mining software to push the more sophisticated tools after challenges and problems that are more consistent with the price of the high-end software.

### Partnerships

With approximately 450 value-added resellers (VARs), Business Objects also holds a privileged position in many data warehouse and data mart initiatives as well as with third-party applications. Of particular note is the company's status in the Microsoft Alliance for Data Warehousing, as well as SAP's designation of BusinessObjects 4.0 as the sole decision-support tool provider in the SAP Complementary Software Partner Program. Integration ranges from providing device drivers for specific RDBMSs, including Oracle, Informix, Red Brick Systems, and Sybase, to scripts for particular data mining partners, and to close integration with the metadata facilities of Informatica and Prism Solutions.

### What Users Tell Aberdeen

BusinessObjects users tend to install numerous copies (in the hundreds and thousands) of the software. Enterprises consistently laud BusinessObjects 4.0 for the depth of its 32-bit technology and its reception by business users, including novices, more seasoned business analysts and users, so-called power users, designers, and administrators. Moreover, each enterprise Aberdeen interviewed had more than 1,000 licenses and were encouraged that Business Objects' WWW strategy with Project Darwin is both substantive and consistent with enterprise directions.

After auditing BusinessObjects technology and Business Objects' internal processes (and comparing them against two competitors) a large user with plans to install about 5,000 copies of BusinessObjects concluded that the combination of strong technology and maturity in terms of reactiveness to problems would translate into "a sustainable platform for competitive advantage."

Help-desk support, however, seemed comparatively lackluster for some, and improved for others. One user declared help-desk support "ragged" during the beta program but improved as support personnel learned the release. Another enterprise with 3,000 licenses claimed that the help desk is a weak link in Business Objects, periodically taking too much time to locate the appropriate personnel. In contrast, still another large user claims that help-desk support has recently gotten significantly better since Business Objects moved its help desk to a Web interface.

### About The Company

Founded in 1990, Business Objects is a publicly owned company, with headquarters in both Paris, France and San Jose, Calif., and a workforce of approximately 550 employees. With installations in about 4,000 enterprises across 60 countries, Business Objects supports about 475,000 licenses. Business Objects booked 1996 revenues of $85.1 million. The company has more than $40 million in cash reserves and zero debt; revenues grew 40% from 1995 to 1996.

Business Objects distributes its software worldwide through a combination of its direct sales force, systems integrators, OEMs and distributors. Strategic resellers include Bull, Digital, Fujitsu, NEC, NCR, Price Waterhouse, Sequent, SHL, Siemens Nixdorf, Toshiba, and Unisys. Telemarketing is responsible for sales of between approximately $20,000 and $50,000. At present, Business Objects books about 70% of revenue from the direct sale of software licenses; an average size sale is at about $150,000.

### Aberdeen Conclusions

In the face of rapid growth, Business Objects continues to demonstrate the ability to manage the complex relationship between satisfying a huge installed base, managing financial performance year over year, and demonstrating technological excellence and innovation.

Having managed through an annual compound growth rate of 80% during the last three years, Business Objects has demonstrated the necessary financial acumen — and should thus encourage decision makers to view Business Objects as a stable supplier in a market populated with many venture-funded, and inherently insecure, startups. These initiatives, which are consistently pointed at making complex technologies accessible to business users, will demand both deep pockets and experience in managing the roll out of customer-satisfying releases across different product sets.

### Prescience Detail

**Key Prediction 1:** Business Objects demonstrates well-established pattern of excellence and is a stable supplier choice.  
**Outcome:** CORRECT. Business Objects grew from $85M (1996) to $1.5B revenue (2007) over 11 years, and was acquired by SAP for $6.8 billion in January 2008.  
Source: https://www.reuters.com/article/business/media-telecom/sap-agrees-to-buy-business-objects-for-48-bln-euro-idUSL07687228/

**Key Prediction 2:** Project Darwin will significantly reduce maintenance and TCO for web BI.  
**Outcome:** CORRECT. Project Darwin became WebIntelligence, which proved successful and was a key product in the SAP acquisition portfolio. Web-based BI became the industry standard.

**Key Prediction 3:** BusinessMiner will democratize data mining and pressure high-end tools.  
**Outcome:** DIRECTIONALLY CORRECT. Data mining and predictive analytics were democratized by 2010s through Python, R, and platforms like RapidMiner. BusinessMiner itself was eventually discontinued, but the broader prediction about democratization was accurate.

**Key Prediction 4:** SAP's designation of BusinessObjects as sole complementary decision-support tool will drive momentum.  
**Outcome:** CORRECT and HIGHLY PRESCIENT. The SAP relationship ultimately led to SAP acquiring Business Objects entirely in 2008 for $6.8B.

**Key Prediction 5:** Business Objects is a "stable supplier in a market populated with many venture-funded, and inherently insecure, startups."  
**Outcome:** CORRECT. Many BI startups of the late 1990s failed (Brio Technology, Actuate's competitors, etc.) while Business Objects sustained leadership.
