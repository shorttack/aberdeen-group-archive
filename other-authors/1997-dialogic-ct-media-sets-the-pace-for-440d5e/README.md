# Dialogic® CT Media™ Sets The Pace For Open-Standards Computer Telephony Development

## Abstract

Aberdeen Group evaluated the computer telephony (CT) development platform landscape in 1997 and concluded that Dialogic's CT Media was the sole open-standards framework for CT application development and deployment available at the time. The study analyzes CT Media's S.100-compliant resource management architecture, its competitive advantages over proprietary platforms from Natural MicroSystems, Rhetorex, Brooktrout, and Mitel, and argues that open-standards CT (TAPI, S.100, ECTF H.100) would catalyze a broader CT market by clarifying roles among developers, integrators, and resellers. Aberdeen predicted CT Media would become the de facto enterprise CT server middleware standard.

## Key Findings (1997)

- CT Media was the only NT-based enterprise S.100 CT platform available in 1997.
- Aberdeen concluded that developing software for CT Media servers was "as sound a business decision as it is a technology choice."
- Open standards (TAPI, S.100, ECTF S.300/S.400) were predicted to transition CT from a craft industry to a standardized mass market.
- Dialogic had >2 million installed ports worldwide and 2,000+ application partners.

## Prescience Assessment

**Score: Medium-High.** Aberdeen's prediction that open-standards CT would win over proprietary platforms proved largely correct — though the specific mechanism was VoIP/SIP rather than ECTF's S.100 framework. Dialogic was acquired by Intel in 1999 for $780 million, validating the commercial importance of open CT infrastructure. CT Media itself was eventually superseded by IP telephony standards (H.323, SIP), but the open-platform principle Aberdeen championed became the industry's organizing principle.
