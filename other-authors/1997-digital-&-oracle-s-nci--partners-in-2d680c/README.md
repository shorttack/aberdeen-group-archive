## Abstract

Aberdeen Group assessed the Network Computer (NC) platform being developed by Digital Equipment Corporation and Oracle's Network Computer Inc. (NCI) subsidiary in spring 1997. The study evaluated the DEC StrongARM-based NC hardware, NCI's software suite (NC Server, NC Access, NC Card, NC Applications), and their joint go-to-market strategy. Aberdeen declared spring 1997 as the moment when Network Computing became "practical and purchaseable" and predicted the Digital/NCI partnership would become "one of the premier Network Computing solutions" by end of 1997. Aberdeen also criticized Intel/Microsoft's NetPC initiative as a "wolf in sheep's clothing" that failed to address core PC ownership cost issues.

## Key Findings (1997)

- DEC's StrongARM 233 MHz chip delivered twice the Java benchmark performance of Intel Pentium Pro and SGI/Sun workstations.
- NCI's software was declared "the most comprehensive [NC software suite] in the industry today."
- PC lifecycle costs ranged from $5,000–$15,000 per user per year; Aberdeen endorsed NC as cost solution.
- Aberdeen recommended NCs as "a no-brainer choice for terminal replacements."

## Prescience Assessment

**Score: Low-Medium.** Aberdeen's enthusiasm for the 1997 NC concept proved premature. Oracle spent four years and lost nearly $175 million before shutting down NCI in 1999. DEC was acquired by Compaq in 1998; the StrongARM chip was sold to Intel. The NC concept was "too early" — bandwidth wasn't available for consumer NCs, and enterprise adoption was modest. However, Aberdeen's broader insight that thin-client/network-centric computing would eventually prevail was vindicated by cloud computing, Chromebooks, and virtual desktop infrastructure 15–20 years later.
