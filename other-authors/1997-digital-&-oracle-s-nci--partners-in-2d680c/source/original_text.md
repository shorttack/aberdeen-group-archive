Preface
A lot of trees have been cut down in the past year to describe Network Computing
and how good this change was going to be by lowering the cost of personal
computing, and driving personal computing to whole new classes of corporate,
home, and mobile users. But the industry debate about Network Computing - often
pitched with near-religious zealotry - has largely been about "how good (or bad) it's
going to be". Now the products are arriving.
Aberdeen believes that the spring of 1997 will be noted historically as the time when
Network Computing became practical and purchaseable. It's time for many
organizations to take a serious look at where Network Computing should ﬁt into
their enterprises. This Aberdeen Group Proﬁle examines the network computing
software platform of Oracle Corporation's Network Computer Inc. (NCI) subsidiary
and the NC(tm) reference-platform plans and network-computing directions of
Digital Equipment Corporation.
Executive Summary
Aberdeen's ongoing ﬁeld research reveals that many large enterprises are more than
willing to listen to the business arguments behind the NC thesis that desktop PC life-
cycle costs are uncontrollably high, and that millions of dumb terminals should be
replaced with Internet/Intranet-enabled, graphical desktop devices. However, until
recently, there have been few NC hardware products and even less software
available - too little to do more than IT pioneering, with all the risks of early
adoption. So for Network Computing to be a mass market, it needs pragmatic
substance, with real products and services. Enter Digital Equipment and Oracle, two
already strong partners who have jointly brought such innovations as Very Large
Memory relational databases to the industry. Digital has been successfully
integrating clients and servers into networks since the 1970s. Oracle is the industry's
second largest independent software company, with considerable experience in
enterprise mission-critical application development and operations. Both companies
believe that Network Computing represents a once-in-a-generation opportunity to
bring new technology that can solve business computing problems better.
Network Computer Inc.
Oracle's Network Computer Inc. subsidiary has moved such industry giants as Sun
and IBM to coalesce around a network computer reference platform proﬁle (called
NC Ref 1). But the NC Ref 1 standard per se generates no revenues. Therefore, NCI
has developed and will ship this spring a powerful NC client- and server-software
suite that encompasses traditional Internet browsing, e-mail and news with a
bundled, NC-optimized version of Oracle's InterOfﬁce suite. The NCI client
software covers both corporate NC desktops and NC consumer appliances. The NCI
server software has the industry's broadest range of functionality and options in
order to satisfy requirements ranging from a PBX-style small-business server to a
large-enterprise Intranet. Yet best-of-breed components can also be substituted. In
short, the NCI offering has the practical functionality needed to put NCs to use
immediately - and the openness to offer substitutes in best-of-breed complementary
components.
Digital Equipment Corporation
In order to deliver fast NC response times, Digital Equipment is harnessing the
industry's most powerful NC microprocessor, the 233 MHz StrongARM chip that is
manufactured in Digital's silicon fabrication facilities, in an NC Ref 1 design. The
StrongARM microprocessor costs well under $50 in quantity, yet delivers the
industry's highest Java performance. This price/performance powerhouse will
facilitate the manufacture of under-$750 NC desktops with the headroom to grow
into emerging, compute-intensive Internet/Intranet applications such as video
conferencing and Web telephones - as well as speed through Java's interpreted code.
Digital's respected networks and systems integration organization will assist most-ly
large enterprises in upgrading their networking infrastructures to be "NC ready" and
in developing Network Computing applications, particularly new Internet business

models. Moreover, Digital will work with selected vertical market suppliers such as
global telecommunications companies and Internet service suppliers in building the
ofﬁce and consumer infrastructure to support wireless/-mobile NCs, NC kiosks, and
consumer-product NC appliances. Of course, Digital also expects to sell a lot of
Alpha servers to handle the demands of NC clients.
Digital/NCI Partnership
The synergy of the Digital-NCI partnership is leading to better engineered solutions,
with Digital taking the lead on hardware and networking issues and contributing on
operating system engineering, and NCI leading with the software integration of
client OS, desktop, and security, server OS and server software, and server
application integration with Oracle and third-party applications. The result is better
than either could do alone, and was facilitated by the close proximity of the
engineering teams in California. Aberdeen Group concludes that the Digital/NCI
partnership is delivering fast NC hardware to meet the desktop demands of today
and tomorrow; integrated client and server software that should more than
adequately handle the ofﬁce computing requirements of many white-collar as well
as shop-ﬂoor, blue-collar workers; and integration services to help IT organizations
plan and execute their initial NC roll-outs. The Digital/NCI partnership brings
practical Network Computing to the industry at a time when there is considerable
pent-up demand. The Digital/NCI products and services, by the end of 1997, are
likely to be viewed by the industry as one of the premier Network Computing
solutions.
The Business Justiﬁcation for Network Computing
The thesis justifying network computing is:
Personal computers have gotten out of control in terms of lack of
manageability, lost user productivity, and high life-cycle costs of ownership
that range from $5,000 to $15,000 per user per year;
Information technology (IT) organizations are caught in the middle: users
demand PCs to do their jobs and their line-of-business managers drop the
purchase order in the IT in-basket. IT cannot improve manageability nor
effectively reduce life cycle costs without active hardware and software
supplier participation. It is easier for IT to "just say yes" to user demands - and
costs be damned;
Meanwhile, Internet technology has blossomed into a worldwide computing
phenomena with a speed and degree of innovation that the industry has never
seen before. TCP/IP has become the de facto Internet networking standard, web
browsers are everywhere, and the Java programming language has matured
rapidly;
To gain back control of the desktop and hence reduce costs of ownership, the
NC will shift ﬁle storage to IT-manageable servers, and offer new software
applications that reduce the enormous software "feature bloat" that has created
hours of non-productive work as users train, practice, then ﬁdget with their
personal computer conﬁgurations. NCs also prevent spreading viruses from
ﬂoppy disks and make it harder to walk out the door with critical corporate
data;
So, it makes sense to take Internet technology and combine it with a redeﬁned,
simpliﬁed, but still enormously powerful desktop - or mobile - NC device in the
new style - Network Computing.

NCI's Network Computer Software Suite
NCI is delivering an integrated suite of software that provides a complete user
operating environment for both an NC client and NC server. This integrated NC
environment can be deployed immediately on NC Ref 1-compliant devices such as
the Digital StrongARM NC, and on servers including Digital Unix, HP HP-UX,
IBM AIX, and Sun SPARC Solaris. In addition, NCOS is a server OS built on
public-domain (i.e., open) BSD Unix that runs on Intel-based servers.
NCI will serve as a software integrator for its OEM hardware partners such as
Digital, providing combinations of NCI, Oracle, and third-party applications as
required by the OEM. As shown in Figure 2, the four components of NCI's business
model are:
NC Server - OS software with options supports any NC Ref 1 NC. Version 1.3
of the OS software, in beta now, will run on Digital Unix, HP/UX, IBM AIX,
Sun SPARC Solaris and Windows NT. There is also a standalone product, NC
Community(tm) Server with the NCOS operating system, that turns an Intel PC
server into an NC server. OS services include authentication, authorization, NC
initialization, ﬁle system, print system, directory services, a database repository
for conﬁguration data, a web server, and a variety of administration tools.
Options include the Oracle7.3 Universal Server RDBMS and InterOfﬁce, a
news delivery service, and NC Safeguard, aimed at child-safe Web access.
NC Access(tm) - a ROM-based, modular OS that requires less than 500K of
memory. The OS manages mouse, keyboard, NC Card and other peripherals,
communications, security, display, the web browser/user desktop proﬁle, and
the Java virtual machine. Server-based terminal emulation software supports
Digital VT, IBM 3270, and Microsoft Windows applications. Multi-character
support is provided for Euro-pean and Japanese languages. There is
considerable built-in support for multimedia, including JPEG and GIF,
Cinepak, Indeo and H.263 codecs, PAL and NTSC video, ShockWave and
RealAudio audio, plus other multi-media enablers. Digital video streaming over
either Ethernet or ISDN is supported through a video player applet, making the
NCI's client videoconferencing-ready out of the box.
NC Card(tm) - a smart card based on industry standard for NCs. The NC Card
is a personal passport to the NC network, containing in a credit-card size device
carried by the owner the necessary public-private-key encrypted information
for authentication and access authorization. Digital signatures and even bank
balances can be stored on the card. With an NC Card, any user can use any NC,
including kiosks and public-use NCs in the future. With a digital photo, NC
Cards could easily become the employee badge of choice. NCI will
manufacture the NC Cards.
NC Applications - Server-based applications include a video server, full
Internet and News access, electronic mail, and e-mail-integrated scheduling
services. In addition, Oracle's "Hat Trick" suite of Java-based personal
productivity applications serve desktops with an e-mail editor and mailbox,
presentation graphics, and a word processor.

The NCI server software will be packaged in two ﬂavors. For large organizations
with many users, the have-it-your-way options will allow IT to choose the hardware
and operating system platform as well as consider substitutes for the bundled NCI
application components. For smaller organizations, NCI plans the summer release
through hardware OEMs of an NC server hardware/software integrated platform.
NC Community(tm) Server is an integrated bundle designed for PBX-like lock-the-
server-in-a-closet situations found in small businesses and in geographically
distributed sites of large organizations (e.g., bank branches). NC Community(tm)
Server includes add-ons such as a fax server and a software ﬁrewall so that the load-
and-go software can be pre-installed and site-deployed with minimal installation
support.
Yes, NCI has borrowed heavily from parent Oracle's software labs. However, NCI's
architecture fully supports snap-in, snap-out best-of-breed components. For instance,
an enterprise could standardize on Netscape's Navigator browser across PC,
Macintosh, workstation, and NCI-compliant NCs.

Importantly, NCI's decision to include terminal emulation software means that NCs
can even run genuine Windows applications -- though probably for occasional use -
so the NC versus PC choice by IT decision-makers does not have to be either/or.
Digital Equipment: Big Plans for a StrongARM
With well-regarded Alpha servers, considerable networking experience, and a
worldwide integration service business, Digital has lacked but one ingredient for a
complete Network Computing game plan - an NC. With the delivery this spring of
initial units, Digital has an opportunity in 1997 to become one of the NC industry's
overall hardware leaders, challenging early leaders IBM and Sun Microsystems.
StrongARM microprocessor is the muscle of the Digital NC
Digital licenses the ARM RISC chip architecture from Advanced RISC Machines
Ltd - the ARM in StrongARM. Digital's Semiconductor Business Unit has
developed the highest performing versions of the StrongARM architecture,
manufactures the microprocessors, and resells the chips to hardware OEM partners.
Largely used in embedded-computer applications, StrongARM may be unknown to
many IT executives, but it is the fourth-highest volume microprocessor in the
industry, behind SGI/MIPS (Nintendo), PowerPC, and Intel x86. For example,
Apple chose StrongARM last spring for its MessagePad product (now available).
Ironically, the StrongARM chip made by Digital Semiconductor has become the
choice of the industry's largest network appliance vendors while an NC product was
unavailable through Digital.
Digital Equipment's StrongARM reference implementation is being given to
interested NC manufacturers. Digital's version of NC Ref 1 has three key attributes
that lead to a compelling NC story:
The 233 MHz StrongARM is enormously powerful, blowing away the
competition with workstation-class performance. Importantly, this chip blazes
through interpreted Java code in the CaffeineMark benchmark, delivering more
than twice the throughput of SGI and Sun Ultra workstations and even Intel's
200 MHz Pentium Pro. The CPU power is enough to power compute-intensive
applications such as digital video conferencing without the necessity and
expense for lots of chips to off-load the CPU. Thus, the StrongARM NC will be
able to run today's Java lightning fast but also be able to support tomorrow's
applications like Internet telephony and video (starting immediately with the
video applet in NCI's suite), an important investment protection;
The enormous microprocessor power of the StrongARM comes at an under-$50
price point, a tenth of the cost of a Pentium microprocessor, for example. This
qualiﬁes StrongARM as the price/performance leader. Moreover, this low price
point means Digital's StrongARM OEMs can price their NCs at no cost
premium to competitors in spite of their signiﬁcant performance advantage;
Electrical frugality: the Digital NC with all of its electronics consumes about 6
watts of power, so little that it could (and will, in mobile versions) run with
penlight batteries. This means no fans are required, so the Digital NC is silent.
Moreover, with no peripherals to wear out, trivial electrical consumption, and
an NC Card required for authentication, we expect users will leave their NCs
on all the time, and hence be able to start up nearly instantaneously.
Digital's goal for its NC is to make it the industry standard based on price/
performance, affordability, and economy.
Digital's Plans for Network Computing
Digital is leery of ﬁlling warehouses with NC inventory, but is being driven by
customers to be a one-stop-shop for NCs, Alpha servers, and Network Computing
services. Digital's services are especially attractive to customers, given its long ex-
perience and strength in global network design and integration. Digital will have its
NCs manufactured by Funai and Aranex, Inc., and is in discussions with other high-
volume manufacturers. We expect Digital to put NCs in the price book, but to fulﬁll
the customer's orders through existing terminal distributor channels. In addition, the
company will package a bundle of NCs with a server, installation and network
infrastructure services. The NCI software suite will be the preferred software
offering.
Corporate desktops for new-breed applications are the ﬁrst marketing target for
Digital's Network Computing initiative. Digital's customers have already
conﬁdentially approached the company about Digital's assistance in architecting,
developing and deploying NC-based applications that replace dumb terminals and
the Intel 286, 386, and 486 PCs that will never be able to run Windows 95, and
extending a computing network to all levels of the enterprise. Aberdeen believes that
Digital and NCI will be particularly successful in ﬁnancial services and the broad
service industry. Moreover, a new generation of mobile NC terminals would have
enormous utility in manufacturing and ﬁeld service environments.
The Competition is Inertia
Having bought millions of PC desktops, many users expected Intel and Microsoft to
lower their aggravation levels and costs of ownership. Stung by a ﬁrestorm of buyer
criticism about the total life cycle costs of desktop computing, Intel and Microsoft
have - belatedly - reacted with an initiative announced on March 12, 1997 called the
NetPC. The NetPC removes the user's ability to (easily) add-in new adapters and

peripherals, can be remotely woken by a LAN server for a fresh software download
at night during off hours, and can be conﬁgured to prevent users from locally storing
ﬁles or modifying critical conﬁguration information.
However, Aberdeen's analysis is that the NetPC is a wolf in sheep's clothing. The
NetPC still has all of the Microsoft desktop operating system maintenance issues,
and Ofﬁce97's application feature-bloat that result in the bulk of costly help-desk
requests. Moreover, to gain the claimed cost beneﬁts, organizations will have to
replace all of their existing yet unmanageable PCs with NetPCs, an idea the cost
accountants will hate. And the support costs for users with Microsoft OS and
application issues will not go away with NetPC.
As Figure 3 shows, the Network Computer will perform all of the user computing

run Microsoft Windows applications such as Ofﬁce 95 with the help of an NT Server

In short, with the announcement of the NetPC, we expect little change in overall
direction or expectations on the PC front over the next 18 months. With the plans of

Aberdeen Group Conclusions
This partnership is not just another marketing marriage of convenience. Digital
needs the NCI software suite to deliver a credible solution to its mostly IT audience.
And NCI beneﬁts signiﬁcantly from the brand and recognition reputation of Digital
Equipment, since the NCI hardware partners to date have not been widely known in
the IT community.
Together, these partners in practical network computing will be delivering the
comprehensive NC solution that IT executives have been expecting before they
commit to high-volume purchases. Moreover, we expect these partners to extend
their work to future NC appliances, particularly wireless devices.
Digital has a credible argument that StrongARM's performance is not overkill, but
necessary headroom that will be consumed during the ﬁve-year depreciation period
of the NC -- headroom for new applications such as Internet telephones and
videoconferencing (including the video technology from NCI on day one.) And the
industry has certainly learned during its 15 year relationship with PCs that an open-
the-box upgrade is a sure way to pour money down a pit. We recommend that buyers
carefully consider NC performance in their evaluations.
Where Digital needs to get cracking is in its go-to-market efforts. IBM has already
launched a credible market program built around a channel-driven NCs-and-server
bundle. We expect an aggressive rollout of Digital's Network Computing story and
products.
NCI's software offering is without doubt the most comprehensive in the industry
today. We give it high marks for architecture and breadth of functionality,
particularly for a ﬁrst release. Desktop users with some ofﬁce collaboration
experience will ﬁnd themselves at ease using the e-mail, word processor, scheduler,
and other functions.
Some NC vendors grumble that NCI grabbed the trademark for "Network
Computer". But the reality is that the Network Computer industry has quietly rallied
around NCI's NC Ref 1 standard, meaning there is likely to be considerable early
and desirable standardization of what an NC can be expected to do.

All of this bodes well for IT executives with a Network Computing choice to make.
Aberdeen believes IT executives should consider NCs to be a practical choice for
desktop computing, and a no-brainer choice for terminal replacements. The
Digital/NCI partnership shows that Network Computing is not only practical, but
has truly arrived.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group assessed the Network Computer platform being developed by Digital Equipment Corporation and Oracle's NCI subsidiary in spring 1997. The study evaluated the DEC StrongARM-based NC hardware, NCI's software suite, and their joint strategy. Aberdeen declared spring 1997 as the moment when Network Computing became "practical and purchaseable" and predicted the Digital/NCI partnership would become one of the premier Network Computing solutions by end of 1997. Aberdeen also criticized Intel/Microsoft's NetPC as a "wolf in sheep's clothing."

### Prescience Detail

Aberdeen's endorsement of the Network Computer was premature by approximately 15 years. The specific 1997 execution — DEC StrongARM NCs + NCI software — failed entirely: DEC was acquired by Compaq in June 1998, the StrongARM chip was sold to Intel in the October 1997 patent settlement, and Oracle shut down NCI in 1999 after spending ~$175 million. Aberdeen's dismissal of the NetPC was directionally correct (NetPC never gained significant traction) but for the wrong reasons — the PC won the 1997-2005 desktop battle before the NC concept re-emerged as cloud computing. Aberdeen was prescient in identifying PC total cost of ownership as unsustainable (which drove managed PC and eventually VDI/Chromebook adoption), and in identifying Java/internet technology as the enabling platform. The broader NC thesis — that network-centric, thin-client computing would prevail — was vindicated by Google Chromebooks (2011), AWS/Azure cloud desktops, and the broader cloud computing paradigm. Eric Schmidt, who endorsed NC at Sun in 1995, led Google to realize this vision.
