## Abstract

Aberdeen Group profiled Digital Equipment Corporation's (DIGITAL/DEC) strategy for the telecommunications industry in May 1997. The study examined DIGITAL's four telecom solution areas — Intelligent Network (IN), Telecommunications Management Network (TMN), Customer Care & Billing (CC&B), and Broadband Services — and assessed five key ISV partners: CGI (billing), CMG (text messaging), Dialogic (computer telephony), Kenan Systems (CC&B), and Vienna Systems (IP telephony). Aberdeen concluded that DIGITAL was well-positioned to expand in key telecom market segments via its Alpha platform strengths, ecosystem of "best in class" partners, and integration capabilities.

## Key Findings (1997)

- DIGITAL's TeMiPs network management platform enabled proactive fault detection.
- Kenan Systems' Arbor/BP showed 50% faster billing processing on Alpha vs other platforms.
- CMG was the European market leader for SMS messaging; entering the US in 1995.
- Vienna Systems offered IP-over-PSTN routing to eliminate long-distance toll charges.
- DIGITAL's 64-bit Alpha platform was positioned as the performance differentiator.

## Prescience Assessment

**Score: Medium.** Aberdeen correctly assessed DIGITAL's short-term telecom positioning, but the underlying DEC enterprise was acquired by Compaq in 1998. Most partner outcomes diverged sharply from the 1997 trajectory: Kenan Systems was acquired by Lucent for $1.48B in 1999; Vienna Systems was acquired by Nokia for ~$90M in 1998; CMG merged with Logica in 2002. DIGITAL's alpha-centric partner strategy was prescient in recognizing telecom IT convergence, but the platform (Alpha) was ultimately killed, and DIGITAL itself was absorbed.
