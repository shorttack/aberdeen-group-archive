## Abstract

Aberdeen Group analyzed Digital Equipment Corporation's High Performance Technical Computing (HPTC) initiative in June 1997, following Digital's announcement of expanded HPTC sales, marketing, and ISV programs. The study assessed Digital's AlphaServer platform (4000, 4100, 8200, 8400), TruCluster clustering technology, workstation lineup (AlphaStation series, Personal Workstations with NT), and software tools (KAP, MPI, PVM, HPF, Digital Visual Fortran). Aberdeen benchmarked Digital against competitors (SGI, IBM, HP/Convex, Sun) and concluded Digital was well-positioned to gain market share in the HPTC space, particularly in 64-bit Unix SMP/clustering. Key application segments highlighted: EDA, MCAD, geoscience, energy, financial services, biosciences, entertainment/digital content creation.

## Key Findings (1997)

- Digital's AlphaServer 8400 used up to 12 CPUs; fastest Alpha 5/466 processor.
- TruCluster Memory Channel interconnect enabled high-speed low-overhead clustering.
- AlphaStation 600a/600au (600 MHz Alpha) started at $10,000.
- Digital Visual Fortran released as leading-edge Win95/NT Fortran compiler.
- Digital won Pro-E Bench97: 500au under 60 min vs SGI's best Unix workstation at 80+ min.

## Prescience Assessment

**Score: Low.** Aberdeen predicted Digital would "make additional market share gains over the next several years" in HPTC. In reality, Compaq acquired DEC just 12 months later (June 1998), and Alpha was phased out by 2004. SGI, which Aberdeen dismissed for NT transition, itself went through multiple bankruptcies. IBM's RS/6000 SP (criticized as overly expensive MPP) declined but IBM Power survived. The HPTC market itself evolved dramatically — Linux clusters, commodity x86, and later GPU computing displaced proprietary Unix/Alpha RISC architectures.
