Digital Ramps Up For The Leadership Role
in High Performance Technical Computing
June 16, 1997 — Last month Digital Equipment Corporation unveiled
expanded sales, marketing, product development and ISV recruitment programs
that focus on the requirements of customers acquiring High Performance
Technical Computing (HPTC) platforms for running numerically intensive and
technical compute applications. To focus its efforts, Digital has declared that
High Performance Technical Computing is one of its major marketing
concentrations. Digital is investing in its High Performance Technical
Computing initiative in conjunction with a complementary organization, the
Visual Computing Workstation group.
Digital’s focus on High Performance Technical Computing will expand the
company’s technology lead in running technical/scientiﬁc application on high-
performance 64-bit Alpha shared memory and Memory Channel™ interconnect
cluster platforms (with trademarked technology contributed by Encore
Computer Corporation). The group will concentrate on building leading-edge
parallelization technology, optimizing ISV (independent software vendor)
applications to take full advantage of Digital’s parallel 64-bit technology, and
supporting customers in taking high performance technical computing to places
where it has never gone before.
In this Announcement Proﬁle, Aberdeen will analyze several key trends
occurring in traditional technical computing markets, including a review of
several high growth application segments that Digital is pursuing in partnership
with industry-leading independent software vendors. These are fast growing,
computationally intensive markets — including both traditional technical,
engineering and scientiﬁc categories, plus numerous emerging applications in
ﬁelds like entertainment, ﬁnancial services, and health sciences. In addition,
Aberdeen will show how Digital’s hardware and software products map to the
demands of these markets.
High Performance Customer Challenges
Aberdeen research indicates that customers evaluate HPTC platform suppliers
according to several important criteria. First, the availability of project-critical
applications software that has been optimized to take advantage of the
supplier’s unique performance-enhancing technologies is always a mandatory
element for customer evaluation. Second, given the increase in server usage,
suppliers must have a scaleable product line — one with robust solutions at the
very high-end for the most power hungry technical and database applications.
Showing competition-beating benchmark scores is very important. Third, the
supplier must provide cost-effective servers — technical project managers want
to achieve the maximum amount of performance for each dollar invested. As a
result, customers evaluate new hardware systems with a watchful eye toward
aggressive pricing. Fourth, worldwide service and support are a key
determining factor in the technical acquisition, especially as HPTC is adopted
by industrial companies.
Finally, today’s HPTC customer evaluates both its server and application
suppliers on how well they have planned ahead for including Microsoft NT in
an otherwise Unix dominated environment. When the customer’s critical
applications become available on NT, the hardware supplier must be prepared
with cost-effective NT workstations and servers. As a result, the hardware
supplier must maintain strong, long-term technology-sharing relationships with
ISV partners.
Recently, the demand for Unix servers as high-end compute servers has
increased. New applications are emerging in almost every market segment
where — after data entry, model development and preprocessing — data sets
and applications must be moved to a server for compute-intensive processing.
One reason for this trend is that the increased use of NT-based personal
workstation (PWS) is expanding the overall technical market and driving a
requirement for complementary high-performance Unix compute servers. This
emerging, NT PWS-Unix server technical architecture in the HPTC market
does not lessen the value of the workstation for technical users — nor does it
mean that compute and graphics performance on the workstation is not equally
critical to customer satisfaction. It does mean, however, that demand for
appropriately priced midrange to high-end dedicated compute servers is
growing much more rapidly than in the past

Furthermore, as the business impact of new HPTC applications become clear,
customers attribute more and more value to compute server reliability and
resilience. This means that clustering conﬁgurations — with availability as well
as performance being a key design goal — are increasingly popular growth
paths for meeting high-end scalability requirements.
Recent changes in High Performance Technical Computing market dynamics
have been the catalyst for a number of new customer acquisition criteria. These
additional criteria can be summarized in following three critical questions:
1. "Have my hardware and software suppliers demonstrated leadership in
their engineering and technical products so that I will be in partnership
with companies that truly understands my business and technical
challenges?"
2. "Does my primary hardware supplier offer a choice in servers and
workstations that includes Unix and NT operating environments and will it
be able to support me in a timely transition from one to the other?"
3. "Does my supplier offer a server family with blazing CPU speed, robust
performance, attractive pricing and scalability for the largest and most
complex applications? And can the supplier support me as my worldwide
business grows."

The result has been a resurgence of acceptance for Digital in the High
Performance Technical Computing market place and a higher-than-the-industry
growth rate. Encouraged by this positive response from the market, Digital is
now investing more marketing dollars to communicate its quiet move to a new
leadership position to technical IS decision makers and increasing its efforts to
optimize the parallelization of application software to run even faster on its
SMP and cluster platforms.
Highlights of the Digital Product Line
Servers
At the core of Digital’s high performance product line-up is the AlphaServer
family of symmetric multiprocessing systems. The 4000 and 4100 deliver
signiﬁcant application performance at midrange price points and now
incorporate Digital’s newest Alpha 5/466 processor. At the high end, the
AlphaServer 8200 and 8400 use up to 6 and 12 CPUs, respectively and give
customers the option of either the Alpha 5/300 or 5/440 processor.

All of Digital’s servers run Digital Unix — a 64-bit implementation that takes
full advantage of Alpha’s underlying architecture and incorporates Very Large
Memory (VLM) capabilities. In addition, they incorporate fast 132 and 264
MB/sec. industry-standard PCI I/O. The advantages for HPTC users are more
instructions processed per CPU cycle, more CPU cycles per second than on any
other processor available in the industry, the ability to use more than 2GB of
main memory, and fast access to data to keep the CPU busy. Several of
Digital’s servers also support Microsoft NT.
Clustering
In order to provide production capacity for the most demanding high
performance technical computing and scientiﬁc applications as well as
increased systems availability, Digital offers TruCluster. TruCluster is a high-
speed, low-overhead Memory Channel™ interconnect technology
incorporating a crossbar switch that provides for dynamic connections of
multiple SMP server nodes. This approach continues to maintain the company’s
industry-wide leadership position in clustering.
Digital’s High Performance Technical Computing group adds value for its
customers by working with leading ISVs to parallelize their applications across
TruCluster. As a result, users have the opportunity to obtain additional
performance advantages beyond just SMP and into the advanced science of a
cluster platform.
In addition, multiple SMP servers clustered together using TruCluster provide
additional availability to server-dependent workstation users. Aberdeen ﬁeld
research clearly indicates that the high performance applications portfolio is not
only becoming more complex — requiring higher and higher levels of system
throughput — but is also becoming more mission critical. Downtime is simply
unacceptable, whether it delays getting a new drug through FDA trials or
whether it occurs during the simulation of crash testing for a leading
automobile manufacturer. In any of these scenarios the ﬁnancial penalties can
total millions of dollars per day. Therefore, high end systems are increasingly
measured not only by price/performance — but also by reliability.
Digital’s TruCluster is an excellent way for high performance customers to
guarantee head room for the largest and most complicated applications, to
increase overall system performance, to ease the burden of system management
and to provide for optimum application and system availability.
Workstations
We have discussed accelerating levels of customer interest in powerful servers
for HPTC applications. In addition, these customers also continue to require
workstations for the design, data manipulations and interactive phases of the
project life cycle. Digital offers a broad range of powerful RISC-based Unix
workstations — the AlphaStation 200, 300, 500 and 600 — as well as its
Personal Workstations that run Microsoft NT. An increasing number of leading
application software suppliers in software development, ECAD, MCAD,
geographic information systems, energy sciences, digital content creation,
advanced desktop publishing and entertainment are aggressively making the
transition to NT. Digital was early to recognize this transition and as a result
can now offer its ISV partners and customers a choice of operating
environments on both Alpha and Intel workstation platforms. Additionally,
Digital offers the customer the option of running either Unix or NT on a single
Alpha-based hardware platform.
Digital is also well-known in the workstation market for its 2D and 3D
PowerStorm graphics accelerator software products. These support OpenGL as
well as the PCI bus and run effectively with both Digital Unix and Microsoft
Windows NT.
With the recent introduction of Digital’s newest Personal Workstation — 600a
and 600au — the company continues to reduce price thresholds for customers
wanting broader access to undiluted PowerStorm graphics for both the Unix
and NT environments. These systems are based upon Digital’s most recent and
fastest 600-MHz Alpha microprocessor — with prices starting at $10,000. Not
only is performance outstanding at each of the various price points in this new
line, but also each of the systems can be dynamically reconﬁgured with Unix or
NT in the ﬁeld — combining a transition to NT at the customer’s own pace
with complete investment protection.
Languages, Development Tools and Utilities for the Technical
Market
A key element in Digital’s HPTC strategy is to develop an advanced parallel
software environment. For optimizing the deployment of applications on SMP
and cluster platforms, Digital provides KAP, MPI, PVM, and HPF. KAP is a
preprocessor for auto parallelization of software for SMP servers. PVM and
MPI are industry-standard libraries for message passing parallel application
which Digital has optimized to minimize latency in cluster conﬁgurations. And
HPF (High Performance Fortran), included with the Fortran 90 compiler, is a
high level language for the parallelization of applications for clusters. These are
complemented by parallel development tools including Atom, DEC FUSE,

Dolphin’s TotalView Debugger, KAI’s KAP/Pro Toolset and Pallas’ VAMPIR
event analyzer for the full life cycle of software development, tuning, testing,
and maintenance.
In addition, another objective of the HPTC group is to allow customers to
develop on Windows and deploy on Unix. Recently, the company continued to
lead the way in Microsoft platform support when it released Digital Visual
Fortran, a leading edge compiler for Win95/NT programmers using either
Alpha or Intel-based systems. Digital’s Visual Fortran is based upon the same
robust Fortran 90 technology that is widely used on OpenVMS and Digital
Unix.
Competitive Positioning
The HPTC market requires hardware suppliers to position their platforms
across the spectrum of customers’ buying criteria. Today’s HPTC decision
makers must review performance, price/ performance, parallelizing tools, NT-
Unix workstation transition aid, global customer support, technology prowess
required for future advances in the HPTC market, and real long-term corporate
commitment to HPTC customers. From this perspective, the three key suppliers
are Digital, Silicon Graphics, and IBM. Two secondary suppliers are
HP/Convex and Sun Microsystems.
Comparing the suppliers’ products from a performance perspective, each will
win the benchmarks for which their particular HPTC servers have been
designed. However, as higher clockrate Alpha processors are incorporated into
Digital’s platforms, Digital is winning an increasingly large number of
industry-standard HPTC benchmarks.
What sets Digital apart from IBM is its commitment to 64-bit parallel Unix
processing. In this space, IBM is relying on its 32-bit MPP RS/6000 SP
architecture. However, while with the aid of numerous technical staff and
programmers this can beat Gary Kaspanov in a chess match, it is looking overly
expensive and slow for the typical production HPTC server user. In addition,
IBM is hindered by its slow-to-market position in 64-bit Unix computing as
well as by the fact that a smaller range of applications lend themselves to the
slower interprocessor communications characteristics of MPP.
And Digital’s commitment to helping HPTC customers seamlessly integrate
NT platforms and transition to NT when appropriate differentiates it from
Silicon Graphics. This customer advantage was highlighted when an entry-level
model Digital 500au ran the Pro-E Bench97 performance routine (a benchmark
developed by PRO/E: The Magazine dedicated to the MCAD-oriented
Parametric PRO/E user community) in under 60 minutes while the best Unix-
based Silicon Graphics workstation took over 80.
Sun has just recently entered the HPTC market through a combination of the
acquisitions of the former Floating Point Systems division of Cray and the
assets of Thinking Machines. At this time, its long-term commitment to HPTC
customers is still uncertain. And HP does not appear to be making major
investments in Convex’ (acquired in 1995) HPTC technology and customer
support — it is simply too small a portion of the company to be relevant.
In Aberdeen’s opinion, Digital has targeted high-growth segments in the
technical space without abandoning its traditional leading-edge technical
accounts in the federal government, in education or in research institutions. It
continues to offer the prerequisite systems for successful penetration of
engineering analysis and geoscience, while dramatically expanding the
company’s presence in energy management and the exploding ﬁelds of
biochemical science and ﬁnancial services.
Organizing Around Today’s HPTC Customer
Digital believes that the server/cluster portion of the traditional technical and
numerically intensive computing market is so important that it should have
greater and more focused resources to support HPTC customers. The newly
created High Performance Technical Computing group — a standalone unit
incorporating Digital’s server and clustering initiatives — is now responsible
for achieving maximum business results and maintaining the highest levels of
customer satisfaction in Digital’s original backyard market.
Digital’s workstation marketing initiatives continue to be led by the Visual
Computing group — a sister organization to the High Performance Technical
Computing group. Working closely together, these two business units are
charged with meeting the full range of customer requirements for computing in
technical, engineering and scientiﬁc accounts, as well as in numerous emerging
high-growth application areas.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group analyzed Digital Equipment Corporation's HPTC initiative in June 1997, assessing the AlphaServer platform, TruCluster clustering technology, and workstation lineup against competitors SGI, IBM, HP/Convex, and Sun. Aberdeen ranked Digital as the #1 HPTC platform and predicted market share gains over the next several years.

### Prescience Detail

Aberdeen's June 1997 assessment of Digital's HPTC leadership was overtaken by events within 12 months: Compaq acquired DEC in June 1998. The Alpha architecture was discontinued in 2001, with final systems sold in 2007. Aberdeen's specific competitive assessments were mixed: the criticism of HP/Convex as underinvested proved accurate; the criticism of SGI's NT gap was technically correct but SGI's challenges ran deeper (bankruptcy in 2006/2009); the criticism of IBM's MPP as expensive/slow was partially wrong — IBM Power evolved into dominant HPC platforms (Blue Gene, Summit). Most importantly, Aberdeen did not anticipate the disruption from Linux-based commodity x86 clusters (Beowulf clusters), which by 2000-2005 had fundamentally reshaped HPTC by making high-performance computing accessible at a fraction of the cost of proprietary RISC systems. This disruption ultimately drove the decline of all the vendors Aberdeen evaluated as leading HPTC suppliers.
