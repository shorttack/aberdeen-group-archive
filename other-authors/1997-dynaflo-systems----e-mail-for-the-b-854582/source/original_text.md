Abstract: Profile
DynaFlo Systems
E-Mail For The Business Masses
The world is changing for Service Providers (SP), especially
those who provide Internet connections and e-mail. Many
Internet Service Providers (ISP) are growing at such a rapid
pace that they barely have time to fill orders and connect new
customers. While they spend most of their time building out
their networks, they spend little time focusing on how to make
money in a changing marketplace. Aberdeen Group field
research has discovered that many ISPs are growing their sales
and losing money at the same time – this is not a long-term
success formula.
Aberdeen has also found that Service Providers must address a
broader market than the crowded arena of early adopters of the
Internet. This will be critical to their bottom line survivability
as competition increases and as larger players begin to offer
Internet services.
For every business on the Internet, there are nine others who
have yet to connect. For these businesses, the costs of Internet
connectivity and perceived security risks are prohibitive.
Many of these businesses would like Internet e-mail to
supplement their internal LAN-based systems.
As Service Providers struggle to find ways to remain profitable
in the Internet business, they seek solutions that they can use
to create enhanced services. These solutions need to have two
characteristics:
Strong investment value, business model and return on
investment
Customer value
DynaFlo's PushMail provides both of these, with the best
price/performance for any Internet e-mail solution available.
PushMail benefits both the end user and the Service Provider.
The end user gets simple, reliable, cost-effective e-mail
service, while the Service Provider increases the operational
efficiency of its dial-up network. PushMail is a good
investment for Service Providers as it gives them a high-
margin way to sell the Internet to a significantly wider
audience.
Peter Kastner & Dan Taylor

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group vendor profile of DynaFlo Systems and its PushMail product, a cost-effective Internet email solution targeting Service Providers (ISPs) seeking to expand beyond early adopters. Aberdeen field research found many ISPs were growing sales while losing money; DynaFlo's PushMail offered ISPs a high-margin add-on service to reach the larger non-connected business market (9 of 10 businesses had not yet connected to the Internet in 1997).

### Prescience Detail

Aberdeen's structural analysis of the ISP market proved highly accurate:

1. **ISP consolidation confirmed:** The ISP market grew from ~1,400 providers in early 1996 to ~3,000 by start of 1997, then consolidated dramatically. By 2000, the consumer segment was dominated by AOL (20M subscribers), EarthLink, MSN, and Prodigy. WorldCom acquired UUNET's parent MFS for $12 billion; GTE acquired BBN Corp for $616 million. (Source: [Secjuice ISP History](https://www.secjuice.com/history-of-internet-access/); [Encyclopedia.com ISP](https://www.encyclopedia.com/science-and-technology/computers-and-electrical-engineering/computers-and-computing/internet-1))

2. **Larger players entering confirmed:** Aberdeen warned larger players would enter the ISP market. Telcos (AT&T, GTE), media companies, and portals (AOL, MSN) did exactly this, squeezing smaller ISPs.

3. **DynaFlo PushMail outcome:** No evidence of DynaFlo Systems surviving. The specific product was displaced by free web-based email (Hotmail, acquired by Microsoft in December 1997; Yahoo Mail launched 1997), which eliminated the paid ISP email value proposition entirely.

4. **'9 of 10 businesses not connected' — correct timing:** Internet adoption among businesses accelerated rapidly from 1997-2002 driven by e-commerce investment. Aberdeen correctly identified the large addressable market.

**Assessment:** Prescience 4/5. Structural ISP market analysis was excellent; specific product recommendation did not survive.
