Hewlett-Packard’s Strategy to Surpass Compaq
in the Enterprise-NT Marketplace
An Executive White Paper
August 1997
Hewlett-Packard’s Capture-the-Enterprise-NT
Strategy
Preface
$38 billion Hewlett-Packard (HP) has declared its intention to become the
market leader in NT-based computing. But to do so, HP must beat $20 billion
Compaq Computer Corporation — no easy task given that Compaq currently
has more than double HP’s marketshare in NT desktops and servers. HP’s plan
is to beat Compaq at the enterprise level — and to do so with the help of key
corporate resellers and integrators that can inﬂuence builds, and with HP’s
enterprise-experienced service and support organizations.
HP is clearly and publicly demonstrating its intent through its recently
announced and maturing Simpliﬁed Enterprise Computing (SEC) strategy. This
strategy encompasses new HP-developed products, new joint-development
efforts with Microsoft, and new enterprise-level NT focused service and
support offerings. The objective of these changes will be to provide a
compelling argument to enterprise-level decision makers that acquiring NT-
based products and solutions with value-added by HP will deliver the lowest
total cost of ownership (TCO) possible.
HP has proven over the last decade that it could add value to what many
considered to be commodity Unix to become the market leader. Now HP fully
intends to do the same in the NT market in the forthcoming decade.
This Aberdeen White Paper examines HP’s NT strategy and concludes
Hewlett-Packard has a legitimate shot at becoming the NT market leader —
provided that it can successfully execute its SEC agenda. And given Compaq’s
lack of enterprise experience, paucity of direct professional services and
support personnel, and its need to protect its primary source of revenue — low-
end PC markets through third-party distributors — Aberdeen believes that HP
can attain its NT market leadership goal, potentially by late 1998.
Background
In March, 1996, Hewlett-Packard (HP) overcame its internal "NT-denial"
problem and formally announced its intention to become the market leader in
the sale of Microsoft Windows NT-based/Intel (Wintel) desktops and servers —
a move that created quite a stir in the press and with HP customers. After all,
Unix and NT compete in many of the same markets — how could HP
rationalize this wholesale adoption of Windows NT Server and still maintain
and grow its Unix base?
In March, 1997 — after a year’s worth of efforts that broadened HP’s PC
distribution channels, streamlined HP’s Wintel manufacturing processes, and
restructured HP’s technology and marketing relationships with both Microsoft
and Intel — Hewlett-Packard answered that NT/Unix question, and also
clariﬁed its overall NT strategy. On March 19th, HP assumed the stage with
Microsoft’s Bill Gates and outlined a joint HP/Microsoft initiative —
"Simpliﬁed Enterprise Computing". In Aberdeen’s assessment, this plan holds
the key to how HP will co-market NT and Unix solutions, coexist with
Microsoft, and co-opt the position of its number one NT competitor —Compaq
Computer Corporation.
Simpliﬁed Enterprise Computing — How HP Will Overtake
Compaq
Ostensibly, Simpliﬁed Enterprise Computing is a comprehensive Microsoft/HP
product/service/support initiative designed to streamline the deployment of NT-
based desktops and servers within an enterprise information infrastructure —
while lowering overall computing costs. The objectives of SEC are to help HP
customers:
1. Reduce the Total-Cost-of-Ownership (TCO) of enterprise information
systems — with products and services that address expenses related to the

deployment and management of desktops and servers within an enterprise
context;
2. Capitalize on the collaborative efforts of HP and Microsoft to create
integrated application and technology solutions — initially in mail and
messaging, Internet/Extranet, technical computing, and transaction
security environments; but ultimately Aberdeen expects numerous other
technology-sharing/joint-development initiatives to be formulated as part
of this agreement; and
3. Solve systems interoperability issues with solutions for heterogeneous
computing environments — including cross-operating environment
systems management tools; cross-system collaborative technical
computing environments; and a wide variety of consulting, training, and
ongoing services and support for NT/Unix system/application integration.
But to Aberdeen, this SEC announcement was far more than another Microsoft
strategic partnership — this initiative contained the core information needed to
ascertain how Hewlett-Packard would establish NT market leadership over
Compaq and all other contenders. Simply stated, the SEC initiative shows how
HP will overwhelm its NT competitors — and, speciﬁcally, how HP will beat
Compaq.
HP will establish the leadership position in NT-based computing by:
Establishing an NT cost advantage — HP will focus on providing
enterprise-grade systems/desktop management products and utilities that
signiﬁcantly lower the overall cost of computing — using these tools
internally, HP believes that it saves almost $200,000,000 annually with its
100,000+ employees compared to enterprises that do not use these tools.
The cost savings that result from HP’s value-add in systems management
will make an NT-on-HP choice one of the least costly approaches to
enterprise computing in the industry;
Using its sheer size and established service/support infrastructure to
provide customers with more and better, enterprise-grade, NT-oriented
service and support offerings — HP has more than 23,500 technical
specialists and innumerable service/support programs already in place to
provide its customers with better options for training, systems integration
(especially in heterogeneous computing environments that entail Unix and
NT), systems/desktop, enterprise networking support, systems availability
services, and more — while PC-oriented suppliers such as Compaq
struggle to build a support organization and gain enterprise experience.
This advantage at the enterprise level will be difﬁcult for up-from-the-
desktop PC suppliers to overcome;
Leveraging its relationship with Microsoft to establish leadership
positions in key markets — HP has carefully chosen speciﬁc technical and
commercial markets in which HP and Microsoft can unite technologies —
creating integrated HP solutions that can be distinguished from other
leading NT platform suppliers. For instance, HP and Microsoft are
collaborating on desktop software products for technical computing, an
area in which the traditional PC suppliers have little experience. Using
their knowledge in technical markets, mail/messaging, the Internet, and
other areas, HP and Microsoft will create integrated and differentiable
solutions for its customers in chosen markets;
Using its direct sales force and sales tools such as Volume Purchase
Agreements for competitive advantage — thereby providing customers
with lower costs and better service;
Using its indirect channels to reach all segments of the NT server
marketplace; and
Using its engineering expertise to establish and maintain NT Server
price/performance leadership — HP is now the leader in NT 4-way server
transaction price/performance with servers capable of providing 9,198
transactions-per minute for $50 per transaction minute.
And given the breadth of the enterprise applications, technologies, and service
solutions that Microsoft and HP have announced — and the size of HP’s direct
service and support organization — Aberdeen expects that Compaq will have
great difﬁculty competing with HP as a single source enterprise solutions
provider.
Simpliﬁed Enterprise Computing — The Target Customers
There are two very different but powerful classes of decision makers in every
major enterprise. One group consists of professional IS executives who are
charged with implementing and prudently maintaining a core information
infrastructure that supports enterprise-wide business processes. The other group
is composed of individual business unit executives who have an IT budget
allocated to meet their speciﬁc objectives. To be successful, an NT platform
supplier must address the needs of both groups.
What both groups have in common from a systems perspective are
requirements for systems Manageability, systems Availability, and optimized

system Performance (referred to by HP as MAP requirements). And both
groups often require professional support resources — consultants and
technical support personnel that can assist in the deployment and ongoing
management of enterprise-grade Windows NT servers.
Where they begin to diverge is the requirement for software that allows for
manageability and interoperability with other operating environments. But the
real contradiction in needs is in the requirements for system supplier support.
IS professionals want assistance in terms of planning, technical knowledge
transfer, training, systems integration, and deployment — often on a global
scale. This type of support is only available directly from a major supplier like
HP. On the other hand, the business unit executive is focused on obtaining and
cost-effectively implementing a system with the highest return on investment
that will meet the department’s immediate functional requirements. This type of
support can be obtained from sources other than a systems supplier, such as
through a systems integrator or value-added reseller (VAR). In HP’s case, an
HP business partner is typically best qualiﬁed and organized to meet the
business unit executives’ demands and provide long-term maintenance and
upgrade support.
As a result of these two differing support requirements and buying dynamics,
HP’s NT sales and distribution strategy must be bifold. HP must position to sell
to and support enterprise-level IT executives using its highly successful direct
sales organization (Corporate Account Representatives — CARs); while, in
parallel, using its business partners to distribute NT servers and workstations to
line-of-business/departmental business unit executives within the enterprise.
Further, HP must help its partners to implementing partner-supplied support
services — particularly in low-end and midrange server environments. To assist
its partners, HP provides packaged support services that partners may chose to
sell to their customers.
At this time, Compaq has primarily serviced the business unit executive
through its army of tens-of-thousands of resellers. This was ﬁne when the
products being sold were low value-add Windows-based PCs and NetWare ﬁle
servers. But Compaq knows that if it is to succeed in selling NT application
servers and NT personal workstations to enterprise-level buyers, it must
structure itself to provide direct sales and professional support personnel that
can take responsibility for a customer’s total success. With this recognition,
Compaq is now attempting to build its own direct sales and support
organization — a move that Aberdeen believes will take Compaq at best ﬁve
years to match HP’s service and support infrastructure.
The Elements of Simpliﬁed Enterprise Computing
To summarize, the SEC initiative contains a number of speciﬁc product and
service components designed to help Information Systems (IS) managers:
1. Reduce computing costs by lowering TCO,
2. Take advantage of integrated HP/Microsoft solutions for the enterprise,
and
3. Improve interoperability between heterogeneous environments
(particularly between Windows NT-based desktops and Unix-based
servers through technolgies and services).

and products — examining SEC from a TCO, joint application development,
and heterogeneous computing perspective.

SEC Objective — Lowering the TCO

Of paramount importance when competing against Compaq and IBM, HP
recognizes that it must reduce the overall cost-of-computing in order to
maintain pricing parity and — if executed properly — potentially gain a pricing
advantage. With Simpliﬁed Enterprise Computing, HP looks to lower the total-
cost-of-ownership for enterprise desktops and servers on three fronts. From
least to most important:
1. HP will build less-expensive desktop and server platforms through a
combination of volume and advanced manufacturing techniques,;
2. HP will expand its production-grade systems management product set to
incorporate Microsoft’s strategic management products and key strategic
management initiatives;
3. HP will increase its NT service and support options, including making
HP’s internally deployed PC-Common Operating Environment (PC-COE)
— a set of management tools and services —generally available. The use
of a common PC operating environment internally has saved HP almost
$200,000,000 in desktop computing costs on a yearly basis — and these
tools, technologies, and methodologies will now be made available to
other enterprises to assist in TCO reduction.
Speciﬁc product and service plans that serve to lower TCO include:
Reduced-cost desktop — HP introduced its plan for a new, low-cost
desktop NetPC — the HP Net Vectra PC — to be made available in
September at an entry-level price of $1,000. HP will target the Net Vectra
to reduce the cost-of-acquisition for NT-based desktops while providing
for increased desktop manageability for environments that have Net
Vectras and NetServers.
Expanded desktop and server management capabilities to incorporate
Microsoft strategic management initiatives — At the SEC event, HP
announced its support of Microsoft’s Zero Administration Windows
initiative as well as its intent to combine various Microsoft Windows
technologies and systems management frameworks (speciﬁcally
Microsoft’s Systems Management Server) with HP’s OpenView in order
to create a highly-manageable, enterprise-grade systems management
environment. By coupling Microsoft management initiatives with HP’s
broad array of management products (including OpenView, TopTOOLS,
and various Data Management Interface-based management products, and
PC-COE), HP is positioned to provide IS managers with one of the
broadest and most open systems/desktop management suites in the
industry — and Aberdeen expects that the use of these tools will quickly
and dramatically reduce NT server and desktop systems management
costs.
Expanded service offerings — As part of the SEC announcement, HP
restated its commitment to provide its customers with the services they
require to design, deploy, and manage NT-based systems — committing to
provide programs that combine up-front consulting, migration tools and
post-sales support as part of a low-risk approach for customers migrating
to Windows NT from alternative platforms such as IBM’s OS/2.
SEC Objective — Windows NT Server-based Solutions for the
Enterprise
Both Microsoft and Hewlett-Packard had a meeting-of-the-minds in the areas
of mail/messaging, Intranet/Extranet, system security, and systems management
— structuring agreements to coexist on all of these technology and application
fronts. Speciﬁcally, Hewlett-Packard and Microsoft will cooperate in the areas
of:
Mail/Messaging — Both HP and Microsoft will adopt a common
messaging-technology strategy. And both companies will promote the use
of Microsoft’s Exchange Server as a strategic Windows NT messaging
solution. In addition, HP will expand its OpenMail messaging service
offering to provide transition services from various mail environments to
Microsoft’s Exchange Server.
Intranet/Extranet — HP will endorse Microsoft’s Internet Information
Server on its NetServer platforms as a comprehensive platform for
Internet, Intranet, and Extranet solutions. And each company will promote
Web-based solutions that can be easily integrated on these platforms.
Virtual Vault — HP will port its Virtual Vault technology (used for secure
transactions across the Web) to NT — thereby greatly enhancing
Microsoft’s ability to provide for secure transactions running in an NT
environment.
Systems Management — HP will make available its OpenView
IT/Operations systems management software on an NT platform by year
end 1997, adding to the wealth of systems management products capable
of servicing NT desktop and server platforms.
In addition, both companies have agreed to work together to optimize HP’s
enterprise offerings on the NT operating environment, announcing a jointly

staffed Enterprise Solutions Center in Redmond, Washington to provide
performance optimization services and proof-of-concept testing for enterprise-
focused software suppliers, systems integrators and customers.
HP has announced a suite of availability support solutions for NT users.
Support solutions will include services aimed at meeting customers ranging
availability needs, up-to and including high-availability. Further, HP has agreed
to endorse Microsoft’s "Wolfpack" clustering technology as its strategic choice
for delivering NT high-availability and performance clusters.
SEC Objective — Solutions for Heterogeneous Environments
In addition to attacking TCO and creating a more integrated HP/Microsoft
enterprise applications environment, SEC has also provided Hewlett-Packard
and Microsoft with the charter to collaborate on joint products, services and
technologies that make it easier for customers to integrate and manage
heterogeneous computing environments. Speciﬁc interoperability solutions will
include:
Reliable messaging — Tools and procedures that leverage industry
standards to provide users with reliable messaging between HP’s
OpenMail and Microsoft’s Exchange Server environments (including
common messaging services, common calendaring, and directory
synchronization);
Cross-platform systems management — HP will now actively sell
Microsoft’s Systems Management Server (SMS) as part of HP’s
OpenView IT/Administration management package; and Microsoft will
start distributing OpenView IT/Administration and IT Operations agent
with the next major release of SMS — greatly broadening HP and
Microsoft cross-platform management capabilities;
Single system log-on — HP and Microsoft will work together toward a
solution for cross-platform single sign-on such that users need only log-on
to their system once and they can then gain access to multiple, disparate
servers and applications without having to relog-on (provided that they
have the right access privileges to log-on to other servers);
Technical Computing — HP and Microsoft will collaborate on basic
technologies for the development of technical and engineering
applications. Speciﬁcally, Microsoft will enable application developers to
link DirectX applications (based-on Microsoft’s three-dimensional,
multimedia DirectX programming environment) into HP’s cross-platform
DirectModel large-scale modeling/collaborative computing envrironment..
This linkage will enable users to create 3D graphics models on their NT
workstations that can be shared and worked-on in real time between
multiple group members. To Aberdeen, this is a highly-signiﬁcant
announcement — HP is positioned, with Microsoft’s help, to capture the
new breed of technical and creative applications that will be developed
using Microsoft’s DirectX — and to drive NT into technical as well as
commercial markets; and
Expanded service offerings for mixed NT and Unix environments — HP
has launched a new support service, Personalized System Support for PC
Servers, providing customers with personalized, proactive, integrated
support for Windows NT and Unix in mixed environments. HP has been a
leader in worldwide enterprise computing support for Unix, and now, HP
has transferred that expertise to the Windows NT environment.
Finally, SEC provides the backdrop for HP to reemphasize that it can provide
NT, HP/UX, other Unix, and mainframe consulting and education programs —
meeting the heterogeneous computing needs of enterprise-level customers.
Competitive Positioning
Compaq: Attempting to Learn New Tricks
Aberdeen attributes Compaq’s lead in the NT marketplace to the aggressive,
cost-saving business model that Compaq pioneered in the late 1980s. Compaq
became the PC industry’s leader because it:
1. Based computing designs on technology standards — enabling Compaq
to capitalize on the economies-of-scale inherent in the use of commodity-
based products to lower platform costs and broaden market appeal;
2. Used innovative inventory management/inventory cost reduction
practices such as the use of just-in-time inventory management methods
and software supplier drop-ships to reduce inventory carrying charges and
ensure that inventory was managed effectively — Compaq has rarely been
left holding-the-bag with aging PC inventory; and,
3. Effectively utilized indirect channels of distribution to lower overall
cost-of-sales — Compaq has almost 38,000 product distributors and value-
added resellers committed to moving its products to market .
When combined, all three factors result in lower overall systems costs —
giving Compaq the strong competitive advantage that has placed it in the

number one position in today’s PC and PC LAN server marketplace. The
historical spill-over effect is that Compaq is now initially number one in NT.
But is the Compaq lead sustainable? For Compaq to sustain its leadership edge
in the NT marketplace, the company will need to counter competitive pressures
in its traditional Windows and NetWare markets while gaining access to high-
end applications markets. And Aberdeen expects Compaq to experience severe
competitive pressure from two directions:
In traditional PC and LAN server markets, direct sales (no middleman)
suppliers like Dell and Gateway, as well as computing giants such as $76
billion IBM, $38 billion HP, and $15 billion Digital Equipment are
positioned to challenge Compaq in NT desktop and server markets on the
basis of price/performance and technology innovation. These suppliers can
now effectively compete with Compaq — they have leap-frogged
Compaq’s manufacturing and distribution models and are positioned to
out-Compaq Compaq in terms of sales, order fulﬁllment, and product
distribution. In order to remain competitive, Aberdeen expects that
Compaq will need to continue price cutting in order to protect its
traditional, mainstream businesses. Further, Aberdeen expects Compaq’s
margins to dip considerably below the current 25 percent range the
company now enjoys as it attempts to remain price competitive over time.
Meanwhile, full service and support enterprise-level suppliers-of-choice
such as Hewlett-Packard and IBM have already stated their intentions to
become NT market leaders. These companies have a major advantage
when competing with Compaq in enterprises with professional IS
departments — both have established direct sales and support
infrastructures in place, as well as decades of enterprise-level computing
experience. Aberdeen estimates that it will take Compaq at least ﬁve years
to build the direct sales force, service, and support infrastructure needed to
address the requirements of IS decision makers in the server midrange and
high-end. By then, the market lead will assuredly have changed hands.
Compaq’s innovative business model provided the company with an early NT
market lead — it was the right-model-at-the-right-time. But Compaq’s
inexperience at the enterprise-server level; its lack of cross-platform
interoperability and management products; and its dearth of direct deployment
and integration services; and inability to support mixed NT and Unix
environments leads Aberdeen to conclude that Compaq will have great
difﬁculty competing with Hewlett-Packard in upscale NT markets.
Compaq’s Great Hope — The Tandem Acquisition
In order to drive a high-end NT agenda, Compaq has needed:
An experienced sales/service/support organization that understands high-
end computing needs — and can assist large enterprise customers in the
deploy-ment of complex, networked, clustered NT-server environments;
and,
Fortune-1000 enterprise server market presence — Compaq has needed to
establish a base in the server market high-end. With the Tandem
acquisition, Compaq gains strong presence in banking, ﬁnancial, and
telecommunications markets. Aberdeen notes that 70% of the world’s
monetary transactions (about $2 trillion daily), 66% of all ATM and credit
card transactions, and 90% of the world’s stock transactions take place
through Tandem computers today.
Tandem, on the other hand, has been in the position of having great high-end
clustering technology, but lacking:
A broad NT product line that could span organizational needs;
Market presence — Tandem has needed to gain NT mindshare; and
Indirect Distribution Channels — Tandem has needed to ﬁnd new ways to
reach the other 95% of the market that does not buy its systems.
For Compaq, this acquisition could yield an instant high-end sales, service and
support infrastructure as well as an established high-end server customer base.
While, for Tandem, this acquisition makes Tandem’s technologies a part of a
broad, expansive NT server line, that gets Tandem’s sales force an instant
entree into every IS buyer’s domicile. In short, this acquisition is a win-win
scenario for both companies — good karma; maybe even perfect synergy.
The primary beneﬁts that customers will recognize from the Compaq/Tandem
acquisition are:
A robust and proven NT clustering architecture — ServerNet hardware
and Non-Stop ServerWare software — available today;
More high-end NT application solutions — Tandem has had pre-
established relationships with the leading suppliers of high-end cluster
solutions dating back to its proprietary days — and has been aggressively
assisting in the porting and testing of these solutions for high-end NT
environments; and

Compaq will now have the ability to directly sell and support complex
high-end NT solutions. Customers looking for an experienced sales and
support organization to address high-end computing requirements can now
turn to Compaq for support.
A broad range of products, from SMP NT systems to NT clusters, and
ultimately Massively Parallel Himalaya systems — all supported by
Tandem’s clustering architecture.
This acquisition represents a fundamental shift in NT market dynamics. To
date, IS managers have not viewed Compaq as a full-service/full-product line,
enterprise- class supplier of NT systems/services. IS managers have seriously
cited Compaq’s lack of a direct sales force and lack of an experienced high-end
service and support organization as reasons that would prevent their adoption
of Compaq at the departmental and/or enterprise level. Suddenly, with the
Tandem acquisition, this will no longer be the case.
But, Compaq’s success in using Tandem to drive into high-end markets will be
predicated on the success of the two companies forthcoming merger — and
their ability to adapt to each others culture and view-of-the-world. And, at this
point, it is still too early to judge whether these two companies will
successfully assimilate.
IBM: Competing With Microsoft Even On NT
IBM, on the other hand, does have enterprise-level product experience, offers
cross-platform interoperability and management products, and can provide
worldwide service and support for its NT-based products and platforms. But
IBM’s NT strategy is somewhat different than HP’s strategy — IBM will sell
and service pure Microsoft Windows and NT environments, but also offers its
own direct competitive application alternatives to key Microsoft product sets.
For instance, IBM offers Lotus Notes and Domino products that compete
directly with Microsoft’s Exchange; IBM offers NT-based DB2 as an
alternative to Microsoft’s SQL Server; and IBM strongly supports the
Java/Corba initiative as an alternative to Microsoft’s ActiveX application
program development environment.
By comparison, HP’s SEC approach calls for a blending of best-of-breed HP
and Microsoft technologies — an approach that Aberdeen’s research shows
enterprise decision makers prefer because of the level of application integration
that results from using as much Microsoft content as possible in their NT
operating environments. As a result, Aberdeen expects HP to compete primarily
with Compaq in the enterprise-NT marketplace rather than with IBM. Aberdeen
suspects that the competition for the number one position will be primarily an
HP/Compaq event, while leaving the number two or three position for IBM and
its loyal accounts.
The Final One-Two Punch
The previous sections have outlined HP’s business and product strategies to
capture the NT marketplace. But, when competing speciﬁcally against Compaq,
Aberdeen expects HP to utilize an important technology edge and a key direct
sales pricing tool to its particular advantage. Speciﬁcally, Aberdeen expects HP
to use:
HP’s cooperative development effort with Intel to build the next, 64-bit,
high-end RISC microprocessor architecture IA-64. HP’s inﬂuence on this
project will most certainly put HP in the driver’s seat in terms of early
access to the initial chip set (code-named Merced) — and can be expected
to give HP a time-to-market advantage when competing against both
Compaq and IBM; and,
HP’s use of the Volume Pricing Agreement (VPA). HP is in the unique
position of owning a major piece of the printer marketplace, as well as key
networking and communications subsystem products — products that
most enterprises already purchase. When these products are collected
under a common purchase agreement, quantity discounts apply — and
these quantity discounts can be extremely signiﬁcant. In this category, HP
can blow Compaq away in terms of structuring a ﬂexible (purchase or
lease), multi-product (platform, peripheral, software, and/or services)
alternative to Compaq’s volume discount agreements. IBM offers its own
VPA — but IBM can not easily offer the same level of discounting on
peripherals or network solutions compared to Hewlett-Packard.
With HP’s technology edge; and with a better pricing model — Aberdeen
expects that Compaq will have a great deal of difﬁculty changing its business
policies and investment tactics to compete head-to-head with HP for enterprise-
level NT business.
HP’s NT Platform Offerings — Not Yet a Panacea
Over the past year HP has broadened its NT desktop and server line — adding
numerous new NetServer products — like HP’s innovative E series for small
business computing — and HP’s Vectra XW for 2-D and 3-D technical
computing.

But, to Aberdeen, HP’s NT server platforms have not been in the industry’s NT
high-availability clustering forefront — a real surprise considering that HP’s
R&D budget exceeds $3 billion annually. And the fact that HP — a company
known for its ability to build-and-cluster enterprise-class servers — has decided
to rely on third-parties (Vinca and Octopus) for NT clustering technology
seems puzzling. Still, Compaq is not considered a leader in high-availability
clustering either — although Compaq does offer NT failover clustering at this
time.
HP’s ofﬁcial statement is that these are interim solutions and that its customers
want "All Microsoft" systems software in their NT operating environment.
Therefore, HP has consistently stated that it is waiting for Microsoft’s Wolfpack
to become a reality — rather than to build a proprietay clustering solution —
before bringing HP-sponsored clusters to market. When Wolfpack is released, it
is reasonable to expect HP to have the service and support expertise needed to
deploy and maintain NT-based, enterprise-grade clustered servers.
Summary Observations
Hewlett-Packard’s Simpliﬁed Enterprise Computing initiative is far more than a
Microsoft/HP alliance — SEC is a strategic plan that shows how HP will
overcome Compaq to become the leader in NT-based computing. To become
the NT market leader, HP will:
1) Lower the Total-Cost-of-Ownership for NT- and Unix-based systems: By
using manufacturing efﬁciencies to lower systems manufacturing costs as well
as using open systems/desktop management tools to lower costs related to
maintaining and administering PC and server environments, HP will beat the
competition on the basis of price;
2) Integrate Key HP Applications with Vital Microsoft Applications: By
integrating HP applications with key Microsoft applications like
OpenMail/Exchange Server or OpenView/Systems Management Server — or
by working with other companies to build collaborative technical computing
solutions — HP will create unique, value-added applications that will beat the
competition on the basis of solution depth;
3) Take the Technology High Road: By aggressively working with Intel to drive
high-end, NT, 64-bit computing — HP will beat its competition on the basis
performance, system headroom and time-to-market. And Aberdeen notes that
HP is a joint developer of Intel’s next generation, 64-bit microprocessor
technology; and
4) Provide the Richest Suite of NT/Unix Enterprise-level Service and Support
Offerings: By bringing customers planning, technical integration, maintenance,
help desk, education, and leasing offerings — HP will beat the competition on
the basis of the depth and breadth of its service and support.
To speciﬁcally beat Compaq — today’s NT market leader — HP will focus on:
Reduced Total-Cost-of Ownership — Both HP and Compaq offer Data
Management Interface (DMI)-based PC/Server management packages
designed to lower overall cost-of-ownership. DMI can be used to manage
not only HP products but also DMI-compliant products from other
suppliers. But HP’s offerings go far beyond simple management of
desktop and workgroup servers, HP can manage an enterprise computing
environment that consists of multiple, heterogeneous servers on a grand
scale. Compaq — by only being able to manage an environment of
Compaq PCs and servers — is simply not positioned to meet this
challenge;
Integrated Application Solutions — Unlike Compaq, HP is a major
supplier of systems solutions — offering mail-and-messaging solutions,
technical computing solutions, and systems software-level solutions in
security, systems/network management. Customers are more likely to turn
to suppliers that have direct experience in application deployment — and
Aberdeen expects that HP will be able to leverage its systems knowlege
and expertise in these areas to better compete with Compaq. In short, HP
is positioned as an application solution provider rather than just a platform
supplier (like Compaq) — and this positioning will work in HP’s favor as
it brings NT into the server midrange and high-end;
Service/Support Infrastructure — HP already has an infrastructure in place
to service enterprise-level customers on a global basis. HP currently has
over 23,500 service/support personnel capable of providing customer
training, consulting/planning services, systems maintenance and the like
— while Compaq can provide relatively few direct corporate resources to
assist IS managers with the deployment of complex, midrange and high-
end systems;
Feet on the Street — HP already has a large, experienced, enterprise-savvy
direct sales organization in place to assist enterprise IS managers in
choosing the appropriate solutions partners and systems needed to address
their speciﬁc enterprise requirements — while Compaq drives the lions
share of its sales through indirect sales channels that are most familiar
with supporting departmental-level managers. Enterprises seeking to
decrease total IS costs typically want to standardize on as few technology

components as possible can expect consistent, global sales and planning
support from HP’s direct sales organization;
Enterprise Experience — HP has proven that it can win in enterprise-level
competition based-upon its current position as number one in the Unix
server market with over 50 percent marketshare. HP knows and
understands enterprise needs for reliable systems and related services for
system deployment, training, management, and ongoing support.
The Volume Purchase Agreement as a competitive advantage — HP can
use its Volume Purchase Agreement as the means to provide steep
discounts to enterprises that buy HP’s NT and Unix platforms, peripherals,
and services (desk-tops, servers, printers, networking, design and
deployment, etc.). And, when enterprise-level IS managers extend HP’s
VPA plan to the departmental level, it is likely that business unit
executives will also purchase HP NT workgroup and desktop systems to
take advantage of lower pricing — creating a desktop-drag effect that will
give Hewlett-Packard an even bigger share of the total NT marketplace.
To Aberdeen, there is one absolute in the forthcoming competition for
ownership of the number one position in enterprise-NT market leadership —
HP has already proven that it knows how to sell to and provide services for
enterprise IS executives, managers, and purchasers. And with Hewlett-
Packard’s advantages in applications integration, numerous technologies,
service and support infrastructure, and the ability to put the enterprise on one
large, volume discount schedule, it will be Hewlett-Packard — not Compaq —
that will prove to be the most formidable competitor in the enterprise NT
marketplace.

## Prescience Detail

### Prediction Assessment: 3/5

Aberdeen's August 1997 white paper showed strong analytical insight into market dynamics but missed the ultimate outcome (HP-Compaq merger rather than HP surpassing Compaq).

#### Correct Predictions
1. **Dell disrupting Compaq in low-end markets (CONFIRMED):** Aberdeen identified Dell's direct model as having "leap-frogged" Compaq. Dell surpassed Compaq as the PC market leader in 2001, exactly as predicted.
2. **Compaq margin compression (CONFIRMED):** Aberdeen predicted Compaq margins would fall below 25% due to price competition. Compaq did face significant margin pressure through 1999-2001.
3. **Tandem acquisition enabling Compaq enterprise credibility (CONFIRMED):** Aberdeen's analysis of Tandem synergies proved accurate. Compaq did use Tandem's enterprise sales force and high-end technology to challenge HP.
4. **IBM NT competitive-alternative strategy (CONFIRMED):** Aberdeen correctly identified IBM's differentiated approach (Notes vs Exchange, Java vs ActiveX) as different from HP's all-Microsoft alignment.
5. **HP enterprise service advantage (ACCURATE):** HP's 23,500+ technical specialists did prove to be a durable competitive moat.

#### Less Accurate Elements
- **HP achieving NT market leadership by late 1998 (WRONG):** HP never independently surpassed Compaq in NT. Instead, HP merged with Compaq in 2002.
- **Merced timing (WRONG):** HP's expected early Merced advantage didn't materialize; Itanium shipped 2001, not 1999/2000. Itanium ultimately failed.
- **IBM as #2 or #3 behind HP/Compaq (PARTIALLY WRONG):** IBM maintained strong enterprise NT presence but the competitive dynamic was more complex.
