Hewlett-Packard V-Class: Providing Enterprise
Customers With the Power Path They Need
May 22, 1997 - The name of the game in enterprise servers is "POWER".
Experienced IS executives demand that their server-supplier-of-choice have the
technology necessary to build the largest systems possible. The reason - new
and often unexpected performance requirements are growing at an increasing
rate. These new high-end performance requirements materialize as the use of
integrated client-server application suites are expanded well beyond original
projections; as enterprises acquire others and merge IS departments; as large
data warehouses to support expensive marketing efforts are mandated; as
legacy mainframe applications are replaced with UNIX-based ones to resolve
Year 2000 difﬁculties; and as enterprise computing reaches the "extended
enterprise" of business partners, customers, service providers and employees in
a comprehensive electronic business framework.
Hewlett-Packard (HP) today proved that it will not be outdone in the area of
high-end performance with the introduction of its new V-Class HP 9000.
Incorporating several new and advanced technologies, HP projects that the V-
Class will initially provide approximately a 50% improvement over today's
highest online transaction processing (OLTP) performance for any symmetric
multiprocessing (SMP) server in the industry. In addition, HP claims that the V-
Class will have the industry's highest decision support performance for a single
SMP system.
But what will excite IS decision makers even more about the V-Class is that the
initial product announcement of the V2200 model merely represents the
beginning of a new, highly scaleable server design from HP. HP believes that
by the end of 1998 it will increase V-Class OLTP performance to roughly twice
its initial throughput and top-end decision support performance over four fold.
In addition, NUMA (non-uniform memory access) in 1999 will extend system
performance beyond 32-way SMP. For enterprise IS executives, this represents
never having to say they are sorry because their systems could not keep up with
new information processing demands.
Executive Summary
Hewlett-Packard announced today the introduction of its new, high-end SMP
servers - the V-Class. Based upon supercomputing technologies that HP
acquired when it purchased Convex, the V-Class will be HP's SMP design for
high-end commercial applications such as OLTP, decision support, and batch
processing.
HP has shown a roadmap for harnessing the power of hundreds of processors to
achieve the industry's highest levels of commercial application computing. The
V-Class is the packaging for the SMP systems required to achieve this vision.
Key to the V-Class is a new 15.3 GB/sec. crossbar backplane called the
HyperPlane. HP believes that the HyperPlane has the bandwidth necessary to
support at least the next three generations of processors along with their
associated large-size caching and fast I/O subsystems.
The initial V-Class enterprise server will be the V2200. Scheduled for general
availability by November 1997 in conjunction with the release of HP-UX 11.0,
it will initially support up to 16 PA-8200 processors and 16 GB of memory. A
32-processor model incorporating the more powerful PA-8500 and the ability to
support 32 GB of memory should be released during the second half of 1998.
This 1998 version should deliver between 60%-90% greater OLTP performance
at the high-end than the initial 1997 V2200 Enterprise Server.
Further pushing the performance ceiling, HP plans on releasing in 1999 the
next-generation V-Class server that will deliver up to another two-fold
improvement in OLTP performance. This may include the use of NUMA
technology and Intel's Merced, or IA-64, post-RISC processor.

HP's objective is to build upon the industry-leading performance of its PA-
RISC processor technology to deliver industry-leading commercial application
SMP servers. And with the ability to meet its customers' highest performance
computing needs, HP will be viewed as a key supplier for building an
enterprise's total information infrastructure. This announcement will
communicate to HP's customers the roadmap for the next two years to regain
and preserve high-end performance leadership for enterprise-level production
computing.
V-Class - More Power Thanks to a New Crossbar Interconnect
The new HP 9000 V2200 Enterprise Server is Hewlett-Packard's most powerful
SMP server to date. It delivers its power through balanced scaleability - and the
key to this performance is HP's innovative HyperPlane crossbar technology.
HP's HyperPlane crossbar is a backplane design - already proven in the super-
computing arena - that is ideally suited to provide the balanced scaleability
required for high-volume OLTP, decision support, and batch applications. For
IS managers tasked with running huge, data-intensive applications, this
backplane can handle massive amounts of data trafﬁc among its 64-bit
processors,
240 MB/sec. PCI I/O channels and large SDRAM boards. And because this
backplane can move large volumes of data among multiple processors,
processors do not have to wait for data to arrive - meaning that the IS manager
will reap the beneﬁt of high-end 64-bit processing without being strangled by
interconnection bottlenecks.
In the HP 9000 V2200 Enterprise Server, the HyperPlane crossbar can
accommodate up to eight simultaneous non-blocking transactions from CPUs
and I/O channels to the memory subsystem (see Figure 1). It operates at 120
MHz (8.33 nanoseconds) over a 64-bit path yielding a crossbar port bandwidth
of 960 Mbytes/sec. in each direction. Unlike some multiprocessing
architectures based on switched on/off access, this design provides non-
blocking access so that all ports can be operating at full bandwidth provided the
target ports do not conﬂict. With this architecture, the aggregate bandwidth of
the crossbar is, therefore, 15.36 GBytes/sec.

The V2200 Enterprise Server incorporates HP's fastest 64-bit PA-RISC
processors (PA-8200 this year and PA-8500 in 1998) packaged in an up to 16-
way (32-way in 1998) symmetric multiprocessing system. Customers can
conﬁgure the V2200 with up to 16 GB of memory with 256-Mbit SDRAM (up
to 32 GB with 512-Mbit SDRAMs in 1998) and obtain up to 1.9 GB/sec. (up to
3.8 GB/sec. in 1998) I/O throughput using up to 24 PCI slots.
Enterprise Parallel Server - Beyond V-Class
For those enterprises that want to loosely connect V-Class SMP servers together
for LAN consolidation, very large data warehousing, or specialized OLTP
applications, HP states that the V-Class will be supported in its EPS (Enterprise
Parallel Server) hybrid architecture. In late 1997, HP will introduce the EPS40,
composed of up to 16 V2200 nodes - a maximum of 256 processors.
While LAN consolidation can lower IS executives' operational costs, the
greatest interest in using V-Class servers in an EPS conﬁguration will come
from power-starved IS managers implementing huge enterprise-level data
warehouses supporting operational data mining functions. HP already can show
TPC-D (the industry standard benchmark for decision support applications)
performance leadership by a sizable margin with its K-Class servers conﬁgured
in an EPS22 and it expects to more than double K-Class-based performance
with the incorporation of the V-Class.
Price and Uptime Protection
HP is well known for protecting its customers' investment in its servers. The V-
Class will be no exception.

The HP 9000 V2200 Enterprise Server is being publicly announced in May for
delivery in November. In order that IS executives can plan with conﬁdence for
their V-Class implementation, HP is offering up to a 100% trade-in credit to
users that purchase either new T600 or K-Class servers as interim systems. This
action reduces enterprises' transaction risk regarding which HP server to buy
and when.
In addition, HP's Worldwide Customer Service Organization (WCSO) has
announced that it will guarantee 99.95% availability (this translates into only
4.5 hours of unplanned downtime per year) for customers that maintain HP
servers according to its prescribed service requirements. If the servers under
contract exceed this downtime level, HP will pay predetermined penalties.
Both these actions demonstrate HP's commitment to putting its money where
its marketing is to protect its enterprise-level customers' best interests.
ERP and RDBMS - Looking Into the Future From Where-We-
Are-Coming
Enterprise executives in the Global 2000 that have successfully implemented
integrated, client-server enterprise resource planning (ERP) or large, complex
RDBMS applications consistently report that they need more POWER. More to
the point, one of their greatest fears is that the success of their application
implementations will create more demand than current systems can provide -
no matter what the price.
In addition, Aberdeen recommends to its clients that they always buy in the
middle of the server-performance spectrum for any new application
deployment. This leaves sufﬁcient headroom to accommodate future capacity
requirements.
The V-Class will have an immediate appeal for both IS executives running
successful ERP and RDBMS applications that need additional capacity as soon
as possible and those that are planning their longer-term requirements for new
enterprise client-server applications. If the V-Class can deliver industry-leading
performance as promised by HP, it must be a major consideration for IS
decision makers looking after the best long-term interests of their
organizations.
Year 2000 Mainframe Replacement and Batch Processing -
Looking Into the Non-Traditional Future
A ﬁnal point to consider - where will all those non-repairable mainframe
applications with the Year 2000 virus go? And when? Aberdeen believes that
many of these applications, including legacy, in-house developed batch
applications, will have to be replaced between now and the end of 1998.
(Prudent IS procedures call for a year of testing during 1999.)
With its high-end performance, large I/O system bandwidth, support for
mainframe-level peripheral devices from Storage Technology and EMC, and
reliability/availability/serviceability characteristics, the V-Class will be an
excellent replacement platform for hosting enterprise-level, formerly-running-
on-a-mainframe applications. And this includes very large batch applications
that need to beat the wall clock to completion.
Competitive Positioning - The Challengers and Their
Challenge
HP's competitive positioning for the V-Class Enterprise Servers is to provide
unprecedented levels of OLTP and DSS (decision support system) performance,
along with proven availability, comprehensive support, and systems
management. In a nutshell, HP anticipates that the V2200 Enterprise Server
will be the leader for enterprise-level, high-end production computing power.
Sun Microsystems - Head-to-Head Against Ultra Enterprise
HP's competitive mission for the V2200 Enterprise Server is to overcome Sun
Microsystem's performance claims for the Ultra Enterprise 10000 for decision
support applications and the Ultra Enterprise 6000 for OLTP applications.
Sun has reported that it will ship a 16-processor Ultra Enterprise 6000 capable
of producing 23,000 transactions per minute (tpm) in August 1997. This server
will be positioned against HP's 16-processor V2200 scheduled for shipment in
November though HP expects the V2200 to be signiﬁcantly more powerful.
HP believes that the TPC-D results it demonstrated in April 1997 clearly blunt
Sun's competitive claims for the Ultra Enterprise 10000. Running the 300 GB
TPC-D benchmark on a 64-processor Ultra Enterprise 10000, Sun achieved a
power rating of 2,009 QppD, a throughput of 1,242 QthD, and a price/perfor-
mance of $3,326/QphD. An HP EPS22, using the smaller K-Class nodes and
only 48 processors, demonstrated a power rating of 3,416 QppD, a throughput
of 1,674 QthD, and a price/performance of $3,007/QphD. Or, to summarize, the
HP EPS had greater performance and a lower price per unit of performance for
decision support applications. HP projects that the V-Class and associated
EPS40 will further magnify its already substantial competitive performance
positioning against Sun for enterprise-level decision support applications.

IBM - The PowerPC Chip, Scaleable Parallel (SP), and
Clustered Mainframes
Once considered to be HP's primary competition in the midrange, the IBM
RS/6000 PowerPC Server and the PowerPC microprocessor upon which it is
built have been serious large-corporate IS disappointments by failing to keep
up with enterprise-level performance requirements. At this time, IBM's highest
reported transactions per minute performance has been under 7,500 with a 4-
way server incorporating its new 604e processor - obviously not in the same
league as the HP K-Class (approximately 15,000 tpm) let alone the V-Class.
The RS/6000 SP - a hybrid architecture blending elements of SMP and MPP
(massively parallel processing) design - has had high-end success for decision-
support and LAN server consolidation applications. To show its capabilities for
decision support applications, IBM has published 300GB TPC-D results of
1,361 QppD, 916 QthD and $11,970/QphD, all signiﬁcantly inferior to HP's
results with EPS22. But more discouraging, IBM's RS/6000 used 128
processors versus HP's EPS22's 48 processors. IBM has not released OLTP
results for the SP.
Finally, IBM is recommending that its mainframe user base migrate to air-
cooled, CMOS-based, SMP mainframes running Parallel Sysplex clustering.
This sounds very similar to the EPS40 with V2200 nodes. While IBM will not
publish industry-standard performance and price/performance benchmarks for
mainframes, Aberdeen would project that datacenter managers will obtain
better OLTP and decision support performance and price/performance from a
V-Class UNIX server and an industry-leading RDBMS than from an MVS
mainframe and DB2. As a result, large enterprise-wide mainframe applications
that cannot be repaired to be Year 2000 safe may ﬁnd a safe harbor by having
their functionality replicated on V-Class Enterprise Servers.
Digital Equipment Corporation - TruClusters And AlphaServer
Digital reports that its UNIX-based TruCluster product offering currently holds
the performance throughput record with over 30,000 tpm. Despite the
performance of this AlphaServer cluster, Digital's sales have been hampered by
its lack of market focus behind Digital UNIX products in favor of NT. Digital's
corporate strategy is now closely tied with Microsoft and its ability to bring
Windows NT and BackOfﬁce products into the enterprise computing
infrastructure. This has relegated Digital to an almost irrelevant position in the
commercial UNIX market.
However, Digital's performance claim for an 8-processor SMP AlphaServer,
using 350 MHz Alpha processors, of over 14,000 tpm is impressive, though not
as impressive as the 4-way K460 at ~15,000 tpm - and Digital will have to
invest in UNIX SMP server engineering if it hopes to catch-up to HP's V-Class
by November.
NT - Maybe Someday, But Not This Millenium
The high-end performance of the NT server line is approximately 8,000 tpm at
this time. However, numerous suppliers are promising eye-popping scaleability
that may be delivered at some future date - but not necessarily with the
reliability, availability, serviceability, and security features IS executives
demand. At this time, the HP V-Class is designed for running an enterprise's
business-critical production applications while NT servers are trusted for
departmental and workgroup applications only.
Summary Observations and Conclusions
HP's May 22nd announcement was a performance event. In addition to showing
the V-Class for the ﬁrst time, HP also announced that it would increase the top-
end performance of the midrange K-Class and current high-end T-Class. In
addition, HP described performance improvements in I/O, high-speed
networking, and ﬁber channel-based peripheral products. Taken together, these
efforts represent the comprehensive design efforts required to deliver high-
performance production systems to enterprise-level IS executives.
But just as importantly, HP is delivering the robust operating environment
software required to manage enterprise-level production systems and integrate
them with more junior departmental servers and end-user desktops. In addition,
critical component products, service and support supplied by HP and its allied
business partners provide IS executives with the resources they need to
implement applications on HP servers. And HP offers the ﬁnancing packages
required to allow for the non-disruptive planning and budgeting of large
enterprise-class systems.
HP is also focusing much of its resources on lessening the woes encountered in
managing large numbers of end-user PCs. Why is this important as part of the
V-Class announcement? Simply because it demonstrates HP's commitment to
supporting senior IS executives across the spectrum of computing solutions
they are required to provide within their enterprise. This broad range and depth
of support of open systems components for delivering products and services
across the entire high-end computing value chain is not matched by any other
systems supplier.
Finally, HP has always been conservative about both its future delivery dates
and performance objectives. While today's V-Class announcement included the
roadmap for future deliveries, this roadmap is required for enterprise-wide IS

planning executives that need to know what servers to expect and when. These
executives have come to rely on HP more than any other supplier to deliver the
products it promises on time. Between now and the November 1997 shipment
of the V2200 Enterprise Server, both the T600 and K570, along with their EPS
conﬁgurations, will have sufﬁcient performance for all but the most demanding
applications.
Aberdeen would recommend that IS executives responsible for managing
enterprise-level production systems of all types - mainframe and UNIX -
evaluate how the V-Class can provide the reliability, scaleability, and pure
performance POWER that their users are currently demanding or will be in the
near future.

## Prescience Detail

### Prediction Assessment

**Overall Prescience Score: 3/5**

Aberdeen's May 1997 assessment of the HP V-Class showed mixed prescience:

#### Correct Predictions
1. **V2200 delivery schedule (CONFIRMED):** Aberdeen trusted HP's November 1997 schedule and HP delivered. The profile noted HP's "conservative" approach to delivery commitments — historically accurate.

2. **Mainframe replacement demand (CONFIRMED):** Aberdeen predicted many Y2K-afflicted mainframe applications would migrate to UNIX servers by 1998. The Y2K mainframe replacement wave was real, though the magnitude was partly overstated.

3. **HP performance leadership (CONFIRMED short-term):** HP V-Class did achieve OLTP and DSS performance leadership in 1997-1999 as promised. The TPC benchmark advantage over Sun and IBM proved accurate.

4. **NUMA as next scalability step (CONFIRMED conceptually):** NUMA did become the standard approach for multi-socket server scaling, though HP's specific V-Class NUMA timeline (1999) was delayed.

#### Less Accurate Elements
- **NT enterprise dismissal (WRONG):** Aberdeen's confident assertion that NT would not be viable for enterprise production workloads "this millennium" proved incorrect. Windows 2000 (released Feb 2000) and Windows Server 2003 gained significant enterprise production traction.
- **Merced in 1999 (WRONG - delayed):** Intel Itanium shipped in 2001, not 1999. Itanium ultimately failed to displace x86-64 servers and was discontinued in 2021.
- **HP long-term UNIX leadership:** The HP 9000 line was discontinued in 2003; HP Integrity (Itanium) had limited success; x86-64 eventually dominated enterprise server market.
