IBM’s NT Strategy: Moving NT Upscale with

Preface
The political, religious, and technological battles between Microsoft with its
NT operating environment and IBM with OS/2, OS/400, AIX and MVS have
ended — and a surprise winner has been declared: the enterprise information
technology customer. IBM has just stepped forward with a powerful, new,
public NT-in-the-enterprise message — one that allows IBM customers the
freedom-to-choose what-ever operating system and application environment
makes most sense to accomplish the application task-at-hand — while IBM
provides the service and support elements that its customers require irrespective
of the operating environment they choose.
To Aberdeen, this announcement represents a major and long-awaited shift in
IBM’s previous anti-NT public position. IBM has refocused on customer
requirements — and has placed particular emphasis on delivering the products,
services, and support that its customers need to move NT upscale within the
enterprise. In fact, IBM will add value to the NT operating environment by
ensuring that IBM’s key enterprise applications, relational databases, systems
management tools, and underlying middleware products are optimized to run
on enterprise-strength NT desktops and servers.
In this Proﬁle, Aberdeen examines IBM’s overall NT strategy — its market
goals and objectives, its core product and service solutions, and its direct and
indirect channel strategies. And Aberdeen concludes that— with its existing
enterprise computing support infrastructure and its unique and differentiateable
set of product offerings — IBM is now prepared to become a major market
inﬂuence in enterprise-level NT computing.
Market Positioning
In the NT market space, IBM has declared its goal — IBM intends to be the
market’s leader in the sale of NT desktops and servers, and NT application
solutions and middleware, as well as the primary provider of NT professional
services and support by year-end 1998!
To become the market leader in sales and services, IBM is broadening its NT
PC desktop and server product lines (recent introductions include IBM’s
Professional Workstation as well as IBM’s Domino servers); increasing its
support personnel headcount (currently IBM has 1,200 NT-trained
service/support representatives); and adding enterprise-strength application and
middleware solutions to its NT portfolio in order to attract large enterprise

customers (discussed in greater detail in the "Core NT Software Products"
section).
From a competitive perspective, IBM expects Compaq Computer (the current
market leader with approximately 35% marketshare) and Hewlett-Packard
(running neck-and-neck with IBM in the 16% marketshare range) to be its key
competitors. And IBM intends to beat these competitors on the basis of the
strength and breadth of its large, well-established, and enterprise-savvy systems
support and integration organizations, as well as with its numerous, IBM-
developed, scalable and replicable enterprise application and middleware
solutions.
Aberdeen expects IBM to emphasize its vast worldwide service and support
organization and its enterprise systems experience as primary differentiators
when competing with Compaq. Compaq does not have a worldwide service and
support organization in place to help address enterprise-level sales, service and
support requirements — and Aberdeen suspects that it would take Compaq at
least ﬁve years to assemble a global support infrastructure capable of
effectively competing with IBM. Further, Compaq does not have a long history
of enterprise-level systems integration or complex systems management
experience — Compaq has made its mark in the NT low-end at the desktop and
workgroup server levels and lacks credibility as a high-end production
applications systems provider. As enterprise customers search for fail-safe, end-
to-end NT transaction processing solutions or for multi-system enterprise
management applications, Aberdeen expects that existing Compaq customers
will turn to the more experienced enterprise suppliers such as IBM rather than
take their chances with Compaq in high-end NT markets.
When Hewlett-Packard (HP) and IBM compete, Aberdeen expects IBM to
focus on differences in the two companies’ NT strategies— especially in the
area of enterprise application/middleware offerings — as the means to contrast
supplier product and service offerings:
From a strategic perspective, Hewlett-Packard, with its recent "Simpliﬁed
Enterprise Computing" initiative, has positioned itself as provider of
Unix/NT interoperability technologies and services — with a major focus
on reducing the cost-of-ownership for both systems environments through
better systems management tools. HP’s strategy revolves around the
melding of its Unix offerings with similar Microsoft offerings — for
instance, Microsoft and HP will work on common directory services and a
common mail system such that HP’s Open Mail and Microsoft’s Exchange
can communicate freely and transparently. Applications unity, linking
existing HP applications with Microsoft applications, is a central theme at
Hewlett-Packard.
IBM’s strategy, on the other hand, de-emphasizes the melding of IBM
applications with Microsoft applications — IBM is clearly showing that it
believes that it can deliver enterprise-strength, robust applications to
market on a more timely basis (and with greater functionality) without
having to merge applications and middleware development efforts with
Microsoft. But, Aberdeen notes that IBM customers who desire pure
Microsoft NT application environments, or best-of-both-worlds hybrid
environments can also turn to IBM for integrated systems solutions.
Initial Aberdeen research has indicated that enterprise Information Systems (IS)
managers are reacting favorably to IBM’s NT strategy of providing application
alternatives to Microsoft products — these managers have expressed general
concern about the maturity of Microsoft product offerings such as Transaction
Server and the robustness of SQL Server — and welcome the more mature and
proven applications/databases that IBM is providing. Additionally, numerous IS
managers have indicated that they are also concerned with being over-reliant on
Microsoft as their only source of supply for the server operating environments
and enterprise-level applications/middleware — they do not necessarily believe
that Microsoft can deliver on all of its product promises. In view of these
ﬁndings, IBM’s strategy of providing alternative-to-Microsoft applications will
have a strong appeal to enterprise customers looking to reduce their
dependence on Microsoft and who require more mature, NT-based enterprise
applications and middleware within their information systems infrastructure.
Core NT Software Products
The basis of IBM’s software philosophy is to provide applications and
middleware solutions that protect its customer’s large investments in corporate
applications, enterprise servers and databases, and lay the foundation for future
network-based computing. To this end, IBM has architected numerous software
solutions that span across multiple systems architectures — and in so doing,
protect their customer’s hardware and software investments while providing a
growth path into new technologies. And this philosophy now carries forward to
the NT Workstation and NT Server operating environments — with key IBM
NT software offerings in the following areas:
Groupware/Internet — with IBM’s Lotus Notes and Domino;
Data Management — with IBM’s DB2 Universal Database;
Systems Management — with IBM’s Tivoli TME environment;
Storage Management — with Adstar Distributed Storage Manager
(ADSM);
Enterprise Communications/Networking — with IBM’s Communication
Server;

Transaction Processing — with IBM’s Transaction Server (includes CICS
for NT and the Transarc Encina transaction monitor);
Tools — with IBM’s VisualAge product set and Java initiatives; and
Middleware — with IBM’s MQSeries.
IBM has positioned these products to compete with, or in many cases to
complement, Microsoft application offerings — an approach that clearly
differentiates IBM from many follow-the-pack NT solutions providers. The
remainder of this section discusses the differences between Microsoft and IBM
NT solutions.
Lotus Notes and Domino
The Lotus Notes environment consists of a suite of personal productivity,
electronic mail, groupware and collaborative computing technologies designed
to enable users to develop common business processes in order to interact
intuitively and efﬁciently with other information workers — Notes products,
therefore, compete directly with Microsoft’s Exchange product offerings.
Lotus Domino is a powerful, Internet-based, back-end server platform that
enables organizations to use highly-collaborative Notes technologies over the
Internet. IBM recently introduced Domino Mail services — a Domino light-
like product — that provide users with mail, chat, integrated calendar and
scheduling functions; as well as Lotus Go, a complete HTTP server
environment that enables users to create their own Web environments(available
in Q2 1997). As an Internet and Mail server, Domino competes with
Microsoft’s Internet Information Server (IIS), as well as with Netscape’s
SuiteSpot.
The key to IBM’s positioning of both Notes and Domino is software depth and
availability —Notes and Domino offer mature, proven, scalable, cross-platform
collaborative computing solutions — solutions that offer rich functionality and
performance today. And the word "today" is key — in recently conducted
interviews with Aberdeen analysts, numerous IS managers described their
disappointment with Microsoft’s late and partial delivery of Exchange — and
have chosen to adopt Notes/Domino environments because of the availability
of functionally-rich products from a reliable, known-and-trusted supplier —
IBM. As Microsoft continues to fail to meet software delivery time tables,
Aberdeen expects this migration trend to continue as IS managers tire of
waiting for Microsoft and adopt non-Microsoft solutions — a trend that bodes
well for IBM’s NT software efforts.
DB2 Universal Database
This PC version of DB2 has been completely rewritten as a native Windows
NT application for NT environments.
One important beneﬁt DB2 on NT brings to the enterprise is continuity — DB2
can now be run as a consistent, multi-platform database across all major
enterprise nodes, meaning that data can be shared in common formats across
multiple systems environments, and existing applications can run queries and
reports against a common database back-end. Another beneﬁt is product depth
— because DB2 development has been taking place for more than a twenty
years, IBM is positioned to claim that is has a more feature-rich product
offering than Microsoft with its relatively young SQL Server product.
The Tivoli Management Environment (TME)
IBM’s TME is a scalable, object-oriented management framework capable of
providing software distribution and installation services, conﬁguration
management, change access privileges, job scheduling and other
monitor/control services for mixed, heterogeneous client/server environments.
TME can integrate various management frameworks such as Microsoft’s
System Management Server (SMS), IBM’s NetView, Hewlett-Packard’s
OpenView, or Sun’s NetManager, as well as dozens of other point products to
manage an entire systems environment.
IBM positions TME as an enterprise class systems and network management
environment with strong security features (see Aberdeen’s TME10 Security
Management Proﬁle — February, 1997) that is capable of managing multiple
disparate nodes within an enterprise infrastructure. Microsoft’s SMS
complements, rather than competes with TME — SMS is more likely to only
be used for the management of local area networks of PCs to perform basic PC-
oriented system and network management tasks.
Adstar Distributed Storage Manager (ADSM)
ADSM provides backup and archive services to nine distinct server platforms
— including OS/2, Windows NT, AIX, HP/UX, Sun Solaris, OS/400, MVS,
VM, or VSE/ESA operating environments. This storage management solution
can provide automated, unattended backups, and perform long-term data
archiving using hierarchical storage management (HSM) techniques. ADSM
also features a Disaster Recovery Manager to help prepare for system failures
and execute a system-wide disaster recovery. Microsoft has no ADSM
equivalent.

IBM’s Communications Server
IBM’s Communications Server provides host and Internet access, multiprotocol
integration, and end-to-end connectivity solutions between Microsoft NT
environments and various other server environments. The IBM
communications server supports multiple protocols including SNA and TCP/IP;
multiple terminal emulators including TN3270 and TN5250 for access to
mainframe and AS/400 environments; an SNA gateway that enables hundreds
of NT users to easily gain access to SNA services; and a Host-on-Demand 3270
connection using a Web-browser that enables users to connect to mainframe or
other server environments.
IBM positions Communications Server to compete directly with Microsoft’s
SNA Server — a healthy competition considering that both environments offer
many of the same services — although IBM’s offering is richer in protocol
functionality and emulation capabilities. For additional details on Microsoft’s
SNA Server, please refer to Aberdeen’s Microsoft SNA Server Proﬁle — June
1996.
IBM’s Transaction Server
IBM’s Transaction Server for Windows NT is an advanced transaction
processing solution for business critical applications — a product that offers
two distinct approaches to distributed transaction processing. Using a Customer
Information Control System (CICS-based) approach, IBM is able to ensure that
NT applications can work with its CICS transaction processing environment —
the most commonly implemented transaction processing environment in the
world. IBM also offers Transarc’s Encina transaction processing
monitor/engine — a more open systems approach to handling distributed
transactions.
Microsoft’s own Transaction Server (formerly code-named "Viper") can
communicate with CICS environments, but does not host CICS on a Windows
NT operating environment as does IBM. And Microsoft’s Transaction Server is
relatively new to the market, having recently been released in January 1997.
IBM positions its offerings as functionally rich, more capable of bridging to
existing CICS application environments, more mature, and applications-rich
due to more than 25 years of application development on CICS and more than
10 years of applications development on Encina.
Application Development Tools
IBM’s VisualAge family consists of multiple application development
environments, including VisualAge for Basic, C++, Cobol, Java, PacBase,
SmallTalk, and RPG. Further, the VisualAge family includes products for
application management, modeling and design, featuring IBM’s Flowmark,
ProModeler, TeamConnection, and TeamConnection DataAtlas. Heavily
featured in the VisualAge family of products are distributed object technologies
including Java Beans and CORBA.
But IBM encounters heavy competition from a tools perspective when
competing with Microsoft. Microsoft and its partners offer a similar array of
application development products when compared to IBM — but Microsoft has
tremendously more marketshare. Aberdeen attributes Microsoft’s application
development marketshare advantage to the preference of IS developers —
developers prefer to buy products from the same company that provides the
operating environment believing that the development tools are better
optimized and better supported today, and will continue to be well-supported in
the future.
The application development tools environment represents a big opportunity
for IBM in 1997 and beyond. The logic goes that if a supplier can capture the
application development environment, that supplier can also capture the
application systems and support sale. IBM, along with Sun, Oracle, Novell and
several other leading suppliers, are endorsing the Java server-to-client
application development — as opposed to Microsoft’s ActiveX-based
development. And initial Aberdeen surveys in the applications development
marketplace indicate that Java will become a very popular development
environment.
Middleware
IBM’s MQSeries (Message Q) is a collection of robust and proven middleware
products that enable reliable program-to-program communications between
more than 20 industry platforms. Message Q middleware operates in a store-
and-forward fashion, and is ideal for such applications as transaction
processing, collaborative computing, groupware, and messaging.
It is the broad acceptance of Message Q across key industry platforms that IBM
uses to its advantage when competing with Microsoft. Message Q is proven,
makes efﬁcient use of network resources, provides guaranteed message
delivery or notiﬁcation of failure and is accepted throughout the industry as an
important middleware tool for a wide variety of applications. Microsoft’s
competing standard — Microsoft Message Queuing (MSMQ), codenamed
"Falcon" — has yet to establish the same reputation. Recent Aberdeen research

shows a growing enterprise acceptance of MQSeries as an evolving enterprise
defacto standard for store-and-forward middleware.
Service and Support
One of IBM’s most powerful differentiators in the NT space is its services
organization — IBM Global Services. Global Services is immense, IBM has
stationed more than 100,000 trained professionals in 159 countries worldwide
— and these professionals are capable of delivering a wide variety of
professional consulting, training, integration, and deployment services.
The NT marketplace represents an enormous revenue opportunity for IBM’s
Global Services — the NT license count is doubling on a yearly basis while the
cost-of-acquisition for hardware and software represents only 50% of the total
cost-of-ownership. In 1996, more than 725,000 Windows NT Workstation and
Server licenses were sold, almost doubling 1995’s NT license sales of 363,000.
And in 1997, Aberdeen expects that the number of NT licenses sold will double
yet again — reaching 1,450,000 desktops and servers. And for every license
sold, enterprises will require a variety of implementation, training, and
maintenance services — services that IBM is already prepared to provide. IBM
(and its business partners) have set their goals high — they intend to capture 20
percent of the NT services market by 1998.
Channels
IBM separates the NT marketplace into General Business versus Large
Accounts — and services these markets differently. For General Business
accounts — enterprises with less than 1,000 employees — IBM delivers
product to market through dealers, value-added resellers, and independent
vertical and horizontal solutions providers. For Large Accounts — greater than
1,000 employees — IBM assigns a direct sales professional (IBM has 6,000
direct sales representatives globally) and fulﬁlls demand through service
partners, solution providers and direct IBM resources.
As NT servers grow in enterprise computing power, Aberdeen expects IBM to
focus heavily on marketing its NT application and middleware solutions to
large accounts — and these customers can be expected to require assistance in
the deployment and management of NT-based servers. These factors lead
Aberdeen to conclude that IBM is extremely well-positioned with its
enterprise-proven product sets and its existing service organizations to gain
marketshare, particularly in the higher-margin, high-end of the NT marketplace.
Summary Observations
At long last, IBM has stepped forward with an NT strategy that focuses on
giving the customer the technology choices and service/support options they
need to deploy NT-based solutions — rather than focusing on technology
comparisons between NT and OS/2, OS/400 and AIX. IBM has realized that its
customers want NT-based solutions — and is now doing everything in its
power to address its customer’s needs.
IBM’s NT strategy is unique. IBM will sell pure Microsoft NT environments
that feature Microsoft products such as Ofﬁce, BackOfﬁce, Transaction Server,
and the like — delivered on IBM Intel-based hardware platforms. But IBM will
also sell alternative-to-Microsoft application solutions — products that compete
with Microsoft’s integrated applications — but are often more mature, more
feature rich, and deliverable today. Further, IBM will provide the professional
services and support necessary to deploy NT-based servers and desktops —
very few NT solutions providers can offer the design, training, and deployment
services that IBM can offer on a global basis.
To Aberdeen, IBM’s adoption of NT is welcome and opportune news. IS
executives have told Aberdeen that they are desperately looking for assistance
in deploying NT on a broad scale — and require capable, knowledgeable,
enterprise-savvy professional assistance as they look to incorporate NT within
their existing enterprise infrastructure. IBM has stepped forward with 30-plus
years of enterprise experience just at the time when NT is pushing up into the
server midrange and high-end — and IBM is ideally positioned to service its
customers while capitalizing on the immense growth potential for NT systems
and services in today’s information technology markets.
IBM’s success or failure in the NT marketplace will revolve around whether
IBM can get its "valued NT partner" message through to both low-end and
high-end customers. IBM has put the distribution channels in place, has a broad
set of NT-based desktop and server offerings, and has already proven its ability
to successfully deploy NT-based systems in numerous large enterprise settings.
IBM has the NT products and has the ability to deploy those products — it just
took IBM a while to ﬁgure out that an NT choice was okay.

### Importance Rationale
Documents a historically significant strategic reversal: IBM formally abandoning its OS/2 vs NT ideological battle and embracing NT. This is a primary source for understanding how Microsoft NT achieved enterprise dominance and IBM's subsequent enterprise middleware strategy. The study also documents 1997 NT license volume, competitive market shares, and IBM's services positioning that prefigures Global Services growth.

### Relevance Rationale
Directly relevant to the OS wars history of the 1990s, IBM's strategic transformation, and the competitive dynamics between IBM, Compaq, HP, and Microsoft. Documents the precise moment IBM shifted from adversary to NT enabler — a pivotal inflection point.

### Prescience Rationale
Aberdeen correctly predicted: IBM would not become NT market leader (Compaq retained dominance through 1999, then Dell surpassed); Compaq would struggle with enterprise services (Compaq acquired DEC 1998, then HP acquired Compaq 2002); Java would become popular development environment; MQSeries would become enterprise standard (IBM MQ active today). Less accurate: predicting IBM would be NT leader by 1998 — IBM remained 3rd behind Compaq and Dell.
