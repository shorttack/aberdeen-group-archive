IBM's Solution-Centric Global Healthcare
Industry Practice - Just What the Doctor
Ordered
During the early 1990s, healthcare providers underwent signiﬁcant change in
terms of reengineering and reshaping their organizations to be leaner and more
operationally ﬁt. Much of this business process reengineering (BPR) proved
quite successful. But many organizations have decided that it's time to put BPR
principles into action by implementing next-generation software applications.
Aberdeen's ongoing ﬁeld research shows that care-providing organizations
believe healthcare information systems will undoubtedly prove to be a
dominant factor in determining whether they succeed in today's highly
competitive environment.
Slowing this market requirement, however, is the fact that hospital CIOs are
reluctant to work with small independent software vendors (ISVs) acting as
prime contractors. Quite simply, users are demanding solutions that combine
advanced software with proven hardware and networking equipment and --
most importantly -- professional services to tie all the components together.
IBM has recognized this fact and as a result, ISVs and care-providers alike are
attracted to its professional services-based industry banner.
In this Viewpoint, Aberdeen discusses the market forces causing smaller ISVs to
partner with larger solutions-oriented suppliers and shows why the synergy of
IBM's Global Healthcare Industry initiative has allowed it to take a leadership
role in providing a safe and successful choice for wary CIOs.
Executive Summary
It is obvious to all industry watchers that competitive forces in the US
healthcare industry are enforcing a "survival of the ﬁttest" environment. Care-
providing organizations -- hospitals, managed care facilities, clinics, individual
ofﬁces -- are increasingly being pressured to reduce costs while improving
quality of care.
Over the past several years, healthcare providers and managed care
organizations have focused on reorganization and process reengineering to trim
the excess fat from their internal operations and drive down the cost of
healthcare delivery. This trend has proven successful, but eventually there are
limits on how operationally efﬁcient an organization can become through
traditional business process reengineering (BPR) alone.
Once an organization is operationally ﬁt, it must look elsewhere to further
reduce costs and improve service delivery. Aberdeen believes that the carefully
planned implementation of next-generation healthcare information systems
presents a tremendous opportunity to capitalize upon the beneﬁts that have
been realized through traditional BPR.
It is important to recognize, however, that reorganization and "right-sizing"
have stripped many internal IS departments of valuable skill sets and resources.
In fact, Aberdeen research consistently shows that IS departments are often
understaffed and simply do not have the time or money to familiarize
themselves with every new technology, much less implement and successfully
deploy next-generation applications. These organizations are now looking to
their supplier to provide the professional service expertise to customize,
implement and continually enhance new applications.
Consequently, the user community often ﬁnds it too risky to place its future in
the hands of a technically-slick-but-short-on-size-and-services ISV. Aberdeen's
interviews with hospital CIOs reveal that they do not want to be the one to
double a start-up ISV's revenues. Perceived vendor instability is forcing care
providers and managed care companies to hold back. A second key factor that
is slowing sales for ISVs is user uncertainty resulting from the unique technical
foundations of legacy healthcare information systems.
Deﬁning Healthcare's Technology Landscape
In the early 1990s, the adoption of high-performance, low-cost Unix servers
that share processing responsibility with front-end PCs, as well as relational
database management systems (RDBMSs), exploded. It can safely be stated,
however, that the client-server revolution has not yet hit on all cylinders in the
healthcare industry. Now an abundance of ISVs have placed the healthcare

vertical in their crosshairs, many of which are touting "pure" client-server or
internet-based technologies.
What these ISVs have not conveyed to the user community is an adequate
understanding of the dynamics of existing information systems infrastructures
in healthcare. The majority of the information systems that are deployed in
healthcare are based upon proprietary, host-based architectures -- mainframes
and/or minicomputers that are accessed by low-cost terminals. Aberdeen has
identiﬁed several key characteristics of these legacy systems that need to be
better recognized and understood by ISVs if they are to be successful:
Existing proprietary information systems are typically running home-
grown or custom developed applications. Until recently, these systems
have been expensive to develop and deploy and consume signiﬁcant
internal IT resources.
Many of the systems that were developed in the 1970s and 1980s and have
been enhanced by adding rudimentary application functionality without
altering the core framework of the system. This is not unlike adding a new
coat of paint to a garage door every summer without ever sanding it down
and starting from scratch. The end result in many cases are inﬂexible
systems that lack an intuitive user interface.
In many cases, dissimilar systems were purchased and implemented at
departmental levels. Now that organizations want to share information on
an enterprise-wide basis, they are quickly discovering that their legacy
systems are not up to the task.
Today both the physicians and executive management of care-providing
organizations are looking to make use of the valuable data that is
generated by clinical equipment and diagnostic medical devices to
maximize proﬁts. Creating the appropriate links to these systems is an
increasingly important service-based offering that most new ISVs have yet
to adequately address.
Aberdeen believes that healthcare providers that want to move to client-server
architectures face additional challenges above and beyond organizations in
other vertical markets. In many cases these care providers ﬁnd that the ISVs
providing next-generation applications are unable to help them with the more
basic, and perhaps more pressing, issues of transitioning to a client-server
environment. While the application functionality may be rich, complex
integration and data migration issues are not comprehensively addressed by the
ISV community.
Too Many "Software-Only" Suppliers
The HIS market is literally ﬂooded with small-but-rapidly-growing software
companies touting the limitless beneﬁts of their ﬁrst-generation client-server
applications that exist without lugging along any proprietary mainframe or
mini-computer baggage. Attendance at the annual HIMSS (Healthcare
Information and Management Systems Society) exhibition continues to grow at
a torrid pace, indicating strong interest on the part of users. Hundreds of new
ISVs seem to emerge with new products each year.
Aberdeen research indicates that while much of the ISV-developed technology
is promising, the inability to effectively implement these next-generation
information systems is impeding buying decisions. Simply stated, from a user
perspective the ability of the ISVs to provide a high level of ongoing service
and support is suspect at best and nonexistent at worst.
In order for software suppliers to emerge victorious as the healthcare
information systems market matures, Aberdeen strongly recommends that they
establish strategic relationships with service-based solution providers. It is
important to reiterate that these service offerings do not have to be internal to
the ISVs. The eventual winners among the ISVs will accrue between 20%-40%
of their revenues from services, as the bulk of the implementation work will
come from third-party service providers, as was clearly seen in the
manufacturing industries.
This will enable the ISVs to maintain impressive growth rates by selling
software licenses and help them establish legitimate solution-based sales and
marketing strategies that will be well received by the buying community.
Getting the ISVs in line with the user community in terms of services and
overall infrastructure should allow both constituencies to each reach their goals
more quickly and cost-effectively.
Needed: Solutions through Partnerships
While some of the upstart ISVs may be struggling to win deals and enduring
lengthy, sometimes painful sales cycles, Aberdeen believes that it is not due to
a lack of technology or robust application functionality. Instead, buying
hesitation is a result of ISVs' inability to package products and services into a
solution that diminishes the element of uncertainty to a fundamentally risk-

averse healthcare information systems buyer. Speciﬁcally, Aberdeen believes
that there are three key market conditions that are limiting the rapid adoption
and installation of healthcare information systems. They are:
The internal IS departments of user organizations are limited and cannot
be expected to play a major role in implementing next-generation software
applications.
There is an abundance of small ISVs in the market. These suppliers are
having a difﬁcult time building credibility with users due to an inability to
deliver a complete solution.
For decades care-providing organizations delivered healthcare services
and were not expected to function as true businesses - that was the job of
the insurance companies. As economic responsibilities have shifted, care
providers are ﬁnding they must redeﬁne their entire IT infrastructure.
However, many suffer from a "deer in the headlights" syndrome caused by
needing to evaluate too many options at once.
These market realities are forcing care-providers to turn to suppliers that can
assume project ownership, offer signiﬁcant professional service resources, and
understand both proprietary technology foundations as well as leading-edge
applications from smaller ISVs. As a result, the traditional systems suppliers
are valuable partners for the ISV community. Particularly attractive as partners
are those systems suppliers that are already in place at a client.
This is especially true for the supplier with the largest installed base among
healthcare organizations - IBM.
Three Key Strategy Changes at IBM that Mirror a
New Healthcare Arena
Since Lou Gerstner's arrival at IBM in 1993, the company has made three clear
changes in its corporate strategy that have had a signiﬁcant impact on the
company's healthcare business and have helped IBM emerge as a trusted lead
supplier that can help take care-providing organizations and ISVs alike to the
next level.
The ﬁrst was Gerstner's decision to create 11 global industry solutions groups
within IBM and discard what was formerly a country-management
organizational structure. One of these industry groups is IBM's Global
Healthcare Industry. This helped IBM focus even more intently on what has
traditionally been a successful healthcare vertical, at least from a hardware
perspective, for the company.
The second and perhaps more important factor when discussing the healthcare
industry was an enterprise-wide adoption of a service-and-solution-based
strategy. In fact, in 1996 IBM's overall services business represented its fastest
growing segment with a 24.8% revenue increase. 1996 services revenues
reached $15.9 billion. More than perhaps any other vertical market, the
healthcare industry has an unquenchable thirst and undeniable need for
implementation and ongoing support services.
A third factor was IBM's vision of networked computing and its ability to
convey a uniﬁed message to both its installed base and its particular vertical
market customers and business partners. The concept of information sharing
has been well-rooted in healthcare for quite some time and IBM's vision for
turning it into a reality continues to hit home with healthcare executives and
technical ofﬁcers. Clearly there is an increasing acceptance of using
information technology to improve the bottom line.
These changes within IBM coincide with an evolution that is occurring within
healthcare environments everywhere - the institution and acceptance of
managed care principles and capitated fee structures. As the burden of
monitoring the bottom line falls on the shoulders of the physicians themselves,
improving the management of information quickly emerges as a mandatory
survival tactic. IBM now ﬁnds itself in an optimal position to capitalize on
these latest market events.
Proﬁling IBM's Global Healthcare Industry
Aberdeen research indicates that the US healthcare landscape is well-populated
with IBM systems and IBM is indeed leveraging its strong historical presence
in healthcare to help existing customers migrate to new technologies from both
IBM and its business partners.
IBM's Global Healthcare Industry group is comprised of over 200 employees in
North America dedicated to coordinating and focusing the company's vast
hardware, networking, software and professional services resources to meet the
needs of each individual healthcare IS organization.
IBM's offerings are made up of three principal elements. The ﬁrst is comprised
of healthcare speciﬁc IBM software and hardware solutions. The second comes

from IBM's partnership portfolio from which it can build best-of-breed
solutions based upon customer preference and need. The third component is its
professional services arm.
More important than any of the above three elements, however, is IBM's ability
to assume the role of lead supplier for new information technology initiatives.
Aberdeen ﬁrmly believes that it is IBM's project leadership capabilities that
provide immeasurable value to both the independent software vendor and end-
user communities.
IBM Health Network Solutions
IBM's Global Healthcare Industry's solution portfolio is concentrated on ﬁve
areas:
Health Network Foundation - base-level intranet products and services;
Health Network Applications - intranet and internet healthcare
applications;
Health Network Advanced Applications - next-generation software that
includes electronic commerce and decision-support applications;
MedSpeak Technology - IBM is leveraging its leadership role in speech
recognition software in healthcare;
Clinical Trials Management - IBM's ClinWare solution for
pharmaceuticals addresses a critical need in the rapidly changing
pharmaceutical industry.
The ﬁrst area, Health Network Foundation, includes software and services for
creating content, establishing or expanding presence on the Web. These
offerings include:
Lotus Notes(tm) and Domino(tm)
HTML authoring and templates
TCP/IP and related networking services
Internet Service Provider services
Business Recovery Services
Security and Consulting Services
Tivoli for systems management
Web content creation and hosting, including HealthVillage(tm)

IBM's HealthVillage(tm) is an information access tool for the consumer.
Consumers are increasingly being asked to make their own decisions about
their healthcare plans and with this responsibility comes a need for accurate
information about their care-providing organizations. IBM offers an internet-
based solution for a particular health plan or provider that allows the plan to
give their members access to wellness information, provider directories, library
services, discussion groups, as well as member services for those seeking
billing information.
The second area, Health Network Applications, helps organizations create
advanced information networks that make legacy information accessible, house
applications that automate business processes, eliminate the need to re-key
redundant data, and consolidate patient information. Often called a Health Data
Network, these solutions include: encounters, global registration, referrals and
precertiﬁcation, eligibility, enrollment forms capture, claims submission, claims
status inquiry and a master person index, which serves to link patient

information across systems regardless of the patient identiﬁer a particular
system uses. A Web-based user interface is designed for ease of use and a range
of integration services links information contained in legacy systems - either
home grown or developed by a third party.
The third area of IBM's Health Network Solutions is comprised of advanced,
strategic applications that help organizations transform the way they conduct
business. This area includes advanced data mining tools that uncover unique
information for outcomes management and fraud detection, as well as decision-
support tools for both clinical and ﬁnancial analysis.
The IBM Executive Suite solution is a combination of software applications and
consulting services. In partnership with APM Management Resources, a
division of Computer Sciences Corporation specializing in IT management
consulting, Executive Suite consists of three software applications including
ExecIS, an integrated executive information system; BASys, a comparative
benchmark analysis system and data service; and PACE, a detailed clinical
utilization and performance tracking system. All three are designed to use
existing data in legacy systems to produce reports for clinical and operational
managers and, combined with best-practice based consulting services, aim to
address the following key issues:
How is the organization performing?
How does its performance compare to others in the market and the
industry?
How can the institution foster continuous performance improvement
among its caregivers?
The fourth focus area in IBM's healthcare solution portfolio turns to the
company's advanced, continuous speech recognition technology to enhance and
improve the clinician-computer interface. IBM's MedSpeak/Radiology product
is a high-accuracy, continuous speech recognition application for radiology
dictation. This innovative solution allows radiologists to dictate -- continuously
and naturally -- into a PC to quickly and efﬁciently create, view, authorize,
store and even print radiology reports. Because reports can be made available
immediately, turnaround times are reduced from days to just a few minutes.
Radiologists are able to get reports to referring physicians more quickly and
easily.
IBM's ﬁfth product area addresses the needs of the pharmaceutical industry.
ClinWare is targeted at pharmaceutical companies and institutions conducting
clinical trials. ClinWare is designed to streamline the data collection process
and decrease time to market lifecycles.
In summary, Aberdeen believes that IBM has a strong portfolio of industry-
speciﬁc healthcare solutions -- all of which put a premium on the ability to
easily and efﬁciently access and share information.
A Comprehensive Partnership Portfolio
IBM's business partners signiﬁcantly enhance the value that IBM can bring as a
solutions provider by adding value in speciﬁc technology and application areas.
IBM categorizes its healthcare business partners into three groups: managed
care information systems suppliers, clinical point-of-care application providers,
and pharmaceutical companies.
IBM allocates signiﬁcant internal resources to managing and developing its
partner program and is very active in playing a lead role in joint marketing
initiatives. IBM also aggressively promotes collaborative sales strategies and,
in some cases, full-scale sales force integration. IBM's comprehensive facilities
serve as excellent benchmarking testbeds - helping to ensure that real-world
implementations meet clearly deﬁned expectations.
By partnering with IBM, the ISV community stands to beneﬁt in two primary
ways. The ﬁrst is the potential for increased sales and access to entirely new
markets through the leverage of IBM's sales and marketing strength, world-
class professional services capabilities and unparalleled installed base of
customers. Perhaps more important, however, is that IBM's presence and
credibility can dramatically shorten sales cycles for ISVs. Aberdeen believes
that for this factor alone many software suppliers have chosen to partner with
IBM.
IBM's Global Healthcare Services
IBM's Global Services organization has been instrumental in IBM's resurgence
over the past several years - from both revenue growth and customer
satisfaction perspectives. Global Services focuses intently on meeting customer
requirements and it is one of the fastest growing divisions within the company.
IBM's professional service offerings in healthcare include the following:
Process evaluation, analysis and reengineering;

Assessment of existing IS resources;
Systems and complementary technology integration;
Best-of-breed application selection, customization and implementation;
Ongoing implementation support;
End-user training.
IBM realizes that implementation services are no longer necessary add-on
modules but are an integral ingredient in any complete solution. Aberdeen
research indicates that this is what healthcare information systems users are
demanding from their suppliers. As a result, IBM's Global Healthcare Industry
group will increasingly be evaluated based upon the ability of Global Services
to deliver the knowledge and expertise needed to deploy, enhance and maintain
next-generation applications.
Two additional service areas that have been very successful for IBM in
healthcare are network services and outsourcing. Its outsourcing capabilities
include desktops, networks, and data centers, as well as operational business
processes. As part of the IBM Global Network, IBM's network services fall into
ﬁve categories: managed collaborative services, managed messaging services,
managed Internet and intranet services, managed data network services and
networking outsourcing services.
Customer Success Stories
Three ﬂagship real-world examples of IBM's ability to provide complete
solutions to customers include:
Arkansas Blue Cross/Blue Shield, part of a joint venture with two medical
centers, is working with IBM to create a comprehensive health network
that will deliver seamless administration, ﬁnancing and care delivery for
almost half of the covered lives in the state. The network will link
approximately 450 physicians in private practices and clinics, allowing
these doctors to share critical patient information and deliver collaborative
patient care.
Kaiser Permanente of Northern California implemented a Notes-based
case management system that provides practitioners with immediate,
secure access to all clinical and patient related data for all of their high-
risk patients.
Allina Health Systems will offer to its members the Allina HealthVillage,
an Internet-based IBM service, that provides plan members access to
information about medical topics and plan logistics. Included in the initial
phases of the implementation are a provider directory, online member
services such as plan enrollment, health and wellness information, and e-
mail links to a nurse line for advice and appointment scheduling.
Aberdeen Conclusions
Healthcare information systems carry with them enormous potential for
dramatically improving care quality while enhancing operational efﬁciencies
and driving down costs. The healthcare community has begun to embrace
leading-edge technologies and realizes that advanced IT adoption and
integration can and must be used as competitive weapons against rival care-
providing organizations. The questions that follow include "which technologies
are right for my organization?" and "how do we implement a total solution?"
IBM provides solutions that combine underlying foundation and base-level
technologies, advanced networking products and expertise, hardware, and
systems integration and professional services capabilities. IBM puts a premium
on service delivery and execution - a combination that neither the ISVs or
internal IS organizations can provide alone. It is for this reason that Aberdeen
believes IBM will continue to be a valuable partner for ISVs and user
organizations. Quite simply, both ISVs and users need the likes of IBM if they
are to minimize risk and maximize the payback on new applications and
solutions.

### Importance Rationale
Historically significant primary source documenting IBM's 1997 healthcare IT strategy at a pivotal moment — after Gerstner's restructuring and just before the managed care / HMO boom. The study foreshadows IBM's later Watson Health initiative (2015-2022) and documents the ISV partnership model that shaped enterprise healthcare IT procurement.

### Relevance Rationale
Directly relevant to understanding the 1990s healthcare IT market structure, IBM's role as solutions integrator, and the tension between ISV innovation and enterprise credibility. The IBM-ISV partnership dynamic described here is foundational to current healthcare IT ecosystem dynamics.

### Prescience Rationale
Aberdeen correctly predicted: ISVs needing 20-40% services revenue (accurate for EHR vendors like Epic/Cerner); IBM's services-led growth trajectory; importance of speech recognition in radiology (MedSpeak anticipates Dragon Medical); and clinical data mining for outcomes management. Less accurate: IBM's healthcare dominance — Watson Health was sold for parts in 2022 after $5B spend.
