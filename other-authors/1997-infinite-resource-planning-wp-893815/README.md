## Abstract

Aberdeen Group introduces the concept of "Infinite Resource Planning" (IRP) as the next major paradigm in enterprise business applications. The paper argues that the convergence of Object-Oriented Technology (OOT), Internet technology, and Network-Centric Computing Architectures (NCCs) will drive a fundamental transformation of enterprise software. IRP is presented as a successor to ERP, enabling true cross-organizational integration, supply chain visibility, and component-based application deployment via Internet-enabled architectures. Aberdeen contends adoption is inevitable and that suppliers will need to remake their products accordingly.

## Key Findings

- Aberdeen coined "Infinite Resource Planning" as the next-generation model beyond ERP
- Three convergent technologies identified: OOT, Internet, Network-Centric Computing
- Internet would provide "first business context" for widespread OO line-of-business adoption
- Best-of-breed EBA definition shifting from vertical (Accounts Payable) to horizontal (Procurement)
- Advanced Planning Engine Technologies (APET) would enable real-time supply chain visibility

## Context & Prescience

This 1997 paper is a remarkably prescient vision. The "IRP" brand never took hold, but Aberdeen's core predictions proved accurate: the Internet did transform enterprise applications; object/component technologies (ActiveX, CORBA, Java EE) did enable integration; the shift from monolithic ERP to best-of-breed horizontal processes became the dominant model by the 2010s; and supply chain integration via digital networks became a $100B+ market. The paper correctly anticipated what the industry would later call SOA, SaaS ERP, and cloud-based supply chain platforms.
