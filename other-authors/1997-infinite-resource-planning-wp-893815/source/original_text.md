Inﬁnite Resource Planning: A Manifesto For 21st
Century Enterprise Applications
An Executive White Paper
May 1997
Preface
What began as Aberdeen research into the multi-dimensional impact of Internet
technologies on business and enterprise computing has led to startling and far-
reaching conclusions. Aberdeen asserts that enterprises are on the cusp of yet
one more industry-rocking, watershed event. This event is being driven by the
convergence of three major technologies - Object-Oriented Technology (OOT),
Internet technology and Network-Centric Computing Architectures (NCCs).
These converged technologies make possible what Aberdeen refers to as
Inﬁnite Resource Planning (IRP). IRP is a new model for conducting business
and inter-business operations. With IRP, the true integration of cross-functional
and cross-organizational enterprise business applications may now be achieved.
Aberdeen believes that economic and business efﬁciency gains embodied in
IRP capabilities are so compelling that leading enterprises will fundamentally
alter their hard-won best practices and processes and that suppliers of advanced
business solutions and technology will have to rethink and remake their
products, functionality and architectures.
Executive Summary
Aberdeen believes that the movement toward and adoption of the IRP model as
the next signiﬁcant form of computing and of conducting business is inevitable.
Consider these driving and global forces: the geographic dispersion of
enterprises and complexity of multi-pronged and -staged supply chain
relationships; the imperative to put in place more effective communications and
management oversight and coordination across complex business processes;
and the interdependence of supply chain partners to reduce or eliminate work-
in-process inventories and extended cycle-time pressures. Only through a
virtual, yet highly integrated means can enterprises hope to overcome and
excel.
Aberdeen ﬁnds that just as systems and network management provided the ﬁrst
technical context for the deployment of object-oriented technologies, the
Internet will provide the ﬁrst business context for the widespread adoption of
object-oriented line-of-business solutions. Astute enterprises are already
evaluating potential next-century and IRP capable solutions on the basis of the
supplier's adoption of the convergent technologies - objects; the Internet; and
network-centric computing.
Enterprises that are adopting and implementing this mode of next-century
computing are:
cashing in on what has to-date been only the promise of multi-
organization supply-chain management;
forging competitive value-chain relationships;
together with key business partners and suppliers; and
establishing a basis for business to business electronic commerce that can
realize the virtual corporation - business operation-wise and
technologically.
Aberdeen research demonstrates that what is occurring must cause a
fundamental remaking of enterprise business applications. All of the issues
confronted in the past as corporations crossed technology boundaries to achieve
better efﬁciencies and competitive advantage are once again before us. Among
those key issues are the need to:
move information beyond traditional conﬁnes;
get new applications into production quickly;
integrate a Best-of-Breed world; and
ﬁnd new ways to effectively attract, retain and expand Supplier and
Customer relationships.
The Aberdeen IRP model, its functional components and the convergent
technologies make the attainment of these objectives possible in ways not
contemplated in the past - the result will be a new means of understanding and
implementing innovative and competitive cross-supply-chain business practices
.

IRP Overview
Enterprise requirements are once again shifting between the poles of fully-
integrated and best-of-breed Enterprise Business Application (EBA) solutions.
Prospective customers no longer pursue single-function, stovepiped
applications nor total solution alternatives with implementation timeframes so
lengthy as to forestall achieving the near-term beneﬁts of reengineered business
processes. Aberdeen research shows that the high-value lesson learned from
business process reengineering (BPR) exercises is that business operations are
cross-functional activities and that enterprise business applications need to be
designed and deployable across that operational landscape. Enterprises now
increasingly seek solutions like procurement operations that directly address
critical business needs, recognize the cross-organizational nature of the activity,
and provide for ease of integration into other existing or planned applications.
Aberdeen believes that the deﬁnition of best-of-breed EBA has now changed
and the solution once vertically deﬁned by function, e.g., Accounts Payable, is
now horizontally deﬁned as a business process, i.e., Procurement.
The IRP Model provides a fundamentally new way for enterprises to consider
and expand best practice and effective business process across their supply
chain. At the same time, tangible business value will be realized within each of
the participating link enterprises. Beneﬁts include:
more efﬁcient, cost-effective manufacturing & distribution; and
better cash & asset management with lower inventories.
From this cross-company planning ability, realized through Advanced Planning
Engine Technologies (APET), enterprises could now have visibility to
immediately handle demand-side orders and near-immediate access to supply-
side materials - two circumstances that have been long sought yet never fully
attained, until now.
The IRP model also allows business to overcome productivity obstacles that
have historically stood in the way of accelerated movement to new application
and technology breakthroughs. An IRP-based environment means organizations
can now:
more easily interface with new, existing and packaged systems;
achieve rapid deployment of new and virtually web-supplied business
functionality;
support enterprise and Internet infrastructures simultaneously;
leverage no-maintenance thin clients; and
hide existing system complexity.
Aberdeen research shows that just as systems and network management
provided the ﬁrst technical context for the deployment of object-oriented
technologies, the Internet will provide the ﬁrst business context for the
widespread adoption of object-oriented line-of-business solutions. Now that the
long-anticipated distributed interprocess communications and connectivity
technologies - OLE Automation and Controls (OCX/ActiveX), CORBA and
Java - are reaching the market, the realization of distributed, component-based,
on-the-ﬂy applications is within reach. Moreover, the availability of these
technologies also indicates the arrival of a new and fundamental shift that will
cause enterprises to:
reexamine and change how they organize and operate their businesses; and
accelerate evaluations and acquisitions of application solutions that are
inherently Internet-enabled, are object-oriented, and architecturally
support a network computing architecture.
When combined, component technologies, advanced business application
solutions and state-of-the-science best business practices produce what
Aberdeen refers to as the Inﬁnite Resource Planning (IRP) model.

## Prescience Detail

### Assessment: 8/10 — Highly Prescient

Aberdeen's 1997 IRP white paper is one of the more remarkable forward-looking documents of the mid-1990s enterprise computing era. While the "Infinite Resource Planning" label never achieved mainstream adoption, virtually every architectural prediction in the paper proved correct within 5–15 years.

**Accurate Predictions:**

1. **Internet as enterprise OO platform** — Aberdeen stated the Internet would be "the first business context for the widespread adoption of object-oriented line-of-business solutions." This proved exactly correct: Java EE, .NET, and web services became the dominant enterprise application architecture by 2001–2005.

2. **Shift from vertical to horizontal best-of-breed** — The paper predicted best-of-breed would shift from vertical functions (Accounts Payable) to horizontal processes (Procurement). This materialized as procurement platforms (Ariba, Coupa), CRM (Salesforce), HCM (Workday), and supply chain (i2, JDA) all organized around business processes.

3. **Supply chain visibility as killer use case** — Aberdeen's APET concept predicting real-time demand/supply visibility was confirmed by Advanced Planning and Scheduling (APS) systems from i2 Technologies, Manugistics, and SAP APO through 2000–2010.

4. **Component technologies enabling integration** — The prediction that ActiveX/CORBA/Java would enable distributed component applications proved accurate; though CORBA and ActiveX faded, Java EE and web services (SOAP/REST) delivered the integration vision.

5. **Thin client / no-maintenance client** — The browser-based SaaS model fulfilled this prediction exactly, though not via dedicated thin-client hardware.

**Predictions That Didn't Materialize as Stated:**

- The specific "IRP" brand/framework never became an industry standard term; ERP+Internet evolved directly into cloud ERP (SAP S/4HANA, Oracle Cloud ERP, NetSuite) without adopting Aberdeen's nomenclature.
- CORBA's predicted prominence gave way to REST/HTTP-based integration rather than object broker middleware.

**Overall:** The paper correctly identified the convergent forces and their business implications; only the naming convention and some technical specifics differed from actual outcomes.
