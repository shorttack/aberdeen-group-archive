Abstract: Proﬁle
Insession, Inc
Insession — Accessing and Leveraging
Enterprise Transaction Systems
Aberdeen research indicates that today's competitive pressures
are driving IS to build new applications in a rapid, well-timed,
and cost-effective manner.
These applications typically must deliver continually upgraded
competitive advantage, and closely match evolving business
strategies, processes, and architectures. Thus, IS' development
efforts need to be able to build on top of enterprise-scale
distributed "accessware" that can ﬂexibly adapt to new
requirements such as the Internet and promote programming at
a higher level for faster, more cost-effective development and
deployment.
In this Proﬁle, Aberdeen examines one supplier, Insession,
Inc., whose objective is to provide a software solution that
enables the integration of heterogeneous distributed
environments with existing host-based systems. Insession can
be particularly effective for an organization whose goal is to
preserve and leverage investments made in enterprise
transaction systems and associated data and integrate them
with new distributed environments (e.g., the Internet and
departmental client-server applications).

## Prescience Detail

### Assessment: 6/10 — Moderately Prescient

This brief profile makes several implicit and explicit predictions about middleware, legacy integration, and competitive IS dynamics.

**Accurate Predictions:**

1. **Enterprise transaction system longevity** — Aberdeen's implicit prediction that ETS systems would require long-term integration support proved correct. As of 2025, CICS, IMS, and HP NonStop remain in active production at major financial institutions. The legacy integration market has grown to billions annually.

2. **Accessware/middleware market growth** — The prediction that enterprise-scale distributed accessware would be a competitive requirement proved accurate. The middleware market evolved from EAI platforms (Tibco, SeeBeyond) to ESB (IBM WebSphere, Oracle Service Bus) to API management (MuleSoft, Apigee) — a pattern exactly matching Aberdeen's "accessware" vision.

3. **Internet integration imperative** — Aberdeen's prediction that accessware must support Internet requirements proved accurate; web services, then REST APIs, became the standard interface for exposing legacy functionality.

4. **Insession commercial viability** — Partially confirmed. Insession was acquired by Transaction Systems Architects (ACI Worldwide), where it became the Insession Technologies business unit contributing approximately 13% of ACI revenues through 2005. The ICE family of products remained a substantial business. However, Insession Technologies was folded into the broader ACI organization in late 2006.

**What Didn't Materialize as Stated:**

- The "accessware" category label did not become an industry term; the market used EAI, middleware, ESB, and integration platform terminology instead.
- Insession Inc. as an independent vendor did not survive; it was absorbed into ACI, though its technology persisted longer than many 1997-era middleware vendors.

**Overall:** Accurate on the enduring nature of the legacy integration problem; vendor outcome mixed but more positive than most 1990s middleware vendors.
