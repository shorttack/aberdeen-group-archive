Abstract: Profile
Knowledge Discovery One
Knowledge Discovery One's Retail Discovery
Suite:
Promoting Retail Data Mining Applications
To raise revenues, improve margins, and reduce costs, retail
professionals need tools to help them make value-added
decisions based on the vast amounts of information contained
within their continuously changing point of sale (POS) data.
This retailers' reality is now taking on new dimensions as
buyer-loyalty patterns increase competition and require
retailers to understand and predict customer behavior and then
target toward that behavior.
To face these challenges, retailers to date have had two — not
fully adequate — choices. They could select from among
several comprehensive retail solutions, which have strong data
analysis functions that help with inventory and financial
planning, but lack the ability to drill into, and make
comparisons at, the detail-data level. The other solution is data
mining, which delivers the analytic depth but requires
substantial, on-going labor and expense to prepare and use the
system. Moreover, data mining tools have not become
proficient at delivering results to all of the retail professionals
charged with daily decision making.
This Profile examines Knowledge Discovery One (KD1) and
its Retail Discovery Suite (RDS). While providing the
analytical depth needed for retail applications, the RDS
architecture eliminates much of the labor until now required
to create a retail data mining system. Moreover, RDS's parallel
processing techniques will allow the system to scale-up and
speedup as the retailer brings more data into the system for
analysis. And by removing the need for data mining specialists to constantly interact with the system before giving the
results to users, RDS will be able to bring the benefits of data
mining to a wider range of retail professionals.
David Hill

## Prescience Detail

### Assessment: 5/10 — Mixed Prescience

This brief abstract profile presents a mixed prescience picture: Aberdeen correctly identified the retail analytics market opportunity but the specific vendor they profiled did not survive.

**What Aberdeen Got Right:**

1. **Retail data mining market explosion** — The data mining software market grew from approximately $50 million in 1996 to $800 million by 2000, confirming Aberdeen's directional view that retail data mining was a major growth opportunity.

2. **POS data as competitive differentiator** — Aberdeen correctly identified buyer-loyalty pattern analysis and customer behavior prediction as competitively essential for retailers. This is now standard practice through loyalty programs and recommendation engines.

3. **Democratization of analytics** — The need to remove data mining specialists from the daily decision loop was accurate; self-service analytics tools from Tableau, Qlik, and embedded AI in retail platforms have achieved exactly this.

4. **Parallel processing for scale** — Aberdeen correctly identified parallelism as the key to scaling retail analytics; modern distributed computing (Hadoop, Spark) confirmed this architectural direction.

**What Aberdeen Got Wrong:**

1. **KD1 viability** — Knowledge Discovery One did not survive as an independent company. Small, specialized data mining vendors were overwhelmed by SAS, IBM, Oracle, and later open-source frameworks (R, Python scikit-learn).

2. **Specialist-free analytics timeline** — While Aberdeen identified this as the goal in 1997, truly self-service analytics at scale took until the 2010s to materialize through tools like Tableau and Power BI.

**Broader Lesson:** Aberdeen correctly diagnosed a real market need (retail analytics democratization) and identified a genuine product gap, but overestimated the viability of small niche vendors in what became an enterprise-platform-dominated market.
