## Abstract

Aberdeen Group analyzes Eastman Kodak Company's completed acquisition of Wang Laboratories' software division for $260 million in cash, resulting in the formation of Eastman Software, Inc. as an independent subsidiary of Kodak. The study examines the strategic rationale, organizational structure, customer implications for both Wang Software and Kodak BIS installed bases, and the expanding market opportunity in document imaging and work management. Aberdeen views Eastman Software as extremely well-positioned to assist Kodak in expanding its leadership in document-intensive businesses, citing synergies in workflow, imaging, COLD, document management, and hierarchical storage management technologies.

## Context & Outcome

Aberdeen's confident prediction that Eastman Software was "extremely well-positioned" proved overly optimistic. Kodak sold the troubled Eastman Software unit in August 2000 to a privately held investment group headed by William Oates for undisclosed terms — significantly less than the $260 million Kodak paid in 1997. The unit struggled to integrate, faced market headwinds from rapid technology shifts to internet-based document management, and became a distraction from Kodak's core digital transformation challenges. Wang Laboratories, meanwhile, continued its decline and eventually filed for bankruptcy in 1992 (pre-acquisition), had emerged, and the services business Kodak did not acquire continued to contract. The document imaging market itself shifted from the COLD/microfilm hybrid model Aberdeen described toward internet-based ECM platforms (Documentum, OpenText, SharePoint).
