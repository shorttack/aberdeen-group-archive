Kodak Completes Wang Software Acquisition
March 17, 1997 — Eastman Kodak Company announced today that its
acquisition of Wang Laboratories’ Software division has been ﬁnalized. The
resulting new entity, Eastman Software, Inc., synergistically combines the
technology, business partnerships, customer relationships and distribution
channels of both the Kodak and Wang Software organizations. Aberdeen
believes that Eastman Software is extremely well-positioned as an independent
subsidiary of Kodak — strategically aligned with
Kodak’s Business Imaging Systems (BIS) organization — to assist that parent
company in expanding its leadership position as a supplier-partner with
customers in document-intensive businesses and now work management
technologies.
In this Announcement Proﬁle, we will highlight several major outcomes and
implications of the acquisition, including the positive impact on existing
customers, resellers and new prospects. We will also brieﬂy describe the
changing technology and document image and work management market
opportunities that originally led these two companies to consider a pooling of
interests.
Alliance Opportunities
Eastman Kodak Company, a $16 billion multi-national corporation, has been a
dominant supplier of robust solutions for production document image
management applications beginning with its entry to this market segment in
1928. For its part, Wang Laboratories, Inc. entered the business segment as an
ofﬁce automation and computer systems supplier, and later became a
technology innovator with specialization in workﬂow, imaging and computer-
output-to-laser-disk (COLD) software.
Recognizing their overlapping business interests, Wang Software and Kodak
had already forged a successful joint development and sales alliance. In 1995,
after an extensive review of the suppliers of digital imaging, workﬂow and
COLD software, Kodak selected Wang as its OEM partner. Through an
alliance, the companies believed they could accelerate the delivery of new
capabilities to existing customers, especially those that had already begun to
supplement traditional ﬁlm-based technologies with the next generation of
document imaging and work management technologies.
As reported earlier, Kodak paid $260 million in cash for Wang’s software
business — including all of Wang’s workﬂow, imaging, COLD, document
management (DM) and hierarchical storage management (HSM) technology,
plus the continuation of an important relationship previously established
between Wang and Microsoft. In addition to the joint development program
that embedded Wang’s imaging technology in both the Windows 95 and NT 4.0
operating systems, Wang Software also had existing agreements with Microsoft
related to joint development initiatives for hierarchical storage management. In
order that both companies have a current, realistic view of these agreements,
Kodak CEO George Fisher actively participated in the ﬁnal stages of the Wang
due diligence and reafﬁrmed the alliance with Microsoft CEO and Chairman
Bill Gates.
Organizational Outcomes
Kodak has long had a customer-centric rather than product-oriented view of the
market. This means that each division of the company is focused on meeting
the needs of a speciﬁc customer proﬁle — not simply responsible for selling
products to any customer who might be interested in them at a certain point in
time. Today the company has nine of these business units focused on speciﬁc
market and customer proﬁles (See Figure 1). Kodak, therefore, views its
customer relationships as a key corporate asset and historically has assisted
customers through multiple generations of technology. This emphasis on
helping customers in document image intensive businesses with the transition
from one generation of technology to the next was the compelling catalyst for
the acquisition of the fast-growing Wang software division.

Eastman Software will now become an independent subsidiary of Kodak and
will report to Candy Obourn, president of Kodak’s Business Imaging Systems
(BIS) division. Robert Weiler continues as the president of the new Eastman
Software subsidiary with its 700 employees worldwide.
Independent Status a Plus for Existing Customers
There are several good reasons for having Eastman Software organized as a
separate business strategically aligned with Kodak’s BIS — but not completely
absorbed by it. This new structure optimizes opportunities without limiting
customer options.
Eastman Software will continue to provide leading solutions to the work
management market and will be primarily responsible for delivering important
new technologies to Kodak’s large installed base of 20,000 customers by means
of BIS division product integration. Eastman Software’s ability to assist these
accounts with the transition to updated digital-based systems was, after all, a
major reason for the original Wang-Kodak agreements. Nevertheless, as a
software company, the subsidiary will also enjoy having the ﬂexibility to
integrate all its work management software technologies with other suppliers
equipment. This type of independence insures that the Eastman Software
subsidiary will be able to manage its own P/L successfully, that it will
incorporate appropriate new technologies into its portfolio as required to satisfy
a wide range of customer requirements, and that it can reach the maximum
number of new customers and prospects with its own sales activities and
industry supplier partnerships. On both the sales and professional services
front, Eastman Software is committed to a partnering strategy without channel
conﬂict.
From the perspective of Kodak’s BIS customers, despite the fact that most will
welcome the addition of Eastman Software’s workﬂow, imaging, COLD, DM,
and HSM technology to add additional value into the BIS product and service
portfolio, there may also be a number who have made previous commitments to
other suppliers’ of imaging/workﬂow and document management software
architectures in conjunction with Kodak equipment. With Eastman Software
closely aligned, but organized separately from BIS, these customers will
continue to have the ﬂexibility to choose solutions freely — adding functional
enhancements as required while protecting their current software investments.
And ﬁnally, from the perspective of Wang Software’s customers, resellers and
partners, Aberdeen ﬁeld interviews indicate considerable enthusiasm that the
new corporate parent has a multi-million dollar business focused entirely on
document imaging and work management as well as resources for additional
R&D funding and expanded marketing programs. Wang has made signiﬁcant
investments in its software technology over the last several years and achieved
more than 100% revenue growth for the last two years in this business unit as a
result. Wang acquired aggressive NT-based imaging, workﬂow, storage and
document management companies like Sigma Imaging Systems, Avail Systems
Corporation, and Vantage Technologies, Inc. to enhance its market position and
accelerate its own transition to new operating systems. Nevertheless, Wang
Laboratories’ corporate future is closely aligned with its worldwide services
initiatives. This group, after all, has the lion’s share of Wang Laboratories’
projected $1.4 billion annual revenue in ﬁscal year 1997. And Aberdeen
believes that gradually it would have become a challenge to synchronize the
two different business models — including distribution channel development
plus research and development costs — in a single company strategy.
Changing Market Dynamics
For years the image-centric document management market focused on turn-key
solutions comprised of image capture, index and display components. For the
most part, customers were most concerned with large scale, production-oriented
systems. The original microﬁlm and computer output to microﬁlm (COM)
equipment suppliers provided a cost-effective archival solution for both printed
material and computer output — but for the most part these systems played a
very limited role in the grander information systems and computer applications
scheme of things. By this we mean simply, that microﬁlm was an efﬁcient way
to capture, store and distribute data, but it was not tightly integrated with the
decision support application itself.

Today, document imaging and work management are entering a new stage
characterized by the potential for explosive growth — and Kodak is positioned
to aggressively participate in this new business opportunity. Until recently, the
sophistication of these technologies was reserved for production environments.
With the tremendous increase in computer performance and the accompanying
decrease in prices, there have been a number of changes to market dynamics.
Traditional applications can now be delivered to new audiences. New
applications can be built and existing application functionality can be extended.
This means that the capabilities of a production level system can now be
delivered at lower price points on more powerful systems than ever before. This
has extended the market opportunity to a variety of distributed departmental
and workgroup environments where technically or ﬁnancially it previously
could not have been accommodated. Eastman Software’s Integrated Document
Architecture and Repository (IDAR) provides the backbone of a complete
enterprise document life cycle solution (See Figure 2).

Expanding Market Opportunities
With the acquisition of Wang’s software technology, Kodak BIS is prepared to
add signiﬁcant functionality to its own offerings as well as to address a number
of emerging market opportunities. In addition to the synergistic business
relationship upon which this acquisition is built, there are several technology
areas that Aberdeen believes are key to the real power of the new combination.
As we have previously noted, Kodak’s position has traditionally focused on the
capture, index and storage components of document imaging management. To
this technical foundation, Eastman Software will add new desktop and
workﬂow capabilities — based upon Microsoft Windows 95 and NT software
— that position Eastman Software and Kodak BIS as a dominant force in the
desktop and departmental server market. All this, combined with Kodak’s
worldwide brand name recognition, makes it the company to beat in production
imaging and work management solutions.
Computer output to laser disk (COLD) is also an important technology now
delivered to traditional Kodak customers through the Eastman Software
acquisition. This output alternative dramatically improves distribution options
for service bureaus and supplements customers’ existing COM capabilities.
Workﬂow is another important element that has extended the capabilities of the
traditional document image management solution. Rather than simply capturing
the image and storing it in an archival environment, workﬂow incorporates the
document as an integral part of the business processes by applying work-in-
process prioritization, routing from one department to another and report
management, to name just three key functions of the technology. For the ﬁrst
time, the image is not just a convenient, economical form of stored output.
Instead, the document has become an active component of the automated
business routine.
Data warehouses have also become a planning priority for most information
systems organizations. Aberdeen research indicates that customers will
increasingly want access to a variety of information formats — not just
computer systems databases — as these mission critical decision support
systems evolve over the next several years. Having access to digital images,
distributed imaging systems and existing document management archives will
soon be a prerequisite for these multiple terabyte/size on-line systems. Eastman
Software’s HSM technology will play a key role in assisting organizations to
optimize the cost effectiveness of a variety of storage types. Aberdeen forecasts
that the combined technologies from Eastman Software and Kodak Business
Imaging Systems will have a dominant market position here.
Summary
The acquisition of Wang’s software division by Kodak as a subsidiary, Eastman
Software, Inc., has several positive dimensions. Wang’s software division
customers and value-added reseller partners are now sponsored by a parent
company dedicated to document intensive and work management applications
with a customer-centric organizational model. In addition, the acquisition
provides Kodak’s existing customers with the ability to supplement and protect
their investments in production microﬁlm systems with distributed

environments. This ability to combine hybrid (analog and digital) capabilities
not only beneﬁts the existing Kodak BIS installed base, it also provides a
compelling archive option for digital-based solutions.
The integration of Eastman Software’s emerging technologies, including PC-
based imaging and manipulation, new media formats, workﬂow, COLD, DM
and HSM with Kodak’s production experience — plus the continuation of a
strong Microsoft alliance — also provides an enviable opportunity to develop
new applications. These include real time desktop access to massive production
imaging libraries and the integration of indexed images into extremely
sophisticated decision support and data warehouse applications. This next
evolutionary step will unlock the real power of what was once merely a cost
effective method for archiving data.
Lastly, Eastman Software technology for desktop, workgroup and production
work management can extend beyond the current business market focus and
add value to the efforts of other Kodak operating units. The utilization of
additional technologies within the Kodak organization will add signiﬁcant
value to Eastman Software’s portfolio while bringing new capabilities to
existing customers and the market. This also provides Eastman Software with
the added functionality to extend its solutions to the needs of new markets.
Once again Kodak BIS is strategically positioned to help its customers make
the transition to a new generation of document image and work management
technology. Simply stated, the Kodak customer beneﬁts by limiting transition
risk while at the same time expanding application opportunities and adding
additional value by protecting investments, to existing systems.

## Prescience Detail

### Assessment: 3/10 — Poor Prescience

Aberdeen's confident assessment that Eastman Software was 'extremely well-positioned' proved to be one of the firm's less accurate M&A evaluations.

**What Went Wrong:**

1. **Eastman Software sold at a loss** — Kodak sold the unit in August 2000 for undisclosed terms, widely reported as significantly less than the $260M paid. The unit had been on the divestiture short list for more than a year (Rochester Business Journal, Aug 23, 2000).

2. **Technology obsolescence** — COLD (Computer Output to Laser Disk) was displaced by internet-based document delivery and PDF archiving by the mid-2000s. The hybrid microfilm/digital approach Aberdeen praised became a dead-end technology path.

3. **Microsoft partnership did not materialize** — The Wang imaging integration in Windows 95/NT did not deepen under Eastman Software ownership; Microsoft's own SharePoint (2001) became the dominant document management platform, not Eastman Software.

4. **Workflow market won by others** — Eastman Software did not achieve 'dominant force' status in workflow. Microsoft SharePoint, Lotus Notes, and later BPM platforms captured the workflow market Aberdeen predicted Eastman Software would dominate.

5. **Kodak's own decline** — Kodak itself filed Chapter 11 in January 2012 as digital photography disrupted its core film business. Aberdeen's confident view of Kodak as a stable 'multi-billion dollar' parent proved too optimistic about the parent company's long-term viability.

**What Aberdeen Got Right:**

1. **Market direction** — The democratization of document imaging to departmental and workgroup environments did occur; Aberdeen correctly identified this trend.

2. **Data warehouse integration** — Aberdeen's prediction that organizations would need document/image access in decision support systems was accurate; enterprise search and ECM integration with BI tools became standard.

3. **M&A rationale** — The strategic logic of combining Kodak's capture/storage strengths with Wang's workflow/document management software was sound; execution failed, not the concept.

**Broader Lesson:** This document illustrates the risk of over-confidence in M&A announcement profiles written immediately after deal closure. Aberdeen's analysis relied heavily on management's stated vision and customer enthusiasm without adequately weighing integration risks, technology disruption vectors (internet-based document management), and the challenge of a hardware company managing a software subsidiary.
