LapLink Solutions Offer Telecommuting Value-
Add
Traveling Software's remote control LapLink solutions bring the corporate
desktop easily and economically to the worker based at home, on the road or
who extends the workday after-hours.
Playing to Strengths
Recognized as a well-established market leader in ﬁle transfer and remote
control functionality, Traveling Software has gained a reputation for
simple, yet tech-nologically sophisticated, reliable remote computing
solutions. Aberdeen research indicates that the company's recent releases
of LapLink software bring valuable enhancements to telecommuters as
well as to the IT personnel who support them.
Changing Work Patterns
Technology advancements have led to fundamental changes in the way we
work. One of the strongest examples today is the increasing adoption of
telecommuting - working away from the ofﬁce, either at home or on the
road. Today, over 9.5 million employees work off-site, and this number is
projected to exceed 15 million by the year 2000.
Telecommuting Beneﬁts
Telecommuting solutions are being adopted to:
add ﬂexibility in work location and the work day to retain valued
employees
accommodate dependent care responsibilities
offer alternatives to relocation
comply with air quality legislation
Signiﬁcant competitive beneﬁts can be gained from enabling employees to
work away from the primary workplace. These include:
increases in employee retention and productivity (between 10 - 30%)
enhanced recruiting
reductions in absenteeism
decreases in ofﬁce space costs
The IT Challenge
Telecommuters working off-site - be they home workers, day extenders or
road warriors - require access to the same corporate network, applications,
shared ﬁles and Intranets as their on-site colleagues. It is physically and
economically impractical to duplicate proprietary, data-intensive
corporate ﬁles such as large databases and accounting packages for off-site
workers, but these are often precisely the types of applications users need
to access. The chal-lenge for IT personnel is to enable the remote use of
often-bandwidth-hungry applications as economically as possible over low-
bandwidth, wide-area dial-in connections.
To further complicate matters, multiple remote access protocols often must
be addressed, as well as security issues. Additionally, IT must provide
adequate off-site IT support, because deploying technicians in the ﬁeld for
on-site intervention is too costly and resource-limiting for most companies.
LapLink Solutions Value-Add
Many at-home and on-the-road employees are familiar with LapLink
solutions, having used them to transfer ﬁles from desktop computers to
portable laptops and notebooks. LapLink automates modem setup,
simplifying dialing procedures so that users can access and transfer ﬁles,
run applications remotely, and read and send e-mail via dial-up
connections - the most prevalent form of connectivity used by
telecommuters today.

Based on remote control architecture, LapLink permits a user to easily
control a host computer from a remote computing device by establishing
connectivity between the two over a phone line. The remote user can
transfer and synchronize ﬁles from the host computer to the remote client.
All programs run on the host's CPU, with the host's video displayed on the
remote client screen. Only the remote client's mouse actions and
keystrokes are transmitted back to the host machine.
Because only a small portion of data is actually transferred, remote
LapLink users can run network applications at close to "real-time"
speeds, optimizing low bandwidth connections. The need for duplicate
applications at the remote site is eliminated. Furthermore, LapLink's
SpeedSync(tm) technology transfers only the changed portions of ﬁles,
reducing transfer times by as much as 80%.
Remote Control vs. Remote Node
Some of the inherent technical difﬁculty of implementing remote access
capability has been alleviated by incorporating remote node architecture
features into Windows 95 and NT. Remote node architecture is best suited
for client-server applications that involve infrequent, small ﬁle transfers
such as remote e-mail. Larger, database-oriented appli-cations are better
suited for remote con-trol architecture. Remote node does not permit end-
users to remotely operate desktop applications, nor does it allow MIS to
take control of remote computing devices in order to perform diagnostics
and troubleshooting. LapLink brings the beneﬁts of remote control to a
remote node connection when combined with Microsoft's Windows 95(tm)
Dial-Up Networking Client.
Security Considerations
Aberdeen research reveals that security is a major concern for IT and MIS
administrators, especially when remote users are involved. Whether using
LapLink over dial-in or a RAS connection, the program adds additional
security in the form of a separate password for each caller and optional
password protection to all user security settings. Access can be deﬁned at
three different levels: Remote Control, File Transfer and Chat.
Keyboard/mouse disabling and screen blanking prevent unauthorized
individuals from observing or manipulating work. A ﬂexible callback
feature keeps unauthorized users from connecting to the host PC, and an
Event Log monitors who calls and which ﬁles have been transferred.
Aberdeen Conclusions
Traveling Software's LapLink solutions bring effective, economical remote
access capabilities to users on the road or working from home - addressing
with ease, efﬁciency and economy some of the major stumbling blocks of
remote information access, ﬁle transfer and synchronization.

## Prescience Detail

### Assessment: 6/10 — Moderate Prescience

Aberdeen demonstrated good prescience on the telecommuting market trend but the specific technology (dial-up remote control) proved transitional.

**What Aberdeen Got Right:**

1. **Telecommuting growth** — The projection of 15M telecommuters by 2000 was broadly correct. BLS and Gallup data confirm telecommuting grew from approximately 9-10% of the workforce in the mid-1990s to 17% by the early 2000s.

2. **Productivity and retention benefits** — Aberdeen's 10-30% productivity improvement claim has been repeatedly validated by academic research (University of Iowa/Texas 2012 study, Stanford/Nicholas Bloom studies).

3. **IT complexity of remote access** — Aberdeen correctly identified security, multi-protocol support, and bandwidth management as the key IT challenges for remote worker support.

4. **Traveling Software's longevity** — Aberdeen's characterization of Traveling Software as a 'well-established market leader' proved durable; the company survived as LapLink Software for 40+ years.

**What Aberdeen Got Wrong:**

1. **Technology path** — LapLink's dial-up remote control approach was a transitional solution. Broadband adoption after 2000, combined with Microsoft RDP and corporate VPN solutions, made LapLink's specific bandwidth optimization features largely unnecessary.

2. **Scale of remote work** — The 15M by 2000 projection proved conservative. By 2015 Gallup measured 37% of U.S. workers telecommuting at least some of the time. Post-COVID (2020) made remote work near-universal.

**Broader Lesson:** Aberdeen correctly diagnosed a secular trend (telecommuting growth) and a viable short-term solution (LapLink), but the specific technology vehicle was a transitional product that broadband deployment would quickly make obsolete.
