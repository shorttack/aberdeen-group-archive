Lucent delivers Internet telephony and carrier-
grade scale with Internet Telephony Server SP
As voice and fax trafﬁc hits the Internet, quality of service and reliability issues
are emerging. Lucent is well-positioned to provide turnkey Internet Telephony
solutions that leverage Service Providers’ infrastructure to provide high-quality
telephony services, such as voice and fax, over IP networks.
The Promise
The widespread proliferation of Internet Protocol (IP) networks, has resulted in
a growing new class of IP devices designed to carry traditional voice and fax
telephony across data networks.
Internet telephony promises signiﬁcantly lower costs for long distance and
international voice and fax trafﬁc; however, implementations of gateway
technology have been limited, as PC-based clients have alienated Internet
telephony from the mass markets.
Meanwhile, the Service Providers, who are in the best position to offer Internet
telephony services, have had incomplete solutions. Few gateway offerings have
been more than pieces of hardware and software, and those companies that
have offered turnkey solutions have been unable to deliver the large-scale
implementations necessary for widespread POTS to POTS solutions.
The Solution
Lucent Technologies has developed the ﬁrst scaleable, carrier-grade, turnkey
gateway platform. Internet Telephony Server SP leverages Lucent’s extensive
call processing experience and international presence.
Now, instead of integrating partial gateway solutions in a few locations, service
providers can globally deploy Lucent’s Internet Telephony Server SP solution
backed by Lucent’s 24x7 international customer support.
High Reliability, Robust Features
Internet Telephony Server SP features:
Real-time, alternate routing
Scaleable (24 ports & up)
Standard APIs for application development
Flexible call routing
SQL call detail records
POTS to POTS calling
FAX to FAX capability
Many Internet telephony technologies give poor quality of service and spotty
reliability. Lucent’s Internet Telephony Server SP delivers near toll-grade sound
quality — the result of extensive call processing experience combined with
elemedia™ leading voice compression technology. To enhance reliability when
a data network goes down, Internet Telephony Server SP falls-back to another
data network or the PSTN.
A Better Proposal for End-Users
Traditional Internet telephony products have relied on PC-based client
applications. Network and PC reliability has made these solutions impractical
for all but the most avid hobbyists.
Lucent’s solution allows end-users to operate their phones and faxes normally.
The Internet Telephony Server SP handles all of the details of routing trafﬁc
across an IP data network, and the end-user sees lower costs.
By guaranteeing reliability and keeping costs competitive, Lucent makes
Internet telephony practical for widespread use in the business world. More
importantly, service is seamless, as users retain their phones and fax machines
— no behavior change.

New Markets for Service Providers
Lucent’s solution keeps costs low in small and low-density markets and
removes the barriers to entry that many service providers face in offering long
distance services. This is relevant to both newer network service providers
(ISPs, cable TV MSOs and utilities) and entrenched phone companies alike
(RBOCs, CAPs and CLECs). IXCs can use Internet telephony to diversify their
long distance portfolio and to enter local markets, while LECs have an easy
way to enter out-of-region long distance markets.
All service providers beneﬁt from the ability to offer low-cost voice and fax
trafﬁc as a complement to broadband data services. One-stop shopping is a
powerful way for service providers to increase customer retention and
proﬁtability.
Compatible Technology
The Internet Telephony Server SP incorporates several technologies from
elemedia, a venture within Lucent, including the SX7300P speech codec.
elemedia’s codec and echo cancellation technologies have also been embraced
by several vendors, such as Netscape in its Media Server.
Complete Solution
Lucent’s Internet Telephony Server SP meets service providers’ requirements. It
is a simple, robust, turnkey solution that enables service providers to develop
Internet telephony products. Backed by Lucent’s international service and
support, the offer is a complete solution.
The beneﬁts of Internet Telephony Server SP are tremendous. Lucent is the ﬁrst
supplier to offer an Internet telephony solution capable of the wide-scale
implementation required for Internet telephony and fax to reach mass-markets.

## Prescience Detail

### Assessment: 8/10 — High Prescience

This is one of Aberdeen's most prescient studies in the archive. The core thesis — that carrier-grade internet telephony would become mass-market and disrupt traditional long-distance economics — proved entirely correct.

**What Aberdeen Got Right:**

1. **Internet telephony becoming mass-market** — Fully validated. Skype (2003) achieved 100M users within two years. Vonage demonstrated consumer VoIP viability. By 2010, most global voice traffic had shifted to IP networks.

2. **Long-distance cost disruption** — Aberdeen's prediction that internet telephony would dramatically reduce long-distance costs was fully realized. International calls fell from dollars per minute to near-zero.

3. **Service providers as key adopters** — Correct. All major telcos (AT&T, Verizon, BT, Deutsche Telekom) eventually deployed carrier-grade IP voice infrastructure.

4. **POTS-to-POTS transparency** — Aberdeen correctly identified seamless POTS compatibility as key; this is exactly how consumer VoIP (Vonage, cable VoIP) was eventually deployed.

5. **Carrier-grade scale requirement** — Aberdeen correctly identified that only carrier-grade solutions would achieve widespread adoption; PC-based hobbyist approaches were insufficient.

**What Aberdeen Got Wrong (or partially wrong):**

1. **Lucent's specific market dominance** — Lucent did not achieve the predicted first-mover dominance in VoIP infrastructure. Cisco became the dominant voice-over-IP infrastructure vendor. Sonus Networks, not Lucent, pioneered the softswitch architecture that carriers actually adopted.

2. **elemedia codec longevity** — The proprietary SX7300P codec was superseded by open standards (G.729, G.711, later Opus). Proprietary codec approaches lost to open standards as predicted by the broader internet standards community.

3. **Timeline** — Mass-market VoIP took longer than the 1997 framing implied; broadband penetration (required for quality VoIP) didn't reach critical mass until 2003-2005.

**Broader Lesson:** This document demonstrates that Aberdeen could accurately identify transformative technology trends while misjudging which specific vendor would capture the opportunity. The macro prediction (VoIP revolution) was right; the micro prediction (Lucent dominance) was wrong.
