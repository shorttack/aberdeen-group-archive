An Executive White Paper

Executive Summary
Increasingly, companies are discovering the competitive value of the Internet
and Web-based technologies. Incorporating wide-area Internet connectivity into
the business process brings faster, more effective access to business-critical
information and distributed, network-centric applications — to employees,
business partners and customers alike. The improved communication chain that
results is ripe with potential, fostering advantages in product development,
marketing, distribution, cost reductions and increased revenues and sales.
Using the Internet as an integral component of business strategy is, however,
not without its hurdles. Because there has been no mechanism for prioritization,
access has been granted on a ﬁrst-come, ﬁrst-serve basis, with no guarantees
that adequate access will be available for anyone and everyone when needed.
Applications have grown in sophistication and more frequently include
bandwidth-intensive graphics that do not travel well on smaller, constricting
"pipes," causing painfully slow downloading and network congestion.
Furthermore, application developers are struggling with new software
architectures that will not, at ﬁrst, be optimized for Internet use.
Aberdeen research indicates that few solutions have emerged which address the
bandwidth, performance and quality of service requirements demanded by
business applications that run on the Internet. Discussions with IT managers
disclose that they face increasing pressure to guarantee users and applications
(especially business-critical ones), access to the Internet; to provide the
bandwidth required for individual subnets on an appropriate, ﬂexible basis; to
adjust the network to meet peak demand needs and to accurately assign
bandwidth charges to the appropriate user. And — they are expected to take on
the responsibility of producing better service levels and business results by
leveraging existing technology intelligently.
Xedia Corporation has forged ahead to help IT administrators resolve
broadband IP networking issues with a broadband access management solution
that, in Aberdeen’s opinion, brings precisely the tools needed to ensure
bandwidth control and management for mission-critical business applications at
a reasonable price.
Bandwidth’s Pivotal Role in Networking
Bandwidth has always been a gating factor in network performance, and it
plays an even more sensitive role in broadband wide-area networking.
Providing adequate bandwidth is one of the essential requirements for
converting the Internet into a true business tool. Otherwise, access is slow,
applications do not run properly, performance suffers, users become frustrated,
productivity decreases and customers become disgruntled.
Aberdeen has noted that throwing bandwidth at a network problem is not
always the complete solution. Service providers have taken advantage of
advanced technologies such as ﬁber optics, T1/T3 DSUs and high-end routers
to deliver cost-effective broadband uplinks into their networks. This, however,
may have little impact on individual corporate applications and users, due to a
lack of tools that could enable IT administrators to distribute bandwidth more
productively through better internal allocation and management from their end
of the network. More effective bandwidth assignment can often accomplish
miracles, and optimizing existing network architectures and trafﬁc ﬂows
through proper management of existing bandwidth resources is essential for
maximum bandwidth utilization.
Trafﬁc Management Considerations
Data trafﬁc is best characterized by its unpredictability. It is not regular or
periodic, but is essentially "bursty" in nature. This means that predictable peak
demands cannot be clearly identiﬁed on a daily, hourly or even ﬁve-minute
basis. Because average throughput can vary widely in short, second-to-second
intervals, bandwidth allocation must take into account and be able to
accommodate the highest spikes of these short-interval transmissions.

This maxim holds true for the wide-area as well as the local-area network.
TCP/IP, the predominant communications protocol of the Internet, is not
designed to accommodate the interactive trafﬁc model that the 'Net has
spawned. TCP/IP cannot deliver controlled bandwidth distribution in and of
itself, and has no ability to prioritize applications or ensure that users gain
access to remote network resources on a fair basis.
This scenario grows in complexity when applications shift to a mix that
includes hefty ﬁle transfers, graphics-laden content and multimedia
applications along with less bandwidth-intensive applications. Network
administrators, therefore, need to be able to reallocate bandwidth on-the-ﬂy if
user/application trafﬁc patterns and available bandwidth are seriously out of
sync. No IT manager wants to over-assign bandwidth if it is not needed, but
they must have adequate bandwidth available whenever it is called for.
What types of trafﬁc management issues do network administrators need to be
able to address? First and foremost is ensuring that business-critical
information takes priority over less important but perhaps more proliﬁc trafﬁc.
Assigning priority levels such as highest priority or lowest priority can address
this concern. Trafﬁc control is another problem. Network managers need to be
able to deliver the access they have promised to users, especially if there are
charges being made back to users for speciﬁed amounts of bandwidth. And,
there will be applications to which managers want to limit or deny access,
either on a temporary or permanent basis. Figure 1 below presents a summary
of the requirements Aberdeen considers essential for managed access.

Other Considerations
As the network grows in strategic importance, Aberdeen has found, ironically,
that network managers are frequently faced with reduced staff and budget. In
order for them to incorporate new technologies into their networks, they need
assurance that whatever is introduced will be easy to install, use and maintain.
Price continues to be a signiﬁcant factor, and IT wants to be able to leverage
their existing investments, not be forced to make costly replacements. A
demonstrable return on investment over the shortest time period possible
continues to be a driver in decision-making surrounding network
improvements.
Xedia's Access Point: Business-Strength Bandwidth
Management
Xedia Corporation tackles enterprise bandwidth administration problems head-
on with its Access Point solution. This managed broadband access device is the
ﬁrst in a family of products designed speciﬁcally to "make business sense" out
of broadband access to the Internet. Access Point combines high-speed access
with the ability control and manage the sharing of bandwidth over high-speed
transmission uplinks. The ﬁrst releases of Access Point support Frame Relay,
which Aberdeen has found to be the most widely used transmission uplink
technology currently being selected by ISP customers. Subsequent releases will
support Asynchronous Transfer Mode (ATM).
Access Point's integrated software incorporates high-performance IP routing,
with Class Based Queuing bandwidth management, as well as advanced trafﬁc
management capabilities. Access Point's Intelligent Queuing (IQ™) software
permits IT managers to establish priorities for trafﬁc, as well as letting them
allocate the appropriate percentage of bandwidth to users and applications. A
class can be deﬁned by a single IP address or a range of addresses, by TCP or
UDP ports.
IQ shapes the trafﬁc offered to the service provider network by specifying a
variety of parameters, including throughput controls for average, peak and burst
ratios and limit algorithms that determine elasticity and discard thresholds.
Deep buffering cuts down on congestion and latency by smoothing out trafﬁc

peaks without losing packets and causing retransmission. Aberdeen considers
IQ to be an especially attractive feature because it gives network managers the
ability to maintain network access fairness, establish priorities for trafﬁc when
appropriate, and manage latency.
IT time has become increasingly valuable and, as a result, ease-of-installation is
greatly appreciated by network managers. Xedia's Access Point can be
conﬁgured at the central site and the shipped to the enterprise site. A non-
proprietary system, Access Point supports industry standards for OSPF2 and
RIP2 and is fully compatible with installed hubs, routers, switches and
networking platforms.
Access Point's management capabilities, which include the ability to monitor
usage of particular trafﬁc classes and how much bandwidth they use, have as
much relevance for IT managers as would a high-speed RMON probe. Access
Point's Web browser, with its Java presentation, provides an easy-to-use
management component. These monitoring capabilities enable IT to monitor
service level agreements in order to be sure they are being enforced, while
giving service providers the tools needed to monitor customer-level service
agreements in order to be sure the service being delivered is appropriate.
Xedia's skill at delivering low-cost, high performance embedded RISC systems
has enabled the company to deliver a software-based architecture that provides
both ﬂexibility and attractive affordability — currently at 50% below the cost
of connectivity-only based solutions currently on the market.
Aberdeen Conclusions
Xedia’s Access Point incorporates the necessary tools required for bandwidth
management at the network administration level as well as at the ISP level.
While the Service Provider controls the route class level of bandwidth,
Enterprise IT is able to deﬁne the appropriate classes of service provided to
each enterprise user by classifying packets at the IP Layer 3 upon entry into the
Access Point. This results in network managers being able to exert control of
bandwidth access under load planning, rather than having to "oversubscribe" to
bandwidth in order to meet peak load requirements. Aberdeen views this
approach as a giant step forward in providing quality access as well as cost
savings
Corporate access to the Internet can no longer be looked upon simply as
connection for the business as a whole. Rather, it should be viewed as access
that must be subdivided among many users and many applications and then
managed in a way that results in business efﬁciency and business success. The
best means of ensuring that these goals are met is to provide IT administrators
with the tools needed to assign the right priorities and the right amount of
bandwith to both users and applications in a dynamic and deterministic manner.
Aberdeen, therefore, strongly encourages any IT administrator whose network
is becoming more IT-centric —and who is building the Internet into its business
strategy — to seriously consider adding bandwidth access management and
Xedia’s Access Point to its bag of tricks.

## Prescience Detail

### Assessment: 7/10 — High Prescience

Aberdeen demonstrated strong prescience in this white paper. The core thesis — that bandwidth management is a critical enterprise priority, not just a connectivity problem — proved entirely correct.

**What Aberdeen Got Right:**

1. **Internet-centric business strategy** — Aberdeen's prediction that companies would make internet access a core business infrastructure requiring active management was fully validated by the mid-2000s.

2. **QoS as enterprise requirement** — The requirements framework Aberdeen presented (traffic classification, priority allocation, peak thresholding, deep buffering) became the standard QoS feature set. DiffServ (RFC 2475, 1998), MPLS QoS, and eventually SD-WAN all implement exactly these capabilities.

3. **Xedia's commercial viability** — Aberdeen's strong recommendation of Xedia was validated by Lucent's acquisition of the company for $246 million in August 1999 (Source: CNET, https://www.cnet.com/tech/tech-industry/lucent-to-buy-xedia-for-246m/).

4. **Bandwidth-throwing limitations** — Aberdeen's insight that 'throwing bandwidth at a network problem is not always the complete solution' remains a standard network engineering axiom.

5. **IT staff reduction under growing network complexity** — The prediction that IT would face reduced staff while managing increasingly complex networks proved accurate; this drove the managed services and network automation markets.

**What Aberdeen Got Wrong:**

1. **Frame Relay longevity** — Frame Relay was presented as the dominant and continuing WAN technology. In fact, Frame Relay was largely replaced by MPLS VPNs and broadband internet by the mid-2000s.

2. **ATM relevance** — The planned ATM support for Access Point proved unnecessary; ATM was abandoned by enterprises as IP/Ethernet won the integrated networking battle.

3. **Vendor-specific prediction** — While Xedia was acquired for $246M (a positive outcome), the Xedia brand and product line did not survive as a market leader. Cisco and Juniper captured the enterprise QoS market through router-integrated capabilities.

**Broader Lesson:** Aberdeen correctly identified a real and persistent problem (enterprise bandwidth management) and a viable vendor solving it (Xedia). The acquisition of Xedia by Lucent two years later at $246M represents one of Aberdeen's better-validated early market assessments in this archive.
