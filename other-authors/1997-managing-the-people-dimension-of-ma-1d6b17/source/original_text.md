Managing the People Dimension of Major
Technology Transitions – Senior IT Executives
Share Insights
In September 1996, Hewlett-Packard engaged the Aberdeen Group to conduct
one-on-one interviews with key project leaders and decision-makers
undergoing or completing major technology transitions in their enterprises.
The project objective was to better understand how the people dimension of
major technology transitions is perceived, prioritized and managed within
enterprises. These are the findings.
Education Causes People to Move to Technology
"We missed the boat by not offering training, education, or communication and
they (managers and end-users) became very resistant to the new systems."
Senior project managers and technology executives leading major technology
transitions face many staggering challenges – not the least of which is building
a skilled and productive workforce that will best leverage the business value of
the new technology investment. Education – including communication, change
management, process training, and comprehensive skills training – was
consistently named as a critical enabler for achieving this objective.
Aberdeen's research found that project leaders strongly believe that education
and training causes people to move to technology. Conversely, they report that
not using education will – in the best case – lead to an underutilization of the
new technology and – in the worst case – cause an outright rejection of the
system by its intended users.
But There is Just Never Enough Time
"It's like being in the Indy 500 and trying to change the wheels on a car without
a pitstop."
Enterprise executives and project leaders experience incredible pressure from
cost containment expectations, competitive positioning requirements for a rapid
project roll-out, and day-to-day business operating demands. As a result,
education – despite the best intentions – often gets short shrift. And yet, it is
education – including communication and change management – that often will
make or break the success of a project.
Less Classroom, More Customized Content and
New Delivery Options Sought
"Classes often overload people – there is a real need for teaching on-demand."
Time shortage may be fueling another key finding – demand for traditional
classroom training is being supplanted by a universal appeal for access to
training in smaller bursts with more on-demand, on-line, real-time, continuous
access. There is a general aversion to classroom sessions which are often
viewed as too long, not specific enough to the unique requirements of an
enterprise, and overwhelming in detail that will likely not be retained.
Project decision-makers are also adamant that training needs to be tailored to
their unique project requirements. As one respondent said, "I don't want a least-
common-denominator one-size-fits-all approach." Project leaders seek training
options that ensure newly-acquired knowledge translates back to an employee's
job responsibilities.
Decision-makers also scrutinize the trainers sent to their organizations. They
seek educators who also offer implementation and operational expertise. And if
these requirements are not enough, decision-makers said that while they prefer
the personal touch of a local training outfit, they would ideally engage an
organization that could deliver consistent educational services on a global,
national or regional basis.
The Best Intentions to Use Education as a Project
Investment Are, In Practice, Difficult to Fulfill
"We learn from one project and try to get education into the next project but
funding and time are always cut out because it is difficult to communicate the
effect on a project outcome of a smaller budget."
Further findings revealed that although project managers strongly believe in the
value of education to the success of their project, they rarely use traditional
ROI measures to demonstrate this to senior management. Instead, the business
case for technology change is often built around a conservative cost-reduction
scenario where educational investments are seen as an off-set to the overall
project savings. This explains why critical educational investments have, too
often, been cut.
In Conclusion...
"Humans are built to avoid anxiety so our goal was to get people through the
transition with grace and poise."
Aberdeen found that most executives and IT managers leading technology
transitions want to talk about the relationship of education to the success of
their project. There was a genuine empathy for the changes that new technology
will bring to individual roles and responsibilities in their organization.
Project leaders that felt they experienced the most project success implemented
disciplined project plans that fully embraced and incorporated education tracks.
Others who deferred, or eliminated, education are playing catch up on the
actual technology roll-out.
Most told Aberdeen that, in the end, the people and process issues were far
more challenging than the technology issues faced during the project. And
many are looking for help with these challenges. As one senior executive said,
"just the fact that HP is asking these questions and is prepared to act on the
responses only enhances my already high opinion of the company."
Aberdeen agrees.

### Abstract

In September 1996, Hewlett-Packard engaged Aberdeen Group to conduct one-on-one interviews with senior IT executives and project leaders undergoing major technology transitions. The study identifies education and training as the critical enabler of successful technology adoption, documents barriers including time pressure and ROI measurement challenges, and finds that on-demand, customized training is preferred over classroom instruction. Author Susan Irving concludes that people and process challenges consistently exceed technical challenges in large-scale technology transitions.

- **Result:** Study after study confirmed Aberdeen's finding; Gartner estimated 55-75% of ERP projects fail to meet objectives, with change management/training gaps cited as primary cause; Hershey's SAP failure (1999), FoxMeyer Drug bankruptcy (1996-1997), and Waste Management SAP suit ($500M, 2008) all attributed in part to inadequate training and change management
