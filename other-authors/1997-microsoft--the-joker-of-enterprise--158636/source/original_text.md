Microsoft: The Joker of Enterprise IS
Computing
Microsoft has done a superlative job of meeting users' needs
and requirements for desktop computing. But now Microsoft's
marketing message is that its NT Server and BackOffice
products are ready to replace mainframes, Unix servers,
AS/400s and every other enterprise-level, production-quality
platform. Yet Aberdeen research has yet to discover
enterprises, including Microsoft itself, that have a history of
successfully running mission-critical applications on
Microsoft's products. More to the point, Aberdeen questions
Microsoft's understanding of and commitment to supporting
the planning, support, product compatibility, and management
requirements of senior enterprise IS executives.
In this Viewpoint, Aberdeen helps its clients better understand
why Microsoft's historical desktop orientation is not directly
transferable to enterprise IS computing. It is intended to help
business unit executives and IS professionals to work together
to build a consensus view of where and where not to use
Microsoft products beyond the desktop.
John R. Logan

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group Vice President John R. Logan argues that Microsoft's historical desktop orientation is fundamentally incompatible with enterprise IS computing requirements. While acknowledging Microsoft's desktop excellence, Logan contends that NT Server and BackOffice have yet to demonstrate mission-critical production capability, and that Microsoft lacks the planning, support, product compatibility, and management culture required by senior enterprise IS executives. The brief is intended to help business executives and IS professionals build consensus on where—and where not—to deploy Microsoft products beyond the desktop.

### Prescience Detail

**Prediction 1:** Microsoft desktop DNA enterprise incompatibility
- **Claimed:** Microsoft's historical desktop orientation is not directly transferable to enterprise IS computing; enterprises should carefully define where/where not to use Microsoft
- **Year:** 1997
- **Confidence at time:** high

**Actual Outcome 1:** Microsoft enterprise trajectory
- **Result:** Microsoft achieved substantial enterprise adoption with Windows 2000/2003, Exchange, SQL Server, SharePoint; but desktop DNA liability persisted — Ballmer's mobile/cloud failures validated Logan's structural concern; Nadella explicitly addressed the cultural problem in 2014. By 2024, Azure and Microsoft 365 made Microsoft a $3T enterprise cloud leader — vindicating eventual transformation but validating Logan's 1997 concern as a durable structural challenge.
- **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes:** Sources: Microsoft annual reports; Satya Nadella 'Hit Refresh' (2017); Microsoft market cap data 2024.

**Prediction 2:** No enterprises running mission-critical on Microsoft (1997)
- **Claimed:** Aberdeen research has yet to discover enterprises, including Microsoft itself, with history of successfully running mission-critical applications on Microsoft's products
- **Year:** 1997
- **Confidence at time:** high

**Actual Outcome 2:** Mission-critical Windows adoption
- **Result:** By early 2000s, many enterprises ran mission-critical Exchange, SQL Server, and eventually SAP on Windows; Windows 2000 and later versions addressed key reliability gaps; but Hershey SAP/NT failure (1999) and other high-profile failures initially validated Logan's caution
- **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes:** The claim was accurate for 1997 but Windows 2000 changed the picture significantly for many (though not all) mission-critical categories.
