Preface
Aberdeen recognizes that FDDI campus backbone environments are still on user's planning agendas. There are a series of successful LAN switching solutions from signiﬁcant LAN players
who support the FDDI backbone as well as emerging fast LAN and ATM networking technologies. In particular, 3Com's Switching Division is an exciting success story which has leveraged
a business reality--that in spite of ATM excitement and increased shipments, installations and industry coverage, FDDI is still prominent as an active and important deliverable in the
planning agendas of many large enterprises. In this Product Proﬁle, Aberdeen examines 3Com's very successful LANplex 2500 workgroup solution, which has found a home solving high-
performance switched Ethernet/FDDI networking, while assuring an investmentprotected path to emerging technologies.
Executive Summary
In December, 1994, 3Com introduced the LANplex 2500 Ethernet/FDDI workgroup switching solution to the market, complementing its ﬂagship LANplex 6000 backbone switch. The
LANplex 2500 was well received as 3Com's high-performance workgroup switch engine supporting multiple switched Ethernet ports and FDDI rings. It also served as the Stage 2
deliverable of 3Com's High-Performance Scalable Networking (HPSN) vision, focusing on both performance and technology migration advantages.
Now almost 9 months after initial shipments, there has been an intensiﬁcation of workgroup networking, switching, virtual LANs and ATM migration on the minds of analysts, suppliers and
users alike. Aberdeen ﬁndings conﬁrm that the LANplex 2500 is doing extremely well in the market, building on the opportunities in the LANplex 6000 installed base, but even more
important, building its own identity as a premier workgroup switch for Ethernet/FDDI networks. Aberdeen research also conﬁrms that numerous high-proﬁle customers are making powerful
use of the Ethernet switching engine by working with real campusbased FDDI infrastructures, while remaining poised for ATM when they are ready. Unlike other Ethernet/FDDI switches
available from Cisco Systems and ALANTEC, 3Com's 2500 is feature-rich and aggressively priced, although competition is intensifying, cutting into 3Com's 6 to 9 month, performance and
featurebased competitive lead.
3Com Vision in Place
Aberdeen recognizes 3Com as one of the leaders in switching, integrating it wherever possible into the network. The company was early to recognize the migration from traditional hub
networking and routers to switching over the past several years. 3Com intensiﬁed its commitment to switching with the acquisition of a fast-moving LAN switching startup called
Synernetics in 1993. Synernetic's expertise was in addressing the need for high-performance switching to FDDI backbones.
This acquisition formed the core of 3Com's Switching Division, developing a family of high-performance LANplex switches for both backbone and workgroup applications. 3Com, in
particular, took a leadership role in switching by pushing the technology envelope, moving aggressively to ASIC technology for higher performance and perceived packaging advantages.
ASIC technology yields products built with fewer components, increasing system reliability.
Over the past year, 3Com has outlined its vision of networks and the role that switching plays in them. It has called this vision High-Performance Scalable Networking (HPSN), with the
LANplex 2500 playing a major role as a LAN-based switching device. 3Com's HPSN addresses how switching and routing ﬁt in the network, including the workgroup ﬂoor switch and the
basementlocated central router.
Stage 1--Take advantage of a collapsed backbone conﬁguration with server farm consolidation in the data center. Install all complex functions in the data center using a router, but
distribute less complex functions throughout the network.
Stage 2--Achieve increased segmentation and resulting available bandwidth to users by installing distributed LAN switches in the wiring closet at each ﬂoor level of the building. Use
high-speed links including FDDI and Fast Ethernet to connect servers and desktops.
Stage 3--Plan migration to ATM with targeted addition of ATM technology starting with the campus backbone and extended to the individual buildings. Use ATM where requirements,
cost model and price/performance justify its installation.
Switching Explosion-Full Speed Ahead
Switching evolved based on the need to address network congestion and assure dynamic highspeed connectivity in the increasing number of client/server networks found in departments or
throughout the entire building or the entire campus. These networks form what 3Com and other suppliers call the "intranetwork," enabling local servers, workstations, PCs and peripheral
devices to be connected to themselves and to central server farms.
Today's demand is for increased scalable desktop bandwidth, making use of existing technology and assuring investment protection. Higherspeed server farms can be connected by several
types of technologies including FDDI, Fast Ethernet (100BASETX, 100BASEFL), 100VG standards, and emerging ATM networks. Switching offers network simplicity, increased
performance and increased scalability.
3Com has developed a dynamic set of three switching packages, which often use the same technologies. The LANplex family is characterized by high-level functionality, modular packaging,
fault tolerance, and technology migration. The low-cost LinkSwitch family takes advantage of 3Com ASIC technology for price/performance beneﬁts and is packaged in the 3Com
SuperStack stackable form factor. In particular, the LinkSwitch 2200 consists of a 16port Ethernet switch with a single FDDI port, and is the repackaging of the LANplex technology into the
3Com SuperStack to address the needs of those users in small and midsize companies who are attracted to the stackable solution. While a number of 3Com's switch families have growth
paths to higherspeed connectivity, including ATM, 3Com's ATM focus is based on the CELLplex family of ATM workgroup and backbone switches.
Introducing the LANplex 2500
The 2500 is a modular, intelligent workgroup switch which is part of 3Com's LANplex switch family. Each 2500 device supports up to 16 switched 10 Mbps Ethernet ports and 2 switched
FDDI ports, with a growth path to Fast Ethernet and ATM technologies. The 2500 has an attractive set of features which address most needs that users have. Thus, 3Com has ﬂexibility to bid
a combination of larger backbone LANplex 6000s for greater capacity and FDDI concentration, and workgroup-based 2500s at an attractive enterprise-wide system price. The LANplex
family breadth is an advantage in the system sell and helps enable 3Com maintain account control in multiple types of conﬁgurations.


Even when the 2500 was truly brandnew at a number of sites, its membership in 3Com's LANplex family, which today is the backbone switching market leader, gave it instant credibility
with a number of users. As expected, a large number of early 2500 customers had installations of the LANplex 6000 or were considering that offering prior to the announcement of the 2500
at the end of 1994. In a number of these cases, the attractive price/performance of the 2500 pushed these users to install the 2500, even though it was a new product in the beginning of
1995.Furthermore, the attractive packaging and lower price/port ﬁgures enabled users to install a larger number of switches in distributed environments. Modularity and footprint allowed the
2500 to be easily moved in these distributed switch environments. In workgroups, users liked the ability to place these devices anywhere in the buildings, even in the backrooms. Size
appears to be an important criteria based on the common spatial limitation in corporate wiring closets.
Built on Intelligence
Taking advantage of its own ASIC technology, combined with RISC processing, 3Com's ISE-chip (Intelligent Switching Engine) architecture is the heart of the LANplex 2500. The ASIC
serves as a "front-end" performance engine to the RISC processor, and effectively creates a "fast path" linkage between ASIC and processors, thus streamlining overall packet processing
time through the switch. Repetitive, but typically time consuming packet processing is done through the ASIC, which frees up the dual processors for the intelligent switching and routing
functions. This innovative design yields high-performance switching (565K pps packet forwarding) and increased reliability based on a reduced set of components. It puts LANplex 2500
switching performance well ahead of competitive offerings from Cisco, Bay and ALANTEC. Since there is no central processing engine, performance is assured to be maintained even as
new functionality is added to the switch. Table 1 includes a summary of Aberdeen's observations as to how 3Com stacks up to major competition.

The ISE-chip ASIC switching engine is new to the LANplex family, starting ﬁrst with the 2500 switch, then adding ISE technology to the LANplex 6000. The industry recognizes the 2500
as a high-performance and powerful Ethernet/FDDI switch. 3Com's claim is that it has the industry's most powerful Ethernet/FDDI switch based on its ASIC technology. The switch is
designed for higher performance, taking advantage of a series of design attributes. Advanced packet buffering serves as an advantage to alleviate port contention and to minimize packets that
are dropped because they can't be received. 3Com's "Elastic Packet Buffering" approach means that a maximum number of buffers is "guaranteed" for each port; more buffering can be
dynamically added to the port as needed. Furthermore, user-deﬁned packet ﬁltering enables trafﬁc control based on thresholds and speciﬁc characteristics.
IP fragmentation more efﬁciently deals with switching packets. The 2500 achieves this by breaking up the FDDI packet, which can be 4500 bytes in length, and sending it to an Ethernet
network that has packets of 1500 bytes in length. This is important where devices are high-performance servers that drive wirespeed links.
User Perspectives
Aberdeen had the opportunity to visit a sample of actual LANplex 2500 customers, including major hospitals, ﬁnancial institutions, universities and manufacturing companies, over the past
several months. The 2500 has been installed in companies with very different organizational structures. As a workgroup switch it appears comfortable where IS has a master plan, or whether
the individual departments do the evaluation and decide whether to install, or even where the decision is made by a corporate evaluation team of individuals from different groups in the
company.
A typical scenario for the LANplex 2500 (see Figure 1) provides switched Ethernet client connections to FDDIbased servers located on a campus backbone. Users may have considered
routers linking the Ethernet LAN to the FDDI backbone, but that solution is very expensive. Typically, the user has routers going out to the outside world, but desires workgroup connectivity
connected to the FDDI ring based on price/performance advantages.

Another scenario is to isolate the server trafﬁc on its own backbone, creating a separate environment from the campus backbone. This can be achieved by using the second FDDI port to
connect the departmental backbone with its local servers. Wiring hub ﬂoor devices are often connected directly to the 2500 since it has multiple MAC address capabilities.
The 2500 is not designed for networks which desire to concentrate FDDI ports. Where FDDI concentration is required, the LANplex 6000 becomes a viable family solution. The attractive
cost point and modularity of the 2500 made this less important in the some of the visited user environments.
Meeting User Expectations
Aberdeen research continues to ﬁnd that end-users have a speciﬁc LAN switching application in mind and increased determination to cut through the marketing hype. Thus, the LANplex
2500 and similar products are expected to be fully operational when ﬁrst installed. As one user we interviewed noted, "there is no convenient time to take our network down." It is this
philosophy which permeates through the higher-end customer and was further matched by the customer's concern to assure that the switches being evaluated in the network had already
shipped to other customers. The user's measure of upgradability was that the device is fully ﬂash PROM and fully downloadable from the central site. No forklift upgrades are acceptable.
There are other critical pieces of the user's planning agenda: Virtual Networking yields security, bandwidth management, and ease of administration of moves, adds and changes. Virtual
LANs are the "logical" grouping of endusers into workgroups, independent of their physical network connections. LANplex 2500 options for virtual LAN support include packet ﬁltering
based on port groups, and MAC address groups. Intranetworking routing software for IP, IPX and AppleTalk networks is also included.
Growth Path Is Addressed
Not surprisingly, the two-way ability to work with the FDDI campus backbone and emerging ATM backbone is a fundamental requirement for those installing LANplex 2500 networks. The
transparency of the 2500 was important in the decision making process, and 3Com's goal to achieve OC-3 ATM support in an uplink which takes up only one slot was very attractive. This
means that there can be a planned cutover and timeframe where ATM is gradually introduced to the backbone. The 2port uplink can thus be a mix and match situation.
Enhanced migration ﬂexibility includes uplinks for not only ATM, but more importantly, Fast Ethernet (100BaseTX). Users noted that they would ﬁnd a Fast Ethernet card, an enhancement
expected to ship by early 1996, very attractive. The OC3 uplink with appropriate LAN emulation and UNI connectivity software will also be shipping in the ﬁrst quarter of 1996, which
appears consistent with the planning agenda of selected users. A number of users noted that their ATM backbone will be put into effect in the 1996 timeframe and appropriate ATM backbone
switches installed in that timeframe.
Aberdeen found that the 16-port LANplex capacity is ﬁne for many users. However, some wanted to work with a higher density given the small physical space of their data closets. They
noted that they wanted this increased scalability without sacriﬁcing performance. Judging by 3Com's past responsiveness, we would expect some of these holes to be ﬁlled quickly.
Building the Management Infrastructure
The three major enterprise management platforms are supported in the ﬂagship application called Transcend Enterprise Manager, including HP, Sun and IBM platform solutions. A Windows
platform version of Transcend Enterprise Manager software supporting the LANplex family will be available in the Q4 1995 timeframe.
However, even with all the enhanced management and extensive per port statistics, Aberdeen found a consistent demand for the ability to access the devices through a telnet server
connection. Telnet support in the LANplex 2500 adds signiﬁcant ﬂexibility to troubleshooting and enables downline loading of software updates. The requirements for this feature have put
competitor Cisco on the defensive, though once again we believe this lead is shortlived.
3Com's dedicated Roving Analysis Port was found to be attractive as an innovative way to enable remote LAN monitoring--resulting in builtin RMON analysis. This feature is not only
designed to work for a single port, but is available across multiple ports over the switch. That is, there is no limit to the number of roving analysis ports per switch. Similarly, it works not
only with a single switch, but is fully available across multiple switches. 3Com supports standard RMON from multiple external hardware and software sources.


Any port can be a roving port across the switches. A user can connect an external device or probe to a port. The logical next step is to integrate a software-based RMON into the 2500 so that
the user does not have to purchase a higher-cost hardware probe or external Sniffer-like device. Aberdeen believes that Cisco's support of RFC1271 which enables RMON access across all
ports and the development of an RMON agent in the box is important. 3Com has recognized this requirement and is presently evaluating alternatives and analyzing relevant impacts on
memory and switching performance.
Conclusion
In a market punctuated by promises and exciting spec sheets it is important to recognize that 3Com's LANplex 2500 has met and fully exceeded its promise--both as an excellent workgroup
solution and as a ﬂagship revenue performer for 3Com's Switching Division.
Aberdeen ﬁnds that the user issue is very straightforward: "Look at the spec sheets, they look great--but does it work?" Product stability continues to be a big issue for today's users, a number
of whom need to quickly drop the switch into a production environment. The LANplex 2500 has come through in these environments with ﬂying colors.
Aberdeen fully recognizes that the LANplex 2500 has played an important tactical role to strengthening the expanding suite of 3Com's switching solutions. 3Com has used the LANplex to
take the high ground and create a proﬁtable niche for addressing high-performance networks which can effectively work with campus-based FDDI LANs. Now the next step is to deliver the
promise of uplink ﬂexibility to assure that users can take advantage of the full gamut of high-speed server connectivity, including Fast Ethernet and ATM.

### Abstract

This Aberdeen Group product profile, published September 1995, evaluates 3Com's LANplex 2500 Ethernet/FDDI workgroup switch approximately nine months after its December 1994 market introduction. Using primary user research across hospitals, financial institutions, universities, and manufacturing firms, combined with competitive analysis against Cisco Systems, Bay Networks, and ALANTEC, Aberdeen assessed the 2500's technical architecture, ISE-chip ASIC performance (565K pps forwarding rate), management capabilities (RMON, Transcend Enterprise Manager), and investment-protection roadmap. Key findings confirm the LANplex 2500 as the market's most capable Ethernet/FDDI workgroup switch at its price point, outperforming competitors on switching performance, feature set, and modular design while leveraging 3Com's established LANplex 6000 installed base. The study predicts that Fast Ethernet (100BaseTX) and OC-3 ATM uplink cards will ship in Q1 1996, that FDDI backbones will remain prominent in enterprise planning through the mid-1990s, and that 3Com's 6-to-9-month competitive lead is under pressure as rivals intensify development efforts. The report frames these findings within 3Com's three-stage High-Performance Scalable Networking (HPSN) vision, positioning the LANplex 2500 as Stage 2 of a migration path from collapsed backbones through distributed LAN switching toward eventual ATM adoption.
