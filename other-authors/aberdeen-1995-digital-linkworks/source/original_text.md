Digital LinkWorks -- Delivering Solutions to MIS and End-Users
Executive Overview
A new category of collaborative software, called Enterprise Groupware, is starting to appear in the market. Unlike older groupware solutions built on proprietary applications and databases,
this new solution utilizes open databases and distributed object technology. It delivers seemingly incredible beneﬁts to developers, users, and MIS. Because Enterprise Groupware is so
ﬂexible and can be applied to so many problems, its beneﬁts appear to stretch credibility; Aberdeen suggests your doubts be set aside this technology is real and deserves careful
consideration.
Aberdeen research has shown that groupware products do not usually satisfy everyone in the company. The failure to gain support across multiple organizations is not unexpected.
Groupware solutions purchased by departments rarely live up to the rigorous needs of MIS, and MIS acquired solutions rarely meet the ease of use and ﬂexibility expected by users. The
ability of Enterprise Groupware to gain cross organizational support will be a key differentiator in the marketplace, since this is a critical success factor in implementing any enterprise
solution. As an example, users who have deployed Digital LinkWorks, the ﬁrst Enterprise Groupware product, state that it has enabled them to roll out mission critical applications in record
time, while garnering the full support of endusers, department managers and MIS.
Distributed Objects as a Groupware Infrastructure
Aberdeen research has recently identiﬁed a new category of collaborative software, called Enterprise Groupware, that is based on distributed objects. This unifying objectoriented technology
is leaving the research lab and ﬁnding its way into the market as an integration framework that encompasses disparate clients, servers, and applications. Corporate MIS beneﬁts because
application development, deployment and administration using this object oriented environment are extremely fast and easy. Endusers also beneﬁt because they can develop solutions using
their own familiar desktop tools. By encapsulating applications and data, Enterprise Groupware can provide users easy access to all corporate data, including data resident on legacy systems,
servers, or desktops. Because the infrastructure also maintains data on each user, data access can be tightly controlled, even within applications that are not "security aware." When users
logon, they have controlled access to all data objects stored in the infrastructure, and because the objects are stored centrally, they have this access regardless of the desktop they log in from
or their location. Figure 1 helps identify what this new class of Enterprise Groupware can and can not do.
Is it Groupware or Enterprise Groupware?
Enterprise Groupware has the following attributes and characteristics:
Solutions are assembled by dragging and dropping components;
A solution can be turned into a basic component, and reused in other components;
A work environment allows new components to be built, tested and distributed;
A change in a component is immediately reﬂected in every solution in which it is used;
Legacy applications and data can be turned into components;
Users and Groups are objects in the system with speciﬁc properties;
Data, Applications, and Users are all location independent;
All elements are searchable and accessible by components and users.
Applying the above litmus test to Microsoft Exchange, or even Lotus Notes, makes it clear that Enterprise Groupware is a signiﬁcant departure from the technologies used today. The
majority of current groupware products lack the above features because they are not implemented in a object oriented environment. Indeed, Lotus Notes is much more like a standard
development environment, supporting multiple development tools, each appropriate for a different task. An Enterprise Groupware system is so ﬂexible that if an important process is already
implemented in a ﬁrst generation groupware product, it can be encapsulated to become a component in the Enterprise Groupware system.

Groupware Deﬁnition Perspective is Everything
Aberdeen has generically deﬁned groupware as "any software that facilitates either a speciﬁc task or a team working environment through the combination of applications, networking and
communications." This generic deﬁnition, however, fails to take into account the speciﬁc tasks departments want to manage using groupware. Since each department is typically trying to
achieve a different objective, they also have signiﬁcantly different requirements. This has caused the groupware marketplace to become crowded with products that appear to have little in
common. As an example, MIS may use groupware to manage, control, and standardize access to resources, execute reengineering plans, and even automate the deployment and support of
corporate client server applications. These MIS tasks typically need groupware products to meet standard MIS requirements, including an environment for rapid development, deployment,
and modiﬁcation of applications that automate multivendor business processes across multiple client and server platforms while controlling user access to corporate data and applications.
Wherever possible, MIS will want these new products to use the company's current database and infrastructure, so MIS can reduce training and administration costs. MIS will also demand
the groupware products be easily backedup and recovered, and will also be interested in tools that assure availability.

Endusers and department managers have an entirely different set of requirements for groupware products. They want groupware to make it easier to ﬁnd information, and to generally support
group collaboration on documents and projects, using the desktop tools they already have installed and are familiar with. This audience judges groupware based on how easily it enables
group collaboration and task automation. It is important that the product support a searchable common repository and the ability to identify who has a particular object, who made changes to
it, and what those changes are.
Looking at these two lists, it is not surprising that MIS and departments frequently ﬁnd themselves at odds (see ﬁgure 2). The department wants to create new forms and databases to use
locally, while MIS struggles to consolidate databases and create a single view of the data it already has. Allowing departments the freedom to create and modify their own databases appears
problematic to MIS. At another level, MIS is being asked to make corporate data more secure because remote access and Internet connectivity is becoming common. To accomplish this task
MIS must narrow down potential areas of security vulnerability, and do this even while departments are putting more people in the ﬁeld.


Digital LinkWorks An Example
Digital Equipment Corporation introduced LinkWorks in 1994, and has just announced version 3, putting them years ahead of the more traditional groupware providers. Based on distributed
object technology, LinkWorks executes simultaneously on both the client and the server to track, manage and integrate all applications, data, and users. Its beneﬁts clearly demonstrate the
power and ﬂexibility inherent in Enterprise Groupware. By maintaining a consistent environment across most desktop and server environments, LinkWorks can ﬁnd any data in its
environment, independent of the application that created it, the platform it resides on, or its physical location. Using LinkWorks tools, users can easily share data with anyone in the
workgroup or the enterprise, and can automate the creation, modiﬁcation and distribution of data. LinkWorks is also capable of integrating and using applications already installed on
desktops, servers, and legacy systems. By incorporating the applications already deployed users can lower administrator and enduser training costs signiﬁcantly. And we haven't even started
to consider the beneﬁts to MIS.
Developers build applications in this object oriented environment with "components." Sophisticated solutions are created from a toolkit which provides many of the most common classes
and components. If a new function is needed, it can often be developed by simply changing the conﬁguration or modifying one of the current components, although new components can also
be built from scratch, as can a new class. An important capability in this environment is that, once a component is deﬁned, it can be used in any solution that needs it. Should the object be
modiﬁed at a later date, all solutions that use that object are "updated" automatically. Perhaps one of the most important features of this environment is that current applications can be
encapsulated as objects, and then used like any other objects to create new solutions.
Establishing the Framework
LinkWorks software executes simultaneously on the desktop and server (see ﬁgure 3). Today, the LinkWorks server is supported on Digital UNIX , HPUX, SCOUnix, OpenVMS, IBM AIX,
and will also soon be available on Windows NT 3.5 servers. The desktops currently supported include Windows 3.1, Windows for Workgroups, Motif (Digital, HP and IBM), OS/2
Presentation Manager and the Macintosh OS. LinkWorks uses RPCs to communicate with the server for added robustness and uses the server as the object store. The client manages the user
interface, and maintains a consistent look and feel across all the clients supported. In addition, LinkWorks can record or execute LinkWorks scripts, making it very easy for users to automate
tasks. Indeed, a LinkWorks script can be executed on any desktop, or on any server. If desired, it can also be encapsulated and used as an object. The desktop also integrates LinkWorks with
the local operating system and ﬁle subsystem, and makes LinkWorks objects available to applications that run on the desktop. For Windows desktops, there is also a very impressive use of
OLE linking that allows Windows applications to become objects in the LinkWorks repository (See Windows Integration).

On the server, LinkWorks tracks each desktop based on speciﬁc user ID and can also establish basic desktop templates based on a group ID. The full object oriented system executes on the
server and is used to create, execute, distribute and manage all LinkWorks objects and their constituent components (applications, data, attributes, and methods). Every object is given an
object identiﬁer that is globally unique, and every instance of an object is saved along with the data that speciﬁes who modiﬁed it, when it was modiﬁed, and what the modiﬁcations were.
This powerful function allows a ﬁle to be made available for group modiﬁcation, and maintains a complete log of who made what changes when, permitting any intermediate revision to be
captured. The server also maintains a complete record of every object, no matter what server the object is located on. This allows searches to be made that can ﬁnd any object in the
LinkWorks environment, regardless of its location.
The server software also provides an interface to the local database which is used as the systems repository. LinkWorks supports Oracle, CAIngres, Microsoft SQL Server or Informix as
repository databases, with support for Sybase planned, and offers several sophisticated tools to manage system resources.
Department and Enduser Beneﬁts
Out of the box, LinkWorks adds information sharing, electronic mail, document management and workﬂow capabilities to existing systems and applications. Current enduser applications can
be easily integrated into the LinkWorks framework to become part of the overall groupware environment.
However, the very ﬁrst beneﬁt endusers are likely to appreciate in LinkWorks is that it remembers their desktop conﬁguration. When endusers sign on, the LinkWorks environment
remembers how they left their desk and what objects they had, then recreates the desk. It does this no matter where you log in from or what operating system the desktop is using. A traveling
user that leaves a Windows desktop in the United States, and logs in from a Macintosh desktop in Belgium, will see the desk just as it was left, with all of the same data and ﬁles available
(assuming the administrator set the permissions to allow this).
Once in the LinkWorks environment, desktop users have a choice to make. While they can use their desktop applications the way they always did, as a LinkWorks participant, they can now
begin to collaborate easily. As an example, a word processing document can be made available to anyone within the LinkWorks environment for editing. LinkWorks makes sure everyone
gets his or her own copy of the data, and synchronizes edits. At any moment, the exact status of the document can be identiﬁed, and the originator of speciﬁc changes identiﬁed.
If the user wants to take a more proactive approach, LinkWorks allows the user to specify a route. Using a graphical interface, the user selects the ﬂow of the object from an organization
chart. Each recipient is dragged and dropped into the path. If desired, branches can be assigned based on any number of conditions. Simple graphical objects allow all of this to be
accomplished in an amazingly easy and nonthreatening fashion. For other users who might want to stay in touch with what's happening on speciﬁc projects, the environment allows a user to
identify objects in which they are interested. When that particular object is modiﬁed, the user is notiﬁed and given the opportunity to view the updated object. LinkWorks does this for ANY
object an Excel spreadsheet, an Access database, or any other object encapsulated within LinkWorks.
MS Windows Integration
Since most users have Windows based desktops, Digital has recognized that close integration with Windows is critical. Digital Equipment and Microsoft have worked together on several
joint efforts, including the porting of NT to the Alpha platform and the integration of OLE and distributed object technology with Digital's object broker product. LinkWorks clearly
demonstrates the power of linking these two technologies. LinkWorks can also use OLE Automation to invoke actions on the desktop, allowing LinkWorks to tightly integrate with OLE
capable applications that operate in the Windows environment.
MIS and Support Beneﬁts
The LinkWorks distributed object architecture enables rapid application development and deployment. Perhaps more importantly, it also enables changes to be deployed instantly, and makes
the incremental modiﬁcations typically needed to meet local department needs extremely easy; easy enough that it can often be done by the department through simple conﬁguration changes.
Another beneﬁt of the LinkWorks environment is that data and applications only need to be integrated into LinkWorks once in order to be available to anyone in the corporation. The
LinkWorks toolset includes templates for many databases and desktop applications, so this encapsulation will move quickly. However, MIS still needs to do its homework and manage this
complex effort.
Moreover, large companies typically have several legacy applications and databases that were "frozen" years ago. While the LinkWorks Application Plus Objects (APO) interface makes this
possible, it does require a developer who understands both objects and the legacy application.
Aberdeen recommends companies confront these difﬁculties for two reasons: ﬁrst, because this effort is extremely valuable. Once properly integrated into LinkWorks, the legacy application
can be tied into any number of LinkWorks solutions, further leveraging the company's investment in it. Secondly, legacy programmers are sure to appreciate the opportunity to become
experts on objectoriented environments like LinkWorks. While their rampup time may be slow, once uptospeed, they can be reassigned to creating more mission critical applications in
LinkWorks, creating tremendous forward momentum for reengineering efforts.
Once these difﬁculties are resolved and the legacy environments are integrated into LinkWorks, several other seemingly intractable problems get resolved. For example, LinkWorks will:
fully implement security across a workgroup environment;
integrate with existing mail clients to produce a robust messaging system;
provide an object repository of all data and resources for easy backup and recovery;
enable complex searches against all the business objects in the network;
leverage endusers' existing desktop and application expertise;
leverage current training by allowing users and MIS to stay in their favorite environments;
maintain objects across several databases and applications;
provide the ability to centrally manage most performance characteristics;


make Microsoft OLE Automation work in a distributed environment.
Beneﬁts of the Server Oriented Architecture
Because of LinkWorks' modular threetier client/server architecture, the server portion can be tuned for different platforms. A Transaction Dispatch Facility optimizes the use of server
processes and existing connections, maintaining a pool of server tasks that dynamically adjusts to the workload. Within this environment, IS can assign priorities to the various server
operations while maintaining a managed performance environment.
The LinkWorks Cell architecture helps assure application availability by allowing the entire LinkWorks environment to be controlled from a central location using the Administration
Manager. This eventbased system maintains the LinkWorks environment on a per Cell cluster basis. A Cell holds multiple LinkWorks objects and many Cells form a cluster. Each LinkWorks
object is located in a Cell that is tied to a particular server. If the server should fail, clients attached to that server will still be able to function, although they will not be able to access objects
stored on that server. As a result, a server failure only has a local impact, and does not bring down the entire LinkWorks cluster.
To provide robust security, every LinkWorks object is assigned access rights when it is created that deﬁne what actions can be performed on that object, and by whom. Administrators deﬁne
access rights for groups of users, individual users, or a combination of the two. LinkWorks supports a highly granular approach to access control, allowing a systems administrator to create
custom access rights as well as using up to eight predeﬁned access controls. LinkWorks also makes application distribution extremely simple. Applications and templates created in the
LinkWorks environment can be "grouped" or "packaged" into software components, and installed or deinstalled across any number of LinkWorks servers. Since every object is uniquely
identiﬁed within the entire LinkWorks environment, inadvertent replication of objects is impossible, and objects are easily referenced across the entire enterprise. The distribution of these
software components can be accomplished through any media, such as disk, tape, or CDROM, or through the corporate messaging infrastructure.
Conclusion
While many of the current groupware tools enable workgroup collaboration, they do so in a closed, proprietary environment for which it is typically extremely hard to create solutions.
Because these tools do not incorporate many of a company's currentlydeployed applications, they require signiﬁcant retraining of endusers, MIS administrators and application developers.
And while these environments do make data sharing easier, they do not typically enable endusers to automate their business processes. Instead, specially trained application developers are
required whenever a task becomes even slightly processdriven.
Enterprise Groupware, on the other hand, solves these problems and many others currently encountered in MIS and user departments. Enterprise Groupware encapsulates current
applications, application data, and user proﬁles into a distributed object environment, managing each as a separate and selfcontained entity exhibiting all the attributes of object technology.
The intricacies of the object environment are transparent to endusers, who merely see graphical representations of the objects that can be easily manipulated and strung together to perform
complex tasks. While developers certainly need to be ﬂuent in object technology, support and administration is made substantially easier by the accommodation of several popular databases
as the repository. Corporate resources already familiar with these databases will easily be able to administration, backup, and manage this core technology.
Digital has established a solid lead in bringing Enterprise Groupware to market. LinkWorks enables MIS, Department Managers and endusers to focus on the same simple objectives making
the company more efﬁcient in its daily operations and being rapidly responsive to changing business conditions. Aberdeen suggests that corporations take a long hard look at LinkWorks with
a view towards ensuring a competitive advantage and saving a considerable amount of money and resources by using object technology today. For a more indepth viewof the product Digital
supports a World Wide Web Server at http://www.digital.com/info/linkworks.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group profile evaluating Digital Equipment Corporation's LinkWorks, a document management and workflow platform for enterprise groupware. Introduces the concept of 'Enterprise Groupware' built on distributed object technology and contrasts it with first-generation products such as Lotus Notes and Microsoft Exchange. Argues that LinkWorks uniquely satisfies both MIS and end-user requirements through open databases, OLE integration, and a component-based development model.
