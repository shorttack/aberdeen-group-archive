Digital's Multia Enterprise Client: Finally Someone Listened
Executive Summary
For years, Information Systems (IS) executives have been asking for a single desktop client device capable of easily and transparently accessing multiple, diverse server environments --
mainframes, Unix Servers, and PC servers, even the World- wide Web -- all from the same screen. These same executives, remembering the days when MIS would ensure that their systems
were operational and available, have also long desired that the management of their desktop systems be returned to IS administrators -- freeing the end-user organizations to concentrate on
their speciﬁc business objectives.
With the introduction of the Multia "enterprise client," and its newest Intel Pentium-based members, Digital Equipment Corporation is the only computer supplier that has listened to and
acted on these requirements.
Digitals Multia is a highly-integrated "universal client" desktop device that can provide access to many, disparate server types using a variety of communications protocols simultaneously. It
can access various applications on Unix servers using its integrated Multia X Server software; it can downline load and run Windows NT, Windows '95 and Windows applications locally; it
can access IBM mainframes using 3270 terminal emulation; it can access servers using embedded VT 340/420 terminal emulation; and, it can access the Worldwide Web with embedded
Netscape Navigator software -- all by merely clicking on the appropriate icon. With Multia, all of these emulators and the underlying communications transports come pre- conﬁgured with
the system. The end-user community is no longer burdened with the complexities of trying to establish the communications links and load the terminal emulators in hopes that they will
ultimately get access to applications.
But the real beauty of Multia is that it has been designed to turn system management control back over to the IS department, allowing IS personnel to regain centralized administrative
control of their end-user communities' computing resources. IS administrators can remotely conﬁgure the end-user desktop, they can determine which applications are available to the end-
user and how those applications are accessed over the network. They can even disable or enable hardware options, such as the local ﬂoppy drive. To IS management, this translates into big
savings -- fewer expensive IS personnel are required to support the end-user community, and end-user efﬁciency increases because systems are made more available.
Market Position
Underlying the Multia architecture is a Microsoft Windows NT and PC-base -- but Multia is not positioned as a personal computer. Instead, Multia is positioned as a prepackaged enterprise
client solution -- and the focus is on Multia packaging. End-users or IS administrators could purchase equivalent-to-Multia, third party, off-the-shelf software emulators, communications
protocols, and desktop management products -- and attempt to integrate those products -- but the resulting installation would likely be less stable, and less robust than the Multia
implementation. Hence, Digital focuses on the Multia integrated interoperability and desktop management solution, not the underlying PC architecture, as the system design points that deﬁne
the Multia product offering.
Multia is positioned as a highly manageable, universal enterprise client desktop designed to service the evolving "enterprise client" market. This market is deﬁned by organizations and users
who require turn-key access to multiple, disparate server types in environments that:
have multiple communications transports;
have requirements for access to a variety of applications (like Microsoft Windows and its derivatives, Unix X applications, Digital VT, and IBM 3270);
want application and conﬁguration management services to be provided centrally by qualiﬁed IS administrators rather than departmental end-user personnel.
Multia is priced "a step above" an off-the-shelf PC -- approximately $500 to $800 more (depending on options) than a comparable PC desktop. But, Digital has done extensive work to
integrate various user interfaces, communications protocols, and conﬁguration/application management utilities -- and when combined with the resultant cost savings in system management
personnel and increased corporate operating efﬁciencies -- these margins are readily justiﬁable.
Core Product
Multia has been designed to be a turn-key, universal client that is highly manageable and simple to use. Figure 1 illustrates the Multia system design architecture. But, from a system design
point of view, Multia is a tightly-integrated PC architecture that includes graphics, audio, and Ethernet facilities all within its base platform. This integration leads to one of the most
impressive aspects of Multia -- its desktop "footprint" -- the Multia base platform is about the size of a family photo album. This tight integration also serves to differentiate Multia from
standard, off-the-shelf PCs -- most personal computer platforms need to make room (and increase their base cabinet size) to accommodate slots for hardware add-ons such as graphics,
memory, disk, LAN, and audio -- Multia combines all of these hardware components into one small base. Multia also features a PCI system bus architecture, 256 kb cache (upgradeable to
512K), and an optional PCI slot.
From a hardware perspective, Multia supports a dual architecture strategy. It has models based on Digital's Alpha 64-bit RISC architecture, and its newly announced Intel 100 architecture.
Multia hardware has been designed to be "chip upgradeable" -- so, as new and more powerful microprocessor architectures become available, Multia allows for upgrades to faster processors
(using a zif socket design to enable the users to "plug-in" technology upgrades). The new Intel model features a graphics accelerator, an S3 PCI graphics cards (with 2 MB VRAM), 16 MB
of memory (expandable to 128 MB), 810 MB or 1 GB hard drive, two PCMCIA slots, and a SCSI-II connector.

The operating system is Microsoft Windows NT Workstation 3.51. Windows NT brings multitasking and an element of stability to the desktop that was not there before, and ultimately makes
the Multia concept and system design possible.
The communications transports made available on Multia include TCP/IP (one of the most common peer-to-peer protocols in the industry as well as the primary protocol for access to the
Internet); IPX/SPX (the Novell network operating system protocol); DECnet (peer-to-peer Digital protocol); LAT (an optimized terminal access protocol largely used for termserver support);
NetBEUI (an IBM LAN protocol); and X Serial Line Protocol (an X session protocol largely used to access Unix environments). NFS is included in the Multia X Server option.
A full range of PC applications can be accessed and run on the Multia desktop -- but even more crucial -- Multia provides a path to remote applications running in disparate computing
environments ranging from mainframes, Unix and Open VMS hosts, and to the Internet. Users can easily switch from desktop applications to applications running on multi-user systems or
remote server environments merely by clicking on the appropriate application icon. Users no longer need to go through an entire hierarchy of windows to get to their applications, nor worry
about the communications and networking interconnects -- preconﬁgured Multia emulators combined with system log-on privileges granted by the system administrator allow for fast access
to multiple system environments. See Figure 2 for a typical illustration of the users view of available applications and emulators.

Information Access products include industry leading Rumba from Wall Data, Kea! from Attachmate Corporation, and Netscape Navigator for entry to the Worldwide Web. But Multia's X-
server is supplied by Digital Equipment and provides numerous X11.R6 functions (especially in the areas of predeﬁned protocols, systems and applications connections, and customization-
for-the-workgroup features) as well as tight integration with Multia's conﬁguration manager. Combined, these features all make the Multia X-server offering unique.
Systems administrators must, of course, conﬁgure user access proﬁles and privileges to allow access to appropriate server environments. And it is in the management of multiple, remote
Multia desktops that this product offering really shines. Administrators can manage and reenable "downed" desktops, can enable or disable ﬂoppy disks (important in government or ﬁnancial
institutions where security is paramount), can remotely obtain systems conﬁguration information, and can even schedule software updates and perform software distribution. The level of
integration exhibited in these ease-of-install and ease-of-management features is what truly differentiates Multia from the rest of the marketplace.
Aberdeen Conclusions
IS management has, for far too long, been locked in an enterprise client desktop bind: increasing management services to end-user organizations while at the same time placing restrictions on
how those organizations could utilize desktop computing technology. On one hand, IS managers have had to make it possible for the user community to access information and choose/tailor
applications to meet speciﬁc business objectives; while on the other hand, IS managers have needed to implement controls to limit operational inefﬁciencies such as redundancy (in hardware
and software inventory purchased); Tower-of-Babel inter-company communications issues resulting from lack of standardization; and compromised corporate data security due to lax
distributed computing data management practices.
Digital Equipment Corporation -- with the introduction of the Multia enterprise client desktop -- has found a remedy for this malaise that enables end-user and IS organizations to stop
working at cross purposes. With Multia, end-users can now have access to the information they need wherever it is located, while IS management can now effectively gain centralized control
of that desktop and provide users with a high-degree of services -- and especially effective desktop system management. This is a positive and long awaited great step forward because,
frankly, end-users want to get their jobs done, not manage their computer systems.
Moreover, Multia provides a hidden beneﬁt -- the systems management costs related to servicing the end-user workgroup computing environments should decrease. The exorbitant costs
related to deploying multiple network and systems administrators at remote sites to maintain desktop environments and establish communications links should decrease due to the strength
and power and integration of the centralized desktop management software environment offered with Multia.
Finally, Multia is an indication that the new, market-oriented Digital Equipment Corporation has ﬁnally arrived -- Digital is the only major computer maker that has been able to comprehend
and translate these speciﬁc IS requirements into a product deliverable.

### Abstract

This Aberdeen Group product profile evaluates Digital Equipment Corporation's Multia 'enterprise client' desktop, introduced in late 1995 with both Alpha 64-bit RISC and Intel Pentium 100 models. The study employs product profiling, technology assessment, and competitive analysis to position Multia as a turn-key universal client that addresses the longstanding IS requirement for a single desktop capable of simultaneously accessing mainframes, Unix servers, PC servers, and the World Wide Web via pre-configured emulators and multiple communications protocols. Key findings include that Multia is priced $500–$800 above a comparable PC but justifies the premium through integrated interoperability, centralized IS desktop management, pre-configured terminal emulators (3270, VT 340/420, Netscape Navigator, Rumba, Kea!), a photo-album-size footprint, and chip-upgradeable dual Alpha/Intel architecture. Aberdeen concludes that Digital is the only major computer vendor that listened to enterprise IS requirements and predicts that Multia's centralized management capabilities will meaningfully reduce IS staffing and support costs at remote sites, and that the new 'market-oriented Digital' has finally arrived as a credible enterprise client vendor.
