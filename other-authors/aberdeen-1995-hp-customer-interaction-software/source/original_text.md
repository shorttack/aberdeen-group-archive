Customer Interaction Software -- The New Market Weapon
Market leaders in every industry realize that the key to success lies in building long-term, proﬁtable relationships with customers and prospects. By partnering with clients, organizations can
increase sales, lower costs and better anticipate future needs. To achieve this objective, companies are rapidly deploying a new generation of client-server applications called customer
interaction software (CIS). These systems are speciﬁcally designed for acquiring and retaining customers and include software for customer support, helpdesk, telemarketing and sales force
automation. Aberdeen research shows that the CIS market is growing extremely rapidly -- 40% or better annually -- as companies rush to deploy these strategic, revenue-generating
applications.
Two forces are driving this market expansion. The ﬁrst is the ever-increasing expectation level of customers for service and support. The second is an entirely new set of applications and
information technologies focused on CIS. In the last few years, several new independent software vendors (ISVs) have entered the market, offering advanced, customizable CIS applications.
Systems integrators have also built proﬁtable practices by helping clients design, develop and deploy CIS systems. Buyers are Fortune 1000 companies transitioning from home-grown, ﬁrst-
generation CIS systems with limited functionality. These companies want to take advantage of new capabilities, such as computer-telephony integration and advanced problem resolution, as
well as the greater ﬂexibility these new systems offer. And, with the dramatic decline in the overall cost of a CIS system, smaller organizations are ﬁnding the technology within reach.
In fact, demand for CIS is so high that market growth is constrained by the industry's ability to implement these systems. User organizations need help in understanding and then identifying
the many software, hardware and networking components required. Once selected, user organizations are often unable to ﬁnd the expertise to deploy the new application. Compounding this
problem is the fact that ISVs no longer have the bandwidth to meet the needs of all their clients.
What has been missing is a full-service systems supplier with the right products, services and partnerships needed to bring a complete solution to user organizations. It is for this reason that
Aberdeen is so excited about Hewlett-Packard's Smart ContAct initiative. A comprehensive program that combines value-added software, strategic partnerships with ISVs and systems
integrators, and HP's market-leading desktop and server platforms, Smart ContAct gives Hewlett-Packard the right to call itself the premier systems supplier for the CIS market.
Strategy and Market Position
The Smart ContAct initiative was publicly unveiled in December 1995. However, HP has a proven track record in the CIS market, having already deployed its computer-telephony
integration software ACT (Applied Computer Telephony) at over 200 customer call center sites. Moreover, the company's HP-UX servers and desktop systems are extensively used in the
deployment of ISV-developed CIS applications, with HP's professional services organization involved in many of these projects. Smart ContAct builds on these past successes by
consolidating and formalizing many of the programs and partnerships already in place at HP. Hewlett Packard is not targeting the CIS market by declaring market leadership in a press release
and waiting for customers to rush to its door. Nor is HP building a monolithic solution entirely by itself. Rather, HP has based Smart ContAct on partnerships with leading ISVs and systems
integrators. HP has added its own software, hardware and services speciﬁcally designed to ﬁll any gaps left by its partners. With the Smart ContAct program, HP has assembled all the
components needed to build and deploy a working system -- eliminating the need for users to search for these resources themselves.
Hewlett-Packard wants to be the systems supplier users turn to for call center solutions. As a result, Smart ContAct is not focused on promoting a single HP product or service, but rather is
an overarching program designed to get Hewlett-Packard involved in a wide range of CIS projects. Thus, while HP has developed excellent computer-telephony integration (CTI) middleware
and dedicated services as part of Smart ContAct, the company's primary philosophy is to cooperate with its partners to solve customer needs. This means that HP's products and services are
an option for the customer. The company recognizes that there will be some overlap between its offerings and those of its partners and will work on a project-by-project basis to ﬁnd the
optimal mix for the customer.
Aberdeen has identiﬁed seven key parts to the Smart ContAct program:
Computer-Telephony Integration Middleware -- Hewlett-Packard's ACT and Customer Contact Manager (CCM) software enable companies to seamlessly integrate their telephone
systems with their information systems. (Both ACT and CCM are described in more detail below).
Professional Services -- The company's Professional Services Organization (PSO) consists of 3,800 individuals with experience in implementing client-server systems. The PSO is best
employed at systems-level tasks -- such as hardware and network conﬁguration -- while HP's systems integrator partners tend to focus on application customization and deployment.
Advanced Hardware and Systems Software -- HP is the leading supplier of commercial Risc/Unix servers and has provided its HP UX operating system with several enhancements, such
as symmetric multiprocessing.
ISV Applications -- In Aberdeen's opinion, virtually every premier CIS application provider is part of Smart ContAct.
Systems Integrators -- As mentioned above, HP has recruited leading professional services organizations, such as Andersen and Cambridge Technology Partners, to help with application
implementation and customization.
Complementary HP Offerings -- Products such as OpenView for network management and IntelligentWarehouse for data warehousing will be used in many CIS installations.
Marketing and Distribution -- As part of Smart ContAct, Hewlett-Packard's direct sales force will work more closely with its ISV and systems integrator partners. HP will also conduct
joint marketing initiatives.
Two other facets of Smart ContAct deserve mentioning in this section. The ﬁrst is HP's dedication to open systems and customer choice. There are multiple ISV and systems integrator
partners under the Smart ContAct umbrella because HP recognizes that no single product or service is right for all customers. It is for this same reason that CCM has been architected with
open APIs, enabling it to interface to a wide range of application software and telephone systems. Secondly, for companies with existing, ﬁrst-generation systems, HP wants to assist them in
enhancing what they already have. User organizations are not required to re-engineer their entire applications portfolio to take advantage of products such as CCM. HP has built open
software interfaces and will provide the services needed to surround existing systems with next-generation applications that build on earlier investments.
Smart ContAct Deliverables


In Aberdeen's opinion, Hewlett-Packard is the ﬁrst full-service systems supplier to identify the CIS market and develop a focused program that draws on existing strengths and new software
products to enhance ISV applications. The products and services described below are what give HP its leadership position.
Computer-Telephony Integration Software
After listening to both customers and potential partners, HP determined that there was a need for advanced CTI middleware to link telephone and information systems. Today's call centers
need seamless integration of voice and data. Quite simply, CTI is too complex for most user organizations to implement on their own, and while viewed by most CIS ISVs as critical to the
overall solution, it is not the forte of most software suppliers. To ﬁll this vacuum, HP provides its CCM product.
As shown in Figure 1, HP's ACT offering is one of several link-level products with which CCM can interface. ACT is a device-speciﬁc interface that provides the actual link between a PBX
telephone switch and the information systems network. ACT includes the translation facilities that enable the telephone and computer systems to send messages and commands to each other.

CCM runs on top of ACT, as well as a range of other link-level solutions discussed below, and contains the telephony processes and features visible to the end-user. These features include
call routing, automatic number identiﬁcation and associated screen pop-up, point-and-click dialout, event history and call center statistics. CCM gives customer service representatives
(CSRs) and administrators additional functionality that is not part of many base-level customer service applications. It is important to note that CCM is both application-and communication
device-independent.
There are two ways that CCM can be deployed. In the absence of an ISV-developed application, CCM has the basic customer contact information screen (i.e. name, address and telephone
number) needed by a CSR. User organizations are then able to establish links to existing data sources and build additional screens for viewing this information. CCM allows simultaneous
presentation of video clips, images, customer records, and screens from existing mainframe applications. CCM's support for 3270 screens is a perfect example of how HP allows its
customers to preserve existing investments by surrounding them with new technology and then migrate the entire system over time.
For call centers where there is an ISV application, CCM's screens can be replaced with those of the packaged application. In this role, CCM runs behind the scenes and provides the
telephony features not inherent in the ISV's application. To further accelerate the process, HP is actively assisting the ISVs to integrate their applications with CCM.
In addition to ACT, CCM supports other telephony software interface products that link to PBX switches and automatic call distributors (ACDs) other than those from AT&T or Northern
Telecom. CCM will soon support (Q1, 1996) TSAPI, a call control standard that is experiencing growing industry support. Hewlett-Packard is also interfacing CCM with Dialogic's CT
Connect product, which includes support for Alcatel, Ericsson and Rolm. Finally, HP plans to support additional communication mechanisms, including facsimile, internet, and dedicated
kiosks. Development of these links is already underway. CCM pricing ranges from $300-$800 per user.
HP Professional Services
The role that HP's PSO plays in the Smart ContAct Program depends upon the capabilities of the systems integrator partner(s) involved in the project and the internal resources of the
customer. PSO's strength is in assembling and conﬁguring the numerous hardware, systems software and networking components of a system. Although capable of deploying and
customizing an application, HP believes that its ISV and systems integrator partners are better suited for this task. HP is also prepared to work with systems integrators that want to provide
all the professional services needed on a project -- including system design and implementation. Again, the PSO's role is completely ﬂexible. HP will provide professional services only
where needed and wants to cooperate -- not compete -- with its systems integrator partners.
HP Risc/Unix Server Platforms
HP's scalable hardware product line, robust HP-UX operating system, and thorough support from leading application and RDBMS suppliers make its server platform a leading choice for
users looking to implement next-generation CIS systems. Systems range from the HP K100 at the low-end to the 12-way, 5621 tpm-C rated T-520 at the top.
HP Desktop Options
HP provides a range of desktop options that includes PCs, X-terminals, and advanced Unix-based workstations.
Complementary HP Products
Although not directly associated with the Smart ContAct initiative, HP offerings such as OpenView and OpenMail, as well as its OpenWarehouse program, provide customers with added
opportunities. OpenView can assist in managing the call center network, while OpenWarehouse and OpenMail can provide additional information and communication capabilities to CSRs
and administrators.
Smart ContAct Partnerships
HP believes the most effective CIS implementations are built out of several best-in-class products integrated together. As a result, the keystone to Smart ContAct are HP's strategic
partnerships. Below is a brief description of the dynamics of these partnerships and how they beneﬁt the customer. Figure 2 lists some of the key Smart ContAct partners.

Professional Services from Systems Integration Partners -- Hewlett-Packard has established partnerships with several professional services ﬁrms whose specialty is rapid application
development and customization. By partnering with HP, systems integrators are able to take advantage of HP's packaged middleware to reduce costs and shorten implementation times.

Sales and Marketing Initiatives
A critical component of Smart ContAct is sales and marketing. HP is conducting its own internal road show to familiarize its direct sales force with the Smart ContAct program. Hewlett-
Packard also conducts an ongoing series of seminars with its ISV and systems integrator partners. Because Hewlett-Packard is able to attract an audience of CIOs and senior line-of-business
executives, these seminars provide HP and its partners with high visibility and direct access to decision makers.
Hewlett-Packard is also providing its ISV partners with discounted hardware and software so that they can set up centers of excellence that demonstrate to prospects the potential of their
applications. HP sales professionals conduct joint sales calls with its partners and the company makes its ﬁeld facilities available to members of the Smart ContAct program. Finally, Hewlett-
Packard has established several Industry Solutions Centers (ISCs) that have recognized expertise in selected vertical industries. Domestically, there are ISCs that cover manufacturing,
ﬁnancial services and telecommunications. In Europe, ISCs are forming for telecommunications and ﬁnancial services.

Who Should be Interested in Smart ContAct
Aberdeen believes that Smart ContAct is of vital interest to three different constituencies: user organizations, ISV and systems integrator partners, and Hewlett-Packard's own direct sales
force.
Clearly the biggest beneﬁciaries of Smart ContAct are users. By coordinating into one program all the components needed to build a solution, HP has eliminated the need for users to track
down and evaluate all the point-product suppliers. Additionally, HP has proven excellence in computer-telephony integration, systems software, advanced Risc-based hardware platforms,
networking equipment and professional services. Finally, the stability and resources that a global, $30 billion organization such as HP brings to the table means that users can implement
these systems without worrying about the ﬁnancial health or long-term viability of their supplier partner.


For independent software vendors and systems integrators, HP's CCM and ACT products ﬁll an important hole in their delivery strategy. Most ISVs do not offer computer-telephony
integration as part of their software and many systems integrators are looking for packaged middleware they can use to reduce implementation times and lower costs. The services provided
by HP's PSO group means that ISVs can rapidly ramp up license sales without having to fund a corresponding increase in their professional services staff. This savings will go right to the
bottom line of the software suppliers. And for those systems integrators that want to focus on customization and implementation of the application, HP's system-level services are a logical
counterpart. Also, the mindshare generated by Hewlett-Packard's Smart ContAct sales and marketing campaigns will also be welcomed by its partners.
Finally, Smart ContAct gives HP's direct sales force the tools it needs to meet customer demands. The total value of all software, hardware, networking equipment, and services associated
with ISV-developed CIS applications will exceed $1.5 billion in 1995 and is growing at approximately 40% per year. Just as Hewlett-Packard's Mainframe Alternative program enabled it to
take advantage of data center downsizing, Smart ContAct positions the company at the forefront of the CIS revolution.
Aberdeen Conclusions
The value of Smart ContAct is not a single product, service, or partner, but rather that it is a comprehensive program that encompasses all the elements needed to build and deploy call center
applications. Whether companies want to improve customer service or have a better telemarketing organization, HP and its partners have an answer. This is possible because Hewlett-Packard
has listened to the market and determined the real needs of users before building a product or establishing a service. And because HP's software and hardware products have been built from
the ground up with openness and ﬂexibility in mind, users can build the exact system they want.
Smart ContAct is based on Hewlett Packard's principle of cooperating -- not competing -- with its ISV and systems integrator partners. CCM and HP's professional services are options,
available when the serve the best interests of the customer. It is this outlook that makes Smart ContAct a win-win program for Hewlett-Packard, its partners, and the customer.
Aberdeen has stated publicly on numerous occasions that the customer interaction software market needs a major systems supplier who will take the lead. With Smart ContAct, Hewlett-
Packard has accepted this challenge. As a result, Aberdeen ﬁrmly believes that Hewlett-Packard will be the CIS systems-supplier-of-choice in 1996.
