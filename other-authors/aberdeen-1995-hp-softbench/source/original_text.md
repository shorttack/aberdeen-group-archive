## Original Document Text

Hewlett-Packard's C++ SoftBench 5.0 -- A Powerful Platform for Professional Developers
Executive Summary
Successful Information Systems (IS) executives have found that by developing ﬂexible applications that closely model the core activities of their organization they achieve two strategic
objectives:
business processes are not only automated, they are also often improved, and;
the company is able to rapidly enter new markets and proﬁtably respond to changing business conditions.
One of the primary development criteria of these new systems is that they be designed to accommodate extensive enhancements and modiﬁcations over a multi-year life span. Unfortunately,
traditional software development environments have proven either too inﬂexible or too expensive to achieve this important baseline goal. Modern systems also need to be extended to support
distributed computing architectures in order to provide access to information and resources, respond to organizational changes, and make optimum use of hardware and networking systems.
As a result of these rather complex new information technology requirements, software development techniques and products must be reevaluated. Simply stated, developers need new and
easier ways to build and maintain the industry's distributed client-server applications. Object technology provides the foundation needed to develop these systems. Aberdeen believes that --
with appropriate levels of training and support -- the time to implement object technology is now.
Although object-oriented products and methods are being used on a wider scale to achieve these objectives, many IS decision-makers remain skeptical that software tools alone are the
answer. Aberdeen's ﬁeld research consistently shows that adopting an object-oriented software development approach does deliver real, long-term beneﬁts -- but only when there is the right
mix of advanced tools and training for the programming staff. The test of a software supplier's mettle, therefore, is not just its products, but also its ability to provide the professional services
that make its tools useful.
This Executive White Paper has two objectives. The ﬁrst is to explain the speciﬁc advantages of object-oriented software development and why it is well-suited for rapid application
development and for building distributed systems. The second is to highlight Hewlett-Packard's C++ SoftBench product and accompanying services, which Aberdeen regards as an industrial-
strength option for use by software development teams today.
Why Objects
Aberdeen ﬁrmly believes that tomorrow's information systems success stories will be the ones that start adopting object technology today. Indeed, most independent software vendors, as well
as several leading Fortune 1000 organizations, are already well into the transition.
Unlike procedural programming, objects combine data and algorithms into a single software module. Objects interact with each other and the outside world through well-deﬁned interfaces
and are able to inherit characteristics from parent objects. Object programming models have a level of power and ﬂexibility that provide signiﬁcant productivity improvements. Additionally,
one of Aberdeen's key research ﬁndings is that the real-life beneﬁts of object technology are often different -- and usually more profound -- than the advantages touted on marketing
brochures and during trade show demos. Speciﬁcally, Aberdeen has found the actual beneﬁts to be:
Easier enhancement of applications -- Object-oriented software is inherently modular. As a result, code is typically better organized. Problems and program elements are more quickly
isolated. And through inheritance, an enhancement or change can be made once and automatically replicated throughout an entire system. Thus, object technology is particularly
appropriate for applications that will be extensively modiﬁed over a long life span.
Better representation of business processes -- The combination of data and algorithms is a much better way to model real-world entities. Programmers and end-users have a common
framework for discussing the various functions and components of an application under development -- resulting in software that more accurately meets user needs.
Software complexity is hidden -- The internal algorithms of an object are encapsulated by an interface, which shields the developer from the complex intricacies of how an object
performs a particular function. The developer simply needs to request the appropriate service of an object -- which makes programming much more straightforward.
Extended life of legacy systems -- Object-oriented applications can be integrated with existing software by building an interface (or "wrapper") around legacy systems. This preserves
previous software investments and makes for a better organization and structure of existing code.
Object technology is appealing to user organizations, independent software vendors, and systems integrators alike. No one wants to be left behind. However, adopting object technology is
not without risk. Companies must identify and understand the hurdles they face in order to plan an effective strategy for moving to object technology.
Why Distributed Computing
In parallel with the move to new software development methods and strategies, an industry transition to distributed computing is also underway. There are several reasons for this. The ﬁrst is
that businesses usually consist of multiple, semi-autonomous groups that are geographically separate. While these divisions want to control the data and information critical to their day-to-
day operations, they also must often share the same set of core databases and information with other units. For example, the accounting and order entry departments both rely on inventory
data and want to keep an up-to-the-minute copy of that information on their local server. As a result, a major responsibility for IS is to support distributed applications and synchronize
distributed, common-access data ﬁles.
Another complexity of distributed, client-server computing is that the various modules of enterprise applications are likely to be running in different locations, each potentially with its own
network and hardware platform proﬁle. This not only means that application development is complicated, but also that administrative and maintenance expenses for the systems can mount
quickly unless the underlying software methods have an integrated mechanism for efﬁciently managing resources. For example, when a business rule impacting the entire enterprise changes,
it will soon become cost prohibitive -- if not technically impossible -- to have the programming staff identify each occurrence of the rule and hand-code changes to it.
A last brief example of the impact of distributed computing is found in an increasing number of corporate mergers and acquisitions. Another critical responsibility of the information systems
organization is to quickly integrate the disparate computer systems and applications of the multiple companies so that the new business can function as a single entity. Among other things,
the integration of various billing systems must occur, operational efﬁciency must be increased, and sales and marketing programs must be created to present a credible, uniﬁed position to the
customer. All of these objectives can only be achieved if a robust, ﬂexible, software development infrastructure is in place.
Critical Factors in Adopting Objects
Aberdeen has identiﬁed four elements that have a major impact in the successful use of object-oriented products and programming techniques.

Robust, Market-tested Tools -- It is unacceptable to move to objects only to lose the robust characteristics of existing software development tools. Developers are accustomed to feature-
rich browsers, editors and compilers. Application development teams need conﬁguration management, version control and a means for testing new software. Applications built must
scale from small groups to enterprise-wide deployment. The new development environment must provide these features.
Training and Education -- There is a steep learning curve associated with object technology. Investing sufﬁcient resources to upgrade the skill sets of developers is essential. Learning
how to use a new toolset or the syntax of a language is only part of the problem. Developers must also become comfortable with new methodologies and programming techniques.
Development organizations will look for tools that provide online help and automatically check for common coding errors in order to ramp up new developers quickly and improve
software quality.
Implementation Expertise -- It is a simple truth that most organizations lack sufﬁcient knowledge and experience to use object-oriented tools -- particularly on their ﬁrst initiative. A
third-party organization must be identiﬁed that can provide not only training and education, but implementation services as well. It is often useful to hire veteran experts to perform
some of the more complex programming tasks, such as developing the overall architecture of the new system.
Leadership Vendor -- Most development groups do not know where to start with objects. To compound this problem, few suppliers provide a complete end-to-end solution.
Programming staffs with little experience may ﬁnd themselves cobbling together products from multiple vendors. Having a trusted supplier that can provide guidance and act as a prime
contractor is invaluable. In addition, mission-critical line-of-business applications call for a supplier that will be around for the long term.
While there are many other success factors, Aberdeen's ﬁeld research consistently shows that when any one of the above four is missing, there is a much higher chance of delays, cost
overruns, and user dissatisfaction. In Aberdeen's opinion, Hewlett-Packard has the right combination of products, services and partnerships needed in today's market.
Hewlett-Packard's C++ SoftBench: Robust and Proven
C++ SoftBench version 5.0 is Hewlett-Packard's integrated development environment for building object-oriented, C++-based applications (see Figure 1) and is a member of HP's SoftBench
Solutions family. A key facet in the widespread acceptance of SoftBench is the 80-plus third-party tools that are encapsulated into HP's development environment. When combined with these
other tools, C++ SoftBench covers the entire software development lifecycle, from analysis and design to testing and maintenance.

Using the Encapsulator, SoftBench is able to embed third-party software development tools so that they share the same user interface and seamlessly communicate with each other. HP's
ability to integrate multiple tools means that the company is often able to cooperate, rather than compete, with other suppliers -- enabling its customers to select the best-of-breed tools that
most closely meet their needs. SoftBench not only supports Hewlett-Packard's HP-UX operating system, but also runs on top of Sun Solaris.
SoftBench itself consists of a core set of robust software development modules, including a static analyzer, a program editor, a program debugger, a performance analyzer, and a browser, as
well as tools for understanding the structure, dependencies and relationships of the ﬁles and objects in an application. SoftBench's static analyzer and debugger have gotten particularly high
marks from developers interviewed by Aberdeen. SoftBench is able to graphically depict the structure of an application and the objects that comprise the software extremely well. These
visualization capabilities make it easier for new developers to quickly understand how an object-oriented application functions and how object inheritance works -- shortening the time
needed to learn the new technology.
The latest enhancements to the SoftBench family were introduced in December 1995 and have three major goals in mind: to reduce the learning curve for new object developers; to improve
the quality and maintainability of code; and to facilitate the management of software in a distributed environment. The speciﬁc improvements to SoftBench are:
Graphical Class Editor -- This new feature of SoftBench 5.0 simpliﬁes code development by allowing developers to visually edit objects and have their changes automatically reﬂected
in the underlying C++ code. As a result, it is much easier for developers to build and maintain mission-critical object-oriented applications. This is now possible because HP has
modiﬁed the static analysis database and graphical viewing capabilities introduced in SoftBench 4.0 from being read-only to read-write.
C++ SoftBench CodeAdvisor -- This add-on module automatically enforces C++ coding rules -- reducing learning time for new developers and improving software quality.
CodeAdvisor takes advantage of the static analysis database automatically created by SoftBench to discover common coding errors such as whether objects are copied properly when
they rely on dynamically allocated memory. The product comes with a set of rules developed by HP's object technology experts and also allows organizations to add their own rules.
Aberdeen believes CodeAdvisor represents an elegant way to teach new C++ developers the subtleties of object-oriented programming and will signiﬁcantly accelerate object adoption
timetables.
Conﬁguration Management -- SoftBench has been designed from the ground up to accommodate developers working in teams. Hewlett-Packard now offers SoftBench CM, an advanced
conﬁguration management tool that facilitates collaboration between developers with such features as version control, checkin/checkout, audit trails, change histories and access control.
The tool is inherently designed to work with SoftBench and provides a low-cost alternative to "big science" conﬁguration management tools. With SoftBench CM, programmers can
work in parallel across multiple HP-UX and Solaris servers.
Other new features include a customizable color ToolBar for frequently used activities, two-way integration with the Xemacs Editor, online CDE or VUE-compliant help that uses advanced
hyper-text technology, and QuickFix for automating the debugging process.

For systems that contain a mix of C++, C and COBOL, HP offers COBOL SoftBench, COBOL/C++ SoftBench and COBOL/C SoftBench. These products enable a developer to build and
maintain a system in multiple languages. Applications can be migrated from procedural to object-oriented programming in stages gradually, rather than having a wholesale upheaval that
overtaxes the capabilities of internal IS staff. SoftBench 5.0 continues to support Micro Focus COBOL, CICS, and SQL as in previous versions. In addition, databases supported include
Informix, DB2/9000, Oracle, Sybase and Allbase.
SoftBench is also well suited for a distributed computing model -- both from a development and deployment perspective. SoftBench CM enables developers to collaborate on a project, even
when they are distributed across an enterprise. Distributed development is also supported through HP's Sub-Process Control Daemon, which is discussed in a later section. For deployment,
SoftBench can be used to build applications that make use of the OSF's Distributed Computing Environment, enabling various software modules to be executed on multiple hardware
platforms networked together.
Aberdeen believes that SoftBench's team development features and ability to encapsulate third-party tools result in an excellent environment for professional developers building mission
critical applications. With the enhancements in version 5.0, Hewlett-Packard continues its commitment to making object-oriented development easier, improving software quality, and
helping new developers rapidly move up the learning curve. Finally, the product's advanced visualization capabilities and multi-language support make it a superior choice for organizations
moving from procedural to object-oriented programming.
Typical Deployment Scenario
Hewlett-Packard's experience is that customers undertake three basic activities. The ﬁrst is to make an inventory of the existing application portfolio to determine which functionality should
be encapsulated and linked to new, object-oriented applications. The encapsulation and interface building is done with C++ SoftBench and UIM/X. The process of identifying, isolating and
wrapping legacy software components also helps to build object expertise within the developer staff and provide a better understanding of a company's information systems infrastructure.
The next stage is to create new, line-of-business object-oriented applications. Wherever possible, third-party libraries of generic pre-built objects and templates are used to provide base-level
functionality. By reusing the functionality of these objects, development time is shortened. The objects that give each application its unique functionality and characteristics are created by
modifying existing objects and writing new ones. These new applications are usually linked to legacy systems that have been wrapped with an object-oriented interface. Once initially
deployed, these new applications are regularly enhanced as user requirements dictate.
Finally, applications are distributed across the corporate network. Each organization has its own agenda and time line for addressing distributed computing and HP is prepared with
appropriate products and services to help its customers make this migration. Distributed computing is discussed in a later section of this white paper.
Companies moving to object technology face two major challenges. The ﬁrst is development staff retraining. The second is migrating the code base. As mentioned earlier, existing software
can be encapsulated. Over time, however, developers may ﬁnd that it is better to re-write selected components using an object- oriented model. Hewlett-Packard has had a great deal of
experience moving both people and code -- including its own process of re-architecting parts of SoftBench -- and has the skills needed to help its customers successfully adopt objects.
HP's Consulting and Implementation Services Accelerate the Transition
As stated before, development tools are only part of the solution. The other part of the equation is the expertise and knowledge to use the tools the right way. Hewlett-Packard provides its
customers with extensive training and consulting assistance.
Hewlett Packard believes that organizations moving to objects have parallel training and consulting requirements. The obvious training requirement revolves around learning the intricacies
of object-oriented programming techniques and understanding capabilities of new tools. HP's goal is to provide the best-quality training and education programs in the industry in order to
smooth the customer's technical transition to object programming.
Because it isn't feasible to move an entire existing software portfolio to objects overnight, a strategy and architecture must also be developed. In other words, the company must identify
suitable application candidates, prioritize their migration, estimate staff resource requirements, create project management timetables, and decide whether to encapsulate the legacy
application in an object wrapper, or simply start development from scratch. In this situation, HP -- through its Object- Oriented Solutions Center -- is best suited to provide assistance with IS
management, organizational and business-related issues. HP also helps with selecting and implementing the appropriate technology for the entire system, including hardware platforms,
network design, software licensing and network and systems management.
Some of the more important services offered by Hewlett-Packard include:
C, C++ and COBOL SoftBench Training -- A two-day basic bootcamp, this service teaches the fundamentals of SoftBench and gets developers up and running in the shortest possible
timeframe. Each training session is tailored depending on the programming languages that will be used and the speciﬁc needs and objectives of each organization.
C, C++, COBOL SoftBench and SoftBench CM JumpStart -- These offerings add one extra day for installing and conﬁguring SoftBench.
Distributed Object Consulting -- Consisting of focused, on-site consulting, this service helps HP clients design and implement distributed, object-oriented applications. A key component
of this program is HP's experience in project management.
Object Architecture Consulting -- This offering ensures that the object design and structure corresponds with present and future business process requirements.
Transition Services for Objects -- HP assesses and provides services for the organizational and training needs that accompany the transition to object technology.
Customer Education -- Includes seminars which address the concepts behind object analysis and design as well as workshops that help hone object development skills.
A Natural Path to Distributed Computing
Objects can be an important facilitator for building distributed systems. By deﬁnition, object-oriented software code is modular in nature and has strong interfaces. It is much easier to
partition an object-oriented application than it is to separate the "spaghetti" code that is so often associated with a traditional procedural system. As shown in Figure 2, HP envisions one
natural migration path in which applications ﬁrst become object-oriented and then become distributed. This facilitates enterprise-wide development.

In addition to its professional services, Hewlett-Packard has several tools and products speciﬁcally designed for building distributed applications. Today, HP provides Object-Oriented
Distributed Computing Environment (OODCE), which can be used in conjunction with SoftBench to create applications that run on top of the OSF's Distributed Computing Environment
(DCE). Also, a Sub-Process Control Daemon (SPCD) is available for SoftBench, which when ported to a range of UNIX and non-UNIX platforms supplies cross-development capabilities.
HP Distributed Smalltalk (DST) is HP's offering that allows organizations to build object-oriented Smalltalk systems conforming to the Object Management Group's CORBA speciﬁcation.
CORBA-compliant distributed applications make use of an object request broker (ORB). An ORB is essentially a software trafﬁc cop that automatically routes requests across the network
and provides translation services. In the ﬁrst half of 1996, HP will roll out its ORB Plus, which will let developers create CORBA-compliant C++ applications with C++ SoftBench.
Aberdeen ﬁrmly believes that distributed computing will become increasingly critical to daily business operations and that object technology offers an excellent path to this objective. As a
result, when charting a course for objects, it is important for user organizations to also evaluate a supplier's strategy and products for distributed computing, since one leads to the other. This
is true even for those companies that have no immediate plans for distributed applications.
Future Directions
HP's vision of the future is that applications will be assembled out of a variety of pre-built components. These components may be developed in house, by third parties, or consist of legacy
systems. As a result, HP will continue to provide SoftBench with the capabilities required for this type of development. Aberdeen has found that Hewlett-Packard puts a tremendous amount
of forethought into the future uses of existing SoftBench features. A perfect example is the static analysis database, which was added in the September 1994 rollout of SoftBench 4.0, and is
one of the key underpinnings for the newly-released SoftBench C++ CodeAdvisor and Graphical Editor. Aberdeen expects HP to further enhance the graphical programming and code
maintenance capabilities of SoftBench, add new functionality for team-based programming, improve facilities for new developers, and continue its efforts in the area of distributed
computing.
Additionally, Hewlett-Packard is in the process of porting Taligent's CommonPoint framework-based application system to HP-UX. Essentially, CommonPoint consists of a tightly integrated
suite of pre-built software components written in C++. SoftBench can be used to modify these components -- or frameworks -- resulting in customized applications. Aberdeen believes that
the more object-adept users of SoftBench will soon want to use CommonPoint to build collaborative, desktop applications that can run on a wide range of hardware platforms.
Aberdeen Conclusions
The pressure to deliver ﬂexible, long-life applications is forcing IS executives to consider new software development options. Object technology -- because of its ability to model business
processes, hide software complexity, and facilitate enhancements -- is considered by many organizations as the best choice. And, the heavy use of objects by independent software vendors
for developing products is accelerating the adoption of this technology. HP is building into SoftBench today the functionality that will be needed tomorrow. Aberdeen believes that those user
organizations that realize the impact of this change in the way applications are developed and take advantage of the advanced features in SoftBench will be the ﬁrst to turn information
systems from a cost center into a revenue engine.
To build these new, revenue-generating systems, companies must change the way they build and implement scalable line-of-business applications. This should not be a careless decision,
however. Deploying new software tools should not mean a step backward for the programming staff. To avoid regression, the new environment must include robust browsers, editors,
compilers and support team development. The entire software lifecycle, from analysis and design to testing must be supported. Perhaps most important, there needs to be a full suite of
training and consulting resources available. Hewlett-Packard's SoftBench and its related services have been proven to meet the above requirements. Aberdeen believes C++ SoftBench
deserves serious attention from organizations contemplating a transition to object-oriented software development.
With C++ SoftBench, Hewlett-Packard provides a professional development environment for professional developers. Furthermore, HP is viewed as a trusted supplier and at $25 billion in
revenues, the company has the resources needed to take on the largest and most complex assignments from its customers. Hewlett-Packard provides the right mix of a superior software
development environment, excellent services and industry leadership to help organizations develop next-generation business applications.
