Preface
Information Technology (IT) executives are now fully occupied, through the deployment of Client-Server Solutions (CSS), in the task of reengineering their enterprises' business processes to attain best-practice quality levels. However, Aberdeen's
on-going research continues to show that one of the greatest I-wish-I-hads on the part of IT executives is a less technically complex client-server computing environment. Rather than focusing so much of their time, attention, and energies on the
very serious issues involved with implementing and maintaining the product components -- server and client hardware, systems software, networks, databases -- of today's distributed systems, they would rather deploy more satisfying the needs
and requirements of their business users.
Stepping up to satisfy this signiﬁcant market demand is a new and unlikely IT supplier partnership -- IBM's AS/400 Division and SAP AG. The combination of IBM's newly reinvented and reinvigorated AS/400 Advanced Series and SAP's R/3
newly released version 3.0 suite of fully-integrated CSS represents a unique technology value proposition -- simplicity and speed of information infrastructure implementation combined with leading-edge business application technology -- that
alternative supplier solutions will be hard-pressed to match. For IT executives and their line-of-business brethren, the IBM/SAP partnership becomes even more attractive when the potential for enterprise-wide access to both IBM and other highly
AS/400- and R/3-knowledgeable suppliers' professional services and customer support organizations is taken into account. This Proﬁle will explore the basis and the beneﬁts of this CSS partnership -- the industry's latest and potentially one of the
most signiﬁcant.
Executive Summary
After nearly a year of hinting at an ongoing development effort, IBM and SAP announced in September 1995 that SAP's R/3 version 3.0, the industry's hottest enterprise-reengineering CSS, would soon be shipping on the RISC processor-powered
AS/400 Advanced Series models. Installation at select beta customers will begin in November to be followed by a controlled release in the ﬁrst quarter of 1996 and then general availability in the second quarter of 1996. Key to this announcement
is that SAP will be releasing the same R/3 application code in its entirety on the AS/400 that it provides to users running Unix and NT operating environments.
For both those that subscribe to the premise that risk aversion should be a basic IT quality, as well as for those that subscribe to the belief that advanced technology is a positive catalyst for business change, IBM and SAP have created a common
platform for IT-facilitated enterprise reengineering. This partnership changes the ground rules governing enterprise technology and product selection so that what some may have characterized as the safe choice is now in fact also the choice that
makes sense.
The combination of IBM's AS/400 Advanced Series (powered with the new PowerPC AS RISC processors) and SAP's well-proven, enterprise-capable, fully- integrated, three-tier R/3 CSS is a highly competitive platform choice for the most
demanding, best-practices business requirements. Aberdeen makes this assertion based on the:
• deep-rooted and native level of integration between the two products;
• new AS/400 platform optimizations that now deliver open systems connectivity and scaleable client-server performance;
• ease-of-use and ease-of-deployment that can shorten the time to production so critical to realizing reengineered enterprise ROI beneﬁts.
For both decision makers that are in the process of starting with a clean sheet of paper to implement a beat-the-competition information infrastructure and AS/400-System/3X installed-base users that need to upgrade to a state-of-the-art
information system, the AS/400-R/3 combination will represent a unique option in terms of speed-of-implementation and simplicity-of-operations for acquiring the industry's leading CSS.
Market Position
IBM has been the leading commercial midrange computing supplier since the mid-1970s with the release of the System/3X line that has now evolved into the AS/400. Since the AS/400 was launched, IBM has shipped over 325,000 systems
worldwide and created a vibrant market for independent software vendors (ISVs) and professional service organizations. AS/400 systems are used by almost all Global 100 enterprises in some capacity as well as being the mainstay platform for
small-to-medium sized enterprises. While the AS/400's supremacy for midrange commercial computing has recently been challenged by Unix-based systems, its revenue grew 25% during the last year and the ISV's that are betting their
organizations' futures on choosing the right platform for their customers are increasing their AS/400 development and marketing efforts.
IBM's AS/400 Division is an industry-leading systems supplier in its own right -- 1994 revenues were approximately $13 billion and its proﬁts represented nearly 35% of all of IBM's divisions.
SAP AG's heritage is as a fully-integrated global supplier of mainframe-based core business applications. While R/2, SAP's mainframe product, continues to be a component of the company's overall strategy, it has been rapidly eclipsed by R/3, its
CSS. Introduced in 1992, R/3 sales have grown astronomically and now account for two-thirds of SAP's worldwide revenues and over 85% of North American revenues. SAP America, the company's US subsidiary, continues to show nearly 100%
growth every year -- primarily through the licensing of the R/3 CSS. Since bursting onto the North American market, SAP has risen to global CSS market leadership and has become the ﬁrst packaged-application supplier to capture $1 billion in
revenue in a single year.
SAP markets its products primarily to large, $300 million and up, enterprises with multi-national and fully integrated business solution requirements. To add further value to its cross-industry CSS, the company has established multiple Industry
Centers of Excellence (ICOEs). ICOEs seek to both ease the adoption and increase the specialization that SAP can bring to a speciﬁc industry. ICOEs have also acted as industry magnets, causing similarly targeted third-party Big 6 consulting
organizations with R/3 expertise to be formed. Currently ICOEs are in place for the following vertical industries: chemical; pharmaceutical; oil & gas; consumer packaged goods; high technology/electronics; health care; insurance; banking and
utilities.
Key Technologies/Products -- IBM
The AS/400 is designed solely for the commercial business computing market. But as the business computing market has changed -- driven by competitive and economic imperatives that mandate radical business policy, operational and process
changes -- so has the AS/400. The AS/400 Advanced Series, introduced in May 1994, represents a major reengineering effort aimed at transforming the AS/400 to support user-demanded decentralized and distributed system topologies, client-
server application architectures, and high-end scaleability and performance.
Over time, the deﬁnition of what constitutes midrange system characteristics has also changed. Aberdeen research ﬁnds that to meet the mark in today's hotly contested midrange system marketplace suppliers must deliver:
• a consistent, entry-level to high-end scaleable architecture;
• support for high-end Symmetrical Multiprocessing (SMP) and clustering technologies;
• application and operational insulation from underlying technology change;
• high-availability functionality;
• distributed-system WAN, LAN and client-server interconnect technologies;
• high-performance, easy-to-administer, parallel-scaleable Relational Database Management Systems (RDBMS).
The AS/400 Advanced Series
At the heart of the most recently announced AS/400 Advanced Series models -- to be available before the end of 1995 -- is the PowerPC AS 64-bit RISC micro- processor. Table 1 summarizes the capabilities of the PowerPC AS-based Advanced
Series Product Line by model. In conjunction with the PowerPC AS-based AS/400 models, IBM introduced OS/400 Version 3 Release 6 (V3R6) to take full advantage of the new 64-bit RISC hardware architecture. Again, please note that SAP R/3
version for the AS/400 is designed to run only on the AS/400 PowerPC AS Advanced Series models with OS/400 V3R6. IBM and SAP engineers have been working over the last year to ensure that SAP R/3 version 3.0 is optimized and tuned at
the systems and operations levels to take advantage of the 64-bit PowerPC AS RISC processor and OS/400 V3R6.
Why Care About 64-bit Implementations?
First and foremost, 64-bit means performance -- the more system-solution attributes that can exploit integrated 64-bit capabilities, the greater the potential for realizing the full compute and throughput characteristics of a given system. For IT
executives and their business peers this means the potential for much greater price/performance advantages and for doing more with less underlying system resources than previously required. Aberdeen research ﬁnds t1hat with a 64-bit RISC-
based system, such as the AS/400 Advanced Series, when compared to an equivalently conﬁgured non-64-bit-based system, the 64-bit RISC-based systems can:

• support more than twice the current number of users;
• handle up to ﬁve times (5x) the transaction workload; and
• efﬁciently address databases orders-of-magnitude greater in size.
Of further consideration are the additional and signiﬁcant performance beneﬁts gained through the latent parallelism found in the microprocessor instruction set and intrinsic board-level SMP architecture, the operating system services, and the
integrated RDBMS. Given all of this, the potential price/performance advantages are especially favorable for buyers -- especially in comparison to previous non-64-bit non-parallel-scaleable technologies.
Enterprises that have put SAP R/3 into production recognize that users ﬁnd the operational value is so great that high-end scalability quickly becomes a key issue. The ability to run SAP R/3 CSS as 64-bit applications supported by a 64-bit
database (DB2/400) on the AS/400 to gain high-end systems performance beneﬁts is an extremely positive beneﬁt that 32-bit platforms will be hard pressed to match.
Ease of Use Pays Beneﬁts
A hallmark and key competitive strength of the AS/400 platform is its integrated and accessible operating environment. OS/400 V3R6 provides a level of "system administrator-free" operation that continues to set it apart from less integrated
component-based competitive offerings. Within the OS/400 V3 environment, leading-edge users most frequently laud the seamless accessibility to, and management of, distributed system operations, WAN and LAN connectivity options, client-
server and Single Unix Speciﬁcation (Spec 1170) interfaces and the low-overhead commercially-proven ﬁle system and database functions. For example, SAP R/3 was able to be ported over to OS/400 V3R6 in a very straightforward manner due
to the comprehensiveness of IBM's implementation of the Single Unix Speciﬁcation.
Architecting for simplicity and ease of use pays off in other ways as well. Aberdeen notes that considerable effort has gone into forward compatibility for existing AS/400 applications. In most cases, simply rehosting and executing an existing
AS/400 application on the AS/400 Advanced Series V3R6 platform automatically converts it to a true high-performance 64-bit implementation. IT buyers should note the signiﬁcance of this simple upgrade capability -- especially in comparison to
other system suppliers claiming 64-bit operation that offer no such conversion capability and who are attempting to emulate 64-bit operation from 32-bit or in some cases even 16-bit developed applications. As a result, SAP R/3 version 3.0 will be
one of the ﬁrst of many 64-bit applications available on the AS/400 Advanced Series. AS/400 customers who need additional applications to comprehensively meet their enterprise's broad and varied CSS requirements will have an extensive
catalogue of state-of-the-science software solutions to select from.
A High-Performance Infrastructure for High-End Business Computing
Most all Aberdeen clients have now decided that proprietary, monolithic, time-shared computing architectures -- both hardware and software -- are a technology that, at best, has only specialized use within a next-millennium IT infrastructure plan.
The order of the day is now adaptable two- and three-tier client-server capable and partitionable application architectures as embodied in SAP R/3. For those who have appreciated the business-wise approach to technology that has been
exempliﬁed by the AS/400, the good news is that with the PowerPC AS-based Advanced Series, today's most demanding deployment conﬁguration requirements are fully supported. Optimized for client-server operation, the Advanced Server line
offers highly competitive and tunable platforms for deploying those rich-in-functionality, fat-client, desktop-centric applications. For more demanding OLTP-oriented applications where server-power requirements are paramount, the Advanced
Systems line offers all the same client-integration capabilities but with a server-centric performance emphasis.
In today's technology parlance, AS/400 Advanced Series products are fully conﬁgurable across wide- and local-area networks supporting intelligent client workstations. Going forward, the question is not if an AS/400 system can be conﬁgured as
an application, or database, or combination open systems server, but which AS/400 should be chosen for which SAP R/3 function in an enterprise-wide, multi-tiered, client-server architecture.

Key Technologies/Products -- SAP
R/3 is an Application-centric CSS, portable over multiple client, server and RDBMS platforms. The applications, developed using the company's ABAP/4 Development Workbench environment, are deployable in a three-tier client-server
architecture and incorporate system-oriented functionality found in few other CSS. Refer to Table 2 for a summary of R/3 CSS fully-integrated application functionality. In addition, for many enterprises that have in the past relied on several
different industry-speciﬁc application packages to run their varied lines-of-business, SAP's breadth and depth of functionality allows them to consolidate on R/3 -- even to manage disparate operations such as discrete and process manufacturing.
A CSS With More Than Just Line-of-Business Functionality
Most other CSS suppliers have chosen to architect their products relying on the current-at-the-time functionality and services provided by the system-level components with which their solutions operate (e.g., operating system services, database
services and graphical presentation services). SAP has taken a different approach -- SAP has concerned itself with integrating its customer's business processes and providing the capability to change these processes as the enterprise's business
environment changes. And it is this customer-oriented, change-based design goal that, in many ways, deﬁnes its real world business-focused, process-based client-server architecture.
As a result, experienced IT decision makers who have lived through one or two application life cycles -- starting with excitement at acquiring a new back-bone application and ﬁnishing six or seven years later with the weariness of attempting to
keep the old system and its too-numerous modiﬁcations running while another team is implementing the next-generation core applications -- are attracted to the long-anticipated use of SAP R/3 within their enterprises.
SAP is investing in the future of R/3 by allocating between 20% and 25% of its annual revenues -- more than most other CSS suppliers' total yearly revenues -- to ongoing engineering and development efforts. Due to the architectural design of
R/3, these resources can be effectively used to meet customers' changing requests for new functionality and product enhancements. The beneﬁt is that R/3's architectural design has the potential to deliver the high-performance business-solution
platforms and open system characteristics that are critical to meet the demands of the most sophisticated distributed, heterogeneous enterprises well into the future.
The focus of SAP's three-tier client-server design is to isolate R/3 business functionality, and thus R/3 customers, from the complex aspects of technology that are proprietary in nature, subject to change, and that could potentially restrict adoption
of new technologies as they become commercially viable. This design approach is one of the key factors in SAP's move to the AS/400 and being able to deliver native R/3 as it exists on all other supported platforms. This application isolation is
accomplished in the following three ways:
• through the meta-level intelligence and control of the R/3 Repository and SAP Information Model;
• by the use of interface layers at critical system points;
• by implementing external operating system services -- usually found in operating systems, communications and RDBMS software -- into R/3.
Flexibility and Inherent Workﬂow Intelligence Starts with the Data Model
R/3 is a highly integrated and adaptable CSS in large part because of its data dictionary and repository functionality. The R/3 repository allows the CSS to achieve its high degree of heterogeneity, and simultaneously maintain the consistency and
integrity of the crucial business rules and data across a distributed environment -- even when new versions of the applications modules with expanded functionality are released by SAP.
The R/3 repository is portable and not dependent on the data dictionary or catalog of a particular RDBMS. In addition to the expected data and domain attributes, the repository also supports unique, performance-enhancing constructs such as
clustered and pooled tables and matchcode objects and views, used to advantage by the R/3 application layer.
The R/3 repository also serves a metadata function for the SAP Information Model. The Information Model is the high-level entity-relationship model for the entirety of the R/3 System. It is comprised of the Enterprise Data Model (EDM),
Enterprise Functional Model (EFM) and Organizational Model (OM). The Information Model is mapped to the lower-level repository constructs, and therefore spans the

Building on this solid foundation with the availability of R/3 version 3.0, SAP will now be able to provide the capability to establish and allow quick and easy modiﬁcation of process workﬂows. For those enterprises that are adapting the basic

Distributed Performance Requires Prime-Time RDBMS Integration
R/3 supplies interfaces for both presentation and database services. The presentation interface allows the common R/3 application to simultaneously support Windows 3.1, Windows NT, and OS/2 graphical user interfaces (GUIs). As a result, the
AS/400 Advanced Series version of R/3 will look and feel the same to users as a Unix or NT version.
The database interface manages the physical data access and mapping of R/3 logical application constructs to the physical domains and tables of the underlying RDBMS. In conjunction with the ABAP/4 Development Workbench environment, the
database interface is also able to both take advantage of speciﬁc RDBMS extensions and to transparently provide for differences between databases. For example, database extensions are not standardized across RDBMSs with respect to either
functionality or their interfaces. The database interface of R/3 shields these functions from the applications. Thus, a function implemented in a given RDBMS as a stored procedure can be implemented in another database via SQL calls if the
language used there is not as a powerful as the 4GL of the ﬁrst RDBMS.
Three-Tier Architecture
R/3 also manages what are typically operating system services and provides functionality traditionally provided by mainframe-class operating environments. R/3 manages its own transaction queuing and resource allocation as well as provides for
application-level buffering of the most often accessed data. Queuing improves overall application performance because the R/3 system has a more intimate knowledge of which R/3 resources are available and where they are located, especially in
a distributed environment, than does the generic operating system. The application buffering, or "client caching" as SAP refers to it, is another application-performance enhancement. In a physically conﬁgured three-tier system, the buffering or
caching of most often used data on the application server both eliminates the need to constantly re-access the data from the database server and the potentially sizable network load arising from continuously moving the same found data over the
network between the two servers.
Where the AS/400 with R/3 Is Most Effective
Initially, both companies will target joint marketing efforts toward high-end -- over $300 million -- accounts. The targeted focus will be multi-national, multi-site enterprises that are fully involved in or beginning serious reengineering efforts, or
drilling down on critical intra- and inter- company supply chain processes. SAP's R/3 has been proven to be the CSS of choice in these types of situations. The availability of R/3 on the AS/400 Advanced Series shows the conﬁdence of SAP in the
AS/400 for this most critical of enterprise activities. The Advanced Series is now technologically and functionally competitive to alternative Unix or NT server choices, as either a high-performance native R/3 application or database server in a
distributed three-tier architecture. The AS/400-R/3 platform will excel in the areas of ease-of-implementation, ease-of-management, and ease-of-distributed connectivity against those same competitive server-choices.
Outside the two companies' initial marketing focus, the AS/400 Division-SAP partnership raises some interesting possibilities that at a minimum bear watching by interested IT evaluators and could potentially be the real bellwether of the
partnership's ultimate success. The future volume market for AS/400 solutions is in the vast expanse of midrange companies -- $50 to $500 million -- that have traditionally acquired ease-of-use turnkey business solutions and are now facing
business and technological imperatives similar to those that are driving Fortune-500-size organizations. Included in this market are installed-base AS/400 customers and users of alternative midrange solutions operating on platforms from Digital,
Hewlett-Packard, Unisys, Siemens, Bull, ICL, and others. These customers typically seek new technologies with ease-of-implementation and ease-of-operation attributes to meet their new challenges. If the AS/400 Division with SAP creates a
virtual solution -- and distribution channels to sell and support it -- that encompasses all the best attributes of distributed client-server architectures and signiﬁcantly reduces or shields the inherent complexities of using and managing that
architecture, the resulting application/platform solution will have a unique and compelling value proposition in the marketplace.
Sales & Distribution
IBM and SAP are global technology suppliers with extensive sales, service and support capabilities. Both suppliers have also created through their market successes a vibrant and extensive network of complementary third-party suppliers spanning
the range from technology and packaged-application ISVs, to management and technical consulting organizations, to distributors and value-added resellers. As IBM's AS/400 Division and SAP embark on their new partnership, each will
cooperatively market and promote the AS/400-R/3 solution and each will directly sell and license its own products. However, IBM's extensive professional services organization, ISSC, will provide the comprehensive, single-source services
necessary to thoroughly support customers' SAP R/3 implementation on the AS/400 Advanced Series.
Aberdeen Conclusions
Aberdeen's ongoing research of the Client-Server Solutions market ﬁnds that SAP's R/3 is today a product suite that is most accessible by and most often licensed to Fortune-500-class companies. The product's highly adaptable and extensive
technology that makes it so amenable to driving and supporting signiﬁcant business process change comes at the price of complexity. The success of SAP, and the thriving consultant industry supporting enterprise installations, demonstrates that
given the appropriate resources, the complexity of modeling the R/3 solution to an enterprise's speciﬁc requirements is reasonable and within the scope of the enterprise-wide reengineering effort.
For those enterprises for which R/3 is the solution and that already employ AS/400 technology at the company and divisional levels, the combined solution choice will be simply evolutionary -- minimizing technology risks while speeding time to
implementation. For enterprises that are evaluating new R/3 deployment platforms, those that do not include the AS/400 as a highly qualiﬁed platform of choice could well be making a critical oversight. The challenge for IBM's AS/400 Division,
and more particularly IBM's ﬁeld sale force, is how quickly it can show key IT decision makers that the AS/400 now has the functionality that they expected to be available only from Unix and NT systems. But, more importantly, IBM must
clearly demonstrate that the AS/400 has the ease-of-use characteristics that will facilitate quickly implementing and effectively operating a state-of-the-science distributed, client-server computing information infrastructure.
Aberdeen expects the new AS/400 Division-SAP alliance to achieve a signiﬁcant presence in the expanding R/3-driven market and potentially to greatly expand the deployment of AS/400's into new accounts. IBM reports that existing AS/400
customer requests to participate in early-release R/3 trials have surpassed all previous demand levels in the division's history. Aberdeen's analysis of these requests also reveals that for the majority of requesters, R/3 on the AS/400 would also be
their ﬁrst production client-server application and systems deployment.
Aberdeen notes that until now it has been unprecedented for IT clients to indicate that they were selecting the AS/400 as their preferred choice for both the ﬁrst client-server-based application and enterprise-scale CSS deployment. Aberdeen ﬁnds
the combination of IBM's AS/400 Advanced Series with PowerPC AS technology and SAP's proven, enterprise-capable, fully-integrated, three-tier R/3 CSS is a highly competitive platform choice -- especially comparing speed-of-implementation
and simplicity-of-operations to alternative component-based operating environments -- for supporting the most demanding reengineered best-practices business
requirements and advanced IT infrastructures. Enterprises interested in achieving competitive advantage by focusing on streamlined business processes through the exploitation of the R/3-based CSS are advised to now also include the AS/400
Advanced Series with PowerPC AS as part of any comprehensive evaluation.
