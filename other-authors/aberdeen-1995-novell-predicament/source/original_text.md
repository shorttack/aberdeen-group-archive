Summary Observations
Novell's bid to remain a leader in departmental computing is being severely challenged. The company has failed in numerous attempts to architect a viable departmental applications server
strategy — and has now fallen prey to the likes of Microsoft with its Windows NT Server and IBM with OS/2 Warp Server.
IT (Information Technology) managers and strategic planners are now struggling with how to position Novell NetWare servers within their enterprise client/server architecture. These
executives tell Aberdeen that their organizations have made the decision to standardize on Windows NT Workstation as the enterprise strategic desktop operating environment-of-choice. And,
these same executives expect that the strong applications linkage between Windows NT Workstation and Windows NT Server will ultimately drive their organizations to replace their Novell
ﬁle and print servers with the ﬁle and print services found in Windows NT Server.
In this Market Viewpoint, Aberdeen closely examines Novell's new strategic directions and the ramiﬁcations that Novell's new business directions will have for IS (Information Systems)
decision makers, managers, planners, and Novell NetWare users. And Aberdeen advises IT executives to carefully evaluate the use of NetWare for their IT needs for the medium and long
term.
Executive Summary
Despite enviable marketshare, a strong world-wide service and support organization, established indirect channel suppliers, and blazingly fast ﬁle and print services, Novell has failed to turn-
the-corner to remain a viable departmental workgroup leader.
After numerous attempts to bring ISV (Independent Software Vendor) shrink-wrap applications to users of its NetWare (the Novell network operating system) servers, Novell has conceded
its role as the departmental application-server to Microsoft, IBM, and Unix. ISVs are porting their applications fast-and-furiously to Microsoft's Windows NT platform, IBM's OS/2 Warp
Server, and a variety of Unix platforms, but not to NetWare -- and where the applications go, the enterprise follows.
Based upon discussions with IT decision makers, business-line managers, major ISVs and system-platform suppliers, Aberdeen believes that the future of NetWare as a cardinal application
server for the enterprise is bleak.
Novell's new strategy reﬂects that NetWare cannot compete head-to-head with Microsoft's NT, IBM's OS/2, and Unix as an application server. Novell now plans to provide its large installed-
base with a combination of products and services centered around Novell Directory Services (NDS). Novell's strategic NDS-based initiatives include: a major NetWare upgrade (also known
as"Green River"); GroupWise for groupware; ManageWise for systems/network management; Inter/Intranet related offerings including plans for Internet publishing, Web services, and an
endeavor to establish Novell NDS as the basic infrastructure platform for Java-based applets.
Aberdeen believes that Novell's NDS-centric strategies does offer value to Novell's installed-base -- but can ﬁnd no compelling evidence that without new, business-critical client/server
applications that Novell's current strategy will succeed outside of the installed base. Longer term, Aberdeen believes that Novell's current strategy will force its existing users to look to other
suppliers for application services -- Novell ﬁle and print services will be replaced by the ﬁle and print services included within the application server environments of other suppliers.
For Novell's users to remain committed in the long run to their investments in NetWare, Aberdeen believes Novell will need to:
Make additional applications available by integrating NDS with Windows NT Server;
Refocus its engineering efforts on application projects that can be delivered to its installed-base in the near-term, rather than on grandiose long-term projects such as NDS-Everywhere;
Enable Novell users to gain access to Internet-directory services and applications by exploiting the emerging Internet directory services (LDAP, Light weight Directory Access Protocol)
standard rather than by trying to force NDS upon the Web community; and,
Succeed in new business ventures (particularly in its Internet, multimedia and telephony-related endeavors) in order to open new sources of income to replace revenue lost by decreasing
NetWare marketshare.

The New Enterprise Standards Do Not Include Novell
Aberdeen recently talked to IT executives of large, Fortune 500-type accounts -- face-to-face, at their sites -- in order to examine their IS (Information Systems) transition plans as they move
to client/server architectures. These IT decision makers told Aberdeen that they are migrating from Novell's NetWare to Windows NT Server, and cite four primary reasons for doing so:
1. IT planners believe that the latest, client/server applications and development tools are to be found on Windows NT, OS/2, and Unix -- not NetWare;
2. IT decision makers believe that NetWare's mainstay strengths -- ﬁle and print services -- are becoming commodities that are provided with application servers. And these decision
makers are now consolidating departmental-level ﬁle and print services for their users on Windows NT Server or OS/2 Warp Server;
3. IS administrators and LAN administrators, the people who manage distributed computing for the enterprise, indicate that supporting multiple operating environments drains ever-
shrinking IS resources. The choice of a more homogeneous operating environment allows them to minimize or dissipate problems integrating, deploying, and supporting client/server
applications; and

---

4. IT executives believe that either Novell will fully integrate NDS with NT; or Microsoft will deliver OLE Directory Services that will interoperate with NDS -- ultimately allowing
administrators to manage large numbers of departmental NT servers in existing NetWare networks.
To triangulate these ﬁndings, Aberdeen talked with key hardware platform suppliers and software ISVs to ascertain their views regarding NetWare trends. Aberdeen found that few ISVs are
actively porting their applications to NetWare environments; and that PC server suppliers are reporting a decline in their NetWare sales while Microsoft's NT continues to ramp up. The
consistency of feedback from IT planners and managers, hardware platform suppliers, and software suppliers regarding NT Server adoption trends leads Aberdeen to project further erosion
of the Novell position within the enterprise (see Figure 1).

Why Customers Will Keep Novell
First and foremost, with the forthcoming release of Novell's next NetWare release -- also known as"Green River"-- Novell will deliver its ﬁrst major update of NetWare in two years to its
users. Pent up demand for new NetWare features such as symmetrical multiprocessing, distributed print services, additional systems management facilities, and improved Internet Protocol
support will satisfy the needs of many in Novell's installed base -- and these users will remain Novell-loyal.
Second, Novell's NDS platform is an excellent interoperability platform for integrating and managing Novell, NT, and Unix platforms. NDS provides both system administrators and Novell
users with an integrated view of the IT infrastructure -- various systems platforms and desktops, printers, network devices and the like. It is this view that NetWare users enjoy; and it

The New Strategy
Novell views itself as"the server for network computing". With Netware, Novell provides the infrastructure basis for a network-oriented server, and Novell intends to add network-oriented
applications and services to its offerings. Some applications/services strategic directions include application development environments (Java-based), applications that use the network
(collaborative groupware, systems management), and Inter/Intranet services.
At the core of Novell's value proposition for customers today is its Novell Directory Services. Novell is placing a major focus and emphasis on establishing Novell Directory Services as the
dominant IT directory standard -- to ensure that NetWare, NetWare's users, and Novell itself, all have a successful future.
NDS is a directory backbone through which users are provided seamless access to any resource connected to a NetWare network -- LAN and WAN consolidation of networks, users,
applications, data, NetWare servers, printers, ﬁle-shares, and other NetWare services.
Novell's new NDS-based product strategy and business plans include:
NetWare and PowerBrands: Novell will continue to aggressively enhance and market NetWare and NDS, not only for ﬁle and print services, but also as a basic
infrastructure/interoperability platform. Novell will also focus on the sales and marketing of its ManageWise (for the management of distributed systems and devices) and GroupWise
(groupware for collaborative computing applications). These are considered critical"powerbrand"applications.
NDS Everywhere Else: To open new business opportunities Novell is trying to establish NDS as the de-facto network directory service -- to cross-sell directory-enabled management,
groupware and other applications to new customers outside the established NetWare base. Novell has established NDS as a competing alternative to DCE (Distributed Computing
Environment) directory services for Unix-based platforms, and actively supports a consortium of ISV partners who contribute to, and integrate NDS with their products. This list
includes such suppliers as Cheyenne, Symantec, Xerox, HP, Oracle, NetPro, and Palindrome, among others.
Bring on the Internet: Originally conceived as bringing NDS to the Internet, this strategy now resembles a grand plan to establish NDS as the preeminent directory service for the
Internet. Novell is planning to establish its presence as an application supplier with Web servers, Internet Publishing, and electronic commerce;
Next-Generation Application Server Engine: Novell believes that NDS-based technologies will provide invaluable infrastructure services for the next generation of Internet-based
application servers. In particular, Novell sees itself as a basic platform for the development of Java-based application services. In this scenario, NetWare becomes the Java application
and development execution platform; and,
New NDS-based Technology and Business Ventures: Novell is also planning to drive NDS into new technology areas such as NEST (Novell Embedded Systems Technology) and new
business areas such as NDS-enabled multimedia. NEST is essentially a stripped down NetWare operating system targeted at integrating intelligent devices on factory-ﬂoors, in health-

Novell's Failure to Execute Makes All Wonder
But, can Novell successfully execute its current strategy? Not if Novell's recent history of technology failures and market blunders serve as examples.
The beginning of the end came for Novell in the late 1980's: Novell had identiﬁed the changing role its ﬁle and print servers would need to play within an enterprise. Servers were to become
ﬁle/print/ applications and communications servers capable of running client/server applications as well as hosting integrated database capabilities. And, Novell proclaimed with great fanfare
its intention to be the next-generation workgroup-server of choice through a continuation of ambitious technology undertakings and acquisitions.
Yet, Aberdeen's analysis suggests that Novell has continually failed to fulﬁll its objective to become an industry leading application server option -- and has failed for three principal reasons:
1. The repeated annual change of tactics and business lines orchestrated by Novell's executives resulted in continued loss of focus, and unattainable and undelivered results;
2. Novell consistently failed in the technical execution of building a client/server application server out of NetWare; and,
3. ISV partners tired of the protracted disorder, the ever-increasing costs to do business with Novell, and their inability to recoup costs from multiple Novell ports -- and decided to look
elsewhere.
Novell put forward multiple initiatives in order to enable NetWare to become the full-function, server-of-choice for client/server applications -- initiatives that consistently failed. Examples
include:
Portable NetWare: an attempt to take advantage of application services on Unix servers by co-hosting NetWare ﬁle and print services on Unix operating environments. Subsequently renamed
NetWare for Unix, this initiative failed because the native versions of NetWare on Intel-based PC servers consistently outperformed all the hosted Unix versions of NetWare. IT executives
hoping to consolidate the number of their installed NetWare servers found their hopes dashed;
NetWare Loadable Modules (NLMs): an attempt to enable ISVs to load their applications directly onto the NetWare operating environment. This initiative failed because both users and ISVs
found programming and conﬁguring NLMs to be too difﬁcult, time consuming, and prone to NOS crashes. NLMs turned many ISVs away from NetWare;
Processor Independent NetWare (PIN): an attempt to eliminate the reputation for poor performance that Portable NetWare had garnered by porting NetWare natively onto Risc-based
(Reduced Instruction Set) hardware platforms. Originally scheduled for shipment in late 1992, PIN kept being delayed until it was ﬁnally canceled in late 1993. By this time, IT executives
that had pinned their hopes and capacity planning on PIN, and ISV's that had hoped PIN would salvage their costs decided to test Microsoft's Windows NT Advanced Server as an alternative
to NetWare;
UnixWare and the Acquisition of UNIXâ from Unix Software Laboratories: an attempt to fuse NetWare with Unix application servers. This initiative failed because Novell made several
misjudgments: Unix was not conducive to becoming a shrink-wrapped software option; users of Unix had already standardized on NFS (Network File System) ﬁle-sharing and distributed
Unix print-sharing; native NetWare users didn't want to install and manage Unix; and ISVs found that instead of a merge of Unix and NetWare, UnixWare was a new, third environent that
cost just as much to port to and support as the other two;
SuperNOS: an attempt to completely redesign and rebuild NetWare to be a multitasking competitor to Microsoft's Windows NT Server. SuperNOS was originally scheduled for shipment in
1995, and then delayed to 1996. The effort to build a complete, system-level operating environment with network services added to it would have duplicated existing Unix or Windows NT
Server services. SuperNOS failed because it was too-much, too-late; and
WordPerfect and Application Suites: as part of its effort to forestall the entry of Microsoft's NT Server into its own user-base, Novell acquired WordPerfect and Borland's Quattro to construct
a suite of application programs that would compete with Microsoft's OfﬁceSuite. Unable to compete with Microsoft in core ofﬁce applications, Novell sold WordPerfect at a large loss in
1995.

Revenue Declines Conﬁrm the Trend
Due to a change in its distribution stocking program, Novell had revenues of only $188 million for its second quarter of ﬁscal 1996. This is quite a come down from 1995 when second
quarter revenues were $530 million.
Novell states that in its FY 1995, completed October 28, it had NetWare revenues of $1 billion, up 19% from the previous year. Yet because the production-quality application version of
Microsoft NT Server 3.1/SQL Server 6.0 did not ship until July 1995, Novell avoided most of the ﬁercest competition in FY95 that it is encountering now. Reviewing Novell's ﬁrst and
second quarter FY 1996 ﬁnancial statements, Aberdeen believes that NetWare is now shipping at an annualized rate of $750 million — down 25% from the previous year. This rate of decline
could accelerate as more enterprises decide to stop all new NetWare installations and upgrades in favor of an evolutionary migration to NT Server.
But please note that Novell is not in a ﬁnancial crunch even after a $55 million 2Q96 loss. It also had $1.5 billion in cash and net receivables on its balance sheet against only $360 million in
total current liabilities. In addition, the company has no major outstanding debt. In other words, it has the ﬁnancial wherewithal to implement a fresh beginning.

Novell's Business Strategy: Refocussed, But Sustainable?
Simply stated, Aberdeen does not believe that the Novell strategy as it exists today will enable it to sustain the momentum needed to appeal to new customers -- to grow the business. Novell
will not succeed with its gargantuan effort to establish NDS as an omnipresent directory services architecture for one major reason -- Novell does not have the compelling client/server
applications that will drag NDS across the enterprise. Novell NDS will appeal primarily only to Novell's installed base of users.
IT executives and business-line managers buy application solutions to run the business -- not because the underlying platforms are easier to manage. IT planners tell Aberdeen that NDS is
simply an enabling technology which is focused principally on making it easier for IS to manage NetWare-based IT infrastructure. IS managers tell Aberdeen that although Microsoft's NT
Server Domain Services are rudimentary compared to NDS, they are more than sufﬁcient for cost-effectively conﬁguring and managing NT application servers. And, these IS executives tell
Aberdeen that NT application servers can easily interoperate with their current NetWare networks. Moreover, these users expect that Microsoft will deliver OLE DS (OLE Directory
Services) that interoperate with Novell's NDS directory services, in the future.
And although Novell offers excellent, highly integrated applications with GroupWise, ManageWise, NetWare Admin, et al -- the leading edge enterprise client/server applications and new
high-end decision support tools are to be found ﬁrst on NT and Unix platforms -- and, in general, not found on NetWare.
Additionally, Aberdeen questions Novell's ability to compete with its NDS-centric applications against some mighty competitors. With GroupWise, Novell has pitted itself in direct
competition with IBM's Lotus and Microsoft's

---

Exchange. With ManageWise, Novell contends with Microsoft's Systems Management Server (SMS), and more comprehensive management framework providers such as Computer
Associates Unicenter and Hewlett-Packard OpenView.
In the groupware market, Novell, with its GroupWise product and InnerWeb Workstation Connectivity initiative intends to compete directly with IBM's (Lotus) Notes, Hewlett-Packard's
OpenMail, Microsoft's Exchange and Netscape's CollabraShare for groupware marketshare. And, if the enterprise is standardizing on Microsoft, or if major commitments have already been
made to early-market-entry Lotus, Novell will have a great deal of difﬁculty convincing IT decision makers that GroupWise bears consideration.
Within the directory services space, there is little evidence that Novell Directory Services will meet with widespread industry acceptance outside of Novell's installed base. With the broad
adoption of Microsoft's NT environment, and the rapid growth in Internet usage, other initiatives will most likely overwhelm Novell's NDS-centric strategies. Microsoft's OLE-enabled
directory service will succeed simply because OLE-enabled applications will drag it into the enterprise. And the LDAP (Light Weight Directory Access Protocol) initiative is already
becoming the de-facto directory services standard for the Internet.

How Can Novell Get Out of This Predicament?
Given Novell's track record of changing its business and technology plans yearly, the last thing its users need is another major change in direction. Aberdeen believes that Novell needs to
tune its NDS message -- NDS should be the best Directory Service on NT bar none, and the best Directory Service for interoperability with other targets such as the Internet and Unix. And,
Novell needs to open new markets that it can dominate in order to remain ﬁnancially viable over time. Speciﬁcally, Novell needs to:
1. Be better than Microsoft Directory Services -- Embrace Windows NT Server by OLE-enabling NDS in 1996, instead of trying to keep Microsoft out of its users' information
infrastructure and drive an alternative Novell network directory services standard;
2. Refocus its planning process and engineering efforts on application projects that can be delivered to its installed base in rapid fashion rather than the longer-term and more grandiose
efforts of Global Network Services, NDS market dominance, and NEST;
3. Leverage the Internet -- Use NDS technology to shape the future of Internet-based and Intranet-based LDAP directory applications, again going-with-the-ﬂow by providing compelling
Inter- and Intranet applications such as electronic commerce for service and goods providers; and
4. Continue its new business ventures in the telephony and multimedia ﬁelds, using Novell's size and core expertise to forge new markets.
OLE-enabled NDS: Novell's users are demanding some form of NDS-NT interoperability. OLE-enabling NDS will provide an opportunity to bring Microsoft-based applications to NetWare
users, making users NetWare networks more valuable, attractive to new prospects, and endearing NetWare to ISVs. In the intermediate and longer term, Aberdeen believes that unless Novell
decides to reposition NDS as an OLE-enabled (Object Linking and Embedding) directory service that inroads being made now by Microsoft's NT Server as an application server, will force
users to reconsider their NetWare-based networks.
Short-term -versus long-term: NEST and the NDS-based Smart Global Network initiatives that have attempted to surround Microsoft or go where Microsoft is not, are all longer-term efforts.
And, these longer-term initiatives are fraught with lower probability of success. And, none of these efforts assist with cash-ﬂow needed to fund critical projects. Because they are longer-term
efforts, that might pay off in three-to-ﬁve years, they should be funded as such. But shorter term, cash-generating projects that deliver value to current users ought to be funded before longer-
term and more speculative bets.
Internet and Intranet directory applications: Novell needs to turn its focus to Internet applications rather than Internet directory services. Novell's NDS/Internet strategy is too heavily focused
on trying to export a too-heavy-weight NDS to the light-weight Domain Name Services and TCP-centric Internet.
Telephony/Multimedia: The big hope for Novell is to ﬁnd new markets that match its core expertise, and in which Novell can leverage its size, relationships, and integration capabilities to
gain competitive edge and market leadership position. Novell's telephony and multimedia effort matches these criteria -- the market is immature but growing rapidly and waiting for a heavy
to establish its presence.

Summary Observations
Novell caught the proverbial software brass ring with NetWare. It had the right product for the right market with the right distribution channels at the right time. But that was then and this is
now.
Novell clearly saw the that the evolution of network operating systems must include application services. Yet it simply did not engineer an appropriate solution -- the technology eluded
Novell, but not ﬁerce competitors like Microsoft and IBM.
Novell's predicament is that as NetWare revenues are declining it needs to ﬁnd a new billion-dollar-a-year software brass ring -- and these are very, very scarce. More to the point, to capture
another brass ring many of Novell's stated strategic directions assume that the company will still be a major force in IS decision makers strategic plans in 1996 and 1997 -- an assumption that
these same decision makers tell Aberdeen is not realistic.
On the other hand, Novell has a strong balance sheet, a large installed base, excellent distribution channels and a well-recognized brand. The predicament it now faces is leveraging these
strengths to become a growing and proﬁtable company outside the network operating systems space that its competitors are simply taking from it. Signiﬁcant strategic and business changes
are called for. Aberdeen believes that if Novell cannot successfully execute on the four required operational actions described in the previous section, Novell's predicament may soon turn into
a deadly dilemma.
