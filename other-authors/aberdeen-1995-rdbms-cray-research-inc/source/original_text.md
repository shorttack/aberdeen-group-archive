**Market Position**

Cray Research is now, finally, serious about the commercial database marketplace. In its new take-care-of-the-customer mode, Cray has delivered product, services and the platform (CS6400) to address customer requirements at the high end of the database market. Cray has installed a large number of business solutions such as Order Processing, Financial Controls, Telemarketing, and Business Analytics -- has them in production mode in user enterprises -- and now is announcing a division solely dedicated to meeting the requirements of large database users.

Large enterprise users who have implemented widely successful RDBMS applications are searching desperately for more headroom with their hardware platform. The requirement for sub-second response time, and super-fast processing with access to vast amounts of on-line data has attracted a number of serious competitors such as IBM with it's SP2, AT&T GIS with its 3600, nCUBE with nCUBE2/3 and others to attempt to satisfy this high end need. But many industry watchers have speculated that Cray Research, who is the leader in very high-end technical/scientific computing, may have the unique high-end technology which will enable the company to successfully segue into large commercial database environments.

Partnering with the same RDBMS suppliers that have committed to compliance with the Sun Microsystem's SPARC architecture and Solaris operating system -- Cray is providing a high end growth path for users of Oracle, Informix, Information Resources, Sybase and many more. If an application runs on a Sun Sparc station, it runs on Cray.

What's been missing from Cray has been the organization and people to deliver the product and services to commercial users. As IS decision makers well know -- the requirements and usages of the technical community are different from the requirements of commercial users. To meet the needs of large enterprises for the long term, Cray Research has established a Cray Research Business Systems Division to focus on this fast growing, high performance data storage and management marketplace.

Based upon recent significant wins, Cray's State-of-the-Science technology and customer service attitude is beginning to pay large dividends. Cray will be a major force in the high end of the commercial database marketplace. Aberdeen recommends that IS decision makers who have been considering IBM's SP2 to meet their high end needs should compare it to Cray's focussed, open and advanced product offering.

**Cray is Seizing the Opportunity**

Cray Research has identified an unmet need in the commercial enterprise -- unlimited performance at the high end to run business critical applications based on Unix and RDBMS technologies. Market growth at the high end continues to be explosive while user demand seems insatiable. Numerous, embryonic technologies are being invested in to allow computing platforms to run very large databases (VLDB).

To capture the opportunity in the growth of database size and the associated deployment of data mining, data warehousing, multimedia and real-time business analysis applications in the commercial marketplace, Cray Research has formed Cray Research Business Systems -- a business division specifically focused on providing Cray system and service solutions to address the requirements of large, commercial database users.

Cray Research, with its current technology embodied in the CS6400, believes it can easily handle relational databases of up to 5 Terrabytes. But due the scalability of the CS6400, Cray is targeting the more common 20-500 gigabyte database with the promise of delivering subsecond response time to user queries.

Cray Research Business Systems is putting forth the value proposition that IS decision makers will want to acquire systems from a trusted and respected supplier -- which Cray Research is respected as being. Cray believes that by offering a high-end "open" system, which leverages "open" software and hardware, and utilizes standard "merchant" databases -- that IS decision makers will choose Cray for large user count, high performance database applications. Those IS decision makers are expected to demand the fastest platforms possible to leverage the very expensive time of decision makers in their enterprise who are depending upon results to make business critical decisions for their enterprise.

**The New Cray**

Many IS decision makers still have a vision of Cray as a maker of supercomputers for technical applications. These supercomputers were bought by educational institutions and agencies through funding provided by the federal government. But with the end of the Cold War, Cray Research has had to adopt to changing times.

While defense related spending declined, Cray restructured. Revenues remained flat -- quite an accomplishment with the loss of its major customer; profitability declined, and the organization was required to face major restructuring challenges.

During this transition period Cray was able to grow its commercial customer base to the point that it now accounts for 50% of all Cray sales revenue. Even more surprising to most IS executives is that 10% of Cray's overall business now comes from revenues related directly to databases and database applications - especially in the areas of on-line transaction processing (OLTP) and decision support.

The new Cray will be focussed on growing commercial database revenues -- and to maintain this focus Cray has established the new Business Systems Division.

And finally, a very important footnote: Cray Research is not associated with the recently bankrupt Cray Computer Corporation, started by computer legend Seymour Cray. Unfortunately, many senior executives have been confused by the similarity in names.

**Organizational Structure**

Cray Research, Inc. recently named J. Phillip Samper -- who revitalized Sun Microsystems Computer Corporation in a mere 12 months -- as CEO. This infusion of new blood into the Cray corporate structure will enable Cray to objectively make the key cost-cutting decisions necessary to ensure ongoing viability -- while pressing into new markets.

The formation of the new Cray Research Business Systems Division represents the merging of two Cray subsidiaries: Cray Solutions (the management consulting organization) and Cray Research Superservers. The resulting Business Systems Division has now been absorbed by Cray Research, Inc. -- the parent company.

The new Business Systems Division (BSD) is headed by Bobbi Hazard (Vice President and General Manager) -- who reports directly to Robert H. Ewald, President of Cray Research. Business units within BSD include Marketing and Strategic Planning, Development, Manufacturing, Service, Cray Solutions, Finance and Human Resources.

Other Cray Research, Inc. divisions include its Supercomputer Division, Government Systems Division, the Sales Division and the Service Division.

**Core Products**

The heart of the Cray line is the CS6400 which is UNIX-based server architecture which can run from 4 to 64 Sun Sparc-based CPU's in Symmetrical Multiprocessing (SMP) systems. It can support up to 64 I/O channels and up to 5 Terabytes of on-line storage (plus expandability far beyond through the use of disk array and archival storage technologies). Its internal system bandwidth is 1.76 Gigabytes per second which allows for high data throughput and fast response for large populations of users. Physical memory can be configured up to 16 Gigabytes.

The CS6400 offers an enhanced version of Sun Microsystem's "Solaris" operating system -- a UNIX 5.4 derivative which complies to many generally accepted industry standards. By basing the Cray system platform and operating system offerings on SPARC and Solaris, Cray users now have the benefit of accessing a vast library of ISV software solutions.

Cray has improved Solaris to make it more viable in large, enterprise environments. Solaris itself complies to many generally accepted industry standards, while also offering extensions especially in the areas of SMP and real-time operations -- which can benefit the performance of OLTP and other time critical applications. Cray extensions focus on the needs of enterprise customers who require high performance and maximum up-time for mission critical applications. Particular enhancements include:

- Processor Partitioning -- Enables system managers to dedicate processor groups to specific tasks.
- Memory Management -- Enables the system to run applications and data in main memory and thusly provide peak performance.
- Reliability/Availability -- For example: a hot swap capability allows system administrators to deallocate power and replace or reassign components.

To capture marketshare at the high-end of the UNIX market a system supplier must provide a platform which supports large user populations while offering a high degree of performance; provide on-line data access/storage facilities to access gigabytes of stored data; and offer "enterprise" extensions to increase system reliability, availability and serviceability. That platform should support a wide range of applications.

Cray contends that its platforms uniquely address high-end system requirements by supporting large user populations while providing the "highest sustained performance" figures in the industry. Further, Cray addresses required enterprise extensions through its RAS (Reliability, Availability, and Serviceability) suite of product extensions. Finally, due to Cray's strict adherence to binary compatibility between the Cray line and the Sun Sparc line, Cray users have access to over 10,000 applications which are currently supported on the Sun Microsystems architecture.

Cray also provides the T90 high-end supercomputer as well as the J916 low-end supercomputer to serve its traditional supercomputer markets.

In addition, for those enterprises who require Massively Parallel Processors (MPP) to drive scientific, engineering and technical applications, Cray Research Business systems will support them with their T3D -- a massively parallel server which utilizes DEC's "Alpha" chip set combined with Cray's UNIX.

**Aberdeen Conclusions**

Technologically, Cray's system design is stellar. Lots of main memory, slick internal bus design, support for loads of on-line data which can be accessed by large communities of users in a high-performance, high-response timeframe.

From an application perspective, SMP and MPP architectures are maturing in a commercial sense, while applications designed to take advantage of SMP and MPP hardware architectures are still in their adolescence. Enterprises are in the process of migrating their high-end, mission-critical applications to open systems and UNIX environments -- witness the recent phenomenal success of SAP's R3 with corporate financial, manufacturing, human resource and distribution applications.

The impediment has been in confidence in very high-end platforms and the professional services required to make the transformation. Cray has the tools, systems platforms, and the services to address the application requirements of enterprise customers who are now willing to migrate from existing, proprietary systems to open systems.

Cray Research Business Systems is open for business. Its products and services offerings are not vaporware or future promises -- they are here now. IS decision makers have a requirement for very high-end Unix and RDBMS-based applications owe it to their enterprise users to thoroughly investigate Cray Business System division offerings as part of their business decision process.
