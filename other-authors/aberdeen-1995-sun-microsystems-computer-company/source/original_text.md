Sun Emerges as a TopTier Commercial Systems Supplier
Market Position
Founded in 1982, Sun quickly became a major force in the explosive growth of the technical workstation market by dominating the industry as unit shipment leader. For more than ten years,
Sun has also been instrumental in shaping the industry's vision of Open Systems and in addition to adhering to all relevant standards the company has contributed signiﬁcant baseline
technology to more than a dozen worldwide standards organizations. Sun continues to offer robust Unix operating system technology with its newest release of Solaris, based on Unix V.4
with extensions that support symmetric multiprocessing and high levels of security.
Over the last thirteen years, Sun has also played a major role in deﬁning the industry's notion of a networked computing architecture. This concept of the "network is the computer" was often
derided by traditional thinkers in the mid1980s in both the information systems organization as well as the supplier community. However, enterprise network computing is now considered a
defacto standard for the design of distributed information systems topologies.
In 1991, the company unveiled its ﬁrst line of servers designed especially for the commercial market. These platforms have been sold as technical compute engines to Sun's large installed
base of workstation customers and have also been used to pursue new business application opportunities. Sun's commercial system solutions have moved the company to the short list of
preferred Unix suppliers in major corporations interviewed by Aberdeen. Based upon expanded marketing, distribution and independent software vendor (ISV) programs targeting new
commercial information systems applications and mainframe downsizing initiatives, Sun has dramatically increased its presence as a supplier of computer systems for business. In fact, Sun
has grown to become the number two supplier of RISC microprocessorbased Unix servers for commercial applications.
In this Proﬁle, Aberdeen highlights the technologies, products, and services developed in several Sun Microsystems divisions. While Sun Microsystems Computer Company (SMCC) is a
major force in the highperformance, technical workstation market, our goal here is to focus on Sun's commercial business initiatives. For this reason, we will focus our attention on issues
related to the company's SPARCserver 1000 and SPARCcenter 2000 enterprise servers.
Organizational Structure and Financials
Sun's business model is based upon aggressive pricing, attention to customer satisfaction, and especially effective use of indirect channel partners. While other suppliers pay lip service to
these three fundamental open systems market requirements, Sun has made them a core part of corporate philosophy and operational programs. As a result, over the last year Sun has delivered
accelerating growth in revenues and proﬁts. In its most recent ﬁscal year ending in June 1995, the company reported $5.9 billion in revenues up more than 25% over 1994. Sun Microsystems
has derived a signiﬁcant portion of this growth by repositioning itself as the leader in networked Unix systems and in particular by providing a variety of innovative products and services for
the Internet.Despite its aggressive pricing strategies and lowcost unit volume leadership, Sun has maintained healthy margins defying many pundits. In FY 1995 its gross margins rose to
more than 40%, and proﬁts hit an alltime high of $356 million.In order to optimize research and development budgets and to leverage Sun technologies in multiple markets the company
created seven major operating companies in 1991. Sun Microsystems Computer Company, known as SMCC, is responsible for systems and Sun's direct channel. SunSoft initiatives include
the Unixbased Solaris operating system as well as Solstice, which is the company's entrant in the network and systems management platform arena. Other divisions include: SPARC
Technology Business for semiconductor design; SunExpress for aftersales fulﬁllment; and SunService.
Commercial Computing Strategies
Sun has been quietly providing a full range of services in ﬁve commercial markets manufacturing, education, ﬁnancial services, telecommunications, and government. We say quietly,
because many suppliers with less than Sun's $2 billion in commercial revenues receive a lot more press and customer "mind share" than Sun for their commitments to these commercial
segments.Within these ﬁve industry markets, Sun is further focusing on three application areas through its recently introduced Network Business Advantage program. This program consists
of complete solutions for customer interaction (Customer Management Solutions), data warehousing (Decision Warehouse) and business/ﬁnancial applications (BusinessWare). To this end,
Sun has built up sales and support teams which have experience in both the industry and the technology needed to service that industry segment, partnered with leading independent software
vendors (ISVs) and system integrators (SI), and has shifted R&D resources more toward commercial customers' needs.One of the hottest markets in the industry today is building software to
manage customer relationships including such things as sales force automation, customer help desk, plus other service and support applications. Aberdeen's recently published report,
Managing Customers with NextGeneration Software Applications, shows that over threequarters of the Fortune 500 companies consider the implementation and growth of customer
management applications a major planning agenda item. Sun has a substantial position in this market and is working with scores of partners to deliver a comprehensive package of customer
management solutions. (See Figure 1.)Sun brings several strengths to these partnerships: highperformance servers, quality support and service (see below) and its networking skills. Since
customer management applications typically require the integration of several databases, including those on legacy systems as well as the ability to combine data, image and voice Sun's
acknowledged strength in network computing is the technical "glue" that holds the disparate pieces together. Additionally, Sun's XTL software provides excellent computer/telephony
integration to make the links to a voice system transparent to the customer. The Customer Management Software and Solutions Market is expected to grow considerably over the next several
years. Sun has already generated an impressive $200 million in revenues in 1995 consistently winning in major accounts where IBM and HP were also on the short list.The second
application area is Data Warehousing. Recent Aberdeen research indicates that more than half of all information systems organizations consider data mining and information integration
initiatives a critical priority for the next 18 months. Sun has been involved in this market for some time and announced its formal solutions program, Decision Warehouse, on July 25, 1995.

In data warehousing, Sun again pairs its platform, networking and support strengths with key RDBMS suppliers (Oracle, Informix, Sybase, IBM DB/2 and CA/Ingres) and with the leading
OnLine Analytical Processing (OLAP) suppliers such as SAS, HOPS and Arbor Software. Current database warehouse installations range to over 850GB and these are growing. To support
customer planning, Sun has opened three competency centers with its partners with at least ﬁve others to follow over the next year.
Sun's third application focus is the development of ﬁnancial and business applications through its BusinessWare program. Sun has developed partnerships with ISVs including SAP, Dun &
Bradstreet and PeopleSoft, to develop solution suites for speciﬁc industries such as automotive and pharmaceutical. These solutions are for customers wishing to move their centralized
applications to a networked model.
To summarize, Sun has effectively built partnerships with select ISVs that specialize in offering customers total solutions in key market segments. The partnerships involve joint product
development, system and performance tuning, joint sales and marketing, and investments in competency centers so that customers can see the products in action before making an investment
decision.
Key Technologies and Products
Currently, SMCC offers a fullyscalable line of servers in ﬁve general categories:

Workgroup servers--SPARCserver 4, SPARCserver 5 and SPARCserver 20 used as communication servers, local application and database servers, development platforms, and network
backup.
Departmental servers--SPARCserver 1000E often used for frontend application and database servers, backend ﬁle servers, parallel backup and restore, or for High Availability

Enterprise servers--SPARCcenter 2000E used for backend database servers and decision support system servers.
Specialty servers--Netra s and Netra i for Systems Management and Internet access, respectively.

Meeting Customers' Requirements
Users tell Aberdeen they use multiple criteria for evaluating servers, and while the criteria and their priority vary depending on the buying company's size, industry segment, and geography,
four are typically at the top of everyone's list:
Performance: Is the system powerful enough to respond to complex queries against large databases?
Scalability: Users begin with prototypes and pilots, but they obviously want to grow the number of users accessing the server's application. Will a server or its applications run out of gas
as demands grow?
Availability: With more "mission critical" applications now being run on Unix servers 7 x 24, how much downtime can be tolerated?
Cost of Ownership: How much will the system cost to develop, implement and support over threetoﬁve years?
SMCC has responded to each of these important user requirements. In the following paragraphs we will describe several of Sun's important technical development programs.
In the area of performance, Sun realizes that most commercial customers buy a Unixbased server to run a relational database management system (RDBMS) and mission critical applications
based on the RDBMS. Sun has devoted considerable resources to database performance optimization. The results of these investments are demonstrated by recent performance testing using
industry standard measures like Transaction Processing Council (TPC) and ISV benchmarks like SAP's.
The performance results are used in two ways: ﬁne tuning the systems to improve system performance, as well as to satisfy customer requests for realworld performance information.
In the area of scalability, Sun continues to invest major resources in its operating system software, currently shipping as Solaris 2.4. For instance, a longterm strength of Sun's systems has
been the ability to scale in a near linear fashion primarily through its multithreading technology. Sun shows continued improvements, with Solaris 2.4 delivering in excess of twotimes more
scalable power than Solaris 2.2.
The results of industry standard performance tests also demonstrate Sun's excellent results in adding more processors without adding the signiﬁcant overhead that degrades overall
performance. Sun, in fact, achieves "nearlinear" scalability with its SPARCserver and SPARCcenter multiprocessing models. Figure 2 shows the results of one such performance test
comparing a single CPU SPARCcenter 2000 running Oracle 7.1.3 Parallel Query versus the same package with 20 CPUs.
Similar performance tests using Oracle Financials, SAP's internal capacity testing, TPCs, and Decision Support benchmarks show equally impressive results. Aberdeen encourages its clients
to review these and other performance benchmarks in order to evaluate a supplier's scalability and efﬁciency with load balancing.
High availability is another criterion used to differentiate between systems, and Aberdeen has been consistently critical of the major suppliers for their relatively mediocre offerings. With its
recent announcement, Sun has fully addressed this issue with three high availability options to be released in mid1995.
The ﬁrst is what Aberdeen classiﬁes as the industry standard using Oracle Parallel Server in a clustered environment. Sun will market this conﬁguration under the label SPARCcluster PDB
Server.
The second is the SPARCcluster HA NFS option. This uses a standby server with dual connections to the primary server, the network and the storage array.
The third is a high availability variation that supports RDBMSs from all three major ISVs, Oracle, Informix and Sybase. With each of these conﬁgurations, customers are not forced to have
duplicate systems in order to have failover protection. Also with this variation, failover does not require a reboot and the host's name and IP address automatically migrates to the failover
system.
In some high availability environments, customers must create complicated scripts to trigger the failover process. With Sun's approach, the failover is automatic. Sun provides customers with
application speciﬁc modules rather than just a toolkit, thus reducing startup time, complexity and costs.Sun has a well deserved reputation for an aggressive pricing policy, and its
SPARCserver/ SPARCcenter product family is no exception. While Aberdeen ﬁnds that most customers rank price as an important buying priority, total cost of ownership is more
compelling. With its modular architecture and track record of no penalty upgrades, Sun customers' investments are protected. High performance, scalability and availability combined with
aggressive pricing and commercial application availability gives Sun's customers' tremendous value.

Customer Acceptance
The advent of Unixbased systems as frontends to legacy systems, combined with PCs as clients all distributed and connected by LANs and WANs has complicated the MIS environment at
the same time that costcutting pressures have reduced stafﬁng levels. Against this backdrop, customers are ﬁnding that integrated service and support are a key differentiator when selecting a
supplier.
Aberdeen called several Sun customers with complex multisupplier installations in production mode to see how the posthoneymoon level of support and service rated. Each talked in glowing
terms about the service and support they received from Sun on the local, regional, national and international level. One issue we tested was Sun's ability to respond to a request for help from
customers having problems with thirdparty products, such as RDBMS and application suites. None of the customers we interviewed had experienced the ﬁngerpointing usually
accompanying an incident where it was difﬁcult to tell what part of the system had caused the problem. Each customer also described occasions where Sun personnel had gone out of their
way working nights, weekends or holidays to install new systems so that business was not disrupted.
Our interviews clearly showed that Sun's high rankings in annual "quality of service" surveys is well deserved. Given Sun's recent investments in the expansion of these programs, we expect
this rating to continue to improve.
Aberdeen Conclusions
Aberdeen's ﬁeld research shows that more commercial accounts are putting Sun on their shortlist when evaluating suppliers for large, complex systems. Based on our analysis of Sun and our
interviews with its customers, Aberdeen recommends that other customers in transition to open systems for missioncritical business solutions, should also adopt this strategy.
Sun Microsystems continues to post impressive ﬁnancial results with both accelerating revenue and proﬁts. Much of the recent gain is a direct result of success with its multiprocessing
servers and continued emphasis on commercial applications. With its independent business unit organizational strategy, Sun has been able to optimize opportunities in several market
segments from technical workstations, to operating systems, to networking, and many others each of which has its own product champions and unique sales push. Although these units can
operate independently, Sun has also been able to link the strategies of its divisions into a coherent, integrated offering. For example, Sun has integrated Solaris into its multiprocessing server
architecture and has successfully managed the transition of its ISV application base especially the major RDBMS suppliers to Sun's new architecture. The successful implementation of this
commercial strategy has produced a dramatic, faststart unit shipment story for SMCC's servers over the last four years.
In particular, Aberdeen continues to be impressed with the effectiveness and reach of all Sun's indirect channel partner programs. Unlike many of its competitors, Sun has made the necessary
changes to its direct sales incentives and training programs to foster an attitude of cooperation with ISVs, resellers, and systems integrators. No doubt, Sun's aggressive server pricing,
database performance, nearlinear scalability, and applications availability are important factors contributing to growth of sales and unit shipments. However, Aberdeen believes that Sun's
ﬂuid organizational strategies, commercial application initiatives and strong partner programs also provide a signiﬁcant edge for the company as it presents the SPARCserver/SPARCcenter
message in new account opportunities. We believe that Sun's incremental investments in signiﬁcant new specialized opportunities like customer relationship management, data warehousing,
and business applications will help to maintain healthy gross margins and provide further credibility for relying on Sun Microsystems as a leading supplier in commercial accounts.
