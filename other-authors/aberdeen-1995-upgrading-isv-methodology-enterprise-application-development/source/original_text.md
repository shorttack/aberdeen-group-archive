Upgrading To ISV Methodology For Enterprise Application Development
Why should in house custom application development be carried out any differently than the way independent software vendors (ISVs) do it?
Enterprises depend upon their custom applications to provide core competencies in critical areas such as improving customer support and creating more efﬁcient business processes.
Likewise, ISVs rely upon their packaged applications to create value for their customers and therefore revenues and proﬁts -- their very existence is dependent upon the success of their
development efforts. Yet the acceptable, traditional way to implement mission critical business applications is very different from the way ISVs bring product to market. Why?
In this Viewpoint Aberdeen examines an audacious claim made by a well-regarded tools supplier, ADM, that it has codiﬁed the best practices of driven-to-succeed ISV developers into its
Product Management Facility. If so, shouldn't in-house enterprise development groups take advantage of what the ISVs have already learned?

Executive Summary
The traditional application development methodologies of the 1980s placed a strong emphasis on planning. The general rule was that the cost of ﬁxing a mistake after releasing an application
was between 10 and 100 times the cost of having identiﬁed potential problems during the planning stage. But this was also before technology cycles became compressed from seven years to
less than a year, before object technology allowed the relatively simple changing of multiple suites of application programs, and before end users demanded overnight-or-sooner
modiﬁcations to applications when their functional requirements changed.
Successful ISVs understand that bringing products to market quickly, so that leading edge IS purchasing decision makers can evaluate their capabilities, is key to their ﬁnancial success. For
example, Microsoft shipped Windows 95 to 400,000 beta users before it was completely debugged. These early users tried it; most liked it; many understood its limitations; some suggested
improvements and (even while Microsoft was still completing the product for general release) committed their enterprises to it for the long term. Traditional application developers were
appalled that Microsoft had the audacity to ship a package that was admittedly not ﬁnished to the best of its abilities and suggest that its user base be part of the ﬁnal buff-and-polish process.
As part of its efforts with many prominent application development organizations, ADM has seen the continuing rift between the winners and losers in application development. Winners
focus on the time to bring a product to market and the means to involve potential customers and partners in the development process. Losers seem to have the ability neither to release
products in a timely manner nor to involve their potential customers in the design and reﬁnement stages of development.
To help traditional application development organizations bridge the gap between the old world and the new, ADM has codiﬁed what it calls the SCRUM methodology and packaged it as its
Product Management Facility product. The basic ﬁve principles of SCRUM are that:
Upfront planning for a new application should be done quickly and with most of the emphasis on user needed functionality and system architecture;
The development effort should be done by manageable teams of 7 or less persons all working on assignments of the same length of time from -- 1 to 6 weeks -- called a Sprint;
All teams must show their progress to each other, senior management, and clients/prospective customers at the end of a Sprint, at which time they are given revised objectives and may
have resources adjusted for the next Sprint time period;
The application is deemed ﬁnished when the development team has achieved the functional and quality objectives set out at the planning stage or has run out of time or has run out of
budget dollars; and
Subsequent versions of an application should be developed and implemented -- the original application should not be upgraded merely through the use of function-speciﬁc modiﬁcations
that over time send maintenance costs through the roof.
For most in-house enterprise application development groups, adopting the SCRUM methodology would be a radical departure from their traditional methods. Yet Aberdeen believes that
without proactively learning from the successes of world-class ISVs, IS will only ﬁnd itself more frustrated with senior management demands for "more everything" but without a pragmatic
approach for increasing user satisfaction.
The Slow ISVs Become The Casualties
Examining ISVs' most difﬁcult problems, Aberdeen most often ﬁnds that they relate to the inability to change their products fast enough. For example, many suppliers of applications for
mainframes could not make the transition to Unix servers, add sophisticated graphical user interfaces, or partition their applications for client server architectures quickly enough. Or think
about the number of suppliers of MS DOS applications that could not make the transition to Windows 3 quickly enough to meet their customer-base's requirements -- and the ISVs that are
today struggling to upgrade their applications to take full advantage of Windows 95 and Windows NT.
The economic reality is that an ISV that cannot provide its customers with new technology capabilities that provide real user beneﬁts will lose market share -- and often its independent
existence -- to those that can. Being ﬁrst or at minimum a very, very fast second, is critical to success in the packaged software industry.
The New Development Realities
Enterprises today internally develop only those applications that cannot be acquired from ISVs -- those applications that are so unique that they are vital to the well-being of the organization.
Because of the increasing importance of these applications, in-house application developers are under different and greater pressures than ever before. Add to this fast changing user

requirements, increasing time constraints, lower allocated budgets, and the challenges and opportunities created by newly introduced technologies, and one quite clearly sees that
development methods in the user-centric future must be much different than they were in the mainframe-dominated era of the past.
In a typical enterprise application development effort, the key measures of success are:
1. Cost -- keep it as low as possible;
2. Compatibility -- the new application must use the enterprise's in place information infrastructure;
3. Functionality -- the application must be very comprehensive, to meet the needs of all the internal constituents who may wish to take advantage of it; and
4. Quality -- since senior management cannot judge the quality of an internally developed application based upon comparative products, it tends to do so based upon stability and the
number of problems that require workarounds.
In startling contrast, successful ISVs view application development primarily as a trade-off among four factors:
1. Time to launch -- the date the product should or must be shipped;
2. Functionality -- after the base level of functionality is included, how much additional functionality that different customer segments will want can be included;
3. Personnel availability -- who within or from outside the organization can be recruited, assigned, or contracted with to work on the development effort. While best efforts are made to
match skills and capabilities to project requirements, the assignments are often modiﬁed to match the available staff; and
4. Quality -- known bugs, performance bottlenecks, component technology incompatibilities, and inconsistencies between the documentation and product functionality will be
communicated to customers as required. Subsequent, planned dot releases will improve quality during the life of the version release.
It is extremely important to note that cost is not a primary trade-off. The ISV's reasoning is that the cost to develop the next application rarely, if ever, impacts its ﬁnancial success. In
addition, backward systems compatibility is much less of an issue for ISVs because the majority of IS decision makers who are acquiring a new software application will also be acquiring
the latest platform technology on which to run it.
The Sprint -- the Key to ADM's SCRUM Methodology
Refreshingly, SCRUM is not an acronym for anything. Rather SCRUM is a rugby action in which the forwards, through skill and strength, quickly move the ball toward their own team.
ADM (Burlington, Massachusetts), a well recognized expert in the area of development methodologies, chose the name SCRUM for its new, ISV practices based methodology to
communicate the same team effort, intensity and focus for application development.
The SCRUM methodology consists of three distinct processes.
The initial process is Planning and System Architecture. Key in this phase is pinning down the date at which the application should be placed in production or released to the market,
prioritizing functionality requirements (ranging from good enough to best possible), identifying the resources available for the development effort, envisioning the application architecture,
and establishing the target operating environment(s). However, compared to other methodologies, this planning phase is conducted relatively quickly because it assumes that pragmatic
managers and the course of events will require that any or all of these initial parameters will be changed during the Sprint phase.
The next process, consisting of multiple Sprints, is where SCRUM radically differs from traditional enterprise application methodologies. The project manager establishes Sprint teams
consisting of between 1 and 7 members (a fully staffed team should include a developer, quality assurance person, and documentation member). Each team is given its assignment(s) and all
teams are told to sprint to achieve their objectives on the same day between 1 and 6 weeks from the start of the Sprint. Each team can select its own object oriented tools and its own means to
achieve its objectives -- these are not selected from on high and then forced on the developers and other team members. However, this process is not as undisciplined as it may seem -- each
team must deliver executable code to successfully accomplish the Sprint.
At the end of the Sprint period -- three weeks, for example -- all the teams meet to review their progress (including executable code delivered to date) with each other, the project manager,
customers/prospects, and the enterprise's senior executives. At the conclusion of the review session, the project manager and his or her superiors have the opportunity to change anything and
everything. This built in ﬂexibility allows the organization to add to (or, rarely, subtract from) the requirements for the application during the Sprint phase.
When the objectives of the application development effort are completed in terms of functionality and quality, or the time/budget constraints are reached, the Sprint phase is completed.
Approved modiﬁcations to the original planning and systems architecture are lumped into a category called backlog and assigned to the teams (whose resources may also be changed to
reﬂect the new objectives) at the beginning of the next Sprint period.
Finally, SCRUM ends with the Closure phase. Closure consists of ﬁnishing system and regression testing, developing training materials, and completing ﬁnal documentation. Approved
modiﬁcations to the original planning and systems architecture are lumped into a category called backlog and assigned to the teams (whose resources may also be changed to reﬂect the new
objectives) at the beginning of the next Sprint period.
As opposed to traditional methods, which try to lock in requirements during the planning phase, SCRUM (and Sprints in particular) provides the development team with a tremendous
amount of post planning ﬂexibility. For example, many enterprises that had selected OS/2 as their chosen client operating environment in early 1995 decided by late 1995 to move to
Windows NT Client. This change -- replace OS/2 with Windows NT -- would have been assigned to the appropriate teams to implement at the beginning of the next Sprint.
The Beneﬁts
SCRUM allows users to adopt ISV best practices without enormous disruption. SCRUM emphasizes small teams, short development cycles, and allowing the developer to pick the right tool
for each job. IS can continue to use the old methods in the planning and closure phases.
SCRUM can also help the IS organization unlock the potential of object oriented technology. Most major IS organizations still do not plan to use object-oriented programming (OOP) for
their mission-critical applications. As a result, they miss out on OOP's beneﬁts -- reusability, improved software quality, and faster implementation. Checks to see if software can be reused
and software quality reviews come at the end of each Sprint, and object-oriented tools ﬁt right in to SCRUM. Most importantly, SCRUM:
Provides management with a window into the progress of the development effort;
Allows end-user/prospect participation throughout;
Lowers risk, since IS can identify problem areas at the completion of each Sprint;
Allows backlog modiﬁcations to be built in as part of the process, so that unforeseen changes caused by the technical and business environment are not viewed as a hindrance to success;
Enables developers to use the object-oriented tools they know or choose or with which they feel most comfortable -- this will reduce developer resistance to "changing my way".
Why Now?
The development of the SCRUM methodology required not only the codiﬁcation of the best-practices of ISVs but also the intellectual work of such development visionaries as Booch,
Rumbaugh, and Jacobson, to create the Unify Metamodel.

In addition, the use of object-oriented technology is critical within the development process. Without using the O O tools available today -- tools that didn't exist even one year ago -- the
Sprint teams could not make the necessary changes to their completed tasks quickly enough to make the SCRUM methodology any more than a theoretical future vision.
SCRUM Is Part of MATE
ADM's business is providing software tools that automate the process of application development within a workgroup. It's ﬂagship product is MATE (Methods and Tool Expert). MATE is
used by numerous large organizations to implement their chosen methodologies.
With the introduction of the SCRUM methodology, ADM has added a fourth module, Product Management Facility, to MATE. The original three MATE modules -- Process Engineer,
Planner, and Developer -- were designed to aid developers using traditional enterprise application development methodologies with a large emphasis on planning. Product Management
Facility adds the functionality to better manage backlog, problems, risks, changes, and issues -- a requirement for controlling the SCRUM methodology.
Aberdeen Conclusions
New database, hardware, application, and networking technologies are not proprietary -- your competitors can use them as effectively as you. ISVs understand this, and bet on high-speed,
quick-U-turn development processes to succeed in cutthroat markets. IS organizations can take the same tack: learn from ISVs and emphasize speed-to-deliver and ﬂexibility rather than costs
and backward compatibility. Methods like ADM's SCRUM give IS a way to drive ISV best practices deep into the development organization.
At the same time, IS should not have to be on the bleeding edge of yet another new software development metholodogy. Enterprise IS is not being a pioneer by following the SCRUM
methodology -- ISVs are already using it. ADM is simply making it available in the form of the Product Management Facility.
Most importantly, IS should consider where this is leading in the long term. The new mission-critical applications can give the enterprise a competitive advantage -- but can the IS
organization keep delivering after the ﬁrst home run? ISVs that stay around for more than 10 years don't rest on their laurels: they use rapid development and ﬂexible processes to gain more
and more competitive advantage. Methodologies like SCRUM aren't just a passing fad or one-shot ﬁx. If IS wants to succeed in the long term like the best ISVs, it should take a good hard
look at ideas like SCRUM.

### Abstract

Aberdeen examines ADM's SCRUM methodology — a codification of ISV best practices for enterprise application development — and argues that in-house IS organizations should adopt ISV-style rapid iterative development over traditional waterfall planning-heavy methods. The study details SCRUM's three phases (Planning, Sprints, Closure) and ADM's Product Management Facility implementation, concluding that IS teams must adopt sprint-based methods with small teams, short cycles, and flexible toolsets to compete with the speed of the best ISVs.
