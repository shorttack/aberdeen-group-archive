**Preface**

Aberdeen research finds that reliable technology is available today to the manufacturing, logistics and distribution community that can interoperate with existing systems while making ready for the globally-enabled enterprise client-server systems of tomorrow. These products have a quantifiable track record for their ability to be rapidly implemented, produce nearly immediate beneficial results and have had order of magnitude return on investment (ROI) reported.

This paper discusses advanced planning engine technologies and their industry benefits. When properly applied, these technologies have produced significant cost reductions -- significant enough to be able to provide funding for additional reengineering efforts essential to carrying firms into the global economy of the 21st century.

Today's manufacturing management is being tasked with matching consumer demand, reducing inventories and cycle times, improving product quality and customer satisfaction -- all while reducing overall costs. The obstacle is that the majority of manufacturing software systems in use today have continued fundamentally unchanged for the past twenty years. Back in the 1970s, manufacturers were in a headlong-rush to implement Material Requirements Planning (MRP) and Inventory Control Systems (ICS). The promise of these systems was better control and increased predictability of the production environment, hopefully culminating in increased bottom-line profits. What these management systems did well was improve cost accounting by providing the capability to: Count and track the production process; Establish standard setup and operating costs; and Obtain a better picture of the true costs associated with producing product.

Today, the problem facing competitive manufacturers is that these now-antiquated systems have not delivered the expected efficiencies and margin improvements.

MRP systems were never architected to provide demand forecasting or production planning and scheduling based on capacity. The focus of those first-generation systems was targeted at managing the flow of materials, providing little insight into the overall manufacturing process. A critical component and output of MRP is the Master Production Schedule (MPS) -- used to define the week's build-plan for the factory. This plan becomes less accurate as the week progresses due to the inability of plant management and line personnel to feed changes back into the plan as events occur on the production floor -- day-to-day, line-to-line, build-to-build.

Infinite plant capacities were assumed with no provision to optimize the actual production process.

**Executive Summary**

Manufacturers today are being confronted with the most competitive and challenging business environment ever experienced. They must contend with competition on a global scale and the demand it places on improving quality and customer satisfaction. Simultaneously, they must grapple with the realities of shrinking margins, compressed product lifecycles, coupled with the need to better manage inbound and outbound logistics of raw materials and finished goods. To persevere in the new global economy, manufacturing facilities must attain the management and production flexibility needed to proactively respond to real-time changes on the production floor, and more importantly, to variations in customer and market demand.

Clients have reported first round "wins" on the enterprise reengineering front utilizing APET. They report better control of existing costs through vastly improved planning and scheduling activities which has led to substantially reducing bottom-line costs -- thus generating needed funding for continued reengineering projects.

Aberdeen believes that for manufacturing enterprises to better compete in the global market, advanced planning engine technologies should be the first solution acquisition and deployment on the way to a fully reengineered and reautomated operation.

**Key Success Factors**

Aberdeen has identified 10 key success factors and areas for improvement being targeted by leading-edge manufacturers:

1. Synchronized production flow -- sharing planning information for improved visibility by all those involved. Empowering the workers.
2. Reduced raw materials inventories -- increased inventory turns, freeing much needed capital for other strategic purposes.
3. Build to order and not build to stock. Finished goods inventories approach zero.
4. Reduced production line down time due to parts outages.
5. Reduced lot sizes -- improves flexibility to adjust product mix based on change in customer order or market demand fluctuations.
6. Setup Scheduling -- reduced downtime caused by poorly sequenced setup activities.
7. Reduced Work In Process (WIP) inventories -- fewer partial builds, frees up corporate resources.
8. Better scheduling of transportation logistics -- reduced transportation costs, reduced warehousing needs.
9. Yields improved on-time delivery.
10. Cooperative sharing of information -- improves materials movement coordination both inside and outside the organization.

**How APET Systems Work**

The APET can use and incorporate legacy system-based data. Typically, data is extracted or retrieved from the existing automated and computerized manufacturing and financial application systems. The APET processes and models the data into a comprehensive plan, then inputs the newly generated APET data back into the legacy system at a point downstream -- much like a heart bypass procedure.

Data is provided to APET using combinations of:
- Bulk data transfer
- Direct interactive access (real-time)
- Event monitoring (process agents)
- Transactional movement
- Event driven activity (rules-based triggers)

APET systems are deployable in client-server architectures consisting primarily of networked workstations and servers. Minimum client PC: 486-66 with 16+MB RAM and 1GB Disk. Minimum server: Pentium with 32+MB RAM and 2GB Disk. Unix server: 64-128MB RAM and 4GB Disk.
