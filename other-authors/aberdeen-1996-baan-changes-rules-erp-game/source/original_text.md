## Original Document Text

BAAN Changes the Rules of the ERP Game
with Dynamic Enterprise Modeling

April 24, 1996 — Today, Baan Company announced the latest release of its Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) application suite renamed BAAN IV. The key significance of this announcement for line-of-business executives and their information systems (IS) peers is that BAAN IV signals the emergence of an important advancement in ERP software design and functionality. Baan is changing the rules by which ERP software will be evaluated, configured, installed, deployed and used. Aberdeen believes BAAN IV represents a significant achievement in integrated ERP solutions. BAAN IV can become a strategic weapon in the arsenal of those enterprises seeking to gain a competitive advantage in a global economy that demands efficiency and rapid adjustment to market changes. Baan is leading the industry to the next level of fully integrated enterprise software systems — Dynamic-ERP for continuous business process improvement in an intranet/internet-enabled open technology environment.

Baan embraces this Dynamic-ERP standard with its BAAN IV Dynamic Enterprise Modeling (DEM) capabilities and is putting an end to the Static-ERP systems of the past. Those Static-ERP systems are characterized by: elongated configuration and implementation times; rigidly imposed predefined business practices; and the plethora of configuration parameters needed to define the application. These static implementations must now give way to the new Dynamic-ERP approach that is able to model a company's existing business practices while simultaneously embracing continuous improvement.

### Baan Reinvents ERP Design and Implementation

Aberdeen research shows that forward-looking enterprises seeking to remake their manufacturing capabilities are seeking solutions that fulfill three key criteria. Those criteria are that the solution must be:

- process-oriented;
- based on best business practices; and
- capable of supporting a changing business model that supports continuous business improvement.

Baan, with the introduction of BAAN IV, is delivering the solution that meets those critical evaluation and acquisition criteria.

BAAN IV is architected and designed to support an enterprise's continuous business process with an integrated family of applications and tools for manufacturing, finance, distribution, transportation, services and project management. BAAN IV is fully configurable to mirror a company's existing business procedures and objectives while providing the power to continually grow and change along with a business.

Baan's effort to reinvent the manner in which ERP solutions are designed and implemented — dynamic versus static enterprise modeling — is based on a solid technology foundation of a key component of BAAN IV, Orgware itself, and its DEM capabilities.

### So What? They Call It Orgware

Orgware is a collection of customer-hardened knowledge tools, methodologies, and business reference models to enable rapid, efficient implementation and ongoing business improvement through the use of reconfigurable business objects. Aberdeen notes that Orgware has been an important decision factor by those companies that have chosen Baan solutions.

Orgware's unique ability to model an enterprise's business processes and organizational structure and then automatically generate a fully configured application system, allows customers to easily bring forward the business information locked within their older software systems to BAAN IV. With a process-approach to migration and using packaged Orgware components, enterprises can create up to 450 customer-specific production-ready BAAN IV business objects, functions and workflows. This capability translates into shorter implementation times and reduced implementation costs.

### Dynamic Enterprise Modeling

Aberdeen has concluded that business process modeling and the development of reference models of best practices within ERP solutions is the key value-added technology that will allow enterprises to achieve their next-century manufacturing and competitive goals. What is more important, this modeling capability needs to be inherently dynamic in nature. To be classified as such, a true Dynamic-ERP system requires an ability:

- to encompass a continually changing business model (new, reconfigured or time-phased);
- to completely re-generate and link the underlying business objects that encompass application logic flow as well as technological advancements; and
- to generate a customized executable system without the need to manually set any system parameters, authorization codes or define user interfaces.

With the addition of Dynamic Enterprise Modeling capabilities to BAAN IV's Orgware, the focus of management, business analysts and even system implementers is now on the elevated plane of functions, best practices and processes — not on intricacies of switchable application minutiae or galactic product configurations.

### A Closer Look At BAAN IV's DEM Architecture and Business Models

The combination of the DEM Business Function Model, Business Process Model and Business Organization Model produces the following enterprise-level models:

- **Reference Model** — typically a market-acquired, predefined best practices business model, designed by industry consultants specifically for a particular industry sector; and
- **Project Model** — a business model uniquely created for a particular customer or company, this model can also be used as an individual corporate template for creating additional project models designating local variations of the base corporate template.

Customers may choose whether to acquire or build the model they will use. Aberdeen notes that the site-specific modeling process can be significantly accelerated by using an industry-specific Reference Model, or company-specific Project Model as the base starting point for creating a new Project Model.

Orgware's modeling capabilities are organized into four functional areas:

- **Business Function Model** — a functional, hierarchical decomposition of business functions and their inter-relationships;
- **Business Process Model** — the formal process description that describes which line-of-business procedures will be supported by BAAN IV;
- **Business Organization Model** — a description of the organizational and personnel structure of an enterprise; and
- **BAAN IV Configurator** — an application and user interface generator that outputs user-specific menus and authorizations and automatically sets the BAAN IV application parameters for system execution.

### Aberdeen Conclusions

The competitive landscape where leading suppliers strive to deliver those next-century ERP systems being demanded by visionary and capable manufacturers is strewn with now-outmoded, statically bound ERP alternatives. Baan has once again bought a new value proposition to the market with the Dynamic-ERP capabilities of BAAN IV. Aberdeen research indicates that those enterprises attempting to implement Static ERP alternatives undergo an imposed serial process of:

- modeling the enterprise's organization and business;
- manually converting those models to the production application configuration; and
- actual system implementation.

The usual result of this static process is that customer investment in the upfront modeling has not returned the expected value in the actually implemented system. This is because that statically defined system is no longer in synch with the new needs of the business. Aberdeen asserts that the Static-ERP systems being sold today must give way to Dynamic-ERP — systems that have the potential for enabling the real-world requirements for continuous improvement and internet enablement.

Aberdeen believes Baan, with BAAN IV, enjoys at least a 12 month advantage over its major competitors — particularly because of the company's leading technology efforts in bringing viable Dynamic-ERP solutions to market and delivering the continuous improvement enabling capabilities of Dynamic Enterprise Modeling. When properly applied, this technology can supply a similar lead for companies savvy enough to recognize its potential.

Baan's commitment to high-quality products, delivery of leveragable technological innovation, combined with marketing agility, persistence and some strategically significant wins, has brought the company from "nowhere" to where Aberdeen recommends that the company now be on every serious ERP evaluation short list.
