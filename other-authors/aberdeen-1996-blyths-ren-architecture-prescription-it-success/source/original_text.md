## Original Document Text

### Preface

Blyth Software's recently announced application-development architecture is designed to advance today's client-server applications onto the Internet and Intranet (hereafter 'Net). Dubbed REN, Blyth's technology architecture and forthcoming products are aimed at mission-critical electronic commerce applications with "Real solutions, Endless possibilities, and No boundaries" — hence, the REN architecture. While the REN name might smack of overly cute marketing, the reality bears close examination. Aberdeen Group believes Blyth today has a more comprehensive vision of the future — dual application-development environments consisting of both traditional and 'Net client-server applications — than the company's competition. Successful execution of the REN technology rollout could easily propel Blyth from being a respected but small application development-tool vendor to becoming an IT household name.

### Executive Summary

Blyth is best known today for the OMNIS 7 client-server application-development environment (CADE), and related database and application-management products. The company's customers typically laud Blyth's products as being industrial-strength and able to handle even very complex business applications. Transparent cross-platform support for Windows, OS/2 and Macintosh application clients (and developers) is a Blyth hallmark.

The REN architecture has four major components: development tools, database tools, repository-based application life-cycle management tools, and reusable software components. Each of the four architecture components has products for traditional client-server and 'Net client-server topologies.

The 'Net track of the REN architecture bears close examination, as Blyth is innovating in uncharted waters. Most Information Technology (IT) development managers view 'Net development today as largely consisting of HTML page layout and Java applets. Blyth does that and goes way beyond into technology areas that will eventually be recognized as of crucial importance for building robust, mission-critical 'net applications. These 'Net products extend critical functionality from the traditional client-server development world into the untamed 'Net development frontier. As a result, Blyth's REN-based products will enable IT developers to readily build and maintain complex 'Net-enabled applications that have the industrial-strength needed for electronic commerce.

The five 'Net-enabled products are:

- **Web Enabler™** — Enables applications to easily access 'Net services such as HTML page creation, electronic mail, news groups, and TCP/IP facilities. Importantly, Web Enabler can connect an application to any 'Net site and extract information from the site. For example, downloading Federal Express parcel tracking information or using the U.S. post office's zip+4 address encoding facilities. Moreover, 'Net information can be extracted from HTML (e.g., 'Net page-scraping) and made available to desktop applications and databases;
- **OMNIS Data Server™** — Allows any OMNIS application to serve 'Net browsers. Typically, the OMNIS application would access data from one or more relational databases and could drive results to either Intranet or Internet applications. There are extensive administration tools;
- **Web Vault™** — A portable database tool that provides secure, scaleable 'Net queries to databases from any desktop Browser. Web Vault will dramatically reduce the involvement and duplicated efforts of Database Administrators in managing 'Net-enabled applications that access enterprise data, while still providing robust security and access control;
- **Web Manager™** — A version control and change management system for 'Net applications that manages all content, including HTML, graphics, CGI scripts, and Java applets. Web site versions can use time-sensitive content publishing that, for example, supports special sale items and prices;
- **Weblets™** — Portable, plug-in objects that promote the rapid assembly of 'Net applications. Blyth envisions this as a content publishing business for components such as workflow, data entry and query, data mining and extraction from 'Net sites.

But there is more sizzle to the REN architecture than the 'Net. Blyth's traditional client-server software will be extended and enhanced:

- **Prometheus** — The well-regarded OMNIS 7.3 CADE will be improved with new ergonomics, application logic-partitioning across clients and servers, deeper object-orientation, advanced use of templates and wizards, SQL-aware objects, and numerous other enhancements.
- **Athena** — The Blyth personal relational database will be improved with features that encompass more complete compliance with ANSI RDBMS standards, improved performance, open interfaces for direct database access, improved object-relational support, and 'Net enablement.
- **Zeus** — Blyth's portable tools for managing the life-cycle of application objects gains improved object reuse and deployment optimization, scaleable granularity, an openness as a repository to all object types, and new audit and administration features.

### Market Position

Blyth Software is an international supplier of cross-platform application development solutions for building, deploying, and maintaining client-server applications — with an emphasis on electronic commerce. The company was one of the first to deliver a cross-platform development tool in the late 1980s and one of the earliest to deliver on Microsoft's Windows operating system.

While Blyth is not a household name, the company has built — largely by word of mouth — a sizable list of discerning customers, including Barclay's Bank, Federal Express, Harvard University, Lockheed Martin, and Northern Telecomm. Blyth's customers and value-added reseller (VAR) partners are typically using Blyth products for mission-critical, bet-the-business applications.

### Key Technologies/Products

#### Web Enabler

Web Enabler is an extension to the OMNIS 7 environment that provides high-level application programming interfaces (APIs) to 'Net services. While competitors are focusing on mere hypertext markup language (HTML) generation, Web Enabler does that and much more:

- 'Net Protocol Externals: applications can access TCP sockets and establish communications sessions across the 'Net — for example, for electronic data interchange of invoices;
- The File Transfer Protocol (FTP) lets applications send and receive data files across the 'Net and examine remote file directories;
- Electronic mail protocols include SMTP, POP3 and MIME for compound documents;
- HTTP support allows applications to act as a web browser or server; OMNIS applications can access any 'Net site, retrieve pages, extract data, and utilize extracted data;
- HTML support: reports can be generated dynamically as Web pages, accessible by any Web browser;
- Client forms created with the OMNIS Form Builder can now generate a complete Java equivalent.

#### OMNIS Data Server

Data Server extends the Web Enabler product for a pure server role. OMNIS Data Server applications can access the OMNIS database or popular relational databases and dynamically generate HTML results to Web browsers via data-driven CGIs.

#### Web Vault

Web Vault is a cross-platform, database middleware product that provides secure, scaleable 'Net queries to databases from any desktop Browser. It runs on NT or Unix server positioned between Web browsers and enterprise databases — including Oracle, SQL Server, and Sybase as well as ODBC access. The DBA establishes vistas — user and group privileges — that define not only the tables but down to the column level.

#### OMNIS Web Manager

Web Manager is a version control and change management system for 'Net applications. It manages all content, including HTML, graphics, CGI scripts, and Java applets, and consolidates all site content into a single, restorable version. Web site versions can use time-sensitive content publishing.

#### Weblets

If applets are application components, then Weblets are application components for 'Net applications. Application developers can buy components in a classic buy vs. make decision from Blyth's online catalog. Blyth foresees customers and third parties submitting Weblets in return for negotiated payments.

### Where Blyth's REN Products are Most Effective

Blyth's 'Net products are early entrants in a soon-to-be-explosive market. Aberdeen believes IS professionals will soon be up to their proverbial necks in a swamp of immature 'Net development products. This will cause frustration and prepare the market to favorably view high-end 'Net development and middleware software such as the REN products.

### Competition

Most client-server development tool suppliers have announced plans to generate HTML to a Web browser-client, so Blyth has no uniqueness there. However, Aberdeen has seen only a couple of products with Web Enabler's functionality for Web services. **Aberdeen believes that Blyth has a twelve month or more advantage on most competitors.** Web Vault will compete largely with RDBMS suppliers' proprietary Web server-to-RDBMS solutions. The Web Manager product stakes out a leadership position in the Internet version control space.

In short, Blyth is early to market with a fairly far-reaching vision of Web development infrastructure, and thus has the early leader's ability to influence and define the market. While competition is sure to come, the company has the opportunity to carve out a long-term, best-of-breed position.

### Aberdeen Conclusions

Aberdeen has been impressed by the satisfaction levels of large enterprises who have successfully developed bet-the-business client-server applications using Blyth's OMNIS application development environment. By focusing on the high-end commercial developer and on the application-development life-cycle, Blyth has established itself as a quality — but small — supplier of development software tools.

Blyth's success in the past in the client-server market has been inhibited by less than stellar distribution and marketing. This time around, the rapid and unexpected growth of the 'Net means the company has an essentially untapped market with a demonstrable need for the kind of products that are built around the REN architecture.

The REN architecture extends Blyth's vision across the burgeoning Internet/Intranet market and opens up access into electronic commerce. Aberdeen believes Blyth has foreseen a variety of major development roadblocks that will likely impede many corporate developers who rush off willy-nilly to build 'Net applications. By proselytizing that "'Net development demands the same application development rigors and classes of tools as client-server", Blyth has an enormous opportunity for success with the REN products.
