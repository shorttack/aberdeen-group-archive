## Original Document Text

### Executive Summary

January 29, 1996 — Cisco Systems' Access Business Unit continues to ramp up its successful penetration of the remote access market with another aggressive rollout that enhances the company position in its traditional segments and attacks new market spaces. This 1996 product launch is backed by a clearly delineated and articulated vision and a continuing stream of both hardware and software offerings. Cisco knows where it is going and has mapped out a well-thought-out and logical plan to get there.

Cisco is an established leader in the corporate enterprise space, with 50% of its $2 billion overall revenues derived from the Access Business Unit (ABU). The company is moving in a multi-pronged, targeted approach both downstream to the small business and individual end-user, and upstream to the carrier and ISP market. The ABU is expanding its strengths in four market areas that Aberdeen considers key to claiming ownership of the remote access space: regional office/branch office (ROBO), dial-up/access server, professional office (also referred to as SOHO), and embedded and server-based systems. Today's announcement of strategic new products indicates that Cisco has a well-defined direction, solidly leveraging its core competencies in order to provide end-to-end access solutions of every scale.

### The Remote Access Market Today

Remote access is characterized by a continuing set of evolving technologies and applications. Aberdeen research reveals that more and more businesses require the ability to access information from a variety of geographical locations. The remote access market today includes three major user groups:

1. The **data-centric, mainframe-oriented enterprise customer** expanded to include branch offices and telecommuters (ROBO market) — growing rapidly; Aberdeen estimates the majority of ABU revenue currently comes from this segment;
2. The **SOHO market** requiring dial-up connectivity to public networks such as the Internet — experiencing rapid growth as access devices improve and pricing falls;
3. The **individual remote user**, stationary or mobile — requiring ease-of-use, interoperability, compatibility, good performance, adequate security and scalability.

### Cisco's Evolving Remote Access Strategy

Aberdeen first evaluated Cisco Systems' initiative for integrated solutions in the remote access market in September 1994. At that time, Aberdeen concluded that Cisco's bold blueprint for penetrating key segments of the remote LAN access market was carefully crafted and backed by a strategic range of remote LAN access solutions. However, Cisco lacked depth in dial-up capabilities and needed to focus more seriously on building channel relationships.

### Establishing a Commanding Presence

Over the past fifteen months, Cisco's Access Business Unit has made a concerted effort to establish leadership through both acquisitions and product rollouts. Aberdeen has followed these business moves as the ABU has marched toward a 50% market share in key remote access segments. Cisco's initiative consisted of a first-phase market engagement, followed by a second-phase rollout of end-to-end solutions. Recently Cisco entered the third phase — enhanced services and enterprise-wide scalability.

Three particular directions which Cisco followed in 1995 serve as a bridge for today's announcements:
- Providing additional IOS software value;
- Increased focus on the WAN;
- Acquiring a major new market segment with its Combinet acquisition.

### Integrating Cisco Software Value-Add

Cisco paid close attention to software value-add. In March 1995, Cisco announced TACACS+ (an enhanced version of its TACACS protocol), which includes support for independent user authentication, authorization and accounting processes, as well as encryption. In November 1995, Cisco announced it would include Powerburst (AirSoft, Inc.'s remote node acceleration software) in CiscoRemote Windows, doubling the speed of remote network access by eliminating redundant file system calls.

### Cisco's WAN Initiative

Cisco launched a series of offerings to enhance WAN capabilities: ATM and ISDN support for the 4000 family; the AS5100 entry-level managed modem pooling device (developed with U.S. Robotics); expanded 2500 series with synchronous and asynchronous serial communications, frame relay and ISDN.

### The Combinet Acquisition

Of great significance was Cisco's acquisition of Combinet in August 1995. Cisco gained instant access to new technology, manpower, and channels, specific dial-up and ISDN expertise, and a build cost considerably lower than internal development. This acquisition spawned the 750 line — simple, secure and manageable SOHO/home platforms. Combinet products became the foundation for the Personal Office segment of the ABU.

### Building Channel Momentum

Unveiled in October 1995, CiscoPro was made-to-order for the Access Business Unit. This channel program, targeted at small to medium businesses, is based upon an exclusively two-tiered distribution model. All reselling of CiscoPro products is through premier channel partners — Tech Data and Ingram Micro — who are responsible for direct sales to their VAR and integrator community.

### The IOS Foundation

Cisco IOS multiprotocol routing software is the core of Cisco's embedded systems. IOS has become the **de facto standard in internetworking**, functioning as a unifying thread. Cisco IOS provides cohesion to otherwise disparate networks and facilitates a scalable migration path. Cisco's strategy is to extend IOS into multiple strategic PC platforms, making IOS the de facto standard for PC routing. IOS can also be embedded in server software such as Microsoft NT, or included in client desktop operating systems such as Windows.

### Cisco's 1996 Salvo

Cisco sets its sights squarely on Ascend and Shiva, highly successful players at each end of the remote access arena.

#### Increased Access Server Intensity

The AS5200 central site access server integrates the functionality of standalone channel service units, communication servers, channel banks, modems, switches and routers into a single chassis. Modular boards provide 2 ISDN Primary Rate Interface (PRI)/T1 lines with integrated CSUs, supporting 48 (PRI/T1) or 60 (PRI/E1) integrated modems. The AS5200 currently supports Microcom and U.S. Robotics modems. A single Ethernet port enables local data routing, while two synchronous serial ports provide ability to backhaul data to other sites.

**CiscoSecure**: Implemented on a per-user basis, provides access control at the server level using TACACS+. Cisco's "triple A" security features include authorization, authentication, accounting and network address translation.

#### Remote Access at the Client Level

**CiscoRemote** client software has both remote node and remote control capabilities. It integrates: Network TeleSystems TCP/IP stack, Farallon's Timbuktu Pro remote control, AirSoft's Powersoft remote node accelerator, Netscape's Navigator 1.2 Internet browser, Qualcomm's Eudora 1.5.2 E-mail package, and Cisco IOS feature extensions.

**CiscoRemote Plus** provides broader platform support including Windows 95 and NT. **CiscoRemote Lite** is a protocol stack and dialer bundled on a per-port basis with the Access Server.

Aberdeen believes these products level the playing field in the remote access arena, enabling Cisco to provide its own end-to-end solutions.

### Conclusions

Aberdeen continues to be pleased by the diversity and depth of Cisco's remote capabilities achieved through its own technologies and targeted acquisitions. Aberdeen recognizes that Cisco fully understands the requirements for integrating internetworking solutions in both legacy systems and client-server networking, as well as providing access to emerging connectivity technologies for public and private Internets.

Two remote access segments have been targeted for on-going attention in 1996: the SOHO market and the ISP access server market. The battle will intensify, but Cisco has a unique weapon on its side — its embedded, value-add IOS software. **It is questionable whether major Cisco competitors will be able to respond fast enough, long-term, to the software sell.**

Firmly believing that the "home of today is the office of tomorrow," Cisco's vision continues to be carrying its networking competencies from the enterprise core through the regional/branch office to the professional office, home and mobile user. The company's goal is to make IOS ubiquitous throughout the industry, becoming a force to be contended with on all fronts of the remote access market.
