Market Position
The flip side of the opportunity associated with open systems is the challenge of having to put it all together -- integrating the pieces of a complex jigsaw puzzle. Systems integration, whether
performed in-house or by outside firms, only partially solves the problems while usually increasing the costs significantly. A better solution would be for the suppliers to ensure that the parts
were designed to work together, while at the same time providing a single point of contact for long-term service and support.
This Profile will examine how one hardware supplier -- Data General -- is working to deliver this type of integrated solution. Data General has formed effective partnerships with several
ISVs (notably, Oracle, PeopleSoft and SAP) to the benefit of its customers.
Data General has undergone significant changes over the past year, not the least of which was a move from Motorola- to Intel-based servers. Data General has surprised the prognosticators
by both growing revenue and becoming profitable during these changes. While Data General's revenues have ranged between $1 billion and $1.2 billion there has been a massive change
behind the numbers. The revenue mix has moved from being primarily derived from the sale and service of proprietary mini-computers, to today where more than 90% of the product
revenues come from open systems -- AViiON UNIX and NT servers, PCs and CLARiiON storage subsystems.
To cover its installed base, and prospect other small- to mid-sized companies, Data General has instituted a program combining a sophisticated telemarketing group with a network of Value
Added Resellers (VARs) and distributors.
Since several of its smaller competitors get good press coverage, many IS executives are surprised to learn how well Data General is doing in the commercial UNIX market. In its February
1996 evaluation of the commercial, multiuser, UNIX marketplace Aberdeen shows Data General with over $620 million in open system sales.
Key Technologies/Products
Data General has long recognized that to deliver the power and performance required by demanding commercial applications, the underlying systems platform has to be able to provide
robust symmetric multiprocessor support. The AViiON system designs -- as its Eclipse predecessors -- were intended to be SMP-enabled from the start. This design intent has been carried
through to the current Intel-based system architecture found in the AV2000, AV3000, AV4800 and AV5800 series of servers. As system and application demands grow, Data General provides
more options to allow IS to move forward cost-effectively with SMP-based systems. SMP technology allows the addition of microprocessors at a lower cost than wholesale system
replacement or upgrade. Data General develops AViiON systems that scale exceptionally from one to thirty-two processors. For even further scalability and reliability, Data General provides
a loosely coupled clustering capability.
Aberdeen research shows, however, that a strong SMP hardware architecture is not sufficient in and of itself to assure IS buyers of scalable performance -- a fact that a number of Data
General's high-end industry competitors have had to struggle with in their effort to deploy SMP-capable systems. The key to scalability is found in the operating system environment, which
also has to be SMP-aware. IS buyers pondering how suppliers "add value" to a standard UNIX implementation could look to DG/UX as a case study. Well in advance of consortium standards
efforts that were seeking to integrate SMP and security support into the UNIX shell, Data General had designed and was offering fully enabled SMP capabilities and modular security with
DG/UX. Several suppliers have since caught up to Data General and its implementation of UNIX V.4, but some major suppliers remain challenged in matching the SMP engineering and field
experience that Data General has.
Supplier Partnerships
Hardware and software suppliers acknowledge the problems IS is having making it all work together, and decided that one way to gain a competitive advantage was to demonstrate their
ability to work with other component providers. From some tentative agreements a few years ago, it now seems that every supplier is partnered with every other supplier and the word
partnership has been used in so many press releases that it has lost its original meaning.
The dictionary definition of partner implies a relationship in which each has equal status and independence, but each also has implicit or formal obligations to the other. Aberdeen assesses the
true value of a supplier partnership by measuring the amount of cross-pollination taking place. Most partnerships end with a listing in the respective price books. Other partnerships lead to
the two companies making joint sales calls. But the partnerships which make the most difference to IS are those where each company specifically assigns staff for joint development, joint
marketing and sales, and, ultimately, cross training of support staff so that the buyer only needs to make one call to get a problem fixed.
Data General's Strategy with ISVs
A large part of Data General's success is due to the development and ongoing focus on alliances and partnerships. Data General has historically derived over half of its revenues from
alternate distribution channels. Aberdeen research shows that suppliers with significant reliance on alternate channel revenues have more mature marketing and sales programs, with a better
operational and management infrastructure to sustain and grow third-party alliance than suppliers influenced predominantly by direct-sales. Data General has attracted industry-leading
partners and maintained dynamic multilevel relationships -- sales, marketing, and engineering -- with those suppliers.
To study Data General's relationship with key ISVs, Aberdeen chose three that dominate their respective markets -- SAP for financial applications; Oracle for relational databases, tools and
applications; and PeopleSoft for Human Resource client-server applications.
After getting Data General's view of its relationship with the ISVs, Aberdeen interviewed representatives from each ISV to ask what Data General brought to the table that differentiated it
from other suppliers. Aberdeen then interviewed customers to gain an understanding of why Data General was chosen over other options, and how well they were satisfied with their decision
after installation.
PeopleSoft
PeopleSoft has quickly become the leading supplier of client-server human resource applications. After Data General approached PeopleSoft with a solid business plan describing how it
intended to do business, things moved quickly. During 1994, Data General formed a strategic alliance with PeopleSoft -- and also purchased PeopleSoft's human resource package for its own
internal use. Data General trained dedicated alliance managers to support PeopleSoft's regional sales, and installed its first customers' systems.
Data General installed a system at PeopleSoft's development center for testing and tuning. Data General can now comfortably predict performance for its hardware with PeopleSoft's
application for companies with 5,000 to 80,000 employees. This led to Data General introducing a money-back performance guarantee, ensuring each customer a specific level of
performance for the PeopleSoft application.
Customers interviewed indicated that they undertook an evaluation of their internal business requirements, and decided to go with PeopleSoft after a lengthy review of its product and all the
competitor offerings. After choosing the application, the customers then went about selecting a hardware supplier. When asked why they chose Data General, the initial response focused on

Data General's known strengths: exceptional price:performance; near linear scalability through Data General's experience with SMP; great high-availability features, especially when tied to
the CLARiiON storage sub-system; and demonstrated ability to support and service the systems on a world-wide basis.
However, after digging a bit more, Aberdeen found a more significant reason for customers selecting a Data General platform for running PeopleSoft's applications. With PeopleSoft's own
staff stretched thin because of its rapid growth, customers found that Data General could provide consulting services and training on the AViiON platform, the PeopleSoft application, and the
back-end database (usually Oracle). One customer, initially skeptical of Data General's ability to perform, has been so satisfied with the product and the extensive support that he is
recommending the package to other divisions within his firm.
Oracle
Data General has been selling Oracle RDBMS, applications and tools for about nine years, and is a member of Oracle's Enterprise Solutions Program. As with the PeopleSoft applications,
Data General is itself a customer of Oracle's, and uses its products throughout the company. Data General provides a central point for a customer wanting to have one supplier responsible for
sales, installation, training and support of a total system package. Initially, Data General is providing this service through its own staff -- specifically, field sales and support, as well as Data
General's own System's Integration group -- who collectively have been trained in all aspects of Oracle. Significantly, Oracle and Data General have jointly hired 25 engineers who are
located at Oracle's headquarters and responsible for development and support activities. Additionally, there are a number of engineers at Data General's Research Triangle Park facility
working on performance and high-availability issues for the Oracle product set.
SAP
SAP's R/3 applications for open, client-server platforms are a comprehensive set of integrated financial, manufacturing, human resource, sales and distribution application software packages.
Aberdeen end-user research indicates that SAP's impressive pace of sales for its R/3 product line of the past 18 months will continue for the foreseeable future. SAP's growth has not gone
unnoticed by the hardware suppliers, with all of the major vendors having relationships with SAP. Data General entered into a agreement with SAP in 1994, and immediately sent five
development and engineering staff to SAP's headquarters in Germany for training on SAP software and to develop configuration and implementation guidelines for AViiON servers and
CLARiiON disk arrays. Data General has since expanded the relationship with SAP to include a Competency Center at its North Carolina facility whose staff configures systems for bids,
performs benchmarks, assists in platform installations, and provides customer support.
SAP R/3 has a long and potentially costly implementation cycle making it less attractive for medium sized enterprises and these enterprises require an easier way to evaluate, purchase,
install, implement, and support R/3. Thus, Data General launched a worldwide initiative to help enterprises in the $50M to $1B revenue range achieve faster and more cost-effective SAP R/3
implementations, while lowering technical and financial risks.
Data General provides "one-stop shopping and support" for the technology platform and related services, while strategic partners deliver the services to implement R/3. Services provided by
Data General include rapid response to customer requests for system sizing and quoting, platform implementation, system and field maintenance engineering, database and system tuning,
network (LAN and WAN) design and implementation, training classes, remote network management, facilities management, 7 x 24 hardware and software technical support centers, and
disaster recovery.
Customers Aberdeen talked with stated the obvious -- given the popularity of SAP, they had a wide range of hardware platforms to choose from. However, each took an initial look at Data
General after selecting SAP because it had the only three-tier SAP implementation with Microsoft's NT Server as the middle tier. Data General has developed a unique link between tier-one
PCs running Windows as clients to quad Pentium-based NT Servers with the SAP application as the intermediate servers. They in turn are clients to either a Data General high end SMP
UNIX-based or NT-based server with Oracle 7.x used as the back-end database for the SAP applications.
Data General, the first vendor to offer R/3 in a combined UNIX/Windows NT Server configuration, provides a range of client/server operating environments (Figure 1) to meet the medium-
sized enterprise's requirements with Intel based AViiON servers and CLARiiON fault-tolerant storage systems. Data General also provides SAP customers with a high level of support for
what is to become for each customer a very large installation. Data General offers a customized service guarantee, which includes coverage of the hardware, operating system, SAP
applications and the Oracle database and related tools.

Aberdeen Conclusions
Each day Aberdeen's office is flooded with press releases from suppliers, many of which announce the strategic relationship which will provide its customers with the ultimate solution
package. So few of these partnerships actually offer anything of value to the customer that we generally avoid commenting on them to either the press or our end-user clients. Therefore, it
has been truly refreshing to have a supplier's claims supported by customer interviews.
Data General has continually impressed Aberdeen with its product, especially its major enhancements to the basic System V UNIX operating system (DG/UX), its very powerful servers
(AViiON), its excellent storage sub-systems (CLARiiON), and its work to provide a high level of system availability. All the people interviewed ran extensive internal benchmarks, and Data
General scored well each and every time. Data General has worked with each of the three ISVs discussed in this Profile to make sure the respective software packages work well on its
systems, and has taken the time to adequately train its staff on the respective applications so that Data General can truly act as the "prime contractor" on an installation.
More than anything tangible, what has impressed the customers -- and Aberdeen as well -- is the quality of Data General's treatment of the customer both during and after the sales process.
Each customer had an opportunity to test Data General's services with questions about the platform, the operating system or some other Data General product, and each was impressed with
both the speed and the accuracy of the response. Each customer also tested Data General's ability to respond to questions about its partner's product, and all were impressed with how quickly
the problem was solved -- without finger pointing.
To paraphrase one customer's response to being asked for the fifth time why she chose Data General over its competition, the company seems to have found a nice balance between being
small enough to respond to customers' needs with great flexibility and care, and large enough to be able to support the customer on a world-wide basis.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group profiles Data General Corporation's strategy of delivering integrated open-systems solutions through deep ISV partnerships with SAP, Oracle, and PeopleSoft, anchored by its AViiON UNIX/NT servers and CLARiiON storage subsystems. The study documents DG's revenue mix transformation from proprietary minicomputers to over 90% open-systems revenues, and validates partner and customer satisfaction through primary interviews. Aberdeen concludes DG has found a sustainable balance between responsiveness and global scale.
