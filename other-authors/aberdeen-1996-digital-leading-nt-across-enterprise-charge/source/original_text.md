### Digital: Leading the NT-Across-the-Enterprise Charge

*Source: AberdeenGroup, July 1996*

#### Introduction

Through a combination of prescient strategic planning and sheer outrageous fortune in competitive positioning, Digital Equipment Corporation is now poised to become the leading supplier of NT-based products and services across-the-enterprise. Digital was one of the first to recognize the immense growth opportunity that existed for a systems supplier that could provide the right mix of products and integration services to assist Information Technology (IT) managers in the deployment of NT-based solutions. And, with a broad suite of NT systems platforms and an enterprise-experienced services organization already in place — Digital is ready to lead the NT-across-the-enterprise charge.

And, by an amazing quirk of fate, none of Digital's major competitors — not IBM, not HP, not Sun — are positioned to challenge Digital Equipment Corporation as it takes NT upscale to capture the high-end of the NT server market. This lack of competition from established enterprise server companies has left the door wide open for Digital to sell and service the entire range of NT client and server environments, virtually unchallenged by the otherwise occupied competitors.

This Profile describes Digital's overall NT strategy: its product set and competitive positioning; its strategic partnerships; its relationship with Microsoft. And, Aberdeen examines the challenges Digital will face as it attempts to become the leader in the deployment of Microsoft's Windows NT solutions on desktops and servers throughout the enterprise.

#### Positioning Digital's NT

Digital Equipment Corporation is a broad-based supplier of computing and connectivity solutions; as well as a leader in providing systems integration and deployment services. Digital's message to the enterprise is clear: Digital has all the right products and services — as well as the experience — to assist IT managers in the deployment of application, system, and network solutions where required within the enterprise.

From a product perspective, Digital offers its customers a choice of three operating environments (Open VMS, Digital Unix, and Microsoft's Windows NT). All three operating environments can operate on Digital's Alpha microprocessor platform; Windows NT is also marketed on a variety of Intel client and server platforms (see Figure 2).

*Figure 2: Positioning Digital's Three Operating Environments — Source: AberdeenGroup, July 1996*

Digital has managed to clearly position its three operating environments in such a way as to virtually eliminate overlap in addressing customer requirements:

1. **OpenVMS**, a version of Digital's popular VAX VMS operating system, allows long-time Digital customers to migrate their existing VMS applications to newer, Digital Alpha RISC-based servers. OpenVMS is positioned to complement rather than conflict with NT — Digital positions OpenVMS as a continuous computing server that can provide back-end database and application services for NT clients. Digital has also enabled OpenVMS users to easily migrate to Windows NT, providing transition tools and services through its Affinity program.

2. **Digital Unix** is focused at the high-end of the Unix marketplace and features an implementation that takes advantage of 64-bit addressing and Very Large Memory (VLM). With Digital Unix, Digital hopes to capitalize on the fast growing market for high-end, 64-bit decision-support systems, data warehouses, and related applications. 32-bit NT is not expected to compete in the 64-bit marketplace for a few years.

3. **Digital positions Microsoft's Windows NT** as its premier operating environment for client/server computing. Digital's Intel-based NT systems are promoted for use on the commercial desktop and in various server situations. Alpha-based NT systems are sold as powerful technical and commercial desktops and as high-performance application and infrastructure servers. Digital recommends AlphaServers in environments that require additional computing power/performance beyond that of Intel-based servers; the support of large user counts; and/or systems scalability/extensibility. Alpha can be configured with more disk and memory than Digital's Intel-based servers.

With Digital's NT product suite, Digital can boast:
- More than 1,400 client/server applications available on its Alpha platform (and provides direct support for 300 PC & NOS applications);
- The broadest set of NT systems platforms in the industry (both Intel and Alpha platforms);
- Product superiority based upon Digital platform extensions (especially in the areas of scalability, availability, management);
- NT Interoperability/connectivity with other systems platforms — including integration with Unix and OpenVMS;
- An enterprise-experienced services organization with more than 5,000 Microsoft support personnel including 1,000 Microsoft Certified Professionals (quickly ramping to 1,700 in the near term);
- A strong business and technology relationship with Microsoft based on a long history of technology sharing and joint marketing agreements as a competitive advantage; and
- A leadership position in the deployment of NT-based Internet solutions.

#### A Competitive Perspective

As IT executives look to deploy NT throughout their enterprises, particularly as the base platform for mission critical client/server applications, they have told Aberdeen that four elements contribute to their choice of an NT platform supplier. These IT managers look for:

1. Corporate strategic commitment to NT;
2. Product breadth and extensions;
3. Integration/deployment services; and
4. Financial stability.

Numerous systems suppliers claim to have a strategic relationship with Microsoft, but several of the major suppliers appear to be running agendas that directly conflict with NT. Examples include:

**IBM** has issued no public statement that it is strategically committed to NT. IBM's OS/2 division continues to push OS/2 as a technology that directly competes with Microsoft NT for file, print, application, and infrastructure server business. Yet, other divisions such as IBM's ISSC systems integration group readily markets NT-based solutions to IBM customers.

**Hewlett-Packard** has stated that it is committed to "NT leadership," yet still needs to protect its massive HP/UX Unix installed base from erosion caused by the advance of lower margin, lower cost NT application servers into traditional Unix desktop and server markets. Further, HP has no plans to host NT on its PA RISC microprocessor, but rather will wait for a combined HP/Intel chip before carrying NT to the enterprise server level.

**Sun Microsystems** actively deploys NT-based clients, but has no strategic commitment to NT as a server platform. Rather, Sun positions its Unix-based Ultra product line to directly compete with NT-based servers.

Suppliers such as Sequent, Tandem, Data General, and Stratus all offer Intel-based NT solutions, but lack the financial muscle to compete head-on with Digital on a research/development and product breadth basis. NCR has added value to its NT platforms in the areas of systems management, transaction processing and processor scalability, but the NCR split from AT&T has raised questions about the company's long term growth prospects and financial viability. Compaq actually meets many IT decision maker requirements for product breadth and depth, but lacks the enterprise-level systems integration and deployment criteria experience necessary to compete with Digital on the enterprise level.

Digital is quite likely the only systems supplier capable of addressing the four basic buying criteria that IT managers consider critical as they deploy NT in mission-critical environments.

#### Core NT Platform Products

Digital's NT platform offerings are organized into three product areas: commercial desktops, personal workstations, and servers (see Figure 3).

*Figure 3: Digital's Core NT Platform Products — Source: Digital Equipment Corporation, July 1996*

**Commercial Desktop** platforms range from Digital's acclaimed HiNote Intel-based laptop to various Venturis and Celebris Intel-based personal computer models; and also includes an Intel or Alpha-based desktop client known as Multia. Multia is an innovative, NT-based "enterprise client" — an NT desktop designed to be highly manageable. A variety of integrated terminal clients come prepackaged with Multia, including: IBM 3270 (for access to mainframes), VT 340/420 (for access to various multi-user systems), X-terminal (for access to Unix servers), and even access to the World Wide Web with embedded Netscape Navigator software.

**Digital's Personal Workstation** line is a series of powerful Intel or Alpha-based desktop platforms designed for complex, graphically-oriented, compute intensive commercial and/or technical applications. The Personal Workstation line consists of the Alpha-based Alpha XL and Intel-based Celebris XL as well as Digital's AlphaStation models 255, 500, and 600 — with microprocessors ranging from 150 MHz Intel Pentium Pro to superpowerful 366 MHz Alpha implementations. And Digital's Personal Workstation line purports impressive benchmark performance, regularly beating Unix workstation competitors in Unix workstation benchmark strongholds such as the latest Pro/Engineer and Ansys — and frequently at less than half the cost of its competitors' systems.

**Digital's Servers** consist of a series of Intel-based Prioris servers and Alpha-based AlphaServers. The Prioris series consists of the LX and XL models positioned as workgroup servers; the HX line is positioned to serve as a low to mid-range application server; and the ZX line that services the mid-range through high-end as an application server. Digital's AlphaServer line is positioned to consistently outperform its Intel-based brethren with superior price/performance. AlphaServers can service the needs of workgroups or small departments where file and print services and groupware are important (models 1000 and 2000), as well as service the needs of larger departments where power, performance, and reliability are key considerations (with AlphaServer 1000 series through the 4100 models). And, to enable Information Systems (IS) managers to deploy NT in mission-critical situations, Digital has released NT clustering capabilities for all Intel-based Prioris servers and AlphaServers (1000, 2000, and 4000 series). Also, Digital has introduced new NT Redundant Array of Inexpensive Disks (RAID) storage options — providing IS managers with a powerful NT server environment with the high-availability and system failover capabilities required for business-critical operations.

#### Core NT Applications

ISVs are rushing to port their applications to the Windows NT operating environment if they have not done so already. And these ISVs qualify their software first-and-foremost on Intel platforms. The task-at-hand for Digital is to recruit and qualify the leading software ISVs and to provide assistance such that ISV application products are able to run on Digital's Alpha platform.

To this end, Digital's application partners have managed to test and qualify 1,400+ NT applications on its Alpha platforms, including software packages from industry leading suppliers of database, business, industry, banking/financial applications as well as solutions in groupware, Internet and software tool/utility technologies.

Digital NT servers have sold particularly well as NT application servers and infrastructure servers. As an application server, Digital has focused its NT effort on providing industry leading software packages in the areas of General Business/Accounting, Manufacturing, and Document Management. As an infrastructure server, Digital has focused on Groupware, Mail/Messaging, Internet/Intranet, and DataMarket (small Data Warehouse) services.

Finally, in a move to capture additional applications on Alpha NT environments, Digital has announced a transitional technology — FX!32 — a binary translation and emulation software utility that enables Alpha system users to access 32-bit native Windows and NT applications in a transparent fashion — and with significant performance improvements over X86 architectures. FX!32, in essence, easily brings Windows and NT applications to the Alpha architecture — and enables users to take advantage of the performance improvements offered by RISC architectures.

#### Systems Management

Managing distributed systems, network infrastructure and software assets can be expensive from a support perspective — and robust management tools are key to keeping these costs down. Digital, in combination with various software partners, offers a suite of products that enable systems managers to efficiently and effectively manage distributed NT systems environments — from the desktop through the enterprise.

At the **desktop level**, Digital provides ClientWORKS — a product that provides users and systems administrators with access to component and diagnostic information. And, due to integration with Microsoft's System Management Server (SMS) and Digital's POLYCENTER, ClientWORKS can be used for asset management.

At the **workgroup level**, Digital offers ServerWORKS Manager, an SNMP-based product that provides systems administrators with standards-based management of Digital servers and related networks. With ServerWORKS, a network administrator can use a standard Windows interface at the desktop to quickly and easily select devices and retrieve a wide range of information, and can set alarms to enable proactive management of critical server parameters. ServerWORKS is included with all Prioris and Alpha servers.

At the **enterprise level**, Digital offers management solutions through both Digital's POLYCENTER and Computer Associate's Unicenter management frameworks. To expand its NT enterprise management capabilities, Digital recently announced an Alliance for Enterprise Management with Computer Associates, a collaborative agreement that will allow for the integration of the POLYCENTER and Unicenter management offerings.

#### The Digital/Microsoft Inter-relationship

Microsoft is a software company that needs both engineering assistance and systems integration/deployment assistance if it is to move NT into the high-end of the server marketplace. Digital, essentially, is a full-scale systems and services supplier, and an excellent integrator of technology. And, for more than two decades, Digital has been involved in the deployment of mission-critical enterprise server environments, enterprise applications, large databases, and complex networks — a profile that identically matches Microsoft's deployment partner requirements.

Digital and Microsoft have been working together as strategic partners for more than a decade (see Figure 5), starting with Digital's distribution of DOS-based systems. In August, 1995, Digital and Microsoft significantly extended their partnering relationship, announcing an alliance that featured a major Digital technology swap in exchange for Microsoft's underwriting the training on Windows, NT, and Office/BackOffice for several thousand of Digital's professional services consultants. The result: Digital now has more than 1,000 Microsoft-certified professional services consultants — the largest Microsoft professional services organization in the world, and Microsoft now has an enterprise-experienced organization aggressively selling its solutions at the high-end of the enterprise.

And, as Windows NT moves up in the enterprise, Microsoft has seen the need to develop 64-bit addressing for NT — and Microsoft will work in conjunction with Digital to develop 64-bit addressing for Windows NT in the next major post-4.0 release of NT.

#### The Key to Digital's NT Success — Channels

For NT to sink-or-swim at Digital, Digital must be successful in driving NT products through multiple channels. Digital has created sales organizations to "pull" business from two directions: 1) direct sales to create demand and fulfill orders directly through Digital; and, 2) indirect sales focused on pulling business to Digital products — but referring the order fulfillment to indirect channels (distributors, Value-Added Resellers, and systems integrators). Digital recognizes that it must be successful using both approaches in order to establish a leadership position in NT systems.

And, Digital understands that Alpha-based platforms are not required for all application environments and exhibits no qualms about selling Intel-based products to its enterprise customers. Digital, through both direct and indirect channels will readily sell Intel-based platforms to prospective customers — a change that indicates that Digital realistically understands NT-buyer purchase dynamics.

#### Aberdeen Conclusions

Digital has everything going in its favor to become the market leader in deploying NT solutions in the enterprise. Digital predicted the rapid adoption of NT in the enterprise and has architected a broad suite of commercial desktop, personal workstation, and server products designed to meet the varied needs of NT users. Digital has structured software supplier relationships with leading-edge client/server solutions suppliers for deployment on Digital Alpha and Intel platforms. Digital has developed a close technology and business partnership with Microsoft that ensures that Digital gets access to the latest releases of NT, while Microsoft benefits by using Digital to deploy NT in the enterprise.

And Digital's competitors are seemingly nowhere to be found. IBM, HP, and Sun are driving distinctly different agendas — leaving the door wide open for Digital to succeed as the major supplier of NT products and services within the enterprise.

The open question is: "Can Digital execute its business, product, and sales strategies before other major competitors make the necessary adjustments to their strategies and aggressively enter the NT market?" Digital is way-out-in-front of its competitors with a strong product mix, a focused channels strategy, NT deployment services with global reach, and an NT-knowledgeable, fully-trained professional services organization. Digital is clearly in an NT leadership position today — this race is Digital's to win (or lose).

Digital believes that if it can successfully execute its NT strategy, the days of Digital's rapid growth and financial strength will return. And Aberdeen sees positive indications that Digital can execute this strategy successfully.
