### Digital's Personal Workstation: New Markets, New Products, New Channels, New Company, New Challenges

When Digital's "Personal Workstation" was introduced, Information Technology (IT) managers wondered what this strange Windows NT-based PC/Workstation hybrid was. After all, it looked like a PC, it was packaged like a PC, it fit on the desktop like a PC... If it looks like a duck, walks like a duck, and talks like a duck — then it's a duck — right?

*Figure 1: Digital Redefines the Power Desktop — Source: AberdeenGroup, April 1996*

Wrong. The Digital Personal Workstation is more than a PC — it is a powerful workstation capable of running not only PC applications but also a whole suite of previously Unix-only technical applications. Digital, and its Independent Software Vendor (ISV) partners, have migrated many of the technical and commercial applications traditionally run on Unix workstations to the Windows NT platform on its Personal Workstation — and are now able to offer the kind of 3-D graphics and performance characteristics required by sophisticated applications — at PC prices. And, because software suppliers are willing to lower software prices in order to capture volume — the associated ISV solutions software costs have also dropped to PC price points.

For Information Systems (IS) managers and PC users looking for a whole new price/performance computing metric, the Digital Personal Workstation simply must be evaluated — the Digital Personal Workstation is positioned to explode into the low-end and mid-range of the Unix workstation marketplace.

This Aberdeen Profile positions the Personal Workstation as a low cost Unix workstation alternative — and as a powerful NT-based commercial desktop environment — and highlights recent Digital product improvements that include the addition of more powerful microprocessor technologies and expanded graphics support.

#### Product Positioning

The Personal Workstation allows users to run a whole range of powerful PC commercial applications as well as complex, formerly "workstation-only" technical applications, on far less expensive but equally powerful PC Intel and Alpha-based componentry. (A fully configured Personal Workstation with appropriate business or technical applications averages about half the cost of comparable Unix workstation products). Digital's Personal Workstation, therefore, has a dual personality: 1) a low cost alternative to Unix workstations, and 2) a powerful NT-based desktop capable of running complex technical and commercial applications.

From an application perspective, the Personal Workstation has proven to be ideal at the entry-level workstation level running technical two-dimensional (2-D) and three-dimensional (3-D) applications in markets such as Mechanical Computer-aided Design (MCAD), Animation, and Digital Effects. And, as more and more technical ISVs migrate to the NT platform, Digital expects its Personal Workstation to broaden its technical market penetration in other disciplines.

Still, some markets will prefer 64-bit Unix workstations for certain high-end technical applications — markets that can make use of 64-bit processing to interact with very large databases, or that have complex 3D solids or graphics rendering processing requirements. It is in markets such as Geographical Information Systems (GIS) and possibly Electronic Design Automation (EDA), for instance, that Unix workstations will continue to claim ownership as Microsoft's NT operating environment will not be in a position to challenge 64-bit system architectures for several years.

The Personal Workstation can also be positioned to act as a transitional technology between traditional Unix and PC environments. Technical, Unix-originated 2-D and 3-D applications can readily be merged into Microsoft Word and PowerPoint documents using 3Space Viewer from Template Graphics Software which is offered with all Personal Workstations. 3Space Viewer allows Microsoft's Office products to import 2-D and 3-D designs using Microsoft's Object Linking and Embedding 2.0 (OLE 2.0) programmatic interface.

Personal Workstations can interoperate with Unix systems by sharing NFS files, terminal emulation, and via the Internet. The Personal Workstation comes with a 30-day free trial and special discount purchase terms for Hummingbird's NFS Maestro for Windows NT, Exceed 5.0 for Windows NT. And, for Internet communications, the Personal Workstation comes with an unrestricted free use license for Microsoft Explorer.

#### Core Products

The hardware product offering consists of single PC base chassis and three families of microprocessor offerings: Pentium, Pentium Pro, or Alpha. The Celebris XL Pentium series offers single and dual processor implementations of Pentium 100, 133, 150 and 166 MHz microprocessors. The Celebris XL Pentium Pro series offers single processor implementations of 150 MHz, 180 MHz, and 200 MHz microprocessors. And the Alpha XL family offers single processor versions of 266 MHz, and the newly announced 300 MHz or 366 MHz microprocessors.

And, in many cases, these systems are field upgradeable. On the Intel-based Personal Workstations and on the Alpha XL 266, Digital provides daughter card upgrades for implementing new microprocessor technologies. Users simply push in a faster processor card for an instant system horsepower increase. The Alpha XL 300 and 366, however, have been optimized to take advantage of a 128-bit data path and do not offer the swappable microprocessor facilities offered on the Alpha XL 266 and Intel-based offerings.

Digital also recently announced expanded graphics capabilities for its Personal Workstation lines. Graphics adapters include products such as the Matrox Millennium, the AccelGraphics 300, and now includes Digital's PowerStorm adapters to address mid-range and high-end graphics requirements.

All systems are shipped pre-configured with Microsoft's NT — ready for the Value-Added Reseller to add its software and deploy to its customer base.

#### Performance

From a performance perspective, the Digital Personal Workstation has been shown to directly rival Unix workstation products.

The latest Pro/Engineer performance benchmarks show that an Alpha XL 366 MHz Personal Workstation can outperform a Silicon Graphics, Inc. (SGI) Indigo 2 R440 (250 MHz) Unix workstation by 69% and an Hewlett Packard (HP) C110 Unix workstation by 81%. And Windows NT Intel and Alpha CADalyst benchmarks show Digital's Personal Workstation outperforming HP's 712/100 Unix workstation by 27%, and Sun's UltraSparc 1 by 60%.

And, the Ansys benchmarks show Digital's Alpha XL outperforming SGI's Indigo by 42%, while HP's J210 actually slightly outperforms the Alpha XL by six percentage points. But bear in mind that the HP J210 priced out at $34,980; SGI's Indigo priced at $33,000 — the Alpha XL listed at $17,413, or almost half the cost of its leading competitors. Given these price/performance figures, the Digital Personal Workstation is signaling that a new day has dawned — that NT can now be considered a valid choice in the technical workstation market.

#### Ultimately, People Buy Solutions

NT runs on multiple microprocessor architectures, including: Intel, Alpha, Power PC and MIPS. In the NT environment, ISVs almost always port their applications to Intel platforms first, and then port to the next platform that offers them the largest business opportunity. Digital has been aggressive and successful in getting ISVs to port to Alpha and has secured that "second-key-platform" porting status.

More than 2,000 NT native applications can run on the Digital Intel-based Celebris XL Personal Workstation platform; more than 1,200 applications now run on the Digital Alpha-based Alpha XL Personal Workstation platform.

Digital has targeted five specific markets for its Personal Workstation:

1. **MDA (Mechanical Design Automation)**: Key software from AutoDesk, Bentley, EDS, Parametric Technology, Matra-Datavision and SolidWorks.
2. **GIS (Geographical Information Systems)**: More than 70 software packages, including ESRI, Bentley MicroStation Mapper, Cadcorp, Environmental System Corporation's ESC CEM, Small World GIS, Valmet Automation's OASys.
3. **Digital Content and Creation (DCC)**: Adobe, Softimage, AutoDesk 3D Studio Max, Saros, Looking Glass — high-end publishing, web creation, animation.
4. **Financial**: Applications requiring powerful number-crunching or highly graphical display capabilities.
5. **Software Development**: Compilers, GUI Builders, 4GL, CASE, CADE, OO tools — ParcPlace Visual Works, Nutcracker, Informix SE.

#### The New Digital — Distribution Through Indirect Channels

When Digital introduced its Digital Personal Workstation product line, Digital made a statement — Digital is playing by 1990's marketing rules rather than the Digital-heyday-past 1970's rules. Digital has been known for building technology first and asking questions later and for its hands-on, direct sales approach to selling computers. With the "Personal Workstation Program" Digital has done its homework 1990's style — it has analyzed the market, built a business plan that focuses on specific markets with specific products, and architected a product that utilizes a common base system and therefore gives Digital basic cost economies-of-scale in the manufacturing process. And — most importantly — Digital Equipment Corporation has hinged its success on the care and feeding of its indirect channel partners — Independent Software Vendors (ISVs), Value-Added Distributors (VARs), and Distribution Partners.

Digital has established an aggressive channels distribution program aimed at providing VARs with products which offer strong margin while minimizing the training and stocking cost impacts on the VAR's people and inventory.

It is Digital's express intent to drive sales of its Personal Workstation through indirect channels — and most specifically through VARs. To eliminate channel conflict, Digital's internal and direct sales force do not sell the Personal Workstation line. Rather, Digital sales personnel turn leads over to VAR partners and are compensated for doing so — a scenario in which everybody wins.

Digital provides VARs with:
- Sales training programs ensuring VAR sales org knows how to position Intel and Alpha products;
- Technical training, including both hardware and software installation and maintenance;
- Sales tools — made available so VARs do not have to reinvent the wheel;
- Promotional programs that reward VARs for volume sales with increased margin dollars.

#### Aberdeen Conclusions

A single base platform; the hottest microprocessor architectures; the NT operating system; a complete suite of fully deployable workstation applications; training; support; lead generation; and cooperative advertising — all aimed at VAR channels chomping at the bit to capitalize on vulnerable low-end Unix workstation and high-end PC market opportunities. Who says Digital doesn't know how to market?

In essence, Digital — with its Personal Workstation line — has changed the rules of competition for the desktop. HP and Sun have "locked-up" the high-powered desktop computing market with their Unix/RISC-based workstations — but are they in a state of denial, missing the shift to high-powered PC desktops? Digital has just changed the playing field — equivalent or greater performance at half the cost of comparable Unix workstations.

Digital has created a Value-Added Reseller's dream machine. The common base platform translates into less inventory and easier training of field maintenance personnel — reducing the VAR's operating expenses. And, to capture the VAR's attention, Digital is providing decent margins and promotional programs which increase the VAR's overall margin based upon volume shipments — incenting the VAR community to buy into the Personal Workstation concept and aggressively market Digital products.

The real winner in Digital's Personal Workstation initiative will be the enterprise. Personal Workstations should serve to halve the cost of current low-end Unix workstations while providing superior price/performance. Unix workstation suppliers in particular will have to cut costs or increase functionality at the low-end in order to remain competitive. The fallout from this maneuver should result in a margin-squeeze in the Unix workstation market — and this phenomena will be ongoing.

With the introduction of the high-powered Personal Workstation, Digital is using new business rules and the latest marketing techniques. Perhaps the new Digital Equipment Corporation has really — at long last — arrived.
