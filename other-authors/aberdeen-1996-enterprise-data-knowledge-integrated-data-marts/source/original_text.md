### Executive Summary

As enterprises craft systems to help them understand their customers, their markets, and their business positions, they must explore their business processes and put in place the technological infrastructure to sustain and drive new business practices. These necessity-driven explorations have created the need for architectures in which data-knowledge-driven growth and change, and tactical revisions to both the business and technology, are becoming the norm.

Whether called a data mart, a data warehouse, or some not-yet-invented lingo — the aggressive data-access systems that are now empowering many household-name companies are able to present different classes of users with different mixtures of summarized and detailed data. They are part, either physically or logically, of a well-planned (and still able to evolve) enterprise-wide-warehouse architecture.

Many of today's highly successful stand-alone data marts, which are frequently commandeered by the business units they serve, have encouraged enterprises to build more data marts. But success has many dependencies. Unless planned for, enterprises could end up with numerous isolated systems, each based on potentially contradictory business rules and each contributing to a deluge of enterprise-systems-crippling requests for data.

Moreover, given the competitively-driven need for an enterprise to understand the deep relationship between its products and its customers' behavior and buying patterns, an enterprise policy of encouraged fragmentation could be disastrous for the business.

But it is the processes, not the data mart technologies, that have been problematic. By starting with a single-subject data mart that integrates into an enterprise-aware whole and by using an iterative method of building a road map for the enterprise to follow, Aberdeen field experience indicates that enterprises have eliminated the perils of enterprise fragmentation. Equally important, enterprises that have followed such a model begin generating an ROI that enables them to underwrite subsequent efforts (and successes!) in other subject areas, as well as leverage enterprise-valuable project-by-project experience.

### Overcompensating

Rightly seizing upon the impracticality of building multimillion dollar first-nail-down-every-detail enterprise-wide data warehouse models, followed by constructing huge enterprise-wide warehouses, many business units have shown their impatience by building data marts.

Data marts can be composed of summarized data, detailed data, or an appropriate admixture of both. These useful information repositories are housed on either relational database management systems (RDBMSs) or proprietary multidimensional database technologies. Optimized for the "domains" they serve, data marts typically reflect a single-subject or vertical-industry slant.

Despite business-unit enthusiasm for the data mart, these systems have not always been successful, particularly in returning maximized information-access benefits. Giving each business unit control and authority over its own data marts without harnessing this power for the collective good of the enterprise, may adequately answer the business unit's short-term objectives, but will prove shortsighted in the not-too-distant-future. Even if the standalone data mart could physically participate in an enterprise-wide query, the possibility — say nothing of the likelihood — of users receiving inaccurate answers to their questions should be high enough to diminish a responsible manager's confidence in the result.

As leading users of data-access architectures attest, most enterprises have one or more inconsistent definitions for the most bread-and-butter enterprise expression: revenue. Other discrepancies exist. It is this awareness of the numerous disparities in enterprise systems that started theorists envisioning data warehouses — on-line repositories of cleaned and scrubbed detailed and summary enterprise data that are based on common business metrics meant to serve the needs of business users, promote accurate enterprise reporting, and defend transaction systems from the overhead associated with query processing.

Aberdeen believes that the farther away an enterprise moves from the spirit of such a vision, the more likely it will squander ROI, miss new business opportunities, and be ill-prepared for the competitive battles waged with the help of data-knowledge-driven architectures.

### Creating A Better Balance

New business drivers — global trading and global risk assessment, for example — suggest both an escalation in the change of business dynamics and an ever greater need to pull the enterprise together. Whatever the technology platforms the enterprise selects, Aberdeen believes that the enterprise should at least work from a high-level (as opposed to detailed) model of, and common metrics for, the business practices. Equally important, the fact that rapidly changing business dynamics toppled many early enterprise top-down efforts to model enterprise processes in a single monumental effort, only argues against the inefficiency of the method, not necessarily the goal.

These high-level models can be quickly built and individually tailored from industry-specific templates honed from years of experience between enterprises and leading suppliers, including NCR Corp., Tandem Computers Inc., and data transformation specialists such as Prism Solutions. Moreover, many enterprises have jump-started their initiatives with time-and implementation-tested methodologies and short-term consulting engagements with the same — as well as other — suppliers.

By using high-level models, successful "data-marting" enterprises have remained rooted in enterprise reality, and saved the enterprises from moving away from competitive position, data mart by data mart, query by query.

### A Logical Approach

Rather than discourage departmental or business-unit eagerness for autonomous data marts, many IS executives capture this decision-support momentum and leverage it for the good of the enterprise.

Applying business and technical commonsense, many enterprises have profited from the best of both worlds — business-unit fervor for autonomous (and affordable) data marts and best-practices IS control — while developing enterprise-harmonious architectures that will enable knowledge workers to answer questions quickly from the summary data best tailored to meet their needs and drill into detail data when doing so is warranted.

The "logical" extension of departmental data marts is an enterprise-wide warehouse built a subject — and therefore data mart — at a time, with each data mart based on common metrics.

By starting with a single subject that links into an RDBMS driven whole, and by using an iterative method of building a road map for the enterprise to follow, Aberdeen believes that enterprises will be able to avoid the pitfalls of uncontrolled data mart proliferation while maintaining the business-unit momentum that makes data mart projects politically viable.

### Aberdeen Conclusions

IS executives must encourage their planners to build business-sensitive data-access systems that reflect the business drivers embedded in end-user behavior. The integrated data mart approach — starting with a single subject, building on common metrics, and iterating toward an enterprise-wide warehouse — provides the best balance between business-unit agility and enterprise data integrity.
