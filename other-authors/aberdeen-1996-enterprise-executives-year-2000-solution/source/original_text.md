## Original Document Text

### How Enterprise Executives Play a Pivotal Role in the Year 2000 Solution

Technology companies have conditioned their customers to delay purchases as long as is operationally feasible. The next version or model is inevitably cheaper, faster, and less-flawed than the current one and customers have come to know this. This is, perhaps, why senior executives are waiting for their IT organizations to bring them the magic-bullet to fix this vexing Year 2000 problem. This approach is wrong and has disastrous implications for many businesses. This Viewpoint argues that the Year 2000 problem is as much about competitive advantage as it is about technology. And non-technology senior executives will find themselves playing pivotal roles in their enterprises' Year 2000 solutions.

### It's Really About Shelf Space, Not Software

For a consumer packaged goods company, the Year 2000 problem is about the gain — or loss — of shelf space (do you think Wal-Mart will let a vendor near its stores without proof of Year 2000 readiness for any integrated inventory and provisioning systems?). A process company may find its Year 2000 problems are about its ability to efficiently and competitively use its asset base. And a services company will find that Year 2000 bugs may undermine its capability to mine datamarts for critical targeted customer marketing. The unifying theme for businesses is that the Year 2000 problem is, in the best case, about determining winners and losers in the all-important game of competitive advantage — and about corporate viability in the worst case.

Why competitive advantage? Because tightly integrated, Web-enabled technology infrastructures are the circulatory system of organizations today. Large, global organizations are interdependent on real-time, on-line worldwide information that can be transmitted from Singapore to Frankfurt, Atlanta, and Johannesburg with a key stroke. These enterprises are also, in many cases, technologically intertwined in the operations of their customers, suppliers, and distributors. If this information flow is compromised, their business is also compromised.

Not being Year 2000 safe at the start of the millennium — and in most cases before — will erode the competitive strengths of companies who, in turn, will lose to industry players who better manage this transition.

### How Enterprise Executives Play a Pivotal Role in the Year 2000 Solution

Competitive strategy is the domain of senior executives. The Year 2000 problem will rock businesses with the same force as any sudden, unanticipated, or unplanned-for market change. For example, the competitive landscape of the airline industry has shifted abruptly several times over the last two decades through regulatory changes, the early and strategic use of computerized reservation systems and scheduling schemes, and international alliances.

The Year 2000 problem will bring market disruption to technology-enabled enterprises and industries. Some company executives will delay decision making and find themselves in a reactive mode as they scramble for resources and technology to ensure the continued operation of their businesses. Other senior executives will look for opportunity in cementing or strengthening their enterprises' competitive footing by ensuring Year 2000 safety for their businesses.

Executives play pivotal roles in ensuring Year 2000 safety for their enterprise by:

- requiring that a comprehensive and proactive plan is in place;
- securing trusted external assistance;
- insisting day-to-day business operations experience minimal disruption.

Above all, senior executives must marshal the collective Year 2000 efforts with a real sense of urgency. This urgency will send a signal to the rest of the organization that becoming Year 2000 safe is a corporate priority and that meeting the millennium deadline is non-negotiable.

### Background — 1960's Trade-offs Force 1990's Strategic Decisions

The Year 2000 problem is straightforward — even perversely appealing to non-technologists in the illusory simplicity of its flaws. Programmers, in the 1960s and 1970s, used two digits in code, rather than four, to represent a year. This saved precious and expensive memory — priorities of the day. This practice lingered into the eighties (and, in some cases, the nineties) as companies attempted to maintain backward compatibility with existing systems. The result is that the year 2000 will appear to many technology systems as 00, not 2000, and be interpreted as 1900.

Because the implications of wrong dates on a universal scale are often unknown or seem remote, senior enterprise IS management and senior hardware, software, and networking supplier management cannot fully describe the extent of the Year 2000 problem to their peers, much less provide comprehensive solutions. Information systems today are so widely integrated that finding all likely corrupt transactions resulting from bad-date calculations in hardware, software, and network equipment is an immense challenge.

### Aberdeen's Conclusions

Aberdeen still sees a major disconnect between what the majority of IS professionals believe will be the very nasty consequences of not dealing with the Year 2000 issues as soon as possible and what the overwhelming majority of senior enterprise managers, especially in the government sector, are willing to invest to fix the real — not potential — problem.

Aberdeen believes that it is highly probable that the majority of senior enterprise managers will understand the severity of this problem at the same time early in 1997. The result will be enterprises initiating crash buying sprees starting in mid-1997 to ensure that Year 2000 compliant systems have been installed, tested, and are running their business critical applications by mid-1999. Professional service providers of Year 2000 solutions are already planning to increase their fees in 1997, 1998, and 1999 as the lack of qualified personnel becomes even more acute.
