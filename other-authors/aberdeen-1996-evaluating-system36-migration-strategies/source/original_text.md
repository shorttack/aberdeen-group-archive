### Evaluating System/36 Migration Strategies

#### Preface

Business owners and executives who depend upon the IBM System/36 to run their operations tell Aberdeen Group that they are now ready to migrate to a newer computing platform. But, and this is their big "but," they are business people, not computer nerds. These executives want to know their options and they demand reasoned advice.

With the objective of assisting System/36 owners with their migration planning, Aberdeen Group has evaluated the realistic options available. This White Paper is based on our long-term experience in helping clients transition to new technologies, analysis of supplier-proffered migration options, and interviews with System/36 users who have already migrated.

#### Executive Summary

Aberdeen interviewed current owners of System/36, and they universally say, "It is time to move!" The reasons they cite are that systems maintenance is becoming too expensive while the quality is declining, value added software resellers are no longer supporting the installed applications — much less enhancing them to meet new requirements, and with IBM's 1994 introduction of the AS/400 Advanced 36, it has become crystal clear that IBM is no longer economically able to keep technology current for the installed base of older System/36s.

According to System/36 owners, there are four realistic options to follow:

1. **Stay put and do nothing.** Keep and maintain the current System/36 running as best as possible realizing that application changes must be made to have them "Year 2000 compliant;"
2. **Replace their current System/36 with a new Advanced 36.** Acquire the IBM-supported Advanced 36 and simply move your SSP-based applications and data onto it, and as business dictates add the OS/400 operating system;
3. **Migrate to the Traditional AS/400.** Buy the AS/400 Advanced System with the OS/400 Version 3 Release 6 or Version 3 Release 7 operating system, run your SSP-based applications in the System/36 Environment, and add new client-server applications to meet broadening business requirements;
4. **Migrate to a Windows NT Server-based PC-LAN.** Buy an Intel-based PC server using Microsoft's NT Server operating environment, acquire all new applications, and replace all 5250 terminals with PCs.

After thoroughly evaluating the four options with the help of System/36 owners, Aberdeen believes that migrating to the Advanced 36 is clearly the best choice for the vast majority of current System/36 owners.

The reasons the Advanced 36 migration option is the preferred path are:
- lowest cost;
- least disruptive to business and data processing operations;
- ability to use current, well-understood business applications;
- requires no additional or new personnel;
- fully supported by IBM.

Not surprisingly, System/36 owners told Aberdeen that the first option they evaluated was migrating to a PC-LAN. However, they quickly discovered the high cost of new applications and computer hardware made this prohibitive — and that was before they added in the cost of installing new wiring, creating a network infrastructure, converting their existing data, customizing new packaged applications while rewriting critical in-house developed applications, retraining their data processing staff, and educating employees on the new applications.

System/36 owners can run their current applications on the traditional AS/400 — after modifications and recompiling — in the System/36 Environment. Nonetheless, Aberdeen felt that the overall costs and technical complexity were too daunting in the short term — even though this was considered a long-term option after gaining experience with the Advanced 36.

The stay-put-and-do-nothing option was the most desirable but the least realistic given the System/36's age and lack of supplier support. The question of migration is no longer "should I," but "when."

Aberdeen recommends that current System/36 owners begin the process of migrating to the Advanced 36 — and cut over to production within the next 12 months. Longer term, System/36 owners need to begin planning budget monies for eventually moving to the OS/400 operating system to gain access to new client-server applications, be able to manage and to maintain compatibility with their historical data, and migrate to Year 2000 compliant applications.

#### Why System/36 Owners Want to Migrate Now

Aberdeen interviews of owners of System/36 show that the majority would prefer not to migrate. It is clear from the interviews Aberdeen conducted with current System/36 owners that there is a very high level of satisfaction with the core System/36 hardware and the individual applications.

The System/36 is consistently described as a very stable platform, operating for years with few problems, requiring fewer resources to manage and fairly inexpensive to operate. Most System/36 owners have run all — or significant parts — of their business on this mini-computer for a good number of years, and are legitimately reluctant to "mess with success." At the same time, they fully understand the rest of the world is moving forward with other technologies and their valued System/36s will soon become relics of a bygone era.

As a result, System/36 owners also told Aberdeen that they believe, "It is time to move!" The reasons they cite to support their understanding for the need to migrate are:

- Systems maintenance is becoming too expensive while the quality is declining. Not only is IBM increasing the maintenance costs each year, but many are discovering that it is difficult to even find parts for the older System/36 models.
- Resellers are no longer supporting the installed applications — much less enhancing them to meet new requirements. The vast majority of System/36 resellers stopped new application development with the advent of the AS/400, and many have now stopped supplying bug fixes and patches. While some System/36 owners have the Year 2000 compliant operating system, SSP-based applications which support Year 2000 compliance are very hard to find.
- With IBM's 1994 introduction of the Advanced 36, it has become crystal clear that IBM is no longer economically able to keep technology current for the installed base of older System/36s. With more reliable components and remote maintenance capabilities, it is much more cost-effective for IBM to maintain Advanced 36s, while exceeding customer satisfaction expectations, than the much older System/36s.
- As business people, the System/36 owners fully appreciate the fact that a declining installed base has a material impact on IBM's ability to effectively support the aging systems still in use. And, since the base is declining at a rate in excess of 20% per year, even third-parties are finding it less profitable to provide support.
- A final and significant factor encouraging owners to move away from the System/36 is the fact that so many of their peers have already done so.

#### System/36 Owners' Migration Options

Specifically for this White Paper, Aberdeen interviewed both current System/36 and former System/36 owners who migrated to the Advanced 36 to better understand the migration options they believed were realistically available to them and their findings from evaluating each.

Obviously, the most desired option is to stay put and do nothing. Under this option, System/36 owners would continue to run their existing applications as is. However, based upon the reasons described above, this option is only a holding pattern until the owners can decide which migration strategy to pursue. Aberdeen would note that we believe most System/36 applications will become dysfunctional well before January 1, 2000 — since businesses use applications for forecasting and "looking ahead" planning.

The option that many System/36 users are evaluating or have evaluated is migrating to a Windows NT Server-based PC-LAN solution. Under this option, the user would acquire an Intel-based server, client PCs, new application and database software, retrain the internal IS staff, rewrite in-house developed applications for client-server architecture, and convert historical data to a new format.

The next most logical option to pursue is replacing the current System/36 with IBM's Advanced 36. Under this option, IBM or a reseller, simply transfers the users' existing SSP-based application code and data onto the new box and connects it to the system. The only thing that changes is the monthly hardware maintenance cost (it goes down) and the user response time (it also goes down).

The last option System/36 owners evaluate is migrating directly to IBM's AS/400 Advanced System. Under this option, users would run their existing applications in the SSP Environment on the AS/400 and add new client-server applications as needed.

Significantly, none of the System/36 decision makers Aberdeen interviewed believed that migrating to a UNIX-based system is a realistic option — UNIX is simply too complex to manage within their organizations.

#### Evaluating the Options

Based on the System/36 owners' and MIS executives' view of managing their data processing environment, and Aberdeen's own experience with migrating to newer technology, the most desirable migration strategy would meet the following criteria, in priority order:

1. Lowest capital cost;
2. Lowest cost to operate;
3. Lowest MIS (applications, data, and personnel) transition cost;
4. Least disruptive to business operations;
5. Responsive supplier support.

Aberdeen evaluated each of the three viable migration options against these criteria.

#### Migrate to a Windows NT Server-based PC-LAN

**Summary:** At first blush, this is the most popular of the options because of the perception that it is the least expensive of the options. The reality is that it has the highest overall cost of ownership of all the options.

**Capital Cost:** The cost to install a 10-user PC-LAN system is estimated to be $24,125 for the hardware and $9,690 for the systems software. Applications software is additional — for instance, a basic accounting package would cost approximately $12,500. Costs for a 20-user PC-LAN system is estimated to be $40,875 for the hardware, $15,040 for the systems software and $22,500 for a basic accounting package. System/36 users choosing this migration option should count on spending at least $50,000 — and very likely much more — for just the basic hardware and applications software.

*Table 1: Hardware and Software Cost Comparison — Source: Aberdeen Group, September 1996*

**Cost to Operate:** The industry rule-of-thumb is that it costs $10,000 per year to manage, maintain, and upgrade a Windows NT Server and $1,000 for each attached PC.

**MIS Transition Cost:** MIS cannot use anything from the System/36 — including personnel skills — when migrating from a System/36 to a PC-LAN. Any move to a new architecture with new applications will disrupt MIS operations until the staff becomes familiar with their use. With the inability of NT Server-based applications to use historical System/36 data, much of this will have to be manually entered for use with the new application.

**Disruption to Business Process:** All new business processes, user interfaces, and reports will need to be designed, implemented, and used. The good news is that an enterprise can start with a clean sheet of paper for re-engineering its business the way it has always wanted to. The bad news is that this can easily creep into numerous long-term projects that are viewed as merely costly sink holes.

**Responsive Supplier Support:** The primary PC-LAN supplier will most likely be a local dealer or independent value-added reseller. System/36 owners who are familiar with IBM and its structured and managed responsiveness will now find that they are being supported by individuals who are often being paid by the hour to fix problems.

#### Move To a New Advanced 36

**Summary:** Moving to the Advanced 36 provides a performance increase, improves the level of maintainability, and does so at the lowest cost of all the alternative options — and the Advanced 36 is a Year 2000 compliant platform. All the former System/36 / current Advanced 36 users Aberdeen interviewed were extremely happy that they had chosen this path.

**Capital Cost:** The move requires a simple server-swap, while all the peripherals and applications software are maintained. There is an initial outlay for the new system — ranging from $9,800 to $21,000 depending on the model purchased and the need for additional peripherals. IBM from time-to-time offers trade-in allowances for System/36s. There would not be any expenses for the applications, since all current System/36 applications run on the Advanced 36.

*Table 2: Packaged Advanced 36 Systems — Running SSP only — Source: IBM, September 1996*

**Cost to Operate:** Advanced 36 is lower in terms of maintenance (dropping from approximately $330 per month for larger System/36s to $95 per month for even larger Advanced 36s) and electricity than the System/36. The real cost savings is in performance — by running batch processing jobs 8-10 times faster.

**MIS Transition Cost:** There are only minimal MIS transition costs incurred when moving to the Advanced 36. IBM has a transition data link (TDL) product which moves System/36 data to the new system at a rate of 45MB per hour. Most software suppliers can provide the transition service for approximately $50 per hour. Owners who have migrated report an average of one day to move from the System/36 to the Advanced 36.

**Disruption to Business Process:** There is little if any disruption or change to the business processes. The most major disruption reported in our interviews was three days for an inexperienced reseller to complete the cut-over to the new system. There is no need for training employees on a new application set — the operating system and the applications are the same.

**Responsive Supplier Support:** IBM supports Advanced 36s directly. Users told Aberdeen that IBM provided fast, comprehensive, and reliable support through a well-trained and motivated field service organization. In addition, the Advanced 36 includes the capability to detect potential problems before they occur and disrupt operations.

#### Migrate to the Traditional AS/400

**Summary:** Since its introduction in 1988, the AS/400 has evolved to be more than a very versatile application server. It can also be used as a PC server, a printer server, a fax server, an Internet gateway, data warehouse, etc. The newest AS/400 Advanced Systems run the latest OS/400 operating systems, Version 3 Release 6 or Version 3 Release 7, which are Year 2000 compliant.

**Capital Cost:** A new system would have to be purchased — entry level price for an AS/400 around $8,000 — comparable to Advanced 36 but may need larger configuration due to performance degradation when running in the SSP Environment.

**Cost to Operate:** No significant differences should be anticipated between operating an AS/400 and an Advanced 36 providing the same services. However, when users acquire new native AS/400 applications, this will require staff training and levels of support.

**MIS Transition Cost:** The AS/400's operating environment is similar to but different from the System/36. As a result, MIS is required to master a new skill set. This involves training costs, and in some cases, the addition of new personnel. However, due to the similarities of the operating environment, it is still less than the PC-LAN alternative.

**Responsive Supplier Support:** The AS/400 division is a multi-billion dollar operation and IBM's most profitable hardware division. IBM has just started shipping the latest version of the AS/400. This level of business and investment means that IBM will be supporting the AS/400 for a significant time to come.

First, all the evidence points to the clear need to migrate from the System/36 to something else. The System/36's hardware design and operating system's underlying architecture is over 20 years old, and naturally has limitations in what it can do compared to more modern systems.

Second, Aberdeen firmly believes that the most costly and most disruptive migration strategy would be the move from the System/36 to a PC-LAN environment. While the PC-LAN may be a good decision for those automating their business functions for the first time and do not understand what is required to manage and operate a bet-your-company, production system, it does not seem a practical decision for current System/36 owners.

Third, for some System/36 users the AS/400 represents the best bridge between the old technology and the new. A base AS/400 purchase price is not much more than an Advanced 36, and the System/36 applications and data can operate on the AS/400 in an SSP Environment.

Finally, Aberdeen has become convinced that based upon their acquisition criteria the vast majority of System/36 owners have only one clear migration choice — the move to the Advanced 36 system with the ability to keep all the current applications.

#### Appendix A: Joining The Crowd — The Tale Of The Numbers

Originally introduced in 1983, the System/36 has continued to provide customers with a reliable way to run their business. However, during the past four years, the installed base of System/36 sites in the United States has declined by more than half, from about 87,000 sites in 1992, to a projected 42,000 sites in 1996.

IBM told Aberdeen that over 90% of the System/36 sites which migrated in 1995 had moved to the AS/400 solutions — mainly the Advanced 36. Many of these System/36 owners realized that migrating to an Advanced 36 was easily justified by the savings realized from environmental and maintenance operating costs alone.

#### Appendix B: The Year 2000 Problem

One of the issues we have alluded to, but not fully explained, is the so-called Year 2000 dilemma. In the past, when memory and disk storage were both expensive and limited, almost all computer hardware, operating systems, applications and their related data-file structures, used a two-digit date field for the year. The "19" was set as a global constant. Thus 1996 would be entered into a record within an application as "96", with the "19" assumed by the system. When the year 2000 begins to be used by an application — and it shows up in numerous calculations much before the actual date — the applications will read it as 1900.

In order to "fix" this problem, all the internal pieces of the computer system have to be made "Year 2000 compliant" — a process that could cost each business significant sums of monies over the next few years as programmers need to review each line of source code and fix and test all with dates.

Each computer supplier is attempting to make their new systems Year 2000 compliant. For example, IBM has announced that the AS/400 platform, with OS/400 Version 3 Release 6 or Version 3 Release 7, and many of the third-party AS/400 applications, now meet the compliance criteria.

In this White Paper, Aberdeen has recommended that current System/36 owners move to the Advanced 36. However, they should be aware that they will need to migrate to Year 2000 compliant applications well before December 1999.
