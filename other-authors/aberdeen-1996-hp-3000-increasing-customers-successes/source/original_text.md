### Preface

The HP 3000 has become an enigma to many. It began life in the 1970s as the technology-leading 16-bit commercial minicomputer designed to take advantage of state-of-the-science database technology. In the 80s it successfully made the transition to much higher, customer-demanded performance levels with the introduction of 32-bit RISC (reduced instruction set computing) processors ahead of the competition. In addition, knowledgeable customers bestowed upon it a well-earned reputation as the most reliable general-purpose midrange commercial system in the world. However, in the early 1990s, after almost two decades of successful HP 3000 innovation, new customer sales declined dramatically. IS decision makers began acquiring portable (although more expensive on a price/performance basis) Unix-based relational database management systems (RDBMSs) and applications for new projects. Even Hewlett-Packard's management at times seemed suddenly unsure of where and how the HP 3000 should fit within its own product line.

Aberdeen believes that after more than two years of internal strategy searching, Hewlett-Packard (HP) finally recognizes what the HP 3000 means to both itself and its customers and is on the correct path for successfully supporting its customers' successes into the HP 3000's third decade.

### Executive Summary

The motto and theme within HP's Commercial Systems Division, the HP organization responsible for HP 3000 product development and management, is "Customer First!"

Customer First has two very powerful implications for the HP 3000. First and foremost, it means that the Commercial Systems Division itself must get very close to the customer base to continuously understand users' current needs and future information systems' requirements. These user-originated functional priorities are then the basis for both new product enhancements and partnering agreements with third-party software suppliers. Second, it quite distinctly indicates that capturing new customers is not a high priority and an area in which the division will not be measured.

HP came to the decision that Customer First was the right strategy for the HP 3000 for the following three major reasons:

1. The overwhelming majority of HP 3000 customer-enterprises plan to continue using their existing HP 3000s to run core business applications well into the future — forever as far as they are concerned. Only a relatively insignificant 5% of all customer-enterprises are actively involved in projects to replace all their HP 3000s. The other 95% of the enterprises are extremely loyal to the HP 3000 both because of its reliability and ability to run the applications they need. These enterprises may be variously engaged in the processes of: 1) growing their HP 3000-based information infrastructure with additional HP 3000 applications; 2) supplementing their HP 3000 systems with other, complementary operating environments, such as Unix and Microsoft BackOffice, networked into their HP 3000s; or 3) replacing only a few specific HP 3000 applications that no longer fit with how their enterprise operates. As a result, HP can count on stable customer demand for HP 3000s for a long time into the future.

2. The HP 3000 is a very large and profitable segment of HP's business. Aberdeen estimates that the HP 3000 is a $1.2 billion annual business (making it larger than most technology suppliers in its own right) providing HP with an incremental gross margin of approximately $500–$600 million. Industry wags and competitors who suggest that HP will kill the HP 3000 obviously do not understand the corporate advantages of positive cash flow and profits.

3. The HP 9000 (the HP 3000's hardware twin but with a Unix operating system), professional service offerings, printers, PCs, and PC servers are all selling successfully into very rapid growth areas of the information systems marketplace and require much, much less selling effort and expense to capture new customers than the HP 3000 does. As a result, HP will invest its limited new-customer-capture marketing and sales budgets to where the returns are higher and more assured.

Key to the success of its Customer First strategy, the Commercial Systems Division will continue to enhance the HP 3000 by building upon its classic benefits — reliability, performance, price/performance and low operating costs — through the strengths of its integrated systems software operating environment and network database with its SQL interface, IMAGE/SQL. Just as importantly, it will partner with leading providers of networking, communications, data warehousing, customer service, imaging, internet-based electronic commerce, systems management and other application solutions to provide new customer-required functionality on the HP 3000 in a timely manner.

Aberdeen believes that HP's Customer First strategy is realistic and pragmatic. It ensures that HP will not mis-position the HP 3000 in the minds of IS decision makers and at the same time distinctly articulates the difference between the HP 3000, HP 9000 (Unix and RDBMS-focused) and HP NetServer (NT and NetWare-focused) within HP. But most importantly, it clearly re-states HP's long-term commitment to the HP 3000 while acknowledging and leveraging the realities of today's changing industry market dynamics and economics.

### HP 3000 Customer Loyalty and Success

Aberdeen's customer research continues to show that senior IS executives are loyal to their HP 3000s because of the benefits that these systems bring to their enterprises' business operations. They can count on their systems being available to users because of the high reliability of the HP 3000. Reliability — the characteristic that minimizes the number of painful calls to IS demanding to know when the system will be up and make sure the problem does not occur again — has been the key attribute of the HP 3000 that has generated an almost zealous loyalty among its users. Very importantly, as many HP 3000 customers establish global operations they are discovering that this reliability easily translates into the capability to provide 24x7 service to users anywhere in the world.

In addition, the performance provided by the HP 3000's integrated network database, IMAGE/SQL, and consequential excellent price/performance are highly desired by organizations trying to "do more with less" and keep IS budgets as low as possible.

Finally, the HP 3000 is regarded as one of the easiest-to-learn and easiest-to-use production multiuser systems available in the marketplace. HP 3000 value-added resellers (VARs) recount how they have taught non-computer personnel, such as staff librarians, to effectively run the systems.

Building upon these strengths, approximately 65% of the HP 3000 enterprise-customer base is adding new applications directly onto their HP 3000s — and new HP 3000s. An additional 30% are using their HP 3000s as business-critical, central production systems even while surrounding them with new, get-more-leverage applications running on new operating environments. As a result, 95% of the enterprise-customer base is relying on the HP 3000's capabilities and functionality to advance the effectiveness of their enterprise's total information infrastructure into the future. Only 5% of the HP 3000 customer-enterprises currently have projects in place to eventually replace all their current systems.

Aberdeen has also noted that with the new generation of more powerful HP 3000s introduced during the last two years, many HP 3000 shops are consolidating systems to lower operating costs. Because IS managers know they can depend upon the HP 3000's reliability to always be available to larger numbers of online users, they are not fearful of putting daily business processes at risk.

When Aberdeen asks users about limitations, they consistently answer that for running production online transaction processing (OLTP), decision support, and batch applications they can always find a product solution to meet their enterprise's ever expanding functional requirements. A handful of users have run into performance limitations — but that is a consistent result with Aberdeen research in the areas of mainframe (MVS/DB2) and Unix/RDBMS computing.

However, the same technology that makes a product successful can also make it vulnerable when users want to extend it to provide capabilities for which it was never intended. The paradox of the HP 3000 is that its users have been successful because of the functionality provided by the integrated systems software architecture and network database upon which it is built. Yet IS decision makers that want component-based systems with a portable RDBMS supplied by an independent software vendor will find that the HP 3000 was not designed to cost-effectively support their desired IS architecture.

### The Path To Customer First

The HP 3000 was the mainstay of HP's commercial computer offerings from its introduction in the mid-1970s until its annual revenues were surpassed by the Unix-based HP 9000 in 1993. HP has spent over five years reconciling within its own organization and with its prospect base the difference between the two midrange product offerings. The trade-off between the two has been that the HP 3000 offers the robust production systems functionality that cost-conscious professional IS executives need while the HP 9000 is the leading platform for running RDBMSs from ISVs (independent software vendors) such as Oracle, Informix, Sybase, and CA-Ingres.

Starting in 1993, the HP 3000 began to drift intellectually. What was it? What should it be? How should it be positioned? Was it open or proprietary? Was it superior or inferior to Unix-based systems — and for what applications? HP offered various messages to the marketplace such as the HP 3000 was for client-server computing, a back-end database for front-end Unix application systems, and/or the perfect platform for worry-free computing.

Finally, at the end of 1995 and after completing hundreds of customer interviews, HP recognized that the satisfaction levels within the HP 3000 customer base were so high based on business successes that users would continue to rely on it to run their businesses for decades to come, if HP would merely keep increasing its functionality in parallel with the state-of-computer science and provide solutions to meet their new, emerging information requirements.

The result was the implementation of the Customer First strategy. The highlights of the implementation of this strategy that are making it a reality are:

1. **Continuous customer feedback resulting in product development response**: The strategy absolutely requires that HP's product management team, the Commercial Systems Division, interview customers on a continuous basis to detect new systems requirements.

2. **Continuous customer feedback guiding ISV application solution partnering programs**: Customer requests for applications solutions — such as imaging, secure internet and intranet operations, database gateways, and client-server development environments — that could leverage their current production applications are consolidated by the Commercial Systems Division which then attempts to obtain a technology and marketing partnership agreement with the leading ISV suppliers in their field.

3. **Direct HP corporate customer dialogue**: To ensure that customers receive straightforward and timely product messages from HP directly about the HP 3000, HP's company headquarters provides information updates for customers. Call 1-800-224-HP3K in the U.S. for updates about upcoming events or to order the transcripts/tapes of previous sessions.

4. **Providing open interfaces and interoperability with other key operating environments ahead of customer demand**: A key issue with the HP 3000 has been to provide interoperability with other operating server environments such as IBM's MVS, everybody's Unix, and Microsoft's NT Server and Windows 3.1.

5. **Improving the HP 3000 operating environment to match best-in-class Unix and NT alternatives**: The development goals for the HP 3000 become very straightforward under the Customer First strategy — ensure that HP 3000 customers have equivalent or better production computing functionality with the HP 3000 than they could obtain from alternative commercial midrange systems.

### The HP 3000 Product Line — Current and Future

From a hardware perspective, the HP 3000 is essentially the same product lineup as the HP 9000. At times, the HP 3000 may include models not yet introduced within the HP 9000 line — other times it may be several months behind the HP 9000 offerings.

The personality of the HP 3000 is derived from the addition of the MPE/iX operating system, high performance IMAGE/SQL network database, the HP-developed ALLBASE/SQL relational database, systems and network management tools, and networking and communications software. Because these software components are all architected, developed, and tested as one integrated whole, the HP 3000 is able to maintain the high availability and reliability features for which it is so well known.

At this time, the HP 3000 line consists of over 30 models (many overlapping but kept in the product lineup to provide upgrade protection for the installed base), ranging from the low-end 918LX/RX designed for less than 64 users with a base price of $26,000 to the 8-way SMP 996/800 starting at $720,000 that can support up to 1,300 concurrent users. In addition, multiple HP 3000 systems can be clustered together using SharePlex/iX to support even larger numbers of users and add warm-standby backup capability.

System-to-system communications in a heterogeneous environment — one that will typically include Unix, NT, and/or IBM mainframe systems — is provided by:

- NFS, NCS, and ARPA/Internet (including BSD sockets) in a TCP/IP environment
- OSF/DCE (Open Software Foundations Distributed Computing Environment)
- LU6.2/PU2.1 in an SNA environment
- Networked printing integrated with print-spooling

In addition, the HP 3000 supports Novell NetWare, AppleTalk, and LAN Manager. LAN connectivity can be provided by FDDI, Ethernet, or Token Ring.

In the future, HP 3000 systems performance will be increased as the forthcoming 64-bit PA-8000 processors are introduced. Aberdeen projects that by the end of 1996 the current high-end 996 class systems and midrange 969 class systems will have almost twice the current performance at only a 10% price premium with the implementation of the PA-8000 processor. The low-end 9X8LX/RX systems should experience a 30%–35% performance improvement with no price change based upon the introduction of higher speed 32-bit PA-7200 processors. Looking even further into the future to the year 2000, Aberdeen projects that HP will both design the processors resulting from the HP-Intel 64-bit microprocessor architecture project into the HP 3000 and rewrite MPE/iX and its supporting systems software to take full advantage of this new chip design.

### Aberdeen Observations and Conclusions

The first rule of successful strategy implementation is that the strategy must accurately reflect market conditions and the enterprise's strengths and weaknesses. The HP 3000's Customer First strategy does that. It recognizes that only a few new customers a year will be acquiring HP 3000s — and those will be for unique and superior applications such as credit union, mail order distribution, or health care information management. But most importantly, the large and loyal installed base does not want to spend several years, millions of dollars, and suffer numerous setbacks merely to recreate its current production applications within a new computing environment.

The second rule of successful strategy implementation is that an enterprise's structure must be organized around a well-thought out and communicated strategy. HP has done that with the HP 3000's Customer First strategy. The Commercial Systems Division has become the focal point for direct customer communications. Most significantly, it has the authority, responsibility, accountability, and budgets for meeting HP 3000 customers' requirements for new systems software and third-party ISV alliances.

Customer First is an HP Company strategy, not just a Commercial Systems Division marketing phrase. HP's long-term objective is to be the trusted supplier to as many enterprises around the world as possible. The Customer First strategy straightforwardly expedites achieving this objective — it provides the long-term, multi-product strategic view of the HP-Customer relationship. It should also dispel any fears that HP is either about to discontinue the HP 3000 or plans to follow a starve-it-and-harvest-it strategy.

Finally, Aberdeen has always been impressed with the energy, pragmatism, and results-oriented business focus of the HP 3000 user base. The Customer First strategy should reaffirm the correctness of their decision for many years into the future.
