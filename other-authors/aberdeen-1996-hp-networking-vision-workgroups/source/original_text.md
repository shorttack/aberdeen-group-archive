## Original Document Text

An Executive White Paper | July 1996

### Executive Summary

A paradigm shift is taking place in the information technology world of small and mid-sized businesses. The enterprise model of a mainframe-centric, top-down designed, hierarchical architecture which worked with large Fortune 500 systems is not applicable to smaller companies that have grown their IT capabilities department by department. As the need increases for more people to have better access to information in less time, networks have mushroomed in markets that not long ago were satisfied with departmental or workgroup computing. One of the major challenges facing the small and mid-size business market today is how to offer solutions that enable companies to grow their networks in such a way that business applications, the IT infrastructure and the network manager can all play in unison.

Hewlett-Packard Company clearly understands the critical role workgroups play as essential building blocks for networks. The company has based its networking strategy and product design on the premise that workgroups grow up into enterprise networks. HP offers a full solution set including: best-in-class network connectivity products, PCs, printers, servers, scanners and network management, all tied together to form complete workgroup solutions.

Our goal in this paper is to determine the accuracy of HP's networking vision, to examine its networking solutions in action, and to chart the direction in which the market and customers expect HP to head. In order to accomplish this task, Aberdeen has conducted in-depth interviews with network managers in key vertical segments who have selected HP solutions over other alternatives.

Aberdeen believes the timing is right for HP's approach, based on buying trends that are emerging in the small to medium-sized business marketplace. Current and next-generation users will purchase networking solutions based on the critical role that network-ready workgroups play. End-user decisions are made with an eye to investing for future growth and change, while at the same time taking into consideration all the requirements of existing needs. HP's competitively-priced workgroup-oriented networking solutions, which incorporate standards-based interoperability with reliability, flexibility, manageability and ease-of-installation, give HP's solutions considerable clout in this market transition.

### Hewlett-Packard's Networking Vision

Hewlett-Packard has played a leading role in the growth of networking, beginning with its early development of technology for 10Base-T networks. Recognizing the importance of networking to its core business since those early days, the company has invested in continued expansion of its network offerings with solutions such as EtherTwist, AdvanceStack 10Base-T, 100VG and 100Base-T connectivity products, NICs, switches and network management. The reward for this investment has been a leadership position in certain segments of the market against established, powerful competition. In particular, Aberdeen believes HP to be a leader in 10Base-T stackable hubs, high-speed hubs and NICs, as well as network printers, print servers, scanners, mobile and desktop PCs and OpenView. HP's commitment to network management, as measured by the market success of HP OpenView Windows and Unix, is backed by exemplary service and support.

Over the years, HP has also been a leader in networking standards development, including 10Base-T wiring standards. HP personnel have participated in various IEEE standards and institutions such as IETF, WINForum, the 100VG and Fiber Channel Initiatives and the ATM Forum. HP is also participating in the Gigabit Alliance.

### Workgroup Connectivity — The Underlying Building Block

HP has based its networking practice upon the vision of becoming the worldwide leader in providing interconnected workgroup connectivity solutions. We consider the company's recent repositioning and renaming of the Networks Division, now called the Workgroup Networks Division (WND), to be an indication of commitment to this approach.

HP's strategy embraces the fact that networks evolve dynamically from workgroups and actual user applications. The company's vision is to provide managed, flexible and reliable interconnected workgroup solutions that will enable users to link a variety of computing and communications devices to create a corporate-wide solution. WND has targeted its customers as small to mid-sized organizations that could most benefit from the intrinsic value of products designed with workgroups in mind.

### Fitting the Vision to the Customer

Aberdeen research confirms that HP's networking vision firmly taps into the realities that exist in the marketplace. We have found that LANs have tended to grow haphazardly, and that networks have indeed evolved from the bottom up. Workgroup networks share the following common characteristics inside user organizations:

**Home-Grown**: Because resources are limited and funds are often not available for outside consultants, networks that evolve from workgroups are often designed in-house. It is not unusual to have both wiring design and the actual "wire pulling" done by in-house staff and the maintenance department.

**Lack of Pre-Planning**: The workgroups that form these networks have been constructed department by department, very often autonomously and with little, if any, coordination.

**Mixed Environments**: Because these networks have grown out of elements that were not pre-planned, they contain a technology hodgepodge which now must communicate and share resources as smoothly as possible.

**Leveraging and Expansion Capabilities**: Users in this environment want to be able to leverage their IT investment to the maximum. They need room for growth and physical flexibility because of continually changing needs. They also want technology that can provide easy migration paths to more advanced, sophisticated technologies without jeopardizing legacy investments.

**Group Decision-Making**: Because they have grown from the ground up rather than from some centralized source, workgroup networks are often augmented and modified by group consensus and collaborative decision-making rather than by the directive of one individual with in-depth IT expertise.

**Guerrilla Networking**: Funding for IT spending has come under increasing scrutiny as companies experience the financial constraints of the mid-1990s. Both IT budgets and personnel have been cut as corporate resources undergo reallocation. The managers of these networks are, in the words of one interviewee, "rebellious spirits" who practice what they refer to as "guerrilla" networking. Usually in charge of a small group of dedicated IT personnel who wear many hats, they are creative problem solvers who attack company network needs by optimizing every ounce of usefulness in their networking products.

IT requirements of these network managers include: low maintenance, standardized equipment, easy installation, reliability, and good network security. They demand sensible, practical and effective transition technologies for dealing with increased bandwidth requirements. These technologies must provide reasonable network life — generally considered by IS managers to be five years — and must be able to be shared and leveraged.

### Enhanced Network Performance Required

One of the major driving forces for network change has been the persistent demand for faster network performance. Today's most widely installed LAN technology is 10Base-T, which has an optimal performance speed of 10 Mbps. This speed is often too slow to enable the use of multimedia applications such as videoconferencing, and does not permit easy access to and transfer of increasingly bulky files.

Two industry responses to the demand for increased network performance have been 100VG and 100Base-T. 100VG is attractive because it is scalable, operating at both 10 Mbps and 100 Mbps, in shared and switched configurations. 100Base-T is another standard that is suited for switched environments. HP supports both standards.

### Testing the Vision

Aberdeen conducted in-depth interviews with network managers in several verticals: education, health care, government and manufacturing.

The networks studied range in size from less than fifty nodes to in excess of 1,000. They are heterogeneous environments, mixing 100VG and 10Base-T, and in one instance, 10Base-T, 100VG and FDDI. These networks qualify as multi-vendor solution environments, combining a mix of HP hubs, routers, workstations, servers, file servers, desktop computers of every kind, printers and scanners.

### Business Factors

Given multiple vendor promises and the accompanying industry chaos and confusion, today's corporations are rapidly gravitating toward "one-stop" solutions which address end-to-end needs. From a business perspective, corporate decision-makers are looking for networks that contribute to a company's competitiveness and viability. Networks must be able to deliver both on today's existing business applications and the applications that will be essential for future corporate success.

Aberdeen research reveals that in today's business environment, networks must be able to do the business job — that is, they must contribute to the profitability and productivity of the company and its employees. The justification for IT spending is directly tied to the impact that the information technology component has on the corporate bottom-line.
