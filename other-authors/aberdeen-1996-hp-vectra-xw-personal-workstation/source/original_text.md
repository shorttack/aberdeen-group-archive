**August 26, 1996** — When the market's leading Unix workstation/server provider announces a new Windows NT desktop aimed at the technical computing marketplace — its traditional Unix workstation stronghold — it's time to sit up and take notice! Hewlett-Packard (HP) has listened to its customers' requests for low-cost, NT-based workstations capable of running both technical and personal productivity applications, and has responded in kind with its Vectra XW "personal workstation" — a Windows NT Workstation/Intel-based PC architecture that has been designed to run complex and compute-intensive technical applications.

And this announcement is significant because, unlike HP competitors Sun Microsystems and Silicon Graphics, who have taken a Unix-only position in technical computing, HP is making a strategic commitment to NT-based technical computing. With NT-based personal workstations as well as its traditional Unix/RISC-based workstations and servers, HP is offering its customers the freedom-to-choose the operating environment and systems platform that best suits their individual performance and budgetary requirements. And, very importantly, HP has committed itself to being the only major workstation supplier to assist its customers in deploying and managing a mixed NT-Unix technical computing environment.

In this Profile, Aberdeen takes a close look at Hewlett-Packard's new Vectra XW personal workstation — analyzing HP's Unix/NT market and product positioning; the Vectra core products; HP's technical software solution supplier relationships — plus the HP/Microsoft relationship; and HP's distribution channels for moving large volumes of personal workstations to market. And Aberdeen concludes that, with the Vectra XW, HP has managed to build the powerful Intel-based platform that its customers have long desired for NT-based technical computing.

### Product Positioning

To properly establish customer expectations, Hewlett-Packard has carefully managed the positioning of its Vectra XU high-end PCs, Vectra XW personal workstations, and VISUALIZE line of Unix-based workstations. In a nutshell, although these systems can use the same basic technical applications code and database formats, and can be managed using the same systems/network management tools (OpenView) — the level of systems performance is the primary differentiator between the Windows/Intel-based (Wintel) personal computer/personal workstation platforms and HP's Unix/RISC platforms — with Unix/RISC generally offering greater overall system performance and high-end processor scalability.

Initial HP internal testing clearly shows that HP VISUALIZE Unix workstations outperform their NT Vectra cousins in compute- and graphics-intensive technical applications, such as power design of large sub-assemblies, assembly modeling, computational fluid dynamics, finite element analysis, and crash and stress analysis. These applications are compute intensive and often involve complex screen manipulation such as rotation, subject spinning, and shading. In other words, VISUALIZE Unix workstations outperform Vectra personal workstations in situations that require the utmost in computing power.

On the other hand, the Vectra XW was shown to perform well in component design and in the design of small sub-assemblies. The rule of thumb — if the user requires less graphics manipulation (fewer spins, less shading, etc.), or compute processing (design simulations, stress analysis, and the like), and also requires access to personal productivity tools that run within a Microsoft Windows NT operating environment such as Microsoft Office, or Lotus, or the like, then a Vectra XW personal workstation becomes a viable desktop choice.

Aberdeen notes that low-end Unix workstations are not immune to a challenge from the Vectra personal workstation series — the Vectra XW poses a legitimate price/performance threat to low-end Unix workstations used for software development and electronic computer aided design. Using Texas Instrument's Bench'95 Assembly Benchmarks — a suite of 59 operations (based upon Parametric Technology Corporation's Pro/Engineer application: Release 16, Build 9620) that test CPU interaction, wire frame, and shading characteristics — HP has been able to show that an HP Vectra XW equipped with 192 MB of random-access memory (RAM) outperformed Silicon Graphics Indigo 2 workstation with 256 MB of RAM — at almost one-third the relative cost (the Indigo 2 rated 133 Price*Bench95 versus Vectra XW rating of 39 Price*Bench95).

And, when compared to other personal workstations, such as the Calisto by NeTpower, the Vectra XW was slightly faster but significantly less expensive — by a margin of almost 30 percent.

HP positions the Vectra XW personal workstation as ideal for 3D technical applications and low-end to mid-size assembly type applications. When more computing power is required, HP advises its customers to evaluate its VISUALIZE Unix workstation series.

### Core Products

Vectra is the series name for Hewlett-Packard's desktop Wintel line that consists of commercial (V-series) and high performance (X-series) PCs and now, Personal Workstations. NetServer is HP's Intel-based server line. And, VISUALIZE is the series name for HP's Unix workstation line — the next logical step for increased desktop computing power beyond the Vectra line. The Vectra XW, the focus of this article, is the model name for Hewlett Packard's newly introduced "personal workstation".

*(Source: Aberdeen Group, 1996)*

HP's performance personal computer line starts with the Vectra XM, HP's commercial PC entry point, a Wintel-based processor with Intel CPU options ranging from 120 MHz to 200 MHz — and priced starting at $1,739. The next step up in the Vectra line is the Vectra XA, a high performance personal computer with a Pentium Pro CPU basis with clock speeds of 180 MHz and 200 MHz — prices starting at $3,000. The Vectra XA is positioned as a powerful PC, but is capable of running two-dimensional (2D) technical applications. The Vectra XU is a Pentium Pro-based personal computer, with processor speeds of up to 200 MHz — priced in the $5,000 range. The Vectra XU manifests the power and graphics capabilities that enable it to compete in the two-dimensional/three dimensional (2D/3D) or 2D+ technical computing markets.

The new personal workstation, the Vectra XW, is a step up the power and graphics ladder from other Vectra models. The XW can be configured with single or dual 200 MHz Pentium Pro CPUs and with memory increments ranging from 64 MB expandable to 512 MB of ECC RAM to reduce constant memory swapping. HP offers integrated cache of 256Kb or 512Kb — useful for applications that need increased floating point calculation capability. Note that the dual Pentium Pro is limited to 256Kb of cache memory for the base model, but single-processor models with 512 Kb of cache have the option of adding an additional 512 Kb. Standard storage ranges from 2GB on single CPU models to 4GB on the dual. For fast input/output, the Vectra XW includes an ultra-fast SCSI-20 interconnect as well as standards-based 10BT/100BT Local Area Network (LAN) capabilities. For sound, the Vectra XW comes equipped with an integrated sound system from Creative Labs — Sound Blaster compatible. For expandability, the XW chassis features 7 bays and 6 slots — 3 PCI, 2 ISA, and 1 PCI/ISA. And list prices (including a 17" monitor) range from $9,100 for single CPU systems to $11,800 for dual CPU models. Personal workstations come preconfigured with Windows NT Workstation revision 3.51 and Netscape's Navigator revision 2.0 — with free upgrades to the next revision of each product when available.

For technical graphics processing, the Vectra XW features AccelPRO's Open GL-based TX 2500 3D Graphics Accelerator. The TX 2500 is based on 3D Labs GLINT silicon, with a Delta processor performing graphics setup. From a memory perspective, the TX 2500 features an 8 MB frame buffer and 8 MB DRAM for texture memory with a 24-bit Z-buffer. In addition to supporting the Open GL application program interface (API), the TX 2500 also supports the Heidi API for programmatic interaction with Autodesk applications.

Also worthy of note, Information Systems (IS) managers have often expressed dismay regarding the complexity of managing mixed environments. HP, with its OpenView product set, can manage both a Unix and NT environment in a transparent fashion.

### Technical Software Solutions Providers

HP has focused its personal workstation efforts on structuring software-solution relationships with suppliers in the Mechanical Design Automation (MDA), Electronic Design Automation (EDA) and Geographical Information Systems (GIS) markets.

The major software suppliers in each market, with few exceptions, have already ported their product offerings to Windows NT — but ported product does not a solution make. HP needs to structure marketing and support relationships with its supplier partners, perform quality assurance to ensure that the ported software works efficiently on the Vectra XW personal workstation platform, and, in cooperation with its solutions partner, tune the personal workstation to maximize application performance.

But, HP has an advantage over many other suppliers of personal workstations. HP has already established relationships with the leading suppliers of technical solutions — HP already sells the leading-edge technical solutions on its Unix workstation line. Knowing the supplier's organization, business requirements and capabilities, and applications should give HP a time-to-market advantage as it looks to expand its technical solutions portfolio.

Presently, HP has established personal workstation solution supplier relationships with Parametric Technology Corporation, SDRC, EDS/Unigraphics, Computervision, Dassault, MacNeal-Schwendler, Ansys, HP MDD and AutoDesk in the Mechanical Design Automation marketplace. In Electronic Design Automation, HP will market solutions from Mentor Graphics, Synopsys, Cadence, and Viewlogic. And in GIS, HP has a long-standing relationship with ESRI, the GIS software solutions leader.

### The HP/Microsoft Relationship

At first glance, Hewlett-Packard and Microsoft make strange bedfellows. NT and HP/UX are both operating environments — and both products can be deployed on desktops as well as workgroup and departmental servers. Both companies, in essence, are competing with each other for much of the same business.

But, at a higher level, Microsoft is a software company — with interests in the ongoing sales of its software products primarily through indirect channels. Hewlett-Packard is a computer maker, a platform software provider (OpenView, Praesidium, et al), and a systems integrator with systems design, deployment, service and support. Although HP participates as a contributor to the development of various Microsoft technologies, HP is more likely regarded by Microsoft as a channel outlet for various Microsoft products. And, given HP's success in the Wintel market, HP is an extremely important customer for Microsoft.

### Channels

Hewlett-Packard has already successfully established direct and indirect channels of distribution — as evidenced by its overwhelming success in the Unix marketplace with 50% of sales being driven through its direct sales force, while 50% is driven though indirect distributors and Value-added Resellers (VARs). Further, HP has established the channels necessary for the distribution of PC architectures and related products — as evidenced by 100% of its PC sales going through indirect channels of distribution.

HP plans to sell the Vectra XW using both its direct sales organization (targeted primarily at enterprise-wide sales) and its indirect channels (targeted at large, medium, and small enterprises). From a direct sales perspective, HP will use its own auspices to generate demand for the Vectra XW personal workstation. HP will use its systems integration organizations to provide pre- and post-sales support as well as consulting and NT/Unix integration services. And HP Customer Account Representatives (CARs) and the HP enterprise sales force will do the missionary work of carrying the message to HP large enterprise accounts. From an indirect channels perspective, HP solutions marketing, Independent Software Vendor (ISV) programs, and VAR programs will be responsible for lead generation and fulfillment. To prevent channel conflict, all XW product fulfillment will be done through resellers.

With the distributor and VAR relationships already in place, the primary task ahead of HP as it attempts to establish the Vectra XW personal workstation in technical markets is one of education. Resellers will need to understand not only how to sell traditional, PC commercial and personal productivity solutions, but also technical solutions in a variety of technical markets. Many HP channel partners are already technical-market savvy; but others will need training and support as they learn how to sell technical applications along with their bread-and-butter, value-added client/server solutions.

### Summary Observations

With the Vectra XW personal workstation, Hewlett-Packard has shown its customers that it does not plan to resist the logical progression of Windows NT into the technical computing marketplace — and, in fact, quite the opposite has taken place. HP has recognized the vast sales revenue potential offered by NT-based, Intel platforms and has implemented a strategy to actively pursue the Wintel market.

The Vectra XW offers HP customers the best-of-both-worlds — a platform that can run the PC applications and productivity tools required to get day-to-day business tasks done combined with the ability to run creative, high-performance technical applications — and all in an affordable price range. To Aberdeen, this is refreshing news, given the resistance of other major Unix suppliers such as Sun Microsystems and Silicon Graphics to offering their customers lower-cost-of-ownership Windows-based, Intel architectures instead of Unix/RISC-only solutions.

HP has been careful to properly set customer expectations for the Vectra XW personal workstation. Although the XW is both powerful and flexible from an applications point of view, there are still technical computing scenarios that optimally require Unix workstations.

Information Technology (IT) decision makers should not overlook the importance of HP's Professional Services Organization (PSO) in this equation. For many IS organizations, NT is a new entity, and professional deployment expertise will be required for systems configuration and management. Hewlett-Packard is one of the few organizations committed to the deployment and support of mixed NT and Unix environments.

Hewlett-Packard has established a strong reputation as a market leader in Unix technical desktops and servers, enjoys some of the highest customer satisfaction ratings in the industry. Further, HP has the necessary technical solutions supplier relationships in place — HP has been doing business with these suppliers for years on the Unix desktop. And finally, HP has an experienced support organization in place to deploy technical solutions and platforms. All of these factors bode well for the future success of HP's Vectra XW personal workstation.
