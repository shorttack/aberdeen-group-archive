### Preface

IBM is a survivor and a builder. Over its long history, IBM has shown that rather than being wedded to a specific technology, it focuses on meeting the expanding needs of its key customer base — information technology executives within Global 500 enterprises. However, rapid industry and business changes have forced both IBM and its key customers to the precarious position of redefining IBM — what services and products it should deliver and how. In this Strategy Profile, Aberdeen describes IBM's transformation to being a service-focused supplier.

### Executive Overview

When Lou Gerstner, IBM's chairman, joined the company in 1993, he quickly began investing in IBM's services areas to profitably satisfy what he rightly saw as unmet customer needs. While many traditional IBM executives viewed services as merely a means to selling hardware and maintaining account control, or as an ancillary offering that was included in the price of expensive proprietary computer systems, the new management team saw services as both a revenue generating and growth opportunity.

To set the stage for launching its services-based strategy, the company established IBM Global Services as the umbrella delivery organization, invested in the people and technologies required to deliver complex, multi-tier information systems, lowered proprietary systems pricing but charged for services that were once free, and re-invented IBM's corporate culture to be outwardly and customer-solutions focused. Initial results in 1994 were encouraging and in 1995 very positive.

But to make this strategy really work, IBM needed a unifying vision of how and what the next-generation information world should be. The resulting vision is IBM's commitment to be the Network Computing supplier-of-choice for enterprises of any size throughout the world. IBM believes this is a once-in-a-generation opportunity to lead its customers in changing their methods of operating. Network Computing — using today's open, network-centric information technologies to connect individuals-to-individuals and individuals-to-organizations — will change IBM's customers' internal business processes as well as communications methods by its customers with their customers, their suppliers, government regulatory agencies, and any other participants within an extended enterprise.

IBM's transformation to becoming a service-driven supplier is paying off. IBM's 1995 revenues of $72 billion are the highest in its history — almost $8 billion more than in 1994. And its 1995 profit of $4.2 billion was the best in recent memory.

### Strategic Objectives and Supporting Processes

IBM's primary strategic objective is to be the first source its installed base customers look to for their next-generation information solutions. Its secondary objective is to create new customers by automating the enterprises and training the emerging professional IS staffs of China, the former Soviet Union, and other countries now entering the modern and global information age.

The strategic principal IBM is employing to achieve these objectives is to be the "Just Say Yes Company" to customer and prospect requests for information-service solutions while creating new levels of demand by establishing a common vision among enterprise leaders of universal connectivity — Network Computing.

### Global Services

The "Just Say Yes" arm of IBM is Global Services. The Global Services organization, which had 1995 revenues of **$20.1 billion** and a backlog of **$29 billion**, now has **100,000 employees** — on-track to have 110,000 by the end of 1996 — out of IBM's 225,000. While $7.4 billion of this revenue was obtained for maintenance services, IBM proudly points to the $12.7 billion in professional fees obtained from systems integration, application customization, outsourcing, consulting, global networking services, and education. This customer-intimate portion of the Global Services business has been growing on average **30% per year for the last 4 years** — compared to the essentially no-growth maintenance portion of the business.

Key to Global Services' success is meeting customer requirements — whatever they might be and however challenging. This includes not only providing new services, such as enabling mainframes to publish data on the Internet, but also implementing application-solutions based on rivals' products, such as Hewlett-Packard's UNIX servers or Microsoft's BackOffice.

An excellent example of the new challenges Global Services is undertaking is its efforts with car manufacturer Mercedes-Benz. IBM not only has field personnel but also headquarters R&D staff assigned to working with Mercedes-Benz to network all the processors in the next-generation Mercedes-Benz automobile together while designing the car-system to function as a node in a LAN (local area network) for maintenance diagnostics.

### One Face To the Customer

IBM wants customers to see only one unified IBM. This unified IBM will propose the best possible information-service solutions that it can replicate from previous engagements to meet customer needs — and the hardware, software, communications and other technology components will merely be derivatives of the appropriate services-based recommendation. This is a change from the past when the different competing hardware divisions (S/390, AS/400, RS/6000, and PC server) would publicly fight with each other in front of customers.

### Strategic Acquisitions

To provide Global Services with critical technology tools to create replicable information-service solutions, IBM will pull out its checkbook and outbid others to acquire key companies, their technologies, and, most importantly, their people.

To gain a functional and technology advantage in Network Computing, it acquired **Lotus** for its collaborative groupware product, Notes. Knowing that in the typical enterprise 20% of the personnel could benefit from Notes, IBM paid what many considered to be a premium for a company whose flagship product, 1-2-3, was quickly losing market relevance. But IBM was able to build on the value of Notes (selling more licenses for it in the first 6 months of ownership than Lotus had in the previous 6 years) by adding it to its information-services solutions. And with the recently released internet-enabled version, Domino Notes, IBM anticipates increasing even further its customer satisfaction levels.

The relatively recent acquisition of **Tivoli** was a requirement for IBM to be able to provide network management services in today's open computing environment.

### Strategic and Relative Advantages

IBM has numerous advantages to be successful in implementing its information-services solution strategy:

1. **Most complete IT product line**: IBM develops and sells the most complete line of information technology products of any company in the world. Very importantly, 25% of IBM's R&D staff is required each year to work with customers through Global Services so there is a direct feedback loop between what customers want and what the scientists are developing.

2. **Closest to truly global organization**: There is a difference between having individual fiefdoms in over 100 countries and having a unified industry information solutions group that treats a global customer's operations in different countries as part of one large, unified information technology implementation project.

3. **Hardened employees**: IBM's current employees have been hardened by the lay-offs and financial hard times of the early 1990s. They understand they will have to prove that they are better in every area compared to the competition.

4. **Installed in every Global 500 datacenter**: IBM knows its customers — their current information infrastructures, individual professional IS staff members, and key business objectives and processes.

### Structural Shortcomings and Vulnerabilities

The most significant downside of the information-service solution strategy is that IBM is losing its focus on delivering best-in-class point products. One example is hardware performance — competitive hardware suppliers are offering better performance and price/performance across the entire spectrum of IBM's product offerings. Another example is innovative database functionality — the DB2 family of RDBMSs would generally be considered a follower in terms of functionality and performance compared to alternative RDBMS engines supplied by leading independent suppliers.

The second concern is that IBM often prizes technology component integration over what end users see as key requirements. A well publicized example here is **OS/2**. OS/2 is an excellent operating environment for Global Services to use on the desktop to implement and manage custom, enterprise-wide, integrated, multi-tier client-server applications — but it does not have the level of independent software vendor support, such as the ability to run Microsoft's Office 95, that most end users demand.

A third vulnerability is that IBM could lose the support of key technology-based business partners, such as independent professional-service providers and ISVs (independent software vendors). As the independent professional-service providers see Global Services competing against them for systems integration, outsourcing, application implementation, and business consulting, they will be motivated to downplay IBM's offerings in recommendations to their clients. And the ISVs, such as Computer Associates, Oracle, Microsoft, SAP, Netscape, and numerous others, will continue to view IBM as a potential competitor and untrustworthy ally.

### Competitive Environment

IBM's new primary competitors are former professional service organization customers and allies: **EDS, Andersen Consulting, Cap Gemini, Computer Sciences Corporation, Deloitte & Touche, Ernst & Young, Price Waterhouse, SHL System House, and Perot Systems**. Or, since these firms rely so extensively on IBM technology, maybe they should be classified as co-opetitors — that special combination of customer, cooperative partner, and competitor.

IBM executives frequently refer to **Microsoft** as their chief competitor. Microsoft's siren song to user organizations: "You can do anything with Microsoft products," undercuts the combined hardware, software, and services messages of IBM.

### Financial Highlights

For calendar and fiscal 1995, IBM reported revenues of **$71.9 billion** and net earnings of **$4.2 billion**. This was a 12.1% revenue increase and 38.3% earnings increase from the previous year. For the first 6 months of 1996, revenues are up 4.4% over 1995 and net earnings down 14.9%. But highly significantly, hardware revenues declined 0.6% during this latest 6-month period while service revenues (not including maintenance) increased 26.4%.

Aberdeen estimates that without the increased emphasis on services during the previous two years, IBM's hardware revenue decline would have been closer to 12% for this period. In other words, Global Services has already become a major pull-through factor for IBM's hardware group.

### Aberdeen Observations and Conclusions

**The Year 2000 Problem Is Real — and a Global Services Opportunity**

Aberdeen still sees a major disconnect between what the majority of IS professionals believe will be the very nasty consequences of not dealing with the Year 2000 issues as soon as possible and what the overwhelming majority of senior enterprise managers, especially in the government sector, are willing to invest to fix the real — not potential — problem.

Aberdeen believes that it is highly probable that the majority of senior enterprise managers will understand the severity of this problem at the same time early in 1997. The result will be enterprises initiating crash buying sprees starting in mid-1997. Professional service providers of Year 2000 solutions are already planning to increase their fees in 1997, 1998, and 1999 as the lack of qualified personnel becomes even more acute. Following the information-services solutions strategy, IBM will find itself ideally positioned to take advantage of the Year 2000 quagmire.

**Real Commitment To Information-Service Solutions Is Key**

IBM has taken the actions — "walked the walk, not just talked the talk" — over the last two years to successfully execute the new strategy. The combination of a major field-force structural reorganization, aggressive reduction in hardware prices, significant increase in new personnel, and a major cultural metamorphosis have all been high-risk business policy changes that were required to make the transformation — and all that IBM has successfully instituted.

IBM is being reconstituted as a nimble, customer-responsive supplier of high-technology services. 45% of its employees are employed in Global Services and this is the fastest growing portion of the company. 25% of IBM's 3,000 R&D personnel are assigned to working directly with customers to co-invent new solutions.

**Conclusions**

IBM is being recreated — and for the better. It has survived a very trying period in the late 1980s and early 1990s. As an information-services solution company it can better meet the larger needs of the entire enterprise, no matter what the customer's, or prospect's, technology and implementation requirements may be.

With the large potential demand for new application functionality to make enterprises more competitive and Year 2000 assistance merely to ensure that they will be able to operate on 1/1/2000, IBM's future indeed looks very bright. And IBM's unique combination of technology development and global service delivery capability will minimize head-to-head competitive attacks for a long time.

IBM is the largest business phoenix — rising and soaring from near disaster — we may ever have the pleasure to observe.
