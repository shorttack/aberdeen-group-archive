IQ Software: Applying the WWW To Reporting Problems
Preface
Sooner or later the enthusiasm for the World Wide Web’s (WWW) ability to support Internet and Intranet decision-support applications will give way to sobering realizations that the power
of the WWW is directly related to the foundational technologies on which it resides and with which it interacts.
The goal of linking business users to information contained within corporate data stores, including personal databases, is driven by a need to automate as many decision-support chores as
possible and to broaden and deepen knowledge worker explorations. These same business imperatives — as well as the need to trim infrastructure expenses and IS labor — have encouraged
enterprises to seek both content and functionality in cyberspace. Although WWW-enabled information-driven computing will take on many imaginative forms, Aberdeen believes that it
represents the natural evolution toward dynamic enterprise-class on-demand information access and reporting.
This Proﬁle examines IQ Software Corporation’s IQ/LiveWeb, which leverages the Internet backbone with the company’s unique amalgam of object-oriented information-access
technologies, its proven three-tier architecture, and the hyperlinking and intelligent navigation of its new IQ/CyberLinks. Aberdeen believes that IQ/LiveWeb will invest cyberspace, and the
Internet and Intranet applications it can support, with real down-to-earth production-reporting utility and on-demand information access.
Executive Summary
IS decision makers are now looking for technologies that are equal to the growing demand among business users for information access. Not only must IS ﬁnd ways to give business users
access to information wherever it resides, but that information must be delivered throughout the enterprise in forms that are appealing to different classes of users.
With the April 16 introduction of IQ/LiveWeb, IQ Software continues to leverage its object-oriented innovations and aim them squarely at these pressing enterprise problems. Enterprises are
now turning to the WWW because it promises the dramatic reduction of many desktop software and deployment expenses. For example, application development on the WWW, although still
relatively primitive, builds on some fairly simple page creation facilities that allow developers to quickly mimic the look and feel (and a considerable portion of the power) of desktop tools.
Moreover, replacing the so-called fat-client architectures with more rational three-tier architectures will eliminate the demand for hefty PCs and the associated strain on network resources.
The WWW also offers an equally strong mechanism for diminishing the expense of disseminating software to clients, including predeﬁned queries.
Simultaneously tapping and elevating the power of the WWW, IQ has delivered a powerful three-tier architecture that will help enterprise users combine business information now residing in
corporate and personal databases, as well as on the vast and growing network of Internet computers. IQ/LiveWeb solves the problems associated with providing easy access to corporate
databases and will prove particularly useful for two types of publishing and distribution:
Scheduled reporting, for production reporting and other repetitive query users; and
On-demand printing of end-user queries for time sensitive and non-standard information.
IQ/LiveWeb costs from $3,995 on Microsoft Windows NT and low-end Unix platforms. High-end Unix servers — including HP/UX, Sun Solaris, and IBM’s AIX — cost $19,995.
Given its object-oriented power, IQ/LiveWeb’s competitive pricing and technology leveraging three-tier architecture, Aberdeen believes that IQ/LiveWeb should be high on the short list of
every enterprise seeking WWW-enabled enterprise-class reporting and information access. Moreover, IQ Software’s 1996 IQ/LiveWeb enhancement schedule, Aberdeen believes, will
continue to solidify IQ/LiveWeb’s position as a pace-setting information-access technology.
IQ/LiveWeb Solution
With IQ/LiveWeb, IQ Software has “objectized” the WWW and taken a signiﬁcant step in advancing the company’s charter of enabling enterprises to quickly create and, over time, collect
the corporation’s best-of-breed mission-critical queries and reports.
IQ/LiveWeb combines several IQ technologies with Internet browsers, and builds a state-of-the-art architecture (see Figure 1) equal to the growing enterprise demand for building and
handling large and complex documents. Moreover, IQ/LiveWeb lays open the contents of these documents to various enterprise requirements, including:
Business users, often technology novices, needing to build simple ad hoc queries and reports or wanting access to "canned" reports;
Business analysts requiring sophisticated queries that may involve reports with several related queries; and
IS developers and "power users" building production-grade reports with complex access, manipulation and presentation.

Each IQ/LiveWeb component plays a singular yet synergistic role. While IQ/Objects helps users create initial and follow-on queries and build powerful reports, the IQ/SmartServer can
automatically schedule and execute jobs. IQ/SmartServer also publishes reports using Hypertext Markup Language (HTML) to an Internet Web server, where they are accessible on-demand.
This up-to-date information is then readily available to users of IQ/CyberLinks navigation and hyperlinking software.
IQ Software has leveraged its object-oriented innovations and has delivered new possibilities in information access and dissemination that will allow WWW-enabled enterprises to:
Navigate among and post queries against resources as diverse as personal, corporate, and Internet databases;
Schedule query executions on a variety of servers, including Unix and NT;
Publish results in object format on Web servers;
Combine all or part of the objects/reports into new documents;
Navigate among reports using IQ’s new IQ/CyberLinks; and
Eliminate the need to either print or E-mail their decision-support results.
IQ/LiveWeb supports data access via standard open database connectivity (ODBC) to relational database management systems (RDBMSs), including CA-OpenIngres, the IBM DB2 family,
INFORMIX-OnLine Dynamic Server, Oracle and Oracle Parallel Server, Progress, Red Brick Systems, and Sybase and Sybase IQ; and indirect access through gateways to Unisys DMS II
and DMS 2200 databases. IQ/Objects also supports access to numerous desktop and workgroup databases, including Microsoft SQL Server and Access.
The Power of Objects
While many suppliers have demonstrated excellence in using forms of object orientation to mask the complexities of SQL, they have yet to harness the power of object-oriented software
where it counts the most: in the business-user query process.
With reusable IQ/Objects building blocks, IQ/LiveWeb has successfully delivered true ease of use to all end users, whether they are novices or experts. IQ Software’s SmartObjects share
three characteristics with other implementations of object-oriented technology: inheritance, or the ability for one object to pass on its attributes to a second object; polymorphism, the ability
of two or more objects to respond differently to the same command; and encapsulation, the ability to combine data and logic into a single entity or object.
Since IQ/LiveWeb builds on the strength of IQ/Objects, it offers numerous other information-access enhancements, including the ability to:
Generate multiple queries in a single request, communicate between objects and thereby share data among them and consolidate their output in a single report;
Share a query response with another SmartObject, or enable a query to drive another query, a process known as parent/child reporting;
Turn an object on or off, depending on speciﬁc conditions, thus allowing the objects to be used for highlighting exceptions, producing alerts, or conditional printing; and
Use a variety of formats, including charts and graphs, multidimensional crosstabs and free form.
Setting Up IQ/LiveWeb
To tap the power of IQ/LiveWeb requires little more than following a series of automated procedures, each dedicated to pointing the system at the appropriate sources and bringing
IQ/LiveWeb components into alignment.
Rather than working with complex programming languages to prepare the systems for querying, IS professionals interact with the Knowledge Base Manager, an object repository that is part
of an administration facility, which is used to conﬁgure and maintain access to multiple databases and the Internet. IQ/LiveWeb administrators deﬁne the Knowledge Base, preparing the
system for users and for collecting mission-critical queries. During the process, the system essentially walks the developer through the data sources necessary for deﬁning the semantic layer.
The system itself automatically imports metadata, or data about data; and automatically re-imports the metadata if changes are made to the database schema. With the help of the automated
administration tool, the administrator can transform, say, tables and columns into meaningful business terms, and, moreover, create business views speciﬁc to a given job function. While the
system itself automatically conﬁgures the joins between different tables, the administrator can override this feature to accommodate special circumstances. For example, where multiple join
paths exist, an administrator can select the optimum path. Aberdeen believes that IQ/Objects therefore sets the foundation for building mission-critical queries; its object inheritance features,
for example, will eliminate considerable IS programming and future support requirements.
Adding Web-Based Publishing
Additional steps include installing IQ/SmartServer on either Microsoft Windows NT or a Unix server, pointing it to the appropriate databases, and deciding where document output should be
placed on the Web content server. The Web server maintains the reports scheduled to run (and those that have already run) within speciﬁc directories, and additional software to help it work
in conjunction with IQ/SmartServer, which contains the database content and the Object Repository.
The additional software is responsible for automatically creating the Web-enabling functions, including the scripting for the Common Gateway Interface (CGI), which is a standard
mechanism for Web servers to communicate with applications behind it. The capture-and-prompt software is responsible for interacting between the two servers, running reports according to
speciﬁcation, and for library management.
In Phase II of IQ/LiveWeb, which is slated for delivery in the third quarter of this year, IQ Software will augment its robust reporting facilities by transforming IQ/Objects into a plug-in add-
on for Internet browsers. By facilitating native IQ/Objects in the browser, IQ users will be able to bypass the relatively primitive HTML interface (which, for example, works poorly with
Microsoft Object Linking and Embedding) and thereby utilize the full functions of IQ/Objects on the Web.


Bridging The Desktop With Cyberspace
But IQ/LiveWeb combined with IQ/CyberLinks already delivers functionality not available in most other WWW-enabled technologies. Users keen on discovering the performance of a given
product can, for example, review sales by region and product line, then automatically launch a query to investigate the performance of a given salesperson — even if any of the required
information resides in a different database, whether within the enterprise or on the Internet. Moreover, users can place these results in perspective by linking to, say, research documents or
favorite news feeds on the WWW. For the user, each step involves no more than dropping one composite object on top of another composite object. And given that these responses have been
driven from within the same report, the results can be consolidated within a single document.
With IQ/CyberLinks users can hyperlink and navigate across databases. When users ship these documents across the enterprise, the links remain active and thereby spare subsequent users
from having to recreate the links to drill more deeply within a report for additional answers.
A Three-Tier-Plus Remedy
Aberdeen ﬁeld research indicates that successful decision-support implementations usually grow 3-5 times approximately every 18 months. To obviate the potential for poor performance,
scalability limitations and excessive network trafﬁc, Aberdeen believes that enterprises should look for an architecture that judiciously divides decision-support processing among clients and
servers. Internet applications demand a three-tier architecture. IQ/LiveWeb accomplishes this intelligent split by working in conjunction with IQ Software’s IQ/SmartServer, a software
module that runs on most Unix platforms as well as on Windows NT, and serves a pivotal role in IQ Software’s three-tier architecture. With this powerful combination, enabled by
IQ/SmartServer, enterprises will be able to:
Create HTML output, place it directly in the Web content server, and thus prepare it for scheduled or production reports;
Build on-demand applications, by allowing queries run with the IQ/SmartServer to take advantage of the CGI interface and on-the-ﬂy HTML translation and present them to the user;
Maintain a library of reports on the Web content server; and
Place information conﬁdential to the company within a ﬁrewall, while non-proprietary information can be placed in public access areas.
Competition
WWW-enabled applications will require sophisticated three-tier architectures, a requirement that will create a ﬂurry of announcements among suppliers in the broad category of WWW-
enabled decision support. At present, IQ/LiveWeb enjoys the distinction as the only solution now shipping for both on-demand and scheduled access to corporate data over the Internet; it
continues to add functionality that will enable it to maintain its lead over suppliers that will follow in its general direction.
Although development tool suppliers — for example, Forte and Bluestone — are jumping into the fray with Internet development tools, these tools are pointed speciﬁcally at professional
developers, not end users.
IQ Software has been successfully competing against frame-based report writers, most notably the combination of Cognos Corp.’s Impromptu and Powerplay, and Business Objects. These
suppliers have yet to deliver three-tier architectures. IQ has had a Unix and NT three-tier architecture since 1995, giving it time to reﬁne its technology, pave the way for offering an elegant
Web technology, and force would-be competitors to play a catch-up game.
Although IQ/Objects is superﬁcially similar to frame-based reporters, these tools are more notable for their differences. The so-called objects in these tools, for example, are not able to
communicate with each other, nor pass attributes between objects. And while Impromptu, which is a top-notch frame-based reporter, is able to give multiple views of a query within a single
report, IQ/Objects steps beyond to deliver multiple views of multiple queries.
Aberdeen believes that IQ/Objects’ ability to generate multiple queries (from heterogeneous databases) in a single request, share data among queries and query results, and consolidate output
within a single document, could signiﬁcantly trim the amount of report building currently needed for sophisticated reporting. However, Aberdeen believes that IQ Software must maintain its
current pace of innovation and delivery schedule to maintain its leadership position.
About the Company
Established in 1984, IQ Software has demonstrated stellar growth since 1990. The company closed calendar 1995 with $21.8 million in revenue. The publicly traded company, which boasts
approximately 1.5 million users, distributes its products through a direct ﬁeld salesforce, value-added resellers and OEMs worldwide.
The company this year also delivered IQ/Vision, an "open" architecture approach to slicing, dicing, and drilling down through data. The company plans to use IQ/Vision, which works with
merchant RDBMSs and proprietary multidimensional databases, as a springboard for integrating on-line analytical processing and query and reporting tools. In addition, IQ Software also
offers a variety of consulting services, including design, installation, and performance optimization; as well as various training options, including Quick Start programs and customized
services. IQ Software also has several technology and marketing alliances, and is a member of Arbor Software, Data General, Hewlett-Packard, Informix, NCR, Oracle, Pyramid, Red Brick,
Sybase, and Unisys data warehouse initiatives.
Aberdeen Conclusions
Enterprises are now trying to automate as many decision-support chores as possible and to broaden and deepen knowledge worker explorations. These business imperatives — as well as the
need to trim infrastructure expenses and IS labor — have driven enterprises to seek content and functionality in cyberspace.
Aberdeen believes that IQ Software’s IQ/LiveWeb strongly responds to these requirements and delivers innovations that will enable enterprises to quickly create and, over time, collect their
best-of-breed mission-critical queries and reports.
Aberdeen believes that IQ/LiveWeb will invest cyberspace, and the Internet and Intranet applications it can support, with all forms of sophisticated reporting, including production reporting
and on-demand information access. While IQ technologies have helped enterprises to essentially stockpile and use an arsenal of true business-user competitive weaponry, the new
IQ/LiveWeb not only enriches the arsenal but gives the enterprise a better means to deploy it.
