Preface
Sooner or later most savvy organizations will discern that a substantial portion of their hard-earned but never realized proﬁts have been pouring through the cracks that have remained — and
continue to widen — between the discrete modules of so-called integrated ﬁnancial management systems. Comprised of loosely integrated software for budgeting, planning, forecasting, and
analysis applications, these systems will be ill-prepared to withstand the analytical rigors increasingly demanded by competitive business behavior and will therefore leave the enterprises that
rely on them with dangerous blind spots in their corporate vision.
Technologies built speciﬁcally to solve problems of the past can be force ﬁt together but can only advance corporate awareness to the degree they expose the deep underpinnings and
relationships between various organizational processes. Only a handful of ﬁnancial management software suppliers recognize the need to give enterprises a multidimensional lens into
interrelated ﬁnancial behavior; even fewer have mastered the ability to do so on massive amounts of data with systems that are built speciﬁcally to adjust to the data-driven discoveries.
Executive Summary
Most suppliers of ﬁnancial management software have found eager allies among the developers of decision-support and on-line analytical processing (OLAP) tools. In combination, they
have delivered loosely integrated systems that give business managers the opportunity to take a peek at the enterprise ﬁnancial position from several perspectives and budget and re-budget
and re-budget again in search of an acceptable enterprise attack plan. However, tying together what are essentially point solutions has saddled IS departments with the task of grappling with
multiple software versions, multiple technologies, and multiple maintenance routines. Equally important, many loosely integrated systems frequently require manual intervention for bread-
and-butter activities such as making sure new accounts are synchronized across all modules; and they force the enterprise to face the likelihood of material error as well as bear the burden of
trying to diminish it.
Business managers now need ﬁnancially intelligent systems that can drill into enterprise costs in search of the activities and processes that independently and collectively inﬂuence the
bottom line. The evolution toward relational-budgeting and activities-based management, for example, requires systems that empower enterprises to control and "benchmark" massive
amounts of detailed data and examine it in numerous combinations with the help of world-class (and integrated) reporting tools. Equally important, the enterprise — and the system itself —
must be able to adjust in response to the ever-changing demands of management and the business. In other words, technological and philosophical underpinnings must be consistent across all
modules, and the conceptualization and design of the software must map to the business.
To satisfy the current and growing requirement for processing huge data volumes, and to guarantee consistency across all charts of accounts, enterprises now need a fully integrated and non-
intrusive multidimensional system (refer to Figure 1, page 3) that is built on "open systems" components. The system must be able to scalable from the desktop to any-size symmetrical
multiprocessing (SMP) or massively parallel processing (MPP) system, and equipped to reﬂect changes down to the root calculation logic and within reports across the consolidation,
forecasting, and budgeting applications. These requirements each underscore the role of technology as an enabler of best business practices.
With the Khalix 2.1, Longview Solutions has further reﬁned the basic criteria by embedding a powerful object-oriented foundation. In Khalix 2.1, which the company announced in May
1996, a graphical (drag and drop) hierarchy editor and a powerful hierarchical engine create a system that lessens the IS coding and maintenance burden, provides the computational power
for regression, procedural, and linear calculations, and makes sure the logic of the system reﬂects the way end users' work. This object orientation is also at the root of Khalix 2.1's superior
abilities in Internet/Intranet applications.
Khalix leverages this strength by transforming industry-standard relational database management systems (RDBMSs) into powerful multidimensional servers that intelligently share their
chores with personal computers, and take advantage of the speed up and scale up of server-based parallelism.
A basic LAN site license (for up to 50 users) starts at $145,000, with an additional $95,000 for data-warehouse components running on industry standard RDBMSs. Factoring the
comprehensiveness of Khalix, the number of disparate technologies enterprises would require to even attempt to approximate its breadth, Aberdeen believes that Khalix will signiﬁcantly
reduce the total cost of ownership of rolling out applications, maintenance, and training.
Aberdeen believes that Longview Solutions has raised the bar for integrated ﬁnancial management software by transforming Khalix into a dynamic consolidation, budgeting, forecasting,
analysis and modeling system that can "express the will of management" through the selective recalculation and instantaneous rollup of ﬁnancial data. By recognizing the inseparability of
these components and delivering them in a truly integrated form within Khalix version 2.1, Longview Solutions has guaranteed itself a worthy position on technology buyers' must-RFP
shortlist of ﬁnancial management/analysis software.

Market Position
After a decade of specializing in building ﬁnancial management systems for some of North America's largest enterprises, Longview Solutions has "productized" its experience and skill into
consolidated ﬁnancial management/analysis software. Longview Solutions, Aberdeen believes, has driven this expertise into the core of Khalix.
Using Khalix, large enterprises now attempting, for example, to integrate data from various regions throughout the world, each using a different currency and each responding to local policy,
can eliminate the many error-prone and expensive forms of manual intervention that have been imposed upon them as a result of using assemblages of not-meant-to-work-together ﬁnancial
tools. With Khalix, rollups and prompt closes can be accurately completed within a 12 hour window that most enterprises can only long for, and equally rapid reporting will supply business
managers and executives with the appropriate level of detail and digest needed to steer enterprises of all sizes into competitive-advantage position.
Aberdeen believes that Khalix 2.1 demonstrates that Longview Solutions can bring its customers and potential customers the vitality and the vision of some of today's hottest startups — and
the beneﬁts and experience of a mature supplier.

A team of ﬁnancial professionals provides implementation support, training and the necessary skills transfer to help companies become totally self-sufﬁcient. Implementation personnel are

Longview Solutions' sales and distribution model includes a direct sales force, partnership programs and agents worldwide. Regional sales teams make up the direct sales force throughout

server-based ﬁnancial applications; with Oracle as an authorized reseller of the Oracle RDBMS; and Pyramid, as a cooperative-marketing partner in the Pyramid data warehouse initiative.
Key Technologies
At the heart of Khalix is the entwined power of application logic and an object-oriented hierarchical/calculation engine, which is written in the C programming language and is responsible
for rapidly compiling end-user calculations, including the most difﬁcult regression, procedural, and linear computations. The combination of application logic and a hierarchical engine forms
an intelligent bridge between Microsoft Windows end users' desktops and the RDBMS-based warehouse, called the Khalix Financial Warehouse; and between the warehouse and the data
contained in potentially numerous enterprise-wide systems.
Enterprises can choose to run Khalix Financial Warehouse as one large central data store or divide it into "logical" units on a network. A robust metadata dictionary contains speciﬁcations
about the transformation rules and data locations. The engine/logic combination helps eliminate the data inconsistencies typically contained in disparate enterprise ﬁnancial systems (without
disrupting critical day-to-day operations) and therefore participates in preparing a foundation of scrubbed and normalized data for end-user queries and enterprise calculations.
Multidimensional Excellence
As part of its duties, the Khalix calculation engine performs the multidimensional analysis; it stores, accesses, and processes the data for the Khalix Financial Warehouse on Informix,
Microsoft SQL Server, Oracle, or Sybase RDBMSs. Embracing the concepts of Relational OLAP, Khalix 2.1 transforms merchant RDBMSs into multidimensional systems that permit
business users to continually drill through layers of data in virtually any direction, slicing and dicing for nuggets of information.
The Khalix Financial Warehouse mirrors each user's three dimensional worksheets, a term Longview Solutions employs to differentiate the Khalix feature from two dimensional
spreadsheets. The parent/child relationships (see below) in each worksheet dictate the hierarchical structure of the database or databases with which they interact; administrators, using
formulas supplied by Khalix, create these relationships in the database.
While typical (and familiar)single-page spreadsheets contain rows and columns, Khalix worksheets contain rows, columns, and can be comprised of numerous individual but linked pages,
which essentially form multidimensional cubes. Khalix Worksheets build on spreadsheet familiarity but increase the depth of analysis. With a compression technique called sparse-matrix
technology, Khalix knocks out unused cells, thus creating a tighter multidimensional matrix that requires less disk and memory. Using a Khalix Extract feature, workers can extract subsets of
their worksheets into Excel and Lotus spreadsheets and thereby drive Khalix data into other widely used analysis tools. However, most other OLAP tools not only permit users to extract data
to spreadsheets, they force them to do so, and thus sacriﬁce some of their OLAP strengths.
Equally important, Khalix 2.1 leverages key features of scalable-parallel databases and the power of the hardware on which they run. Unlike most ﬁnancial management software, Khalix is
prepared to exploit the emerging power of 64-bit technology. These scalability beneﬁts include the ability to match increases in both end-user populations and queries with enterprise-
affordable processor increments that will allow the system to both scale up and speed up in lock step with increasing demand.
Object Orientation
The easy-to-use graphical (drag and drop) hierarchy editor interacts with Khalix's powerful OLAP engine, creating a system that minimizes the IS coding and maintenance burden, provides
the computational power for calculations, makes sure the logic of the system reﬂects the end-user logic, and facilitates best practices.


With parent/child relationships at the heart of Khalix, ﬁnancial behavior is an exercise in combining calculating hierarchies; ﬁnancial analysis becomes a simple process of graphically
manipulating parent/child relationships between variables. Picture an organizational chart transformed into an expression of, say, the company's chart of accounts: balance sheet; income
statement; cash ﬂow; and miscellaneous, which can be further subset into types, i.e., tax rates and exchange rates. These accounts can roll up based upon whatever ﬁnancial calculations
reﬂect the organization's practices. For the purposes of ad hoc analysis, knowledge workers superimpose variables using the hierarchy editor. Instead of working with difﬁcult Structured
Query Language (SQL) commands, or business language translations of those commands, workers use the power of objects, simply dragging and dropping relationships.
Behind the interface, Khalix 2.1, through inheritance, is able to recognize those portions, and only those portions, of a process that are affected when performing a computation (when
running, say, a what-if scenario). This reduces the processing load involved in the completion of a task; it contributes to the system's ability to remain in a consolidated state and is the
foundation of Khalix 2.1's superior abilities in Internet/Intranet applications (see below). Khalix leverages this strength by applying relational database server-based parallelism against an
already signiﬁcantly reduced processing load, thus creating technology adept at exception processing.
Khalix 2.1's object-orientation will help:
Manage all data relationships;
Assist in the creation of multiple rollups or views, i.e. operating, legal, and product consolidations, and simplify changes;
Facilitate what-if analysis, crucial for both budgeting and other ﬁnancial-planning applications, as well as for testing global assumptions;
Emulate a user-familiar spreadsheet metaphor by using icons and ﬁle folders, while overcoming spreadsheet limitations;
Keep the entire system current as of execution time and selectively recalculate where needed to speed up processing; and
Automatically establish security and attributes throughout all applications, and minimize IS's need to "baby sit" the system.
'Netting It Out
With many applications, enterprises have been able to use the Intranet as a fundamental piece of their wide-area networks (WANs), but have been forced to tolerate bottlenecks, particularly
at either the Web server or Web browser. These slowdowns notwithstanding, Intranet users have been able to trim IS labor, infrastructure costs, and massive amounts of paper-based
reporting.
Few technologies, however, are as well prepared as Khalix to leverage current (and future) Intranet technologies. (Longview plans to introduce a fully Web-enabled version by third quarter
1997.)
Khalix can run in many conﬁgurations, as a single database server or on multiple unconnected database servers periodically updating a central site, or in a distributed architecture within a
WAN. It is the combination of Khalix's superior server-based abilities and object-oriented inheritance that make it an ideal candidate for Intranet applications.
Inheritance enables Khalix to recognize and process only the relevant parts of a computation or rollup, and thereby sets the stage for Khalix to cut down the amount of Intranet trafﬁc. By
adding compression to both the sending and receiving sides of a transmission, Longview Solutions will thereby dramatically reduce the physical volume of transmitted data and thus increase
the beneﬁts of client-server computing. Khalix's ability to take advantage of parallelism will process these smaller volumes even faster.
Most approaches to the Intranet rely on Hypertext Markup Language (HTML), generally requiring enterprises to predetermine the query needs of speciﬁc users, constructing the HTML and
the paths a query will take, and then listing the available HTML documents. Khalix, in contrast, will add its hierarchy (and thus its integrated processes) to the Internet; because the hierarchy,
for example, will lead the user through the search, it will obviate the need for predetermine the directions that speciﬁc searches will take.
These same mechanisms, Aberdeen believes, contribute to Khalix's ability to facilitate live reports on the Intranet, and will leverage Khalix's Report Launcher, a mechanism that will give
users a list of pre-deﬁned reports, which can deliver up-to-the-moment results, or can serve as the foundation for ad hoc reports, a process that requires combining headers with portions of
the report body.
What Users Tell Aberdeen
Companies that use Khalix typically either have the need to produce consolidated and/or legal results by product, function, geography or region, or soon discover that Khalix functionality
easily permits them to do so. Moreover users consistently laud the company for the responsiveness and knowledge of Longview Solutions personnel.
One of the largest Khalix installations has been contentedly using the software since 1995, having been drawn to it as a cross-enterprise modeling tool in a highly decentralized business in
which each functional area has its own way of modeling. Khalix has helped the company to eliminate the multi-business-unit download-plan-upload cycle of the past. According to the
executive in charge of the project, the company continues to work with Khalix in "RAD mode," stretching Khalix's core functionality to the limits, but helped by Longview personnel. To set
expectations for others, the executive also notes that a networked Khalix application requires a solid network infrastructure to support the applications.
After a draining two year search for a true integrated consolidated ﬁnancial management/analysis software, one household-name company with headquarters in the U.S. found many tools
that demonstrated excellence in a speciﬁc area, for example OLAP, but were either missing one or more crucial components, or couldn't scale to meet its rapidly increasing data-volume
requirements. The user was particularly wary of "bridgeware" solutions because they required training users in the intricacies of the technologies on each side of the bridge.
A high-level executive at the company, which works within "a fairly complex legal-entity structure," believes that Khalix will permit one learning curve throughout the enterprise as well as
allow the company to "ﬂip people between accounting and budgeting" departments. Moreover, Khalix's ability to offer different users different views of same data will enable budgeting, tax,
and accounting departments to work independently and from different perspectives on the same data, eliminating contention that often arises when personnel from different departments need
to access the coveted data of others. The company ofﬁcial expressed conﬁdence in the ability of Khalix to handle large volumes of data within a single database.
Competition
Khalix faces competition from several directions — square on from Oracle Corp. and Hyperion Software, less directly from SAS Institute, and peripherally from a bevy of multidimensional-
tools suppliers, including Arbor software, and the suppliers of ﬁnancial software with whom these tools providers have allied.
Khalix puts multidimensional technology in a context of highly integrated and usable applications, and does so while relying on open systems technology, speciﬁcally parallel-scalable
RDBMSs and complementary parallel-scalable hardware. Nevertheless, Aberdeen believes that Longview faces signiﬁcant competition from the mindshare and marketing might of Arbor
Software's Essbase, which has been adopted, through applications programming interfaces, as a popular proprietary multidimensional engine by many developers of ﬁnancial software
applications.
Khalix's deep integration aside, Aberdeen believes that Khalix's Relational-OLAP approach eliminates the need for the enterprise to maintain non-mainstream technologies and prepares
Khalix users to beneﬁt from continual innovations in hardware parallelism and very large database technology. Given its leveraging of the RDBMS to perform multidimensional analysis,
Khalix can be superﬁcially compared to other Relational OLAP tools. Khalix, however, differentiates itself from other Relational OLAP tools by maintaining a deep ﬁnancial awareness
embedded within its technology.
Thus, one of Khalix's chief competitors is Oracle Financials, which enables the company to counter Khalix with parallelism, 64-bit enablement, and the deployment of its RDBMS across the
full spectrum of platforms, from a single system to clustered SMP and high-end MPP systems. However, the underlying driver behind the Oracle Express 6.0 multidimensional database and
its linkage to the Oracle ﬁnancial applications is not the relational database, but rather a proprietary multidimensional database technology. Although Oracle offers a strong API between its


relational universe and its strong multidimensional database (and has been moving toward a comprehensive integrated offering) its current ﬁnancial management/analysis software requires
that the enterprise factor in the addition of a non-standard database type into its architectural mix. Khalix, in contrast, leverages the best Oracle RDBMS technology. Moreover, as Oracle
completes its "Universal Server" in the future and is able to incorporate new data types within its RDBMS, Khalix will be able to leverage the new features as well.
Longview's years of experience in the ﬁnancial arena, particularly its ability to embed accounting practices deeply within its technologies, nicely counters SAS Institute's recent arrival in the
ﬁnancial arena. Moreover, Longview's ability to scale its integrated applications using open systems technologies gives it an edge, particularly in large enterprises with multiple business
units.
This full scalability within the RDBMS fold separates Khalix from Hyperion Software, which has both a strong and loyal user base as well as numerous years of experience in Longview's
chosen competitive space. However, rather than a single RDBMS-based approach, Hyperion Software offers a layered architecture.
As such, each application uses the underlying data stores speciﬁcally targeted toward the applications need or requirement. Hyperion's layered architecture includes a proprietary
multidimensional database for reporting, an RDBMS for its accounting and transactional abilities, and an essentially proprietary data schema to support consolidations and budgeting.
Ultimately, enterprise decisions will be based on the organizational culture — between whether the company requires a strict adherence to RDBMS technologies, or whether it is prepared to
deal with the complexity of a multi-database product suite.
Aberdeen Conclusions
With Khalix 2.1, Longview Solutions has stepped well-beyond most competitors, which typically retroﬁt ﬁnancial management software with proprietary multidimensional tools. With
enough brute force and money, these tools can be made to behave as part of a ﬁnancial management/analysis system, but jerry-built systems will fall far short of management's desire for a
prompt close and will require that the enterprise accept material error in the result.
Khalix 2.1 solidly responds to the needs of business managers who are looking for ﬁnancially intelligent systems that can drill into enterprise costs in search of the activities and processes
that independently and collectively inﬂuence the bottom line. The growing trend toward competitive behavior based on intelligently processing huge data volumes requires systems that can
guarantee consistency across all charts of accounts, at the same time assuring the enterprise that it will have the processing power to meet its expanding requirements.
Longview Solutions' Khalix 2.1 combines the task-streamlining power of object orientation with integrated ﬁnancial management software into a system that can diminishes the IS coding
and maintenance burden, provide the computational power for regression, procedural, and linear calculations, and make sure the logic of the system reﬂects the way end users work.
Aberdeen believes that Longview Solutions has demonstrated that the drag-and drop ﬂexibility of object orientation also has the power to transform its integrated ﬁnancial management
software into vehicle that can "express the will of management" through the selective recalculation and instantaneous rollup of ﬁnancial data. With Khalix version 2.1, Longview has
guaranteed itself a worthy position on technology buyers' must-RFP shortlist of ﬁnancial management/ analysis software.
