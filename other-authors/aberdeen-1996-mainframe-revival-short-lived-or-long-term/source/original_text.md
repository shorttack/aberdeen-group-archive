# The Mainframe Revival: Short Lived or Long Term?

## Executive Summary

After a period of decline, mainframe business is recovering. New generation mainframes are dramatically different from earlier ones. They are a lot lower priced. They are less bulky, don't require plumbing, and use substantially less electricity. Some can even run UNIX and Windows NT applications in addition to legacy applications. Moreover, with new clustering technology, they offer a cost-effective alternative to large UNIX server farms.

The leading contenders in the mainframe market are IBM's S/390 Parallel Enterprise Server G3/G4 and Amdahl's Millennium series. These products demonstrate that the mainframe is not merely a legacy platform but a modernized, competitive enterprise computing solution.

## Market Context

The mid-1990s saw a significant reversal in mainframe fortunes. After a period when client/server architectures and UNIX workstations were widely predicted to displace mainframes entirely, enterprises discovered that the economics of managing large UNIX server farms were far more complex and costly than anticipated.

Key factors driving the mainframe revival include:

- Dramatically lower pricing compared to earlier mainframe generations
- Reduced physical footprint — no longer requires raised floors, extensive air conditioning, or plumbing
- Substantially lower electricity consumption
- Ability to run UNIX and Windows NT applications alongside traditional legacy workloads
- New clustering technology providing cost-effective alternative to large distributed server farms
- Single system image simplifying operations compared to managing hundreds of UNIX servers
- Improved price/performance ratios bringing mainframe TCO competitive with UNIX farms for high-volume workloads

## Leading Products

### IBM S/390 Parallel Enterprise Server G3/G4

IBM's S/390 Parallel Enterprise Server represents the core of IBM's mainframe revival strategy. The G3 and G4 generations introduced:

- Parallel Sysplex clustering technology for high availability
- CMOS-based processors replacing bipolar technology (dramatically reducing cost and power)
- OpenEdition MVS for UNIX application support
- Capacity-on-demand pricing models

### Amdahl Millennium

Amdahl Corporation's Millennium series represented the primary IBM-compatible mainframe alternative, offering:

- S/390-compatible architecture
- Competitive pricing against IBM offerings
- Focus on existing IBM mainframe customer base

## Competitive Analysis: Mainframes vs. UNIX Server Farms

Aberdeen's analysis addressed the central question facing enterprise IT in 1996: for large-scale workloads, is it more economical to run a mainframe or a farm of UNIX servers?

The analysis identified several dimensions where mainframes showed competitive advantage:

**Total Cost of Ownership**: Large UNIX server farms require proportionally larger system administration staffs; mainframes provide single-system management for equivalent or greater throughput.

**Workload Density**: Mainframes can handle workload peaks more efficiently through dynamic resource allocation within a single system image.

**Reliability**: Mainframe availability metrics (five-nines and above) remained superior to clustered UNIX alternatives in 1996.

**Legacy Integration**: Enterprises with substantial COBOL and IMS/DB2 workloads found mainframe retention more economical than rewriting or migrating.

## Aberdeen Assessment

Aberdeen's assessment was that the mainframe revival was substantive rather than temporary. The combination of dramatic price reductions, new CMOS technology, improved price/performance, and the genuine difficulties of managing large UNIX server farms created a durable case for mainframe retention and even new deployment in specific enterprise scenarios.

The study did not predict the complete displacement of UNIX/NT servers but argued that the mainframe would occupy a defensible tier in enterprise computing for high-volume OLTP, batch processing, and mission-critical applications.
