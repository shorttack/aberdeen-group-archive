NCR: Finally a Fail-Safe Choice For Enterprise NT
IT (Information Technology) managers charged with evaluating Microsoft's NT Server operating environment for running their corporate mission-critical applications have cited NT's lack of
management tools, scalability, and overall reliability as primary reasons for delaying the deployment of NT in their enterprises.
Yet, Aberdeen has found numerous early adopters of NT who have already deployed mission-critical NT-based On-line Transaction Processing (OLTP) applications using solutions provided
by NCR.
In this Viewpoint Aberdeen analyzes the reasons why IT managers have adopted NCR's NT-based products. And, based on user research, Aberdeen concludes that NCR is highly capable of
delivering the key enterprise-grade operations management, reliability, avail-ability, and serviceability features needed to deploy fail-safe NT-based OLTP applications.

Executive Summary
In August 1994, AT&T GIS told the world it was going to take Windows NT Server into the enterprise by providing the scalability, manageability, reliability, and serviceability features
required by users to deploy the operating environment for mission-oriented OLTP applications. In the intervening years a lot changed: AT&T GIS has been reconstituted as NCR; NT has
matured; and many early NT-doubters have now become NT-endorsers.
NCR recently challenged Aberdeen to"progress-check"the viability and suitability of of its NT-based products and WorldMark servers for mission-critical OLTP applications by asking its
users,"Has NCR delivered on its promise to address NT scalability, manageability, and availability for your OLTP applications?"
And, Aberdeen found that numerous, heavy-duty sites had in the last quarter of 1995 deployed NCR's NT-based solutions for production-mode, mission-critical OLTP applications. NCR,
through efforts to address NT scalability, availability, reliability, and serviceability requirements and direct user support and consulting, has captured the hearts and minds of its NT/OLTP
users.
IT executives told Aberdeen that the primary reason they adopted NT was to take advantage of new NT-based client-server applications to gain a competitive edge. Most executives
interviewed were eager to highlight the business-beneﬁts that their enterprises had derived by deploying NCR's NT-based systems and solutions. The beneﬁts most commonly cited by these
successful NT/OLTP users include:
1. Reduced time to deploy major business client-server applications, enabling the enterprise to gain a competitive-edge using advanced technologies;
2. Lowered cost to deploy mission-oriented applications; and
3. Increased growth of new business and market-share — because client-server NT provided the business with additional ﬂexibility to rapidly adopt computing technologies that met new
opportunities.
After analyzing the ﬁeld research, Aberdeen agrees with NCR's users — NCR possesses the products and talent pool that IS decision makers must have to make a fail-safe choice when
implementing NT for mission-critical applications.

The Driving Force: Time-To-Market
Aberdeen recently interviewed some of the largest early-adopters of NCR's NT-based solutions for mainstream, bet-the-business applications to understand what worked, and what did not. A
common theme emerged from the discussions across all decision makers — an urgent need for new client-server application architectures to step ahead of dramatic time-to-market shifts
experienced by their line of business organizations.
The early NT adopters told Aberdeen that their ﬁrms had to react much faster than competitors to retain existing business while growing new business with NT-based client-server
applications.
Many decision makers stated their market window-of-opportunity used to be measured in years, but had dwindled to only a few months (or less), and that current IT systems were incapable
of keeping up with required technology changes. And, for some decision makers, critical opportunities were being missed due to continued reliance on mainframe (primarily MVS-based)
information systems architectures.
To provide useful information for their users, these IS managers believe they needed to:
modify their computing infrastructure to provide more useful information to decision makers and users; and

reorient the objectives of IS to support the enterprises business strategy, tactics, and policies.
The overhaul of the IT infrastructure from legacy two-level architectures to components-based multi-level architectures was identiﬁed by all decision makers as necessary to keep pace with,
and move ahead of, continued business change.
And, all of the IS decision makers now deploying NCR's NT-based systems observed there were two critical factors responsible for meeting their objectives to deliver timely information
while changing the IS focus and budget. The two changes to IT and business practices involved:
more aggressively supporting make-the-money applications and make-the-money decision makers; and
buying off-the-shelf applications software with embedded best-practices while only developing custom applications when packaged software was unavailable.
Decision makers said that only after IT and business-line managers had prioritized critical business revenue functions, and separated revenue functions from support, was it possible to fully
realize the objective of providing more timely information to key decision makers.

NCR's Impact: Ensures New Service-Level Agreements
Users in retail, ﬁnance, telecommunications, and transportation segments told Aberdeen that customer service is critical for their businesses to gain, or maintain, a competitive advantage.
Line managers using NCR's NT-based products told Aberdeen that an important attribute that must be met by IS was an agreed-to level of service for critical OLTP applications. NCR's
LifeKeeper and TOP END availability products provided IS management with the ability to agree on very high service-levels that would be delivered with the new NT/OLTP applications.
More signiﬁcantly, some enterprises even extended the internal service-level agreements to external customers. Contracts containing high levels of service were provided to support-sensitive
customers. With conﬁdence in NCR's NT value-added products, these IS executives were able to offer their enterprise's customers guaranteed levels-of-service, and in so doing provided a
differentiator competitors were not easily or cost-effectively able to duplicate.

NCR's Impact: Rapid Application Deployment
Users found that the unexpected beneﬁt of NCR's NT for OLTP solutions was the ability to rapidly deploy business-critical applications. RAD, on the lips of most NCR users Aberdeen
interviewed, had come to mean Rapid Application Deployment — not rapid application development. RAD, made possible by NCR's fail-safe products, would cut approximately 25% off the
projected time to implement new applications. But what made the users especially pleased is that this time savings was for all applications and most had more than ten applications in
backlog. Deploying these applications on-line 25% faster was viewed as a major business advantage.
Initial projects for formerly centralized-mainframe shops required much longer to complete than originally anticipated. The primary reasons for the unexpected delays were due to the lack of
cross-training of IS staff on the front-end of the project and changes in the way line organizations were expected to participate. IS executives noticed signiﬁcant improvements in delivery
schedules after their initial projects.
Coming from MVS and other mainframe environments, the common practice had been to isolate and test-out single points-of-failure in critical OLTP applications in the DBMS code.
Unfortunately, many mainframe-trained IS staff members continued following these practices in their brave new NCR/NT OLTP environments. However, after the initial project reviews, IS
managers determined that by using the interfaces provided by NCR's LifeKeeper and TOP END, such efforts were not required. Instead of needing to perform exhaustive integration-testing
for client-server applications, smart IS managers tested the components only. The application components were placed in test-harnesses consisting of at least two NT servers and NCR's
LifeKeeper, and if it worked, the applications were deployed immediately. Most IS managers stated the typical schedule to deploy working OLTP applications on NT had been reduced by
20% to 25%, while a few had beneﬁted by decreases of 50%.

Enterprise NT: The Beneﬁts....
IS decision makers who chose to implement NT for mission-critical OLTP applications believed the NT/SQL Server operating environment offered their enterprises the following beneﬁts:
Homogenous Environment — IS decision makers indicated that they wanted a standard operating environment that could be run transparently across different hardware platforms. Suppliers
would then be chosen by the value-add they could bring in the areas of system performance, scalability, reliability, and manageability features necessary to deploy critical applications;
Reduced System and Application Costs — IS decision makers perceived that the systems and software pricing associated with NT client/server computing to be substantially less costly than
the platform-based pricing models employed by traditional mainframe and minicomputer manufacturers. For example, Microsoft's SQL Server 6.5 relational database management system
(RDBMS) running on NT can be as much as 90% less costly than alternative RDBM's required for other operating environments such as Unix, OpenVMS, and MVS;
Synergy with the Desktop — IS managers whose staff mastered MS-DOS and MS-Windows over the years and now like what they see in the upcoming NT Workstation 4.0 release, want a
mission OLTP environment similar to, and closely linked with, their desktop. And business-line managers frequently stated they wanted to deploy front-line OLTP applications using NT
because it provides them the ability to hit the sweet-spot of their business customers, right on the desk;
Applications Development Continuity — Some development managers desired that the same applications that had been developed for one purpose be made available to other applications
through the use of OLE objects. This would, in their opinion, reduce the cost and time required for application maintenance and deployment; and
Ease of Management/Deployment — IS managers want the same people that manage their desktop and LAN computing environ-ments to be able to do the same for their mission-application
platforms to reduce operational expenses.
According to IS decision makers, NCR has focused on providing the production-level necessary RAS (Reliability, Availability, and Serviceability) features required for Windows NT Server.
IS decision makers said NCR's products and services enabled their deployment of mission oriented NT-based applications and provided all the hoped-for beneﬁts from OLTP-based NT.

NCR's Value-Add: RAS to Deploy and Manage NT/OLTP
NCR has improved NT for OLTP applications by adding the RAS characteristics smart IS managers demanded. NCR's integrated value-added products for NT include:
LifeKeeper: processing of OLTP applications continues even if an entire NT system fails by transferring control to a clustered NT server;
Server Availability Manager: allows the remote monitoring, repairing and restarting of critical NT OLTP application servers;

TOP END: automatically and dynamically restarts transactions in production use even if entire networks fail;
NMC4000: allows PC LAN administrators to easily manage large distributed networks of NT systems, and integrates with Microsoft's SMS (Systems Management Server);
NetVault: archives and retrieves historical or little used data saved off-line on tape libraries; and
CommVault: provides remote, unattended backup across the network, to optical disk.

LifeKeeper and TOP END: NCR Innovates Highly Available NT
If any component, such as a processor node, a LifeKeeper-managed NT Server platform, an I/O controller, disk, memory, an OLTP application module, or even the NT Server operating
environment fails, LifeKeeper automatically and gracefully switches the workload to another system on the availability-cluster (an availability-cluster is the networked NT systems — up to 3
— managed by LifeKeeper, see Figure 1). This ensures users access to critical applications and data with minimal disruption, typically between 2 seconds and 5 minutes depending upon the
design and size of the database.

In the event of a failure, LifeKeeper provides dynamic failover between NT systems without operator intervention. But for safety reasons, the failed node will not go back into service until
the administrator puts it on-line again after the failure has been corrected. Note that LifeKeeper does not require a non-productive standby system in the availability cluster and due to its
architecture can move applications from the failed node to healthy ones. These capabilities minimize a user's costs for higher levels of availability.
IS managers told Aberdeen an unexpected beneﬁt of LifeKeeper was the ability to upgrade both software and hardware while applications continued servicing users. LifeKeeper enabled IS
to temporarily move applications and data to another system while upgrades were per-formed. IS managers stated that LifeKeeper provided their staff with the ability to schedule up-grades
without worry about impact on critical operations.
TOP END directs OLTP-client requests to the appropriate database server, just like other transaction monitors. However, the high-availability feature of TOP END that users lauded (see
Figure 1) was its ability to redirect client-server transactions to avoid any failures in the system — either servers, applications, or network.
By providing a robust solution for must-be-up-and-running OLTP applications, the combination of LifeKeeper and TOP END running on NT was cited by IS executives"as the client-server
software-equivalent for fault-tolerant OLTP on NT."

NCR and Microsoft: Scalability for NT/OLTP
A number of IS executives told Aberdeen their NT-based, client-server applications could easily consume the throughput of an additional 8 to 12 CPUs if that level of scalability were
available today with NT Server.
And NCR has been responding to user demands by working directly with Microsoft for supporting more than four processors in an SMP-conﬁguration (Symmetric Multiprocessor) with NT
Server and SQL Server.
Software development directed by user feedback is critical to SMP scalability engineering. It takes time for engineers to integrate real-world lessons about how SMP locks work with
different RDBMS engines. In addition, real-world user-application loading on systems impacts how scalability techniques should best be implemented. The combination of new hardware
design and more granular SMP-locks results in iterative loops between product releases. This learning experience is the primary cause why scalable SMP solutions require time to improve
and mature.
NCR is transferring its hard-earned knowledge, acquired both from years of scaling its UNIX MP-RAS environment and working with its NT Server customers in their production
environments, to Microsoft. As a result of these efforts, NCR believes the SMP scalability of NT/SQL Server should improve to 8 CPUs with the planned release of Windows NT Server 4.0
in the fall of 1996. And both Microsoft and NCR have plans to provide a 12-to-16 CPU NT/SQL Server platform by mid-1997.
In the interim, the early-adopters Aberdeen interviewed are deploying more NT-based servers to accommodate heavier demand-loads. Intermediate-term, they have strong conﬁdence in NCR
and Microsoft — not just Microsoft alone — to deliver the high-performance SMP NT Server hardware and software platforms they need.

Multiprocessor Workload Balancing: NCR's Multiprocessor Utilization Manager for NT in 1996
To provide users with NT Server platform consolidation, NCR plans to introduce an SMP Utilization Manager in 1996 that will allow IS administrators to assign a single application to run
on any number of processors on a 16-way SMP-WorldMark system. (However, please note that most NT Server applications can only effectively use up to 4 processors at this time.)

This feature will make it possible for IS managers to assign an OLTP application requiring only 2 CPUs is dedicated to 2 speciﬁc CPUs while reserving the remaining CPU's for other NT
applications, such as Microsoft's Exchange.
The major cost advantage of NCR's SMP Utilization Manager for NT is that it allows IS departments to consolidate the number of NT servers that need to be conﬁgured, managed, and
supported by their staffs. Equally as important, NCR's SMP Utilization Manager for NT running on WorldMark servers will enable IS to safely and quickly upgrade their applications when
NT/RDBMS combinations can be scaled to over four processors.
Aberdeen believes NCR's SMP Utilization Manager for NT will be an example of a best-of-class solution that will continue to fuel the wide-spread adoption of NT for mission-critical
business application.

The Future: Data Marts for NT
Building on experience from deploying large data warehouses using Teradata for leading-edge IT users, NCR will begin providing data mart solutions beginning with the release of NT
Server 4.0. A data mart is a local subset-copy of a much larger and denser data warehouse. Departmental data marts on NT promise to be a cost-effective decision support tool for business
line executives responsible for making timely decisions with a ﬁnancial impact.
NCR plans to introduce a data-mart-up function to route complex queries from NT/SQL Server systems up to NCR's large-scale data warehouses for detailed data. NCR also plans to
introduce an option for the request to go over the internet and connect to Teradata-based data warehouses via Microsoft's Internet Information Server with ODBC links on Teradata.
To compliment the data-mart-up function, NCR also plans to introduce a data-mart-down function. With this, a subset of a centralized Teradata-based data-warehouse is replicated onto local
NT systems.

NCR: The Fail-Safe Choice For NT
Initial releases of NT, circa 1993, made clear to IS decision makers that additional RAS features were critical if NT was to move out of the laboratory and onto enterprise production systems.
IS managers did not want to be awakened in the middle of the night to repair what was considered an avoidable problem if Microsoft had had the time to further develop and mature NT.
Users told Aberdeen that they initially selected NCR servers to run NT because if they had major problems with the operating environment they could always replace NT Server with NCR's
UNIX MP-RAS. However, the fail-safe products — LifeKeeper and TopEnd — and the reliability of the hardware systems from NCR allowed them to deploy their NT-based client-server
applications without resorting to their Unix-contingency plan. Most IS managers said that the selection of NCR was the fail-safe answer for using NT for business-critical applications —
availability for 7 days a week x 24 hours a day x 365 days a year operations.
The maturing of NT and SQL Server, and the peace of mind provided by NCR's NT-based RAS products enabled these early NCR-NT adopters to roll-out their OLTP projects in the fall of
1995. According to users, NCR is now a fail-safe choice for OLTP application deployment for the following reasons:
1. The client-server applications these users wanted are all running on NT-Intel. There has been no need to wait for software to be ported to NT-Intel;
2. NCR earned the depth and breadth of its NT/OLTP expertise by assisting early adopters and now has the wisdom that comes from experience to help other users planning to rapidly
deploy client-server NT applications; and
3. Complimentary product solutions from NCR have ﬁlled the gaps in NT, and provided IS managers an operating environment they now depend upon.

Conclusion
In 1994 NCR announced its plan to aggressively bring the necessary management infrastructure tools to Microsoft's Windows NT Server. In addition, NCR made a commitment to provide
the scalability, reliability, and serviceability levels that professional IS executives demand for their production OLTP applications.
Now, two years later, NCR is claiming not only to have scaled NT and improved overall systems reliability, but also to have positioned NT for explosive growth with production applications
within enterprises.
When Aberdeen was challenged to prove whether NCR had delivered on its promises, we did not expect to ﬁnd all of them fulﬁlled. NCR's users told Aberdeen, in emphatic terms, that NCR
had indeed met — and exceeded —their expectations for reliable and manageable, fail-safe NT systems.
IS executives told Aberdeen that NCR's RAS products for NT have made it fail-safe for them to bring NT-based OLTP applications into their enterprise.
However, most IS managers are deploying more NT/OLTP application servers than they would like to, to meet their application performance requirements. Despite NT's current scalability
limitations, IS executives are convinced NCR and Microsoft will deliver the horsepower to meet their needs, sooner rather than later.
IS executives were proud of the many unique beneﬁts their enterprises had seen after deploying NCR's NT-based OLTP applications. Such beneﬁts included the increased service-levels that
provided support-sensitive businesses in retail and ﬁnancial services with the opportunity to increase their customers' satisfactions levels. Additional beneﬁts included the use of RAD (Rapid
Application Deployment) techniques that led to dramatic time-to-market application deployment advantage for these enterprises.
IS decision makers who understand the beneﬁts of NCR's development efforts to deliver further SMP scalability and multi-processor workload management will stay committed to NCR as
their primary NT server supplier-of-choice.

NCR has proven that NT can be deployed for production-quality, business-critical OLTP applications within commercial enterprises. And NCR has proven itself capable of helping users take
every advantage of new client-server application architectures for very compelling business advantages. Decision makers can take advantage of NCR's ﬁeld-proven ability to deliver value to
the enterprise, and fast-track their critical OLTP projects.
Based upon Aberdeen's user interviews, NCR's users will tell you that NCR is an experienced, fail-safe choice for NT-based OLTP application deployments.
