Introduction
Information Systems (IS) executives, alarmed by the increasing cost and complexity of deploying and managing heterogeneous distributed systems architectures, are yearning to simplify
their IS procedures and budgets. And, many experienced IS executives have found that dedicated networked ﬁle-servers are simplifying the storage requirements for the enterprise while
reducing IS budget expenses and frustrations. These IS executives are weaning the enterprise away from application-server based storage architectures, to network-attached data-access
storage servers.
In this Proﬁle, Aberdeen examines networked computing problems that IS decision makers and managers struggle with, and how Network Appliance’s (NetApp’s) solutions resolve these
problems. Aberdeen analyzes the company’s core products, product positioning, and competition in the rapidly emerging market of networked data-access and storage appliances, and
concludes that IS decision makers have an opportunity — to grab a golden ring.
The Wheels on this Bus — Go Round and Round
In the new world of networked computing, a cyclical problem has emerged for users, business-line managers, IS managers and decision makers:
• users and make-the-money managers complain about application response time and lack of storage in data-intensive networked computing environments — when business and
customer service is impacted or forfeited;
• IS executives are forced to reallocate IS budget moneys to improve the performance of networked applications — to run the business;
• application projects for new business initiatives are delayed — creating a loss of conﬁdence in IS; and
• IS executives become caught in a no-win cycle of juggling budgets and schedules to run the current and deploy the IS future — for the business.
The Common Problems — and Expensive Cyclical Solutions
The primary frustrations cited by users include: an inability to ﬁnd needed applications and information; degraded response times as more users vie for the same applications and data; and a
lack of space to store critical business data.
Overcoming performance problems: To respond to complaints about degrading performance, IS managers traditionally upgrade application platforms with higher-speed CPU platforms,
additional memory, and newer and faster disk drive technologies. But, knowledgeable IS planners and architects say this traditional solution does not scale to moderate and large networked
computing environments.
Managing distributed data: In the evolving networked computing environment, data storage is commonly added to individual workstations and servers, resulting in an expanding population
of dispersed data-storage containers that places additional pressure on IS to maintain vital data. Experienced IS managers say the ability to securely protect, reliably backup, and archive vital
data over dispersed networks is still an expensive art — not a science. The result for IS executives is a continuing strain on IS budget moneys to manage distributed data.
Integrity, availability and reliability of networked data: As networked data-intensive applications support more make-the-money operations, the demand for highly available data increases.
IS executives are investing in highly-available data-storage solutions based on Redundant Arrays of Inexpensive Disks (RAID) to provide round-the-clock access and protection for critical
information. But, IS managers have found deploying RAID storage silos on many distributed application servers and workstations too expensive to be practical.
The Solution — NetApp’s network-attached data-access and storage ﬁlers
The conventional rule of thumb has been that data must be physically connected to platforms where critical applications operate. And, this vestige of glass-house design has some validity to
it. For transaction-intensive database applications, near real-time updates are necessary to update transactions before providing access to data. However, in non-database application
environments, where transaction updates do not exist, the primary factor motivating "ﬁt for use" is whether users are provided fast access to data.
Instead of automatically adding more memory, faster processors and local storage; smart IS planners are looking to improve Input and Output (I/O) by adding networked data-access and
storage solutions. NetApp is commonly able to provide faster access to data from its networked data storage solutions than is available to users from local attached disks. NetApp’s products
are designed for delivering fast, inexpensive, and easy to manage data for networked applications, and based on what its users say — resolves the cyclical headaches for IS by providing users
with seamless, cost-effective access to data for networked computing.
Product Positioning

NetApp’s postulate is that a fast, reliable, and easy to manage networked data-server ought to be just that — with no user applications running on the data-server. NetApp’s products (see
Figure 1) provide users with seamless access to data for almost all non-database applications (including Windows, NT, Unix, and Inter/Intra Net), while enabling IS to consolidate data for
these diverse environments onto an easy to install and manage data-access and storage solution.

Core Products — NetApp’s ﬁlers
NetApp’s hardware platform consists of three rack-mounted models, the F220, F330, and F540. The platforms (see Table 1) differ by storage capacity, speed, and the maximum number of

3.1, Windows 95, Windows NT, and Unix workstations. And, applications operating on Unix servers and Windows NT work seamlessly with NetApp’s ﬁlers.

Core Products — NetApp’s operating software
The core values delivered by NetApp’s products are a result of the company’s investments in sophisticated, high-performance operating software. Data ONTAP, NetApp’s crown jewels,
provides users with high-performance ﬁling and overall systems reliability. The architecture of Data ONTAP (see Figure 2) is based on tightly-coupled but independent software modules,
designed to provide fast response to networked computing applications.

Multi-application data-access modules: Three software modules, CIFS, NFS, and HTTP ﬁle sharing protocols provide a diverse range of workstation client users and server-based
applications with seamless access to data. Each module can operate individually to create a dedicated-application data-access server, or multiple modules can be run on the same server —
creating a multi-application data-access server. As a result, NetApp’s ﬁlers are able to provide simultaneous access to data for Unix-based, NT-based, and Internet-based applications across
the network. Each module contains a suite of features important to applications that users depend on:
• HTTP Module: Providing access to data for users and applications operating on any platform with a web-browser, NetApp’s Hyper Text Transfer Protocol module enables web-based
applications to access a wide variety of data types, including: HTML, audio, MIME, video, postscript, or binary ﬁles. And, the HTTP module easily redirects requests for dynamic data
(CGI requests or database lookups) to general purpose web-servers on the network;
• NFS Module: Providing access to data for users and applications operating on Unix and PC platforms, the Network File System module supports access to ﬁles up to 4 GB. Employing
fast hash table lookups on directories, NetApp’s NFS module eliminates time-consuming linear searches found on general purpose Unix ﬁle servers. Supporting NFS protocol versions 2
and 3, NetApp maintains the stateless server design and simple recovery of NFS version 2 with the ability to support a distributed ﬁle service among cooperating protocols.
• CIFS Module: The Common Internet File System (Microsoft’s enhanced Server Message Block network ﬁle sharing protocol) module provides access to data for users and
applications operating on Microsoft Windows 3.1, Windows 95, and Windows NT platforms. Supporting LAN Manager using NetBios over TCP/IP, NetApp’s solution will simply
"pop" into existing networks. And, for Windows-based networked computing environments, the CIFS module also supports local passwords or the use of NT Domains if an NT Primary
Domain Controller is available. Further, NT access control lists are supported on all NT shares for integrity and privacy.
Additionally, users of Unix and Windows workstations can gain access to their native ﬁle system as well as non-native ﬁles (for instance a Windows user could access NFS ﬁles and a Unix
client can access Windows CIFS ﬁles).
For decision makers who need to provide business-line users with interoperable access to data no matter how it is formatted, NetApp’s ﬁle sharing solution provides an attractive
interoperability solution. And, for IS managers responsible for maintaining data for production networked computing environments, NetApp’s multiprotocol ﬁlers offer an compelling
consolidation solution.
Advanced ﬁle system architecture: Data ONTAP’s Write Anywhere File Layout (WAFL) provides dramatic performance improvements over general-purpose Unix and NT ﬁle servers.
Eliminating all the overhead of seeks found on these general purpose operating environments, WAFL provides staggering improvements in read access times. And, using advanced features
such as copy-on-write, double-buffered intent logs, NVRAM, and integrated RAID 4 features, WAFL provides equally stunning results for write-intensive applications. The net result: faster
reads and writes of data over the network than traditional local data storage attached to multi-function, general purpose application servers are capable of providing.
Snapshots: NetApp’s Snapshot provides several features IS administrators clamor for. Providing on-line read-only copies of ﬁle systems, Snapshot allows access to on-line copies of archived
ﬁles. Able to maintain up to 20 simultaneous Snapshots, this feature is useful for performing on-line backups and archives while applications are actively reading and writing. And, instead of
having to deploy multiple web-servers, decision makers can consolidate multiple web-servers onto
NetApp’s ﬁlers by using Snapshots.
Integrated reliability and availability of data: Data ONTAP delivers integrated and high-speed RAID 4 features —resulting in data availability in excess of 99.99 percent. If a disk should
fail, Data ONTAP automatically reconstructs data on a dedicated spare drive. Known as a "hot-spare", this feature is normally only found in very expensive RAID silos. And, once the data
on the failed disk has been restored on the spare disk, the failed drive can be replaced with a good disk, with the good disk becoming the spare.
Management software: Data ONTAP provides a range of commonly used commands and interfaces for local and remote management. From SNMP MIB II v2, to 30 easy to use commands,
to a web-based Graphical User Interface (GUI), to a standard Windows-based management interface, to standard telnet and rsh commands — almost every style of local and remote
management is supported. And, NetApp plans to introduce an HTML/Java-based administrative front-end that can operate across multiple workstations, providing a consistent administrative
interface — that should result in lower operational costs. For critical backup and archive, administrators will ﬁnd rdump and rsh commands, local tape backup and restore, support for
Legato’s Networker, PDC’s Budtool and SpectraLogic’s Alexandria for remote unattended backup. And, NetApp is leading the effort to standardize the Network Data Management Protocol
(NDMP) to provide IS executives with greater centralized control over networked data and choice of interoperable networked data management applications.
Competitive Positioning
Network Appliance competes with other network-attached data-access server makers such as Auspex, Falcon Systems, and Invincible Technologies; and multi-function, general-purpose ﬁle-
server suppliers such as Hewlett-Packard and IBM. And, Sun Microsystems and Data General have already announced intentions of ﬁelding low-cost, high-performance ﬁlers.
When competing with single function ﬁlers, NetApp emphasizes cost, speed, and its multi-application data-access modules — the latter because only NetApp provides seamless access to so
many common networked computing environments. And, when competing with multi-function general-purpose application servers, NetApp has distinct advantages in cost, speed and
support for large user populations.
Summary and Observations
Network Appliance, founded in 1992, is a pioneer of the concept of network-attached, high-speed, data-access and storage servers for data-intensive networked computing environments.
Riding a revenue-rocket since its founding, revenues have zoomed from $2 million in 1994, to $14.5 million in 1995, to $47 million in ﬁscal 1996. And, the FY’97 run-rate indicates NetApp
is fast approaching the $100 million revenue mark.
During this time, NetApp has broadened its appeal from being a supplier for technical (CAD and CASE) applications, to become a supplier that is now almost evenly split between
commercial and technical application environments. NetApp’s success has been based on its reliable, high-speed data-access servers running the NFS protocol. Users of NetApp’s storage
appliances say they have seen a reduction in the number of networked application servers previously dedicated to ﬁle-services, faster application response times, and lower overall expenses
to maintain vital data for the business. And, users say that like toasters, NetApp’s data appliances can simply be plugged-in, left unattended, and reliably perform.
But, NetApp is no longer a one-horse company. Based on its recent technology investments, the company is bringing innovative solutions to market — well ahead of competitors. By
supporting Microsoft’s CIFS and the Internet’s HTTP, NetApp is positioned to uniquely empower IS decision makers resolve budget and time-to-market pressures. The combination of the
right products at the right time bodes well for IS decision makers.
NetApp’s solutions are currently leading a larger industry-trend toward network-attached data access and storage solutions. And, what should keep NetApp in the lead is its focus on adding
value through advanced operating software features. IS decision makers responsible for network computing, and those looking to break the cycle of juggling operations and project budgets
ought to look at and closely evaluate NetApp — for an already proven solution.
