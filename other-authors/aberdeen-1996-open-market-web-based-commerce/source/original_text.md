Preface
Companies of every size are seeking the path to proﬁtable electronic commerce. One business decision that seems very obvious to electronic commerce veterans is that they must base their
enterprise’s electronic commerce infrastructure on an open software platform and production-quality Web technologies. However, quickly going past the obvious, they have also learned that
they must link new front-ofﬁce Web sites with back-ofﬁce business processes without limiting business options. Open Market is focused on providing these back-ofﬁce needs.
In this Proﬁle, Aberdeen describes Open Market’s suite of products architected from day one for allowing open, ﬂexible electronic commerce — and compares the Open Market approach to
today’s most popular alternatives.
Executive Summary
Open Market has based its entire product development around a simple but powerful concept — true electronic commerce will require the ability to implement Web-based applications that
have the same comprehensiveness as today’s in-house run-the-business applications. This approach is in sharp contrast from Open Market’s major competitors that are supplying software
that merely delivers relatively simple point-to-point transaction services.
Open Market deﬁnes its customers and prospects as Commerce Service Providers (CSPs). CSPs can be both internal IT departments providing Web capabilities for multiple divisions within a
corporation or companies such as banks, telcos, or Internet access providers that are extending their own offerings to include Internet commerce services. Open Market’s products are often
acquired by CSPs that have found their home-grown solutions to be inadequate as users’ demands for comprehensive, reliable make-the-money functionality increases rapidly.
Examining Open Market’s software architecture from the user-level up, Open Market’s products are designed to support any popular browser — this satisﬁes the primary market requirement
for open access by users. At the next level, the content server, Open Market’s products support any HTTP software server — including both Netscape’s and Microsoft’s current and future
products.
Key to Open Market’s uniqueness is its support for transactions from the content server, or even more impressively, multiple content servers, to however many back-ofﬁce — make-the-
transaction-occur — applications as necessary. The beneﬁt for users is ﬂexibility built upon best-in-class Web technologies.
Open Market’s products have the ﬂexibility to allow IS executives to quickly build the complex electronic commerce infrastructure that their enterprise will soon require to support
traditional order entry and fulﬁllment processes. Its challenge is to communicate its message above the background noise being created by newbees who have never been responsible for
implementing real world, production application systems. Aberdeen recommends that enterprise executives charged with making their organizations successful in the quickly emerging and
changing world of Web-based commerce closely evaluate Open Market’s software as an underlying long-term, trustworthy Web infrastructure.
Open Market’s Products
At the heart of Open Market’s product line is its OM-SecureLink technology. Any Web content server that uses OM-SecureLink technology and produces HTTP output, including tools from
Netscape, Microsoft and others, in conjunction with OM-Transact (described below) is centrally manageable and capable of completing secure online transactions.
OM-SecureLink software allows front-ofﬁce content Web servers to securely and transparently broker back-ofﬁce management and transaction processing services by using the industry
standard HTTP protocol. OM-SecureLink geographically and technologically emancipates Web servers — information need not be hosted on the same system or even at the same location
with any of the back-ofﬁce applications to complete transactions or manage user access. OM-SecureLink technology combines a ticketing technique with the MD5 hash algorithm and SSL
security to complete secure transactions and server management.
Open Market’s key products, all utilizing OM-SecureLink technology, are OM-Transact and OM-Axcess. These are complemented by several other products which extend the functionality
of these two core products and OM-SecureLink. When independent CSPs architect their systems with Open Market’s products and technology, the resulting open infrastructure may be
referred to as a commerce solution.
Open Market’s commerce solution is best described by following a generalized transaction process involving a customer, a retailer, and a CSP. As illustrated in Figure 1, a potential customer
goes to a retailer’s Web site (1) and downloads HTML Web pages that describe products the customer would immediately like to order(2). Included in the pages are modiﬁed HTTP-
compliant URLs, basically custom hot links called digital offers, that the OM-SecureLink software uses to securely broker back-ofﬁce services provided by the CSP using OM-Transact.
The about-to-be customer then clicks on the digital offer to make his or her purchase (3) and the request is routed to the CSP which checks authorization (4) and veriﬁes the means of
payment. The CSP then (5) creates a digital receipt, which the buyer keeps. Upon veriﬁcation of the digital receipt, the (6) retailer fulﬁlls the order — which may require additional OM-
SecureLink messages being sent to the retailer’s normal process-based distribution applications and (7) the retailer is notiﬁed of the completed transaction by the CSP.
To the user, this purchase is as simple as clicking on the item of interest and supplying the required information — the user sees none of the complex passing of information required to
complete a secure, multi-site, Web-based transaction. And note, that a CSP can just as easily be a node in a corporate Web site as an independent Internet service provider.
Aberdeen researched current OM-Transact and OM-Axcess users and they said these are the only products on the market that can meet their real world, multi-site electronic commerce needs.

OM-Transact
The back-ofﬁce operations of steps (4), (5), (6), and (7) in Figure 1 are handled by Open Market’s ﬂagship OM-Transact application. Designed to centrally manage and execute secure on-line
transactions, OM-Transact does the legwork of electronic commerce and can be located independently of the Web content servers.
OM-Transact uses either an Oracle or Sybase database engine — depending upon the user’s preference. The database-centric approach of OM-Transact allows users to have a modular,
extensible, plug-in architecture. The beneﬁt of this plug-in architecture is ﬂexibility — it will assist users in expanding their Web-based commerce capabilities in such areas as electronic
document exchange (EDI) and other back-ofﬁce services where appropriate. In addition, the plug-in architecture allows independent application suppliers’ products to openly work with OM-
Transact.
Upon receipt of a customer’s digital offer, OM-Transact typically:
• authenticates customers to prevent fraud;
• automatically and securely processes payment transactions;
• facilitates customer service with real-time account statements and online order status for hard goods;
• provides comprehensive order management;
• fulﬁlls both hard goods and digital goods;
• automatically calculates shipping charges and tax for U.S. and Canadian purchases.
OM-Axcess
OM-Axcess manages users’ access to any database or Web server using OM-SecureLink software, as illustrated Figure 2. This ability to securely manage the Web infrastructure is key to the
acceptance of electronic commerce solutions.
Web managers and IS executives responsible for distributed systems are all too familiar with immature management tools and will welcome both the central access management features of
OM-Axcess and its enterprise adaptability. Using OM-Axcess, individual enterprises can set user-entry privileges consistent with their business policies and processes — securing privileges
at the HTTP layer, individual database layer, or a combination of both.
Active Commerce DB
Active Commerce DB is a front-ofﬁce application development tool that integrates databases with Web content servers to create dynamic content. (Dynamic content is the generation of
HTML on the ﬂy when information is retrieved from an existing database). Active Commerce DB is bundled with OM-SecureLink and sold to Web content site owners, CSPs, Web hosting
companies, and Web developers to build front-ofﬁce applications that use OM-Transact or OM-Axcess in the back-ofﬁce. In addition to providing Active Commerce DB, Open Market has
partnered with 13 leading Independent Software Vendors (ISVs) to guarantee users’ front-ofﬁce Web site development options (See Table 1).
Target Markets and Customers
Open Market’s products are ﬁnding early acceptance with customers who are CSPs today. The industry leading CSPs using Open Market’s products include AT&T, Time, BBN Planet,
UUNet PIPEX, iSTAR Internet, MCI, BT, Toronto Dominion Bank, First Union and CM Telecommunications. These early users are distinguishing themselves from their competitors by
neither restricting their customers’ choice of analytical and content building tools nor forcing them to rely on any one method of funds transfer. Using OM-Transact the CSPs can easily,
quickly and cost-effectively deliver safe transaction solutions to any Web site. This is especially appealing to the independent CSPs’ largest target market — small- and medium-sized
organizations. These downstream customers can avoid cost-prohibitive systems integration, database connectivity, transaction management and manual credit card transaction processing by
contracting with a CSP that has implemented Open Market’s Solution.
Upstream customers implementing business-to-business electronic commerce will appreciate Open Market’s ability to complete transactions between many buyers and many suppliers.
Many-to-many relationships will develop in Web-based commerce because most business relationships are not simply point-to-point. In fact, Aberdeen research has clearly shown that
manufacturers who attempt to sell their products directly to end-user customers through the Internet risk alienating their business-critical wholesale and retail distribution channels. To
facilitate businesses interaction, Open Market has formed strategic alliances with Trilogy Development Group and OneWave, Inc.. The alliance will let users integrate Trilogy’s sales force
automation software, OneWave’s Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems, and Open Market’s back-ofﬁce OM-Transact and OM-Axcess to build complete solutions.

Competitive Positioning


Open Market’s chief competitor is in-house custom programming groups. These organizations will acquire a content server from Netscape or Microsoft and then embark on a large
programming and systems integration effort. Open Market notes with satisfaction that many of its new enterprise customers have abandoned this approach in frustration in favor of the
beneﬁts of Open Market’s packaged and pre-tested solutions.
Open Market’s next most formidable competitor is IBM. IBM has announced a series of future products and services for electronic commerce that has slowed down market investment.
However, IBM’s products do not have the advanced functionality of Open Market’s and are intended more to support IBM’s value-added services — note that IBM is itself attempting to
build a substantial CSP business— than to facilitate enterprises’ sophisticated business-to-business Web-based transactions. IBM is approaching Web-based commerce as though all
applications required to complete a transaction will be hosted on one central site, the "cyber mall" approach, while Open Market, more realistically, assumes that suppliers of all the services
required to complete transactions will be geographically and organizationally dispersed as well as demand control over their own sites.
Finally, there are literally hundreds of suppliers claiming to offer the tools necessary to build secure, Web-based transaction processing applications. But, on close examination, one ﬁnds that
these newbees are most often offering proprietary, point-to-point solutions. While this approach sometimes sounds encouraging, it is too simplistic to

Challenges Ahead
Open Market faces two key challenges today. One, it needs to get its message heard over the advertising and PR blitzes of Microsoft and Netscape for what in reality are merely commodity
HTTP servers, not electronic commerce infrastructures. Two, businesses in general must further commit investment resources for production Web-based commerce infrastructures to fuel
Open Market’s growth.
The high-proﬁle press battle between Microsoft and Netscape for the commodity HTTP server is dominating the market mindshare of executives and mirrors the shift from Web technology
experimentalism to initial Web-commerce applications. Open Market’s challenge is to communicate to decision makers the value of its scaleable, open infrastructure as compared to the
limitations in mere HTTP servers and point solutions. The issue of mindshare is intertwined with the timing of the implementation of production systems — until senior executives feel they
can commit to a safe Web-based commerce architecture, they will resist a full deployment of resources for building comprehensive electronic commerce solutions.
For Open Market, building mind share, establishing more widespread creditability, and becoming proﬁtable are essentially the same core business challenge.
Conclusions
Companies of all sizes implementing their next-generation electronic commerce solutions should look closely at Open Market’s infrastructure approach in general and its Web-based products
in particular. Both senior enterprise and IS executives can better understand the dynamics involved in real world Web-based commerce from both Open Market’s vision and experience. More
clearly than most, the company understands how Web-based commerce will evolve and how it must integrate with existing enterprise applications.
New technology standards, new performance level requirements, and new enterprise functionality needs are a daily part of the life of an IS executive charged with implementing Web-based
commerce capabilities. Exciting innovations typically come from different suppliers and are not integrated with each other — they need a plug-in architecture such as OM-Transact to be
most effective for users. Open Market’s approach of establishing a ﬂexible, scaleable infrastructure can furnish the unifying platform for IS professionals to work from to meet the rapidly
changing requirements for best-in-class electronic commerce systems.


Finally, and very importantly, Open Market’s support for many-to-many Web-based transaction relationships is unique in the marketplace. This approach and capability has now been
validated by the most sophisticated and leading-edge CSPs and ﬁnancial institutions in the world. Based upon this already glowing track record in a rapidly growing and quickly changing
application area, IS executives would be highly remiss not to fully evaluate Open Market’s products for their own Web-based commerce infrastructure.
