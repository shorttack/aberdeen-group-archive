Remote Access: Cisco’s Untold Story
Executive Summary
November 25, 1996 — Dominating the overall remote access market and
undisputedly king of the branch-to-branch segment, Cisco Systems continues to
aggressively implement its remote access strategy with a sophisticated array of
competitively-priced, cost-effective hardware and software solutions. The
company is making solid progress in both traditional and new market segments.
Its aim is now squarely set on the rapidly evolving, high-end Internet Service
Provider (ISP), carrier and enterprise markets, where the company has quietly
scored some early "big wins." Cisco’s multi-pronged remote access thrust also
extends to the lower-end of the market, targeting regional and branch ofﬁces,
small- to medium-sized Internet Service Providers, and corporations building
intranets or seeking next-generation Internet access. Cisco’s attack plan in these
markets is centered on modular, cost-effective solutions that take advantage of
a full complement of Cisco IOS™ software features.
Aberdeen foresees that 1997 will be a year of consolidation and change at the
upper levels of the remote access market, as Internet access becomes a
commodity and ISPs and carriers scramble for marketshare. The dominant
business issues Service Providers face are straightforward. They must improve
revenues and margins by retaining existing business and attracting new
customers while efﬁciently and economically developing the infrastructure
needed to support them. To be successful with Service Providers, vendors must
deliver to ISPs the ability to enable new and differentiated services that can
meet emerging end-user needs — namely, security, availability, reliability,
Quality of Service (QoS) and management — and the ability to scale as the
number of customers increases dramatically. Only suppliers who understand
and can cost-effectively meet those criteria will succeed in this market.
Cisco has demonstrated a clear understanding of the wide-ranging needs of the
remote access market. With solid leverage of its core hardware and software
competencies, Cisco’s end-to-end remote access solutions incorporate the
scalability and Quality of Service required for next-generation ISPs. Based on
its performance over the past six months, Aberdeen believes that Cisco does,
indeed, have all the potential needed to extend its leadership position to the
Service Provider market.
The Changing Character of the Remote Access Market
As businesses today recognize the strategic value of quick access to
information from a widening variety of geographic locations, their focus is
turning to remote access solutions. Fueled by technology developments that
continue to reshape corporate information environments and the clamor for
Internet and corporate intranet communication, the booming remote access
market continues to grow at both ends of the scale. Aberdeen estimates the
overall remote access market this year to be close to $4 billion and predicts it
will reach almost $20 billion by the year 2000. The dial-up market alone is
estimated to reach over $5.5 billion by that time.
The changing character of this market is driven by three business needs:
• Business access to information services;
• Internet access to intranets over Virtual Private Networks;
• Intranet access.
The requirements of intranet access are now bringing ISPs face to face with
new solution requirements. Whereas access points of presence have
traditionally been concerned with modem and ISDN access, they now face the
challenges posed by integration and platform consolidation, modem and
network management priorities, integrated access, WAN switching, route
processing, and intelligent control.
Market Snapshot
At the data-centric, mainframe-oriented enterprise level, demand for remote
access connectivity continues to accelerate. Here, connection requirements take
several forms: branch ofﬁces and telecommuters seeking connection to the
enterprise network, independent branches linking together in peer-to-peer
conﬁguration, and several smaller ofﬁces connecting to a larger branch ofﬁce.


The enterprise-branch segment has been the sweet spot for Cisco. The company
has moved swiftly from a 50% marketshare position to one of more than 60%,
with over one million installed units.
At the low end of dial access, where Cisco holds the number two position and
is gaining marketshare, giving Shiva some vigorous competition, the small
ofﬁce/home ofﬁce (SOHO) and individual remote user market segments are
experiencing unprecedented growth, as dial-up connectivity to public networks
becomes a business essential. Driving this expansion are improvements in
access device functionality and scalability, now available at more attractive
price points. Growth in these three user segments has directly impacted the
highest end of remote access, the ISP and carrier markets, riveting the attention
of the vendor community and mandating a new set of considerations for vendor
and buyer alike.
Second-Generation ISP Market Demands
Over the course of the past eighteen months, Internet Service Provider
requirements have changed dramatically. The initial needs of the ISP market
focused almost exclusively on providing connectivity. Service Providers were
most interested in supplying their customers with access as quickly and
inexpensively as possible. Their view was that the only requirements for public
information access were hardware availability and PPP. Technology selections
were determined primarily by price. ISPs looked for best-of-breed products,
were willing to absorb integration costs, generally provided unmanaged
modems and offered no meaningful security. Infrastructure was not a
consideration at that point.
As the ISP market matures, Aberdeen has noted a shift in buying-decision
thinking. Today, Service Providers’ solution selections revolve as much around
expanding infrastructure port capacity as price. ISPs now ﬁnd themselves
operating in higher-density, centralized environments with networks composed
of multiple, distributed points of presence (see Figure 1). The sheer volume of
incoming calls cannot be practically or cost-effectively processed by an
architecture that includes hundreds of analog lines. Additional considerations
include network management and the ability to meet the requirements of
multiprotocol environments.
Infrastructure Requirements
In this environment of unprecedented growth, small-to-mid sized ISPs have
operated in a situation analogous to trying to build an airplane while ﬂying it.
They are suddenly awakening to the need for an organized, underlying structure
which can meet their most pressing requirements:
• Increased bandwidth for remote access;
• Increased efﬁciency in managing the growing number of remote users;
• Investment protection for future technologies.

An infrastructure incorporating larger-pipe, leased-line alternatives such as PRI
or T1 that concentrate incoming dial-up calls (or simultaneous sessions), and
process them through a bank of digital managed modems offers today’s ISPs
and carriers more feasible, ﬂexible, manageable and economical solutions than
the current hodgepodge of unmanaged modems upon which ISPs have built
their businesses. Aberdeen believes that the majority of ISP connectivity
upgrades will be sub T3 installations over the next twelve months.
Another market-inﬂuencing dynamic is a customer base that has segmented
into two distinct components: the occasional user dialing in over an
asynchronous telephone line via a modem connection, and the more intense
user requiring the higher bandwidth of ISDN. As a result, Service Provider
requirements now include ﬂexible platforms that can accommodate both digital
and analog dial-in access. These platforms must allow a high concentration of
ISDN and modem users to be serviced in as compact a space as possible, while
still offering the capacity to expand the network as needed.
Differentiation: Requirement for Success


As competition among Internet Service Providers heats up, expansion and
consolidation of service provisioning will accelerate. The number of points-of-
presence (POPs) for which a Service Provider is responsible will increase,
requiring solutions that expand platform breadth, software functionality and
network management requirements. To stay in the game, Service Providers will
be forced to differentiate themselves in order to expand their business. This will
drive the need for value creation through the provision of Quality of Services,
including high availability, enhanced security and multi-media support. All of
these conditions ratchet up the requirements for a sound remote access
infrastructure. Price-per-port declines and infrastructure management needs
will drive expansion in this market.
This shift should play to Cisco’s advantage. Although the company was late to
the ISP market, it has developed a solid strategy to capture leadership there.
Cisco is aggressively moving up the dial-access chain by adding signiﬁcant
value that gives competitive advantage to individual ISPs. Cisco has increased
the capacity of its integrated access server, fulﬁlling the requirements of today’s
ISP customers and establishing a stronger presence there. Cisco’s multi-
pronged attack, featuring scalable solutions, service differentiation and a broad
menu of user-centric capabilities, ﬁrmly positions Cisco as a next-generation
intelligent network services vendor.
Cisco’s Unique Opportunity in the Dial-up Market
Cisco’s own extensive technology development and experience with both
routing and transporting legacy SNA trafﬁc over the wide-area (Cisco claims
70% of that market today), enable the company to bring a unique perspective to
the remote access market. In fact, Cisco is a remote access powerhouse
worldwide. Ever vigilant, the company has been honing its strategy to meet
changing market requirements and customer needs, especially in the extremely
dynamic and ﬂuid dial-up arena. Cisco now has a growing family of offerings
centered in the dial-up market (see Figure 2).
Because the dial-up market is characterized primarily by change, market
advantage can be gained by technology-leapfrogging. A fundamental element
of Cisco’s strategy is the timely acquisition of technologies needed to address
rapidly changing market requirements. In addition to its earlier remote access-
focused acquisitions of Combinet and Newport, the company recently acquired
Telebit Corporation’s high-density digital Modem ISDN Channel Aggregation
(MICA) technologies.
The Telebit Acquisition
Aberdeen believes that 1997 will be the year of "price per port" as Service
Providers struggle for survival. The Telebit MICA acquisition gives Cisco
better economic control of its modem-focused remote access solutions and the
ability to remain competitive from a pricing standpoint. MICA also provides
Cisco an opportunity to deliver IOS software more efﬁciently, both in a
centralized manner or with per-port processing, depending on product feature
sets.

The MicaBlazer solution aggregates both standard analog modem and ISDN
trafﬁc onto primary rate ISDN T1 or E1 lines, eliminating the need for
hardware-based digital-to-analog conversion, and provides a 400% increase in
modem density over typical solutions, reducing the cost and complexity
normally associated with central-site remote access equipment. This acquisition
brings Cisco solid integrated modem and ISDN technology that ﬁts extremely
well into its central site access server solutions as well as its modular mid-range
and Internet/intranet access offerings.
On-going Integration of Cisco Software Value-Add
Cisco clearly understands that the real competitive differentiator of the
internetworking vendor play is software. This is true for two reasons: software
content is driving down product price and software is the vehicle through which
new technologies can be brought to market quickly. True to form, the company


continues to pay close attention to the software value-add it can incorporate
into its offerings.
Cisco’s IOS multiprotocol routing software is the core of Cisco’s embedded
systems. IOS software has become the de facto standard in internetworking,
functioning as a unifying thread. Cisco IOS software provides cohesion to
otherwise disparate networks and facilitates a scalable migration path for
growth and change. It brings value-added intelligence to internetworking in the
form of Multilink Point-to-Point Protocol (MPPP),Virtual Private Dial-up
network capabilities, WAN optimization services, adaptive routing
functionality, reliability, management and security services, and scalability. The
result is a cross-platform software that enables the construction and
enhancement of a single, integrated information systems infrastructure. This is
cost-effective to network Service Providers and to end-users alike.
Importance of End-to-End Solutions
Aberdeen end-user research reveals that addressing internetworking demands
of both the local- and wide-area with end-to-end open solutions is increasingly
becoming a requirement for IT decision-makers. As networks increase in
complexity and scale, the value of integrated, end-to-end solutions in which all
the components can function and be managed in concert is the argument that
enables technology vendors to make the corporate "short list" of solutions
providers. Cisco has built its impressive internetworking presence on its ability
to offer customers integrated end-to-end solutions through both hardware and
software capabilities and now extends this capability to the Service Provider
community. Figure 3 illustrates the multiple beneﬁts ISPs can derive from
Cisco’s end-to-end solutions approach.
The AS5200’s Quiet Success
Forays into new markets pose interesting challenges and unexpected results.
Such has been the case with Cisco’s entry into the high-end remote access
market, which, up until recently, has been the province of a small group of
single-focus vendors such as Ascend and U.S. Robotics. This market, known
for its competitiveness, complexity and challenges, is now in the sights of every
major internetworking vendor and telecommunications provider.
Cisco announced its ﬁrst entrant in the high-end, universal access server market
in January, 1996. The AS5200 central site access server integrates the
functionality of stand-alone channel service units, communications servers,
channel banks, modems, switches and routers into a single chassis. The CSUs,
router and modems can be fully managed remotely. The AS5200 is based on a
Cisco 2500 engine in a new chassis with full Cisco IOS software compatibility
and functionality. This offering is entirely digital, with the capability of
integrating digital and analog mixed-media dial-in environments. Modular
boards provide 2 ISDN Primary Rate Interface (PRI)/ T1 lines with integrated
CSUs or two PRI/E1 lines and can support 48 (PRI/T1) or 60 (PRI/E1)
integrated modems. The AS5200 open architecture currently supports
Microcom modem management technologies; next-generation solutions will
incorporate MICA technology for increased concentration. Stacking capability
will enable the construction of sizable points-of-presence that can scale to meet
increasing access demand.

After an overly optimistic product announcement at the beginning of 1996,
Cisco came up against the realities of launching a new class of product and
some technology speed bumps, not an uncommon occurrence with a new
product launch. Despite the need for ﬁne-tuning, the AS5200, which has
beneﬁted from enhancements, still made signiﬁcant inroads and received better
performance reviews from third-party testing labs than its major competition.
This point has not been lost on Service Providers. As of October, 1996, Cisco’s
customer list of over 700 AS5200 worldwide customers included 10 major
telcos, 5 ISPs, and several IXC services in addition to hundreds of enterprise
accounts. Units per customer on new orders have tripled since August.
Cisco’s success in this arena can be clearly credited to its ability to offer end-to-
end solutions that lend themselves to integration, combined with well-
respected, responsive technical support. This combination is especially
attractive to larger enterprise, ISP and carrier customers, and has played a major
role in their willingness to adopt a "ﬁrst-of-its class" solution.
Fleshing Out Remote Access Solutions
At the mid- and lower range of the remote access market, Cisco is
strengthening its Integrated Services Digital Network (ISDN) dial access
offerings and modular access routers. These October announcements, now
shipping, offer affordable, cost-effective solutions which take advantage of
Cisco’s internetworking technology value-add.
The 3600 Dial Access Family
True to form, and with a keen eye to cost-effectiveness and investment
protection, Cisco continues to augment its solution set in dial access in order to
meet an expanding range of user requirements. The recently released 3600
modular access router family is targeted at regional and branch ofﬁces as well
as small- to-medium sized ISPs, and joins the 2500, 4000 and AS5200 series of
dial servers.
The 3600 series brings integrated routing capabilities to dial access users at
new price/ performance points. The 3600 can process 15,000 to 40,000 packets
per second. Its design eliminates the need to purchase separate equipment for
dial-up WAN connectivity, as the series highest-density network modules can
support asynchronous and synchronous serial, ISDN BRI and channelized
T1/ISDN PRI concentration in a single chassis. Additionally, the 3600 series
supports a full suite of Cisco Internetworking Operating System (IOS) software
features, including dial-up connectivity, LAN-to-LAN routing, WAN
optimization, integrated management and multimedia features such as Resource
Reservation (RSVP), Weighted Fair Queuing (WFQ), Real-Time Transport
(RTP) and IP Multicast. The Cisco 3600 series also provides enhanced security
features via CiscoSecure.
The Cisco 1600 Series
The Cisco 1600 Series modular access routers combine multiprotocol routing
and transparent bridging with conﬁguration ﬂexibility, affordability, and
integrated security and management. The 1600 series consists of one built-in
LAN and one built-in WAN port as well as support for an additional WAN
interface card (WIC) for future growth and new WAN technologies. The WICs
can be shared with the Cisco 3600 series, further increasing cross-platform
ﬂexibility and investment protection. Fixed port choices include a 10BaseT port
and synchronous serial or ISDN WAN interfaces with or without an integrated
Network Termination 1 (NT1) device.
The Cisco 1600 series supports end-to-end security with a rich set of Cisco IOS
software security features and will be able to offer end-to-end Quality of
Service with upcoming support for multimedia protocols, including Resource
Reservation (RSVP), Weighted Fair Queuing (WFQ), Real-Time Transport
(RTP) and IP Multicast. Aberdeen sees this combination of end-to-end security
and end-to-end Quality of Service to be a differentiator for Cisco that will
maximize customer ﬂexibility and investment protection.
Targeted at businesses connecting to the Internet or building corporate
intranets, as well as independent businesses connecting ofﬁces together, the
1600 series is designed for ease-of-use. An integrated Web browser-based
conﬁguration and monitoring tool, Cisco’s ClickStart,™ allows PC or
workstation users to install, conﬁgure and monitor a Cisco 1600 series router in
minutes with any Web browser. The AutoInstall feature allows router
conﬁguration to be downloaded over Frame Relay or across leased lines from
one central location.
Future Initiatives


All of Cisco’s business units are focused on the challenges posed by the
paradigm shifts created by the Internet and intranets, and will continue to forge
ahead in the areas the company as a whole perceives to be its value-add. High
on the list are security enhancements, including Radius servers, proxy agents
and encryption. At the low-end, ease of use will be improved by auto install and
PC cards that enable access options. Integrated ISDN and asynchronous
modems will be available at price/performance points both above and below
the AS5200, addressing the requirements for improved density, performance
and hardware scaling. To ensure investment protection and accommodate
multimedia applications, new IOS software capabilities and platform support
for voice and video will be added, as will differentiated services capabilities in
both hardware and software.
Aberdeen Conclusions
Cisco’s impressive depth and diversity in its remote capabilities have made the
company a force to be reckoned with at all levels of the remote access market.
To maintain its overall leadership position, Cisco’s Access Business Unit
continues to diligently do its homework, carefully identifying platform choices,
increasing feature depth and richness, and balancing affordability, ease-of-use,
management features and enterprise-wide scalability. Cisco has demonstrated
its ability to respond to the changing needs of the enterprise and Service
Provider markets with integrated routing capabilities and feature-rich IOS
software technology. After an overly optimistic product announcement at the
beginning of 1996, Cisco came up against the realities of the remote access
marketplace. To its credit, aided and abetted by technology enhancements, an
end-to-end solutions focus and engineering support, Cisco has neatly
surmounted these challenges. Today the company can lay claim to laudable
early market penetration in the enterprise, small-to-medium ISP and carrier
markets — penetration that has not been fully recognized beyond Cisco’s
corporate walls.
As competition among ISPs becomes more heated, the stakes move higher.
Successful technology providers must offer increased ﬂexibility, multi-
connectivity support, interoperability and enhanced security. Intelligent
software and intelligent network services are fast becoming the differentiating
factors for success in the ISP and carrier markets. Aberdeen believes that IOS
software will prove attractive to ISPs as they differentiate their services by
looking to offer more value to their customers, and that Cisco is better
positioned, long-term, to respond more quickly to the rapidly evolving needs of
this new and vibrant market than are its major competitors.
Competition in all segments of the remote access market will intensify. As
technology lifecycles shorten, guaranteed sustained leadership in any market
segment becomes less likely. Cisco has four very powerful weapons in its
competitive arsenal that offset a competitor’s ability to leapfrog into the
market: its IOS software, whose robustness and depth continue to be Cisco’s
value-add; the resources to acquire technology as needed; a very large and loyal
customer base, which Cisco has successfully leveraged moving into new
market territory; and an excellent reputation for customer support. When
coupled with Cisco’s hardware technology capabilities and trained on the high-
end of the remote access market, this combination is hard to beat.
