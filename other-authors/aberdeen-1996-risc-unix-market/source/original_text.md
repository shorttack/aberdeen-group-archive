Executive Summary
As enterprises around the world continue to increase their reliance upon Risc/Unix systems to run their business-critical production applications, Aberdeen's research show that the market
grew 43% in 1995 over 1994 as measured by revenue. Note that this growth rate is much higher than even the closely-watched PC marketplace whose revenue grew at a comparatively
meager 25% during the same period.
The market leader, Hewlett-Packard, was able to set a blistering pace for the rest of the industry by growing at 57% in 1995 to achieve an impressive 49% market share with $7 billion in
revenues. IBM, with a strong boost from it's high-end massively parallel RS/6000 SP system, achieved a 45% growth rate to remain number two in the market with a 19% market share. Sun
remained in third place with a 9% market share and a below-market but very respectable growth rate of 35%. The highest growth rate in the industry was recorded by Digital Equipment
Corporation at 73%. But because it is growing from a relatively small base, Digital's total revenues of $475 million left it with a 3% market share and in sixth place overall.
The commercial Risc/Unix market continues to remain extremely strong as technically capable enterprises rely upon this platform to run a new generation of client-server and integrated
production applications that take advantage of advanced RDBMSs (relational database management systems). Enterprises are investing in these systems to increase the effectiveness of their
business processes — they have found that there is a real and signiﬁcant payback to do so.
In 1995, there were two major alternative platform classes — Intel/Unix and Intel/Microsoft NT — whose leading suppliers had anticipated making in-roads into the highly successful
Risc/Unix market space. However the leading Intel/Unix suppliers — AT&T GIS (now NCR again), Unisys, and Sequent — as a group showed lackluster results as IS decision makers
continued to deem this approach to enterprise computing to be comparatively underpowered. And Microsoft NT Server, with the BackOfﬁce bundle most appropriate for creating production
applications, was both relatively late to market and experienced more technical implementation difﬁculties than its vocal advocates had anticipated at the start of 1995.
Looking ahead to 1996, Aberdeen believes that the market for commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix systems will continue to move ahead at a 40% plus rate as applications that were rolled out in
previous years continue to grow rapidly and prototype systems developed in 1995 are placed into production. We believe that despite all the hoopla about Microsoft NT Server/BackOfﬁce-
based systems they will not slow the growth of Risc/Unix platforms for business-critical applications. Rather BackOfﬁce will complement Risc/Unix production systems as workgroup ﬁle,
print, communications, and workﬂow management servers. Finally, Aberdeen anticipates that HP will continue to gain market share in 1996 as an increasing number of IS executive decision
makers decide to standardize on one Unix/Risc platform across their entire enterprise and HP continues to enhance its already comparatively strong technology and partnership positions
using resources the other suppliers simply do not possess.

A Strong 43% Market Growth During 1995
The worldwide commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix systems platform market grew to $14.3 billion in 1995 — an increase of 43% over 1994's $10.0 billion total revenues. (See Figure 1.)

Aberdeen's market deﬁnition encompasses revenues paid by end users for core systems platform components — Risc-based multiuser systems with attached mass storage, Unix operating
system and systems software licenses, and direct installation and maintenance service charges — used to implement production OLTP (online transaction processing), decision support and
batch processing applications. Aberdeen believes that the total market would be several times larger if one also included the numerous other components associated with a production

The Risc/Unix market's 43% growth in 1995 is truly extraordinary when compared to other information systems hardware market segments. PCs, for example, grew at 25% in revenue terms
in 1995 fueled by strong demand from the consumer market — a market segment which purchased no multiuser Risc/Unix systems. The total mainframe market may have had an excellent
year by its standards by growing at approximately 5%. But it would have most likely suffered the same double-digit percentage decline as in the previous ﬁve years if users had not migrated
to less costly to maintain CMOS-based systems.

Change Paves The Road To Risc/Unix
1995 was a year of change for enterprises around the world. After numerous years of strategizing and planning, enterprises in 1995 were aggressively implementing new multiuser
applications to lower internal process costs and cycle times as well as improve customer service and responsiveness. For enterprises over $25 million in revenue, it appears that the anchor
multiuser, commercial platform of choice for aligning corporate goals and objectives with new IS infrastructure capabilities was Risc/Unix platforms. More signiﬁcantly, almost all IS
decision makers Aberdeen interviewed in 1995 stated that when they were assigned to task forces charged with changing the way their enterprises operated in an increasingly competitive and
global arena, the selection of multiuser Risc/Unix technology to run new production applications was generally not even a point of debate or discussion — it was a given that this platform
would provide the greatest ﬂexibility for acquiring and operating production applications.
Three inter-related trends in 1995 fueled the growth of the market for commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix systems. First, RDBMS technology advances led to the opening and broadening of
new IS-based capabilities. Second, enterprises dramatically increased their implementation of a new generation of integrated business process applications. Third, Windows 3.1/PC-based
client-server OLTP computing became the norm, not the exception.
As the three leading independent suppliers of RDBMS technology — Oracle, Informix, and Sybase — continued to enhance their products' capabilities to provide new functionality in 1995,
IS decision makers selected Risc/Unix platforms as their hardware engines of choice for running the multiuser database portions of these suppliers' offerings. For example, one of the
relatively newer uses for RDBMS technology that grew in signiﬁcance in 1995 was data warehousing and mining on Risc/Unix servers. The Risc/Unix-RDBMS combination has become a
key IS component for changing enterprise production applications.
Enterprise managers and strategists have recognized the critical requirement to lower overhead costs and cycle times before their direct competition does. One result of this realization has
been an almost frenzied effort to implement a new generation of integrated business process applications, such as those available from SAP, Oracle, BAAN, People-Soft, Lawson, etc, as
soon as possible. The overwhelming majority of the successes for getting these new applications up and running have been on Risc/Unix platforms. Enterprise decision makers tell Aberdeen
that organizational change issues are so signiﬁcant — after all, backbone systems are generally changed only once during an individual's career — that they needed to minimize the
technology risks they were possibly incurring. And Risc/Unix platforms have been deemed the safe choice for supporting enterprise-wide integrated process applications.
Client-server applications using what has become relatively inexpensive Windows 3.1-based PCs to support an easy-to-learn, easy-to-operate graphical user interface (commonly referred to
as a thin client) became the standard for new OLTP applications such as order entry and customer support in 1995. Within this architecture, Risc/Unix servers, generally running an RDBMS,
were the preferred platform compared to mainframes or proprietary midrange system.
Finally, Aberdeen observed one interesting trend that fueled the Risc/Unix market's growth but also sometimes tarnished its image. Based on governance boards' instructions, numerous
trailing-edge adapters were required to acquire Risc/Unix systems with the mandate to quickly implement production applications on them. However, the IS organizations in these enterprises
were both ill-prepared and culturally resistant to changing their existing information infrastructures. While their acquisitions contributed to the revenue growth of the Risc/Unix market in
1995, many trailing-edge practitioners vocally complained about what they saw as technology and operational ﬂaws in Risc/Unix- and client-server-based production applications. But as
every IS veteran should know, each new technology solution creates a new set of unique problems to be managed.

HP: Going For Technology Leadership and More Market Share
With $7.0 billion in direct commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix revenue in 1995 representing 57% growth over 1994 and a 49% market share (up from 45% in 1994), HP was number one for the
sixth straight year since Aberdeen began its annual survey. HP's revenues in 1995 were slightly larger than the entire size of the market in 1993 — a remarkable achievement considering the
annual 35% plus annual price/performance improvements it has been consistently achieving.
HP continued to be viewed as the safe choice by IS decision makers for Risc/Unix platforms, in a market where safe was generally equated with goodness, for the following four reasons:
1. HP is the industry leader in terms of production systems installed. The large installed base of HP 9000s assured IS decision makers that they were going with the majority of their peers
in choosing HP for new applications. In addition, existing HP installations required expansion capabilities as more users and additional application functionality were added to the
original production systems.
2. HP's strong partnering program has created close and good working relationships with even competitive independent software vendors, systems integrators, and consultants. In most
cases, the HP 9000 is the leading and recommended Risc/Unix platform for their products and services. As a result, the HP 9000 is the safe choice for enterprises that are concerned that
they might possibly be dissatisﬁed with their initial choice of an RDBMS or application — they will not lose their hardware investment even if they change software or service
providers.
3. HP's technology roadmap is very compelling without being viewed as overpromising. The 1995 transition from HP-UX 9 to HP-UX 10 was well supported, including the availability of
upgrade utilities, and HP-UX 10 included many production features that were never envisioned by Unix' original designers and successive standards committees. HP's processor
technology continues to supply higher performance CPUs with the promise of 64-bit systems availability in 2Q96 and delivery of a microprocessor based upon the joint HP and Intel
architecture effort in 1998. New packaging, in the form of the K- and D-Class systems, showed excellent in-box upgrade paths for users who needed to plan for future upgrades. Finally,
HP's clustering products and Enterprise Parallel Server (EPS) massively parallel processor (MPP) architecture showed a continuous growth path for decision makers concerned about
high-end scalability.
4. Executive decision makers trust HP as exempliﬁed by Forbes magazine naming HP"Performer of the Year"over all other U.S. companies — not just high-tech. As the pace of technology
change increases and IS implementation solutions become too complex for any one individual to grasp, enterprise executives tell Aberdeen that they simply trust HP to do the right thing
by them — both today and in the future.
In 1995, HP appears to have appropriately resolved its own internal conﬂicts about growing market share versus proﬁtability, Unix versus NT, PC distribution channels versus HP 9000
channels, which technologies to invest in and which to depend upon partners for, and where and how to grow its Professional Services Organization (PSO) to support its entire product line.
As a result, and in conjunction with the weaknesses of size being experienced by smaller suppliers, Aberdeen expects to see HP grow its market share of the Risc/Unix market in 1996.

IBM: ISSC and SP Were Key For Growth
IBM's commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix revenues grew to $2.75 million — up 45% from 1994. Aberdeen was able to attribute this growth to the following three major factors:
1. The RS/6000 SP (formerly known as the SP2) system was aggressively sold to commercial accounts desiring the highest performance and storage capacity possible. Aberdeen believes
that approximately 50% of IBM's Risc/Unix growth in 1995 can be attributed to customers who wanted the MPP-technology embodied in the RS/6000 SP from a large and leading
supplier such as IBM.
2. IBM was unable to deliver the PowerPC 604 processor upgrade that it had promised by mid-1995 for the RS/6000 PowerPC 601-based systems. As a result, customers with successful
production applications unexpectedly needed to acquire additional PowerPC 601 systems to meet their performance requirements.
3. ISSC, IBM's professional services division, included the RS/6000 as part of a total consulting package for assisting enterprises wishing to implement a multi-tier, client-server
architecture as part of their IS redeployment effort. The customers' real value gained was in the professional services provided and the Risc/Unix platform decision was relatively
insigniﬁcant. Aberdeen's interviews with customers indicated that IBM would heavily discount the RS/6000 component of larger ISSC opportunities to make the total package extremely
enticing from a cost perspective.
Aberdeen believes that in 1996 ISSC's knowledge and experience with the RS/6000 will continue to play a major role in its success with customers that want as much of a single-source
solution from IBM as possible. The greatest concerns facing the RS/6000 line are the comparative lateness in its ability to provide users with 64-bit computing capability (not anticipated
until 1998) and the scarcity of installations that have managed to implement successful production applications on the SP.

Sun: Gaining Credibility — Just In Time
Sun ﬁnished 1995 with $1.3 in commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix revenues representing a slightly below market growth rate of 35%. Sun established the credibility of Solaris as a production-
ready operating environment and the scalability of SMP (symmetric multiprocessing) systems for business-critical commercial applications. In addition, Sun left no doubt that its systems
software would be able to lead the charge into Internet-transaction applications in the future.
Dampening Sun's growth was concern about the anticipated 1996 transition to its new 64-bit operating environment of UltraSPARC processors and Solaris 9. In addition, as number three in
the industry (with a market share of 9%) stemming from its relatively late start it simply did not have the installed base that HP and IBM did from which to more rapidly grow its 1995
revenues.

The Unique-Strength Suppliers
There are over ten additional suppliers of commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix platforms. Each has its own unique capabilities that makes it attractive to its customers. In this section, Aberdeen
will proﬁle these strengths.
SNI/Pyramid: Siemens Nixdorf Information Systems (SNI) acquired its US technology partner, Pyramid, in 1995 to ensure access to advanced Risc/Unix products and to establish a stronger
US marketing position. SNI is a leader in Europe in providing comprehensive systems integration services to its customers, including the ability to implement SAP R/3 within many
industries. However, the hoped for synergies from Pyramid's technology prowess and SNI's systems integration did appear to result in better-than-market revenue growth as Aberdeen
believes SNI's Risc/Unix revenues grew just 10% in 1995 to $825 million.
Data General: With $620 million in 1995 Risc/Unix revenues, Data General was a solid ﬁfth in the market. DG's strong relationship with its value-added resellers and large accounts appears
to remain solid and was reinforced in 1995 with the greater market acceptance of its Clariion storage array subsystems outside of the DG Aviion base. Dampening DG's growth to 11% in
1995 was the beginning of its transition from using the Motorola 88000 microprocessor to an all Intel-inside design.
Digital Equipment Corporation: Digital claimed the performance leadership role for Risc/Unix platforms with the highest TPC-C test results reported. In conjunction with Oracle, Digital was
able to demonstrate that it could deliver over 11,000 tpmC (transactions per minute under the TPC-C benchmark) using its fast clock rate, 64-bit Alpha processor and Oracle's large scale in-
memory technology. The revenue result was a 73% growth rate — the highest in the industry. Digital ﬁnished 1995 with $475 million in Risc/Unix-derived revenue and a 3% market share. It
anticipates growing this market share by several points in 1996 as it wins back the trust of IS decision makers that it lost in the early 1990s.
ICL/Fujitsu: ICL/Fujitsu continues to build on the SPARC technology it has licensed from Sun Microsystems with its Teamserver and Superserver SMP platforms. In addition, it is
aggressively targeting its MPP Goldrush into vertical industries for data warehousing and mining applications. Aberdeen believes that ICL/Fujitsu's 1995 revenues were $340 million —
basically the same as in 1994 — as it also focuses on its Intel-based product line and its larger competitors picked up Risc/Unix market share at its expense.
Bull: Bull's Escala line, using the IBM/Motorola PowerPC processor and IBM's AIX operating system, had revenues of $300 million in 1995. Most of the sales came from Bull's installed
base and through resellers competing on price against IBM's RS/6000.
Tandem: Tandem was relying upon its very advanced ServerNet communications technology to boost revenues above the market rate in 1995 — but delays in delivery after announcement
limited its growth. Aberdeen believes that total Risc/Unix revenues were $250 million with an associated 32% growth rate for 1995.
Additional suppliers, such as Stratus, Motorola, Concurrent, Cray, Amdahl, Harris, and Silicon Graphics each did under $200 million in commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix revenues and each
obtained less than 2% market share. While each has a unique market proposition, such as Cray for very high-end MPP and Silicon Graphics for visualization of large quantities of data, they
did not individually affect the revenues of the worldwide marketplace in a material way.

Where Is Intel/Unix?
The three leading suppliers of Intel/Unix systems are AT&T GIS (now reverting to the original NCR name), Unisys, and Sequent Computer Systems.
AT&T GIS' suffered ﬁnancial loses in 1995 to the point that it helped trigger AT&T's proposed break-up into three different companies. Aberdeen estimates that AT&T's commercial,
multiuser Intel/Unix revenues in 1995 were approximately $1 billion, down from $1.1 billion in 1994.
Unisys also reported a substantial loss of $745 million for 1995 and its intention to restructure itself as three highly independent operating divisions. Aberdeen estimates that Unisys'
commercial, multiuser Intel/Unix revenues in 1995 were $620 million, down from $840 million the year before.

Aberdeen believes that almost all of both AT&T GIS' and Unisys' revenues came from existing customers — very few new account names were added based upon the strength of their
Intel/Unix platform offering by itself.
By focusing on the high-end data warehousing market and taking advantage of its SMP technology, Sequent was able to grow 20% to $540 million in 1995.
While other suppliers such as Compaq, ICL/Fujitsu, Data General, Sequoia, and others participated in the Intel/Unix market, Aberdeen does not believe that any one of these ﬁrms obtained
signiﬁcant revenues from this market.
The total 1995 revenues of the top three Intel/Unix suppliers was $2.26 billion. This was approximately one-ﬁfth of the revenues ($11.05 billion) of the top three Risc/Unix suppliers.
Due to the relatively small size of the Intel/Unix market and organizational turmoil of the two top suppliers, the crucial support of the RDBMS suppliers continues to focus on Risc/Unix
platforms. As a result, IS decision makers for new application implementations tend to be wary of this technology combination.
Looking ahead to 1996, the major Intel/Unix suppliers will need to decide whether to devote more resources to their Unix or NT Server operating environments depending upon their
customers' demands. In addition, the delivery of PentiumPro may signiﬁcantly improve the performance of Intel-based commercial, multiuser systems. However, Aberdeen does not believe
that the technology and distribution infrastructure is in place for the Intel/Unix suppliers to gain market share at the expense of the Risc/Unix suppliers for commercial, multiuser applications
in 1996.

Where is NT?
At the beginning of 1995, many industry inﬂuencers predicted that this would be the year that NT would be replacing Unix for production applications. While many experimented with NT,
only a handful or less were able to overcome the technical issues required for bet-your-company (and job) production applications.
As 1996 begins, IS decision makers are realizing that to take full advantage of NT Server for implementing fat-application client- server architectures requires NT Workstation with the
Windows '95 interface shell which is not expected to be released until April 1996 as NT 4.0. In addition, Microsoft still needs to solve BackOfﬁce server scalability and stability issues.
Therefore, Aberdeen does not believe that in the short term IS executives will trust their business-critical applications to the NT operating environment. However, many do plan to use NT
Server to complement their production Risc/Unix systems for workgroup functionality such as print and ﬁle services and workﬂow management. As these NT Server applications are
interfaced with production Risc/Unix platforms, Aberdeen anticipates that they will increase the demand for more Risc/Unix performance and expand the revenues for suppliers in both
markets over the intermediate term.
In addition, during 1996 Aberdeen believes that all the major Risc/Unix platform suppliers, except for IBM, will be aggressively moving to 64-bit operating environments. This technology
transition, which will probably not begin in the NT environment for at least four more years, should reinforce the positioning of Risc/Unix technology for enterprise-wide business-critical
applications and Intel/NT for workgroup support.

Conclusions
The technologies, products, and services represented within and by the Risc/Unix marketplace continue to provide enterprise IS decision makers with the tools they need to transform how
their enterprises operate and function. As the use of information continues to increase at a rapid pace, the suppliers of Risc/Unix platforms see more opportunities than they can leverage —
including the continuing opportunity to replace installed mainframe systems before the Year 2000 issue becomes a crisis. At the same time, the suppliers to the Risc/Unix market are running
nervous and paranoid — looking over their shoulders at competitive offerings based upon Intel PentiumPro and Microsoft BackOfﬁce. And these products are often being developed by other
divisions within their own companies!
In 1995, the adoption of production RDBMS, client-server, and integrated process applications created tremendous momentum within this market. Those suppliers that invested during the
early 1990s in the technologies, products and distribution channels to meet this demand have achieved phenomenal growth rates in recent years. Yet to be successful in the future, these same
suppliers will need to continue their frenzied efforts.
Aberdeen projects that the commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix suppliers will have the following four product agenda items in 1996:
1. Advance the production systems functionality of their Unix offerings. This will make it even easier for IS professionals to run their operations with the Risc/Unix operating
environment.
2. Start, or continue, the transition to even more scalable, powerful, 64-bit Unix operating environments.
3. Increase support for industry-standard workgroup products such as Novell NetWare and NT Server. Since the Risc/ Unix suppliers cannot effectively compete against the breadth of the
Intel-server market's distribution channels, they must provide the technology underpinnings for interoperability with the major workgroup operating environments.
4. Invest in maturing MPP platforms for very large-scale enterprise-wide application deployments. MPP platforms will enable a new breed of high-end applications that users will require
to remain competitive.
Aberdeen believes that HP, IBM, Sun, and Digital will drive the progress of the commercial, multiuser Risc/Unix market in 1996. Each of these four companies will invest in the above
agenda items as their resources and core competencies allow to both grow the marketplace in general and to gain market share from the smaller suppliers and each other where possible.
In conclusion, Aberdeen projects another 40% plus growth for the Risc/Unix marketplace in 1996.

### Abstract

This Aberdeen Group Market Viewpoint (Volume 9, Number 5, February 12, 1996) presents the firm's sixth annual review and analysis of the worldwide commercial, multiuser RISC/Unix systems market. The study measures market size and growth by end-user revenue for core system platform components—RISC-based multiuser servers with attached mass storage, Unix operating system and systems software licenses, and direct installation and maintenance services—deployed for production OLTP, decision support, and batch processing workloads. The methodology combines primary field research (IS-executive interviews), revenue benchmarking against supplier-reported and estimated figures, and competitive profiling of the leading and niche platform vendors. Key findings reveal that the worldwide commercial RISC/Unix market reached $14.3 billion in 1995, a 43% increase over 1994's $10.0 billion, far outpacing PC market growth (25%) and mainframe growth (~5%). Hewlett-Packard dominated with $7.0 billion in revenue (57% growth, 49% share), maintaining its position as number-one for the sixth consecutive year; IBM ranked second at $2.75 billion (45% growth, 19% share) driven largely by its RS/6000 SP massively-parallel system and ISSC professional services engagements; Sun Microsystems placed third at $1.3 billion (35% growth, 9% share), establishing Solaris as a credible production environment. Digital Equipment Corporation posted the highest growth rate (73%) reaching $475 million, anchored by a world-record TPC-C result of over 11,000 tpmC achieved in conjunction with Oracle. Among the unique-strength suppliers, SNI/Pyramid reached $825 million (+10%), Data General $620 million (+11%), ICL/Fujitsu $340 million (flat), Bull $300 million, and Tandem $250 million (+32%). By contrast, the top three Intel/Unix suppliers (AT&T GIS/NCR, Unisys, Sequent) combined for only $2.26 billion—roughly one-fifth of the top-three RISC/Unix total of $11.05 billion—with AT&T GIS and Unisys both posting significant losses and revenue declines. Microsoft NT Server, despite widespread anticipation, failed to displace RISC/Unix for business-critical production applications in 1995 due to scalability and stability issues; NT 4.0 was not expected until April 1996. Aberdeen's forward-looking conclusions project continued 40%+ growth for the RISC/Unix market in 1996, driven by expansion of production applications already deployed and movement of 1995 prototypes into production. The study predicts HP will further increase its market share as enterprises standardize on a single Unix/RISC platform, that NT Server will complement rather than displace RISC/Unix, and that all major RISC/Unix suppliers except IBM will transition to 64-bit operating environments during 1996. The year-2000 mainframe replacement cycle is identified as an incremental opportunity. Four product agenda items—production Unix functionality advances, 64-bit transitions, workgroup interoperability (Novell NetWare and NT Server), and MPP platform maturation—are prescribed for all leading suppliers in 1996.
