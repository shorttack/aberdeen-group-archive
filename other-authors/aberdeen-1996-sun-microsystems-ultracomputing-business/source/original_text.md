UltraComputing for Business
Market Position
When Aberdeen initiated its coverage of Sun Microsystems' thrust into commercial market segments in 1995, we noted that more senior level business executives were putting Sun on the
short list when evaluating suppliers for large complex systems. In this Proﬁle, Aberdeen will review several important reasons that Sun's acceptance in the commercial market has increased.
We will analyze Sun Microsystems Computer Company's (SMCC) recent major rollout of new UltraSPARC servers and three major programmatic efforts that deliver high levels of support
to commercial customers.
Founded in 1982, Sun quickly became a major force in the explosive growth of the technical workstation market by dominating the industry as unit shipment leader. Since its inception, Sun
has also been instrumental in shaping the industry's vision of open systems and — in addition to adhering to all relevant standards — the company has contributed signiﬁcant baseline
technology to more than a dozen worldwide standards organizations. Sun continues to offer robust Unix operating system technology with its newest release of Solaris (2.5), based on Unix
V.4 with extensions that support symmetric multiprocessing and high levels of security.
Over the years, Sun has also played the major role in deﬁning the industry's notion of a networked computing architecture. This concept of the "network is the computer" was coined by Sun
but often derided by traditional thinkers in the mid-1980s — in both the information systems organization as well as the supplier community. However, enterprise network computing is now
considered a defacto standard for the design of distributed information systems, and Sun continues to provide the industry with a clear vision of network computing.
Based upon expanded marketing, distribution and independent software vendor (ISV) programs targeting new commercial information systems applications. In 1995 Sun solidiﬁed its
position as one of the top three suppliers of Unix-based servers and solutions for commercial applications — with over $2 billion in sales derived from this market. For 1996, Aberdeen is
forecasting another signiﬁcant increase in Sun's revenues from commercial accounts.
Organizational Structure and Financials
Sun consists of six major operating companies designed to optimize research and development and to leverage Sun technologies in multiple markets. Sun Microsystems Computer Company
— the focus of this Proﬁle — is responsible for systems and Sun's direct and indirect channels. SunSoft initiatives include the Unix-based Solaris operating system as well as Solstice, which
is the company's entrant in the network and systems management platform arena. Other divisions include Sun Microelectronics, SunExpress, JavaSoft and SunService.
Sun's business model is based upon aggressive pricing, attention to customer satisfaction, especially effective use of indirect channel partners, and a focus on its partnering with key ISVs. In
1996 Sun continued its accelerating growth in revenues and proﬁts. For the nine months ending in March, the company's $5.1 billion in revenues were up almost 20% over 1995 — nearly the
total revenues for all of ﬁscal 1995. Sun has derived signiﬁcant growth by re-positioning itself as the leader in networked Unix systems — and in particular by providing a variety of
innovative products and services for the Internet.
Commercial Computing Strategies
Sun calls its program for commercial markets the Network Business Advantage. It has three key elements: BusinessWare, Customer Management Solutions and Decision Warehouse. These
three initiatives are outlined in greater detail in the following sections. As customers are making the move to open client/server systems in the commercial arena they require a high level of
integration, service and support. Sun is responding to this requirement by heavily investing in its professional services units.
Sun has solidiﬁed its leadership position with Internet products and services for commercial applications. For instance, in March the company announced a variety of new Internet-based
electronic commerce programs. These included enhanced Netra Servers which are easy to set up, install and maintain — even at remote locations. Sun's professional services unit specializes
in helping companies to build links between existing production applications and new distributed Java applets. This will help customers take advantage of new Internet and Intranet
applications without having to abandon existing data center investments. Java has taken the ISV community — including the industry giants — by storm, and we can expect many business-
critical applications being made available over the following months. In Aberdeen's opinion, Sun has once again set the industry vision for an important emerging information systems
opportunity.
Sun is gaining momentum in commercial accounts because it is delivering robust production systems based on the Network Business Advantage program, because it is taking the lead on
Internet applications for commercial accounts, and because it provides customers with a robust family of high performance UltraSPARC servers.
Meeting Customer Requirements
Aberdeen research shows that IS' buying criteria have not changed much over the years. Those well know criteria include the ability to efﬁciently and cost-effectively scale the system as
needs grow, the ability to keep the total system availability 7 x 24, the ability to easily manage the system, and the ability of the software packages to work well with the total system solution.
Our ongoing interviews with business decision makers has shown a steady increase in Sun's level of acceptance in the commercial market — even before SMCC's major April 1996
announcement of its new product line and its emphasis on being the best at meeting the criteria. Thus, we expect Sun's standing among commercial customers to be further enhanced once the
details of this announcement gets widely broadcast.
Key Technologies and Products
In April 1996, SMCC announced the availability of six Unix servers for the commercial market based on the new UltraSPARC RISC microprocessor architecture. UltraSPARC — with 64-bit
virtual addressing and overall advanced system design — delivers twice the power, ﬁve times the band-width and at 20% of the latency of the older SPARC. The ﬁrst server shipments are
based on the 143MHz, 167MHz and 200MHz UltraSPARC processors, but according to Sun's SPARC roadmap, the processing speed will continue to be ratcheted upwards over the next
several years. While the UltraSPARC engine is 100% binary compatible with SPARC, the new line of Enterprise Servers is not just a simple update of the older SPARCservers — rather a
totally new design inside and out (See Table 1).

Scalability
Operating System
SMCC does not suffer from a server operating system (OS) identity crisis — it is a pure Unix play, with Solaris 2.5 at center stage. For the potential of any server to be realized, even the best
hardware architecture is dependent on its OS, and Sun's excellent scalability is largely due to its SMP implementation. Unlike its other top-tier competitors, Solaris 2.5 is ﬁnely-tuned for
multiple processor conﬁgurations.
Other top-tier Unix system suppliers have consistently struggled with their inability to achieve near-linear scalability as processors are added to SMP servers. Many Unix SMP
implementations require between 15% and 20% of the added CPU performance for overhead — and a poorly tuned Unix SMP implementation can use up to 30%. Further, recent NT Server
performance tests show that NT's overhead is so high that only a doubling of performance is realized when moving from one to four processors. Sun does not have this problem. It has tuned
Solaris to the point that only 3%-to-8% — depending on the database — of performance is lost by adding a CPU to the system.
Super Fast Bus
The new Gigaplane bus introduced with the Ultra Enterprise 4000s, 5000s and 6000s works at the incredible speed of 2.6GBps burst and 2.5GBps sustained — approaching throughput found
only in mainframes, and exceeding anything available from other Unix systems suppliers. Gigaplane can host either combined CPU and memory boards or I/O boards on either side of the
plane. Each CPU/memory board has two CPUs and up to 2GB of memory per board and a high-speed (1.3GBps) Ultra Port Architecture bus for connections between devices and system
boards.
For MIS shops confronted with continual growth in their computing needs, the combination of a fast bus with the well tuned Solaris 2.5 means that the UltraSPARC Enterprise servers can
scale on two important levels:
Constant query response time is provided as the dataset grows;
Constant query response time is provided as the number of users grow.

built with fewer components, and each has redundant power and cooling modules. The boards, power and cooling modules and disks are all hot-swapable.

The Ultra Enterprise 3000s to 6000s ship with Solstice SyMON — a systems management tool for predictive failure testing, capacity planning and remote monitoring. SyMON can be
conﬁgured to alert administrators to system problems, or the information can be fed into Sun's enterprise management information system platform, Solstice Domain Manager.
All the system features, and those noted below for storage, dramatically reduce planned and unplanned system down-time — approaching near-continuous availability levels which exceed
that offered by Sun's top-tier competitors.
SPARCstorage
SMCC's Storage Products Group is an independent business unit within SMCC, with its own P/L responsibilities. Aberdeen estimates that the Storage Product Group's current revenues are in
excess of $1 billion.

SMCC offers several SPARCstorage arrays for its servers, and uses an industry-standard ﬁber channel interface for very high-speed interconnects. The SPARCstorage Array Model 214 RSM
— and one that Aberdeen believes will stand-up to the demands of mission critical applications — has numerous high-availability features: hot-pluggable disk modules, hot-pluggable
redundant fans and power supplies among them. The 4.2GB disk drives that are in a 3.5" form-factor plug into the centerplane, eliminating the need for cables. While SMCC buys its drives
from the major suppliers, it has its own rigorous quality program. Since all drive suppliers have been experiencing worsening MTBF (mean-time between failure) over the past few years,
Sun's watchdog approach is crucial. This attention to detail means that the drives OEMed by SMCC are shown to have a better MTBF rating than the same drives shipped to SMCC's
competitors.
In addition to pre-shipment quality management, SMCC has a RAS Customer Action Team (Reliability, Availability and Serviceability) in place to focus on customers' storage-related issues.
Service and Support
Over the past three years, SunService has taken major steps in building a service and support organization appropriate for its emergence as a top-tier supplier in the commercial market. For
instance, it has added staff at a 40%-45% annual growth rate for the past three years, and expects to continue to expand the group.
SunService is also contracting with the major software suppliers — CA, Informix, Lotus, Oracle, SAP, Sybase, and Tivoli as of this writing — to provide what it calls front-end and back-end
problem resolution to prevent customers from getting trapped in a round-robin of ﬁnger pointing. Back-end problem resolution is fairly common; hardware and software suppliers work
together on a customer problem, but only after it has escalated to a certain level. Front-end resolution is more unusual; the specialist at the site taking the problem call is fully prepared to deal
with it immediately. Customer Service satisfaction surveys, both those conducted by Sun itself as well as by external organizations, show a major upturn for Sun. According to most surveys,
Sun is among the top three suppliers in customer satisfaction in North America, Europe and Asia.
Integration
SMCC has focused its marketing efforts on ﬁve major vertical segments — manufacturing, education, ﬁnancial services, telecommunications, and government — and is beginning to move
into others, such as health care and retail. SMCC brings to market its own built or bought products up to the application level, at which point it often turns to its numerous partners for
customer solutions. Sun also relies heavily on System Integrators (SI) — such as Andersen Consulting, Price Waterhouse, EDS, and Deloitte & Touche — to provide the implementation
assistance to the customer. SMCC places its solution packages in three buckets — BusinessWare, Customer Management Solutions and Decision Warehouse — although there is often
overlap across the three.
BusinessWare
Through its BusinessWare program, Sun and its partners provide horizontal applications — such as ﬁnancial, human resource, distribution and manufacturing applications — to Sun's target
vertical markets. Partnerships between SMCC and ISVs such as Oracle, PeopleSoft, SAP, Dun & Bradstreet and Baan manifest themselves in many ways, from joint sales and marketing
efforts to dedicated engineering resources to the operation of competency centers. Sun has developed a worldwide network of competency centers with its ISV and SI partners to provide
customers assistance in project planning, application evaluation, hardware sizing and conﬁguring, and project implementation. Sun also shares its operating system code changes with the
ISVs and they in turn share application code with Sun. Sun's engineers are then able to tune its systems to ensure top level performance and scalability.
Customer Management Solutions
Since it is vastly less expensive to keep a customer than it is to woo a new one, Aberdeen's 1995 report on Customer Interactive Software not surprisingly noted that 75% of the Fortune 500
companies considered implementation of customer management solutions (CMS) a top priority. CMS pulls information from a number of sources to either support internal operations (i.e., an
internal help-desk), or provide customer services such as taking insurance policy information over the phone and having premium calculations done dynamically. Sun, with over $300 million
in annual CMS sales, is a leader in this still nascent market, and has built a solid infrastructure to continue its leadership position.
Decision Warehouse
A data warehouse, which typically resides on a relational database management system, is an on-line collection of data speciﬁcally prepared to respond to user queries. The research recently
completed for Aberdeen's April 1996 Proﬁle of SMCC's Decision Warehouse program determined that SMCC has the requisite servers, storage subsystems, partnerships with multiple
RDBMS and software tools suppliers, technical and business support staff, and knowledge of its vertical markets to be a formidable force in the data warehousing market.
Customer interviews conducted as research for this Proﬁle conﬁrm our initial ﬁndings that Sun provides near-linear scalability as customers move from small pilot implementations to
production applications with steadily increasing amounts of data and numbers of users making queries against the data.
Price/Performance
Aberdeen believes that SMCC has a secret weapon which gives it an advantage over the other top-tier competitors: the behind-the-scenes Database Engineering group which has a mission of
"making the major database products run as fast as possible" on Sun products. The results of its work? As of this writing, Sun has the highest TPC-D benchmark numbers with Oracle's
RDBMS; the leading TPC-C numbers with Sybase RDBMS; and is number one with PeopleSoft's internal benchmark. Sun is also the fastest growing supplier of SAP platforms, accounting
for over 15% of SAP's sales.
The UltraSPARC-based servers combined with Solaris 2.5 have given SMCC a major performance boost, while its business model allows it to be the low-cost provider in the Unix market.
For example, being able to use all the same components in the various Enterprise servers drives down the component costs, while development of the Gigaplane drives up the system's
performance and value.
For small data warehouse implementations, or proof-of-concept projects, Sun even competes well against a NT Server implementation. But NT Server's scalability pales in comparison to
Solaris. As one MIS executive stated, it didn't hurt that the best overall solution for her data warehousing project was also the low bidder.
Aberdeen Conclusions
Last year Aberdeen recommended that accounts with sophisticated commercial application needs put SMCC on the short list of suppliers for Unix- and RDBMS-based systems, especially in
speciﬁc areas such as Customer Management Solutions, Data Warehousing and effective use of the Internet/Intranet. Since then all the evidence continues to point to accelerating momentum
for SMCC in the commercial multi-user application business market.
There were several important reasons for the accelerating trend. First, Sun delivered on its promise to develop a second generation 64-bit microprocessor, the UltraSPARC chip, with
dramatically improved performance characteristics — and a built-in graphics instruction set to optimize execution of the new class of multi-media applications. Second, the company has
shown its ability to package these microprocessors into extremely powerful multiprocessing servers. As we have noted in this Proﬁle, the Ultra Enterprise business servers were rolled-out in
April of this year. Third, Sun has followed through on its stated commitment to build solid partnerships with key ISVs and System Integrators. Fourth, Sun continues to prove its ﬁnancial
viability and long-term staying power with an uninterrupted series of quarters with increased revenues and proﬁts.
Given Aberdeen's assessment of SMCC's total offering it is clear that senior executives needing an information systems solution must look to Sun when developing a short-list of information
systems solution providers. When the executive decision maker adds Sun's business Internet leadership and its expanding capabilities in the professional services organization, the conclusion
seems obvious — Sun can meet current and future business needs as an established supplier in the world of networked client/server computing
