Taming SAP R/3 With Consultant-Led Enterprise Introspection
What is the big deal about implementing SAP R/3? Aberdeen has recently published "The Decision-Makers Guide to Professional Services Support for SAP America Implementations" in
which we have found that enterprises that properly prepare for implementing R/3 and wisely use independent professional service organizations teamed with empowered internal staff will be
successful. In this Viewpoint, Aberdeen focuses on one key aspect of preparing for SAP — determining your organization’s preparedness and desire to change its business operations.
Executive Summary
Implementing SAP’s R/3 throughout an enterprise is a once-in-twenty-year project. The operational infrastructure of an organization is transformed — how it works on a daily basis and the
capabilities it will need to be successful into the future. No such undertaking can be taken lightly — it must be done right.
While SAP customers tell Aberdeen that R/3 has more functionality than any other client-server, integrated business process application, they recognize that they themselves do not have the
expertise or number of staff required to implement it to full advantage. Aberdeen believes that the vast majority of senior executives within enterprises that are either evaluating the suitability
of R/3 for their organization or are already in the process of implementing it clearly recognize that the use of outside professional services ﬁrms is mandatory.
Even more critical is selecting the R/3 implementation path that best meets individual enterprise requirements. For example, departmentally-focused so-called "Big Bang" implementations
have an appeal to customers anxious about costs and the heavy use of precious corporate resources. Yet this approach misses an opportunity many customers need — that is to reevaluate and
recreate best business practices across the entire enterprise to become more customer responsive, more competitive, and ultimately, more proﬁtable. Conversely prolonged, blue-sky
reengineering planning projects have proven to be wasteful — customers are frustrated and a multi-million technology investment is underemployed.
This mismatch of an R/3 implementation strategy with sought-after business improvements can be avoided if SAP customers, particularly at the executive and senior management levels,
engage in very real and decisive enterprise introspection at the beginning of an R/3 implementation. This is best facilitated by an outside ﬁrm who has both strong knowledge of R/3
capabilities and the business grounding to help translate strategic goals into technology requirements. An early analysis will determine the best path for an R/3 implementation and drive
professional services support requirements.
Enterprise introspection is necessary prior to the start of an R/3 implementation and must honestly evaluate:
1. the corporate need, and desire, for fundamental enterprise change;
2. the company’s ability to adopt best practice processes embedded in R/3;
3. the availability of internal skills and resources to accomplish both anticipated business and technology changes.
Resolving these three issues early and honestly will set an R/3 implementation path and timeframe, dictate the need for consultant support and ultimately determine a realistic R/3
implementation budget. In other words, tame the R/3 implementation process before it can roar out of control
The Need, and Desire, for Change
The ﬁrst question that an organization’s executives need to answer is to what degree do they want to change their organization — and to what extent should this be accomplished through
professional services support for the R/3 implementation. The answer will be based on both an organization’s strategic need and enterprise desire for change.
The Need for Change
An organization’s executives and managers must be clear on whether they are seeking strategic changes or operating efﬁciencies to the business with an R/3 implementation. Strategic
changes are typically at the enterprise level while improving operating efﬁciencies are more tactical and likely to be at the business unit and departmental level. The difference between these
two holds signiﬁcant implications for R/3 implementation requirements.
Strategic change will require a wholesale analysis of a company’s processes and organizational structure. R/3 pioneers report that massive reengineering is required along with strong change
management expertise. End-user project control is critical — the questions that an R/3 implementation will raise need to be answered at senior levels quickly and decisively. Consultants can
play a critical role in raising and framing these questions. But they should not answer them. These questions should be answered by the project team proactively informing an executive
oversight group.
The R/3 implementation requirements for a strategic change include:
• explicit objective consensus on business objectives and process improvement details;
• evaluation of all critical processes’ ability to support a new strategy that will include changing operational tactics;
• coordination of reengineering planning with R/3 functionality;
• aggressive change management.
Professional service implications for a strategic change include:
• project control should reside with the customer:
• there is likely to be heavy consultant involvement at every stage;
• potential installed support needs for the R/3 system should be addressed in the implementation plan;
• a large, full-service professional service provider is the best choice for a primary consultant role.
Improving operating efﬁciencies requires signiﬁcantly less time and resources. In this case, the current strategy remains, business policies stay the same and process changes typically occur
within the targeted area for improvement only (human resources for example).
The R/3 implementation requirements for an operational change include:

• the customer is likely to accept most embedded R/3 business rules and best practices;
• the implementation is likely to be constrained to a single module within a functional area;
• a knowledge of speciﬁc R/3 capabilities is required;
• the preferred implementation methodology is likely to be based on speed.
Professional service implications of an operational change include:
• project control is not as important;
• there will be less project complexity;
• requirements for professional services support will be much lower;
• smaller implementation partners have the potential to offer tremendous value.
Corporate Preparedness for Change
Choosing R/3 for an enterprise means that, either implicitly or explicitly, an organization is primed for change. Realistically acknowledging the corporate readiness for change will help
enterprise executives determine the breadth of professional services support to engage.
Clearly change management skills must be part of any large implementation. However, managers will want to evaluate the degree that they will need consultants to prepare the organization
before the tough questions about jobs, people and functions are raised. Consultants can frame difﬁcult questions in terms of overall beneﬁts to the organization without having strong personal
attachments within the organization. Making decisions about jobs and functions will be inevitable and difﬁcult. Consultants can make the process more objective.
Demand for Process Customization
The ability and willingness to adopt R/3’s embedded processes will shave months off an implementation timeframe and will greatly lower project costs. Part of what customers are buying
with an SAP system is the accumulated best-practice experience of companies who have already begun the process of implementing modules. The R/3 Reference Model is a repository of
these processes available to companies. This Reference Model for R/3 3.0 contains over 800 pre-deﬁned business processes.
Yet as comprehensive as R/3 is, Aberdeen’s customer research revealed that most companies have unique needs that require them to customize some of their applications even with the
availability of this repository. Often these requirements for process customization are related to an enterprise’s size or industry.
The Implications of an Organization’s Size on the Demand for Process Customization
The increased functionality, new tools and evolving methodologies of the recently released Version 3 of R/3 have created a broader appeal for this application. Traditionally an option for
Fortune 500 manufacturing organizations in the Americas, R/3 is now being evaluated by service organizations, government and educational institutions, and smaller, mid-tier companies
who have the same information requirements as their manufacturing and/or larger counterparts.
Aberdeen’s research into customer experiences found that larger companies — deﬁned as having revenues greater than $1 billion — were more likely to employ unique, custom processes
than smaller organizations. The reasons early SAP adopters needed to modify R/3 are 1) these organizations adopted R/3 earlier with a smaller repository of best practices available and 2)
large companies are often industry leaders with a proprietary interest in maintaining their own best practices.
Often at the forefront of their industries, large companies need to reevaluate and rebuild their processes to better address today’s business and market needs and leverage new opportunities.
This requires a huge, and necessary, upfront investment in people, capital and resources that must be paid for in a reduction in both overhead costs and cycle times. As a result, some business
practices embedded in R/3 are a generation behind what they consider to be best for their enterprise and industry or simply not yet included.
Mid-tier companies will most often buy R/3 to establish a platform for growth. (Note that non-growth focused mid-tier companies have shown minimal interest in R/3.) Successful mid-tier
companies — often deﬁned as revenues between $50 million and $1 billion, depending on their industry — want to adopt most or all of the business processes embedded in R/3 for the
purpose of moving to industry best practices as soon as possible. Smaller companies also believe that these best practices will facilitate their capability to grow — their own organizational
processes will be less of a hindrance.
Industry-Speciﬁc Capabilities
SAP and its implementation partners continue to capture learning and best practices by industry — a real value customers see for continuous improvement using R/3 as a catalyst. SAP
incorporates this new, often industry-speciﬁc, knowledge into the R/3 Repository for best practices. The professional services providers frequently develop or acquire successful
methodologies and tools that are industry-speciﬁc and combine these with the R/3 repository. This knowledge is centralized in Industry Centers of Excellence (ICOEs) and is available to
prospective and current SAP customers.
Becoming familiar with the service offerings focusing on the needs of a speciﬁc industry is an important step in the decision-making process. Service providers offer value by coupling
established industry expertise with a knowledge of R/3 functionality. R/3 buyers may choose a service provider who has worked extensively within their industry and can better understand
industry implications of proposed organizational changes.
A good professional services ﬁrm should be able to provide clear guidance and support in evaluating the capabilities of R/3’s embedded processes against organizational requirements for an
individual industry. A professional services provider not only brings a knowledge of SAP modules, but also the collective experience of their end-user base by industry. This is becoming
even more of a major differentiator between service providers and even senior executives within the service providers.
R/3 Implementation Strategies
Deﬁning a company’s support requirements for an R/3 implementation is done by balancing the organization’s need and desire for change against the demand to customize processes — or
conversely, a company’s ability to accept the best practices embedded in R/3. The chart on the next page brings these two factors together to determine an enterprise’s R/3 implementation
strategy and support requirements.
Aberdeen’s research has shown that as companies analyze their initial inclinations for how they would like to improve their businesses against this matrix, they learn through introspection
how they are actually structured to
hinder change. Many organizations told Aberdeen that they initially were in the upper left-hand quadrant of this matrix and did not realize it. They quickly realized that they had to radically
rethink their approach to business improvement. More pointedly, attempting to apply R/3 using the same principles ones would for last-generation mainframe-based custom, in-house
application development projects would likely lead to disaster.

The power of SAP and its professional service implementation partners has been to convince company management to change their style from being an upper left quadrant company to a
lower right. True, the company gives up some of its uniqueness but it gains increased focus on doing those things that it should be doing better than anybody else in the world.
Companies implementing R/3 will fall into one of the four quadrants on the this chart. Each has its own implementation and professional services support implications. These implications
are detailed below.
High Change and Low Customization
Accept embedded best practices in R/3
when possible
An organization that can critically assess its corporate-wide process needs in light of the capabilities embedded in R/3 will see the most immediate value from its R/3 implementation effort.
Taking this route means knowing business needs very well, including sought-after improvements, and picking best practices from the R/3 repository unless absolutely forced to make
modiﬁcations.
Mid-tier customers building a platform for growth will ﬁnd that they may be able to take most of their processes from R/3 because these processes are based on industry best practices and
will ﬁnd themselves in this quadrant. On the other hand, many very large companies may ﬁnd a combination of embedded and customized processes must be used to meet their enterprise
requirements and will ﬁnd their strategies are based upon the upper right quadrant implications.
Professional services providers offer tremendous value to companies with high change and low demand for customization requirements. They each offer methodologies and tools that will
allow an organization to reengineer critical processes in tandem with an evaluation of the capabilities of R/3. Their SAP and reengineering knowledge, methodologies and custom
applications capabilities will be required. Change management, technical skills, and training and documentation will also be critical skills to secure. A professional services provider will
offer an organization a roadmap to put all these pieces together while minimizing implementation time and costs.
Companies that can manage within this quadrant are in the sweet spot for SAP implementations. They can move quickly and use experienced professional service organizations to tame their
R/3 implementations with relative ease.
Low Change and Low Customization
Match existing process needs against
R/3 repository of industry best practices.
Organizations implementing R/3 that have a low desire for either change or customization are likely to be looking to implement a single module to increase operational efﬁciency. In this
case, users will look to the R/3 repository to ﬁnd processes to best match their current business rules. Minimizing implementation time and costs is most important to customers with low
requirements for change and process customization. These users want to get a system that works in place quickly. SAP and current users refer to this quick and straightforward
implementation methodology as the "Big Bang Approach." (Possibly the "Big Kahuna Approach" would be an overnight enterprise-wide implementation of all 11 modules.)
Reliance on professional services such as reengineering, change management and custom applications development support will be minimal. Some technical assistance may be required
along with training and documentation. And a knowledge of the module to be implemented will be best secured from a consultant with module-speciﬁc knowledge. For this type of
implementation, a specialized National or Regional implementation partner will provide tremendous value. They have methodologies to speed implementation and a strong knowledge of R/3
capabilities by module and by industry requirements.
High Change and High Customization
Customize critical processes
independent of R/3 capabilities
Just a few years ago, this implementation approach was the norm for large organizations. Planning for reengineering was a discrete step that happened before an evaluation of R/3, rather than
concurrently. Today organizations that choose this implementation path are typically in the unique position of requiring proprietary processes for competitive reasons or are in an industry not
yet comprehensively documented within R/3.
This is, by far, the most costly approach. The implementation timeframe will be lengthened by the extensive reengineering efforts followed by the equally lengthy development of custom
applications. Professional services ﬁrms will be heavily involved at every stage of an implementation working and reworking the technology to best meet the process requirements. It will be
in an organization’s best interest to ensure exceptional internal project planning and control throughout the duration of the implementation. If not closely managed, R/3 implementation
efforts under the conditions described by this quadrant can easily go wild — become untamed — and even the best help of the best professional service organizations may not be enough to
rein them under control.

enterprise under these conditions
Organizations implementing R/3 that have a low need/desire for change and a high demand for customization are rare. It is precisely R/3’s combination of enterprise-wide improvements with
a packaged approach that appeals to enterprise decision makers and line executives. As a result, most companies that start with the premise that R/3 will allow them to easily customize the
software while making few changes to the enterprise tend to change one or both premises before proceeding to the implementation stage, simply not go with R/3 — or complain loudly how
R/3 is "impossible to implement and absolutely untamable."
If, however, a company has these requirements and chooses R/3 it will likely ﬁnd a strong need for ABAP4 programmers for applications development. These changes will be virtually
invisible to the end-user so there will be few change management issues. However requirements for end-user training and process documentation will be high and will almost certainly
require a professional services provider.
Availability of Internal Resources
Once the desired R/3 implementation strategy is identiﬁed in-house capabilities should be assessed against the required professional services offerings. This step should evaluate the
following two resources:
1. Current employees and their skill sets
Few companies will have speciﬁc R/3 experience on their internal staff and will, therefore, need to retrain at least some of the staff for this new system. This step must be planned in
advance. Many members of an IT organization may not enthusiastically embrace either packaged software or new technology. Providing a career plan for each member of an IT
organization in light of the proposed changes will be essential in keeping them on-board the project.
Hiring plans in anticipation of implementing and maintaining R/3
Current IT organizations will likely not have the comprehensive resources to support R/3. Before a consultancy is engaged
to ﬁll the gaps, companies should assess the long-term needs that R/3 will create.
Retraining members of the current IT staff to ﬁll the long-term R/3 system requirements is optimal. Following the assessment of long-term critical skills to retain, companies should create a
hiring plan to ensure that SAP skills remain at the conclusion of the R/3 implementation.
It is this ﬁnal step that will give the clearest outlook on professional services needs to support R/3 implementation requirements. By determining R/3 implementation requirements followed
by an evaluation of internal capabilities to meet these requirements, enterprise decision-makers will ﬁnally be able to determine the unique professional services support needs.
Aberdeen Conclusions
When you are evaluating your company’s implementation support requirements for R/3, you are really examining your entire enterprise’s capability to change gracefully. The combination of
R/3 and SAP implementation partners are the positive catalysts and drivers for improving your enterprise on your time schedule. Compare this to save-the-company-quickly changes made in
the face of hostile take over bids, stopping mounting quarterly losses, or dramatic changes in the regulatory environment. R/3 and SAP implementation partners can facilitate pragmatic, well-
reasoned changes for making your company a next-generation enterprise. SAP decision-makers would be remiss not to take this into consideration when acquiring professional services to
intelligently tame your SAP R/3 implementation process

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group's Viewpoint argues that successful SAP R/3 implementations require enterprise introspection before deployment: organizations must honestly evaluate their need for change, ability to adopt R/3 best practices, and internal resource availability. The study introduces a three-question framework for determining implementation path, critiques both 'Big Bang' and 'blue-sky' reengineering approaches, and recommends consultant-led strategic analysis to determine the correct path and budget before R/3 deployment.
