# Telechips' Access — the New Benchmark for Personal Computer Telephony Interaction

Surpassing products that mate Windows based PCs and telephones, Telechips' Access raises the bar for network client Computer Telephony Integration (CTI) through its ground-breaking architecture and the superior quality of its human interface.

## A New Breed of Appliance

The true value of Access products from Telechips Corporation lies in the implications of its underlying core technology. This next generation CTI product seamlessly combines a touchscreen Windows based computer with a telephone and access to the telecommunications infrastructure. The result is a fully functional network client that sets a new benchmark for ease of use, simplicity of network administration and application openness. Everyone understands a telephone and is comfortable using one. Access products make using a PC that simple.

Aberdeen research confirms that Access' transparent blend of computer, telephone, and network components is being developed into a variety of useful follow-on desktop appliances. This family of open standard products shares the following advantages:

- Devices anyone can use — with no learning curve;
- Built-in "transparent" communications capabilities;
- A sealed, tamper-proof container, yielding virtually no crashes or mechanical failures.

## Developer's Dream

The first Telechips appliance, Access, delivers true PC-based CTI in the same footprint as a business telephone. It runs Windows 3.1 in ROM and connects a business telephone to a variety of standard LAN/WAN infrastructures. In addition to its touchscreen, the Access user interface can include a keyboard, mouse, scanning wand and magnetic stripe/smart card reader. This gives developers maximum flexibility in designing applications and uses for Access. In partnership with Microsoft, Telechips has built-in full software compatibility and hardware support for Microsoft TAPI.

Beyond Access' standard Windows based platform, key benefits for OEMs and corporate IS developers derive from an internal architecture which includes an extremely efficient E1-like synchronous bus. The distinguishing characteristics of this bus are its variable-length time slots, and the fact that these time slots are addressable by all the processing and peripheral control elements of the Access products. Because these elements can, of course, also address the conventional data buses of the embedded Access PCs, the system can efficiently transport all the data associated with multimedia applications regardless of the source or destination — e.g., main storage, mass storage, PCMCIA devices, LAN or the Public Switched Telephone Network (PSTN).

## The Computer That Isn't

For end-users, the added value of Access is its user interface. Access sets new standards for interacting with an information device. The quality of the user's experience is one of full utility, intrinsic familiarity and technological transparency. Some users of the device at a world-renowned theme park found it so tele-intuitive, they didn't know it was a computer! Access' user-friendly values and durability have been proven in several field trials by first-tier entertainment, hospitality and insurance industry players. Following successful trials, Tandem Computer, for example, anticipates using Access as a ruggedized lottery terminal in convenience stores requiring inexpensive, easy-to-use lottery products that provide instant gratification to players.

## Quality Network Citizen

Based on our research, Aberdeen believes the markets for Telechips' "new CTI" technology and Access are broad as well as deep. Increasingly, technology managers in diverse Global 1000 enterprises share the common goal of automating lower levels of their organizations or of distributing their products and services to new legions of non-PC-literate users. They also seek network clients with improved stability and communications characteristics. Key drivers for this market segment include lower maintenance, administrative and lifecycle costs. From this perspective, the Access user terminal is well-conceived for client-server environments. Aberdeen concludes that Telechips' underlying technology is inherently leverageable into a range of enterprise-scale office products and specialized application oriented tools.

## The Benefits of New CTI

What is "new CTI" and what does it mean to IS? Most PCs with telephones aren't well integrated. Most phones with screens are built into proprietary platforms so they can't function as computers and become expensive application development platforms. What differentiates Telechips' CTI is the hardware bus design and software glue connecting the telephony, computer and human interfaces — a new design approach that creates a fully integrated communications facility. When coupled with its Windows platform in near-indestructible ROM format, controlled by a touchscreen and packaged in a sealed case with software that can only be modified or upgraded from the server (or by trained personnel), Telechips Access yields a level of robustness, utility and ease of use currently unavailable in the marketplace.

Access' lower (hardware) level of integration requires a sophisticated understanding of both data and telephony technologies. To date, we find little evidence that most computer suppliers understand telephony as much more than just another application. Nor do we find evidence that telco suppliers are capable of developing software applications and applets in a timely and cost effective manner. Telechips demonstrates strength in this domain. Aberdeen sees significant further opportunities for a company like Telechips that can be master of both universes.

## Conclusion

Access is well suited for dedicated Windows applications that require no learning curve, integral communications capability and a mechanically rugged device. Automating order entry or temporary personnel; delivering informational services to non-technical audiences and the general public and; providing kiosk-based sales or service support at the point of sale (e.g., as automated shoppers' helpers instead of store personnel) are just a few examples of Access' applications. We believe that Telechips' careful blending of computer and telephony functions at the hardware and software levels creates a new standard in how people interact with computers and opens the door for a new class of CTI.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group's October 1996 product impact report evaluates Telechips Corporation's 'Access' device, a touchscreen Windows-based PC integrated with a telephone in a rugged sealed enclosure. The study argues that Access sets a new benchmark for Computer Telephony Integration (CTI) through its hardware bus design and software integration, creating a new category of 'new CTI' suitable for kiosk, hospitality, and enterprise point-of-sale applications. Aberdeen predicts significant market opportunities for Telechips as the master of both computer and telephony domains.
