## Original Document Text

**Volume 9 / Number 6 — March 25, 1996**

The benefits of collective technological innovations for data warehousing have been typically restricted to well-heeled enterprises — and then only aimed, with few exceptions, at examining the enterprise's behavior and gauging customer responses to it. Only through considerable effort and expense have leading-edge enterprises been able to pry open their information strongholds and share and reshape the content with customers and supplier allies in ways that reflect the specific business demands of the enterprise.

Enter the World Wide Web (Web); exit the extraneous costs and technological weaknesses that have been data warehouse buy-in barriers for many IS decision makers and that have kept the data warehouse, and consequently many potential users both inside and outside of the enterprise, from integrating and interacting with corporate data in enterprises of all sizes.

This Technology Viewpoint examines the transformative combination of the Web, Relational on-line analytical processing (OLAP) and the parallel-scalable technologies that will bring Web-enabled data warehouses to the masses.

### Executive Summary

The true business-altering potential of data warehousing has yet to be realized. Not for want of an IS understanding of that potential, but because huge up-front expenditures — in warehouse design, desktop software, hardware and connectivity, dedicated lines to supplier allies and regional offices, and the almost certain obligation of having to replicate many of those expenses and procedures as the system grows — have forced most IS executives to restrain their budget-sensitive imaginations.

Here's an IS mind opener. The highly successful union of RDBMS-driven technologies, including parallel-scalable hardware, huge almost-any-size data warehouses, and Relational OLAP (which leverages the best of the relational world while giving business users multidimensional analyses of detailed corporate data) has adopted the Web. The addition, Aberdeen believes, will finally deliver true business content to a medium that has been virtually devoid of bread-and-butter enterprise utility, enable enterprises to build Intranets that offer a many-faceted decision-support savings and potential, and eventually conduct decision-enabled electronic commerce with valued suppliers and customers.

As the now highly useful Web conduit continues to evolve and Web browsers mature to a point where they seamlessly interact with the full complement of current desktop applications, Aberdeen believes that Chief Executive Officers and other high-level decision makers will mandate that IS build many forms of Web-enabled decision support.

### Filling a Void

The Internet and the Web has captured the world-wide imagination but not the confidence — or the data — of IS decision makers looking for the most appealing technologies to steer the enterprise into competitive-advantage position. A pay-per-view service, the Web links words, phrases and pictures from a document to related files using the hypertext format — and has created a powerful electronic medium, the enterprise misuse of which has been more of a concern to IS than the Web's potential as a true business utility.

But the medium need not be the (current static) message. Aberdeen believes that this vast empire of Internet computers will evolve into a secure electronic backbone that will serve both Intranet and business-to-business applications.

The natural information-gathering, no-update affinity between most decision support and current Web and Internet usages makes decision support the first high-impact Web candidate.

### Reallocating Costs

Aberdeen believes that the Web will reapportion warehouse costs, offering relief from many desktop software and deployment expenses.

For example, Web application development (although in need of some much anticipated augmentation) builds upon some fairly simple page creation facilities. While giving IS the ability to produce whatever look and feel the user desires — an Executive Information System (EIS) or simple report writer, for example — the Web also offers an equally strong mechanism for diminishing the expense of disseminating software to clients.

Moreover, a Web-centric scheme can reach considerably more potential users — and eliminate the biggest inhibitor for data warehouses for the masses by driving down the cost per seat for each user. Compare the typical $50 per browser (in quantity one) to the average cost of $1,000 per seat of most decision-support software.

### Relational OLAP: Standing Behind The Web

Relational OLAP is a value-added technology that strongly supplements the Web's core ability to support server-centric architectures that fit neatly with IS's continuing vote for RDBMS technologies. Leveraging the RDBMS, Relational OLAP suppliers create powerful systems that can repetitively deliver a sliced and diced (or multidimensional) view of corporate data and facilitate types of comparative analysis that will reveal the true business drivers embedded in enterprise data.

### The Fat Benefits Of A Thin Client

Because the browsers are still too rudimentary compared to Windows front ends — lacking taken-for-granted features such as OLE (Object Linking and Embedding), cut-and-paste ease of use, and the ability to inherit formatting — Aberdeen believes that these absences will limit the applicability of Web browsers for so-called power users, necessitating an initial coexistence between client-server and Web-based Relational OLAP.

Nevertheless, browser-based desktops already offer the ability to drill down, with extensive graphic and tabular reporting, as well as strong interactive features. But while the cadre of power users will likely have both types of systems on a single desktop, most other users will be able to function equal to or better than they can today with their current (if they have them at all) decision-support desktops.

### Signs of Change

Evidence of intent and progress is coming from numerous sources, including Oracle — which recently introduced its WebSystem suite of database, Web server, browser, and development tools that offer a multi-threaded substitute for the Common Gateway Interface (CGI), the now standard single-threaded bottleneck for communicating from a Web server to the applications behind it. Both Netscape and Microsoft also offer their own multi-threaded alternatives.

Other strong signals of innovation include the recent melding of the Web and Relational OLAP, a combination that will leverage mature industry standard RDBMSs, well tuned parallel-scalable processing platforms, and sophisticated drill-anywhere decision support. Relational OLAP innovators MicroStrategy Inc. and Information Advantage Inc. (along with database partner Red Brick Systems) are leading the charge. These systems use the HyperText Markup Language (HTML) along with authoring tools and translators, Web servers (Netscape, Microsoft, Spyglass, and Oracle) and cache-enhanced Structured Query Language (SQL) engines to create an architecture that delivers multidimensional analysis on the Web. In addition, many suppliers of other types of decision-support tools are now recognizing the benefits of the Internet and are preparing similar architectures to adapt to it.

### Restraining the Web

Nevertheless, Aberdeen believes that Web-based decision support must overcome several potential obstacles before becoming firmly entrenched within the enterprise:

1. **Security**, beyond the data transformation or encryption mechanisms deployed by browser servers, and the log-in authentication used to grant and block access to data
2. **Delivery of a multi-threaded alternative to the CGI**
3. **Browser maturation** to accommodate dynamic portfolio analysis in addition to current posting of static financial data
4. **Application development tools** equipped to quickly augment HTML with pop-up windows and radio bars that mimic most GUI-enabled desktops
5. **Better integration with desktop applications** such as spreadsheets

Aberdeen believes that most of these impediments will be swept away over the next year to two years, in turn driving the creation of new and power-hungry decision support/data warehouse applications.

### Investing in Power

The Web-related reallocation of expenses will place the lion's share of the decision support/data warehouse expenditure precisely where it belongs — in the data warehouse preparation phase and the particularly crucial processors and software that will be capable of meeting the decision-support challenge. Leading-edge enterprises that have built now huge data warehouse architectures have turned to parallel-scalable systems to satisfy the need for several core functionalities:

- **Scalability**: the ability to incrementally add processors and disk, particularly RAID drives as the system grows
- **High availability**: including on-line backup and recovery, component redundancy and failure
- **RDBMSs**: capable of supporting any-size databases, and the underlying parallelization of queries and query traffic
- **Systems and network management**: including performance monitoring, tape and storage management, robust DBMS tools and network configuration utilities

To anticipate the grow-and-change cycle of Web-enabled decision support, IS will also demand processor technologies that can support:

- The widest range of parallel-scalable RDBMSs, particularly for synchronizing with suppliers in business-to-business warehouse applications
- Operating systems refinements to increase the power of the RDBMS/processor relationship
- High speed internodal bandwidth, especially useful for information intensive queries
- Server technology that can run as Symmetrical Multiprocessors, clusters, or as Massively Parallel processors without recompilation
- Ability to dynamically allocate processor and bandwidth resources between decision support and OLTP

### Decision Support Pressures OLTP

As Web-enabled decision-support demands closer-to-the-moment information, IS must look for solutions that can effectively use parallelism to increase data warehouse refresh rate and decrease (and eventually eliminate) the data warehouse down time.

Moreover, the entire purpose of enterprise decision support is not only to find out what the enterprise is to its customers and suppliers, but what the OLTP architecture must become to continue or enhance satisfaction. In other words, Aberdeen believes that decision support is becoming so enterprise critical that it is rapidly being placed in a reciprocal relationship with OLTP.

### Ready, Set, Oops!

Prudent IS organizations are now looking for supplier/integrator partners with the skills and experience to clear the mission-critical path to decision support. Aberdeen field experience indicates that enterprises already engaged in mission-critical decision support, particularly on an Intranet basis, had not been prepared for the enthusiastic, give-me-more business-user response to decision support. Caught by surprise, many early-in-the-game IS organizations have had to add months to their ROI goals while retuning their systems to meet the fervent decision-support response.

### Tearing Down Barriers To Success

Yet the Web also promises to tear down barriers between the enterprise and its data, and between the corporation and its customers and suppliers. Aberdeen believes market-driven partnerships between suppliers is the most effective way to transcend the nobody-has-it impediments to decision-support success.

NCR and Relational OLAP trailblazer MicroStrategy are an example of much needed warehouse-supplier synergy. NCR has approximately 12 years experience in designing, managing and implementing data warehouses of all sizes, in virtually every industry. Its experience at Walmart alone gives it an industry-leading lens into the potential pratfalls of business-to-business integration. That leadership position is reflected in its industry specific hardware/software packages for retail, financial and telecommunications. Moreover, NCR's well proven Teradata database is backed by its Worldmark servers, which include SMP, Clusters, and MPP systems that can be used for all enterprise decision support, from data marts to multi-terabyte detailed warehouses.

By partnering with MicroStrategy, NCR has leveraged its ability to run other industry-leading RDBMSs with a Relational OLAP toolset that transforms the underlying technologies into an enterprise-class decision-support engine. Moreover, MicroStrategy also brings considerable decision-support expertise from its own installations at leading-edge companies and from mutual MicroStrategy/NCR engagements.

### Aberdeen Conclusions

The Web will remove many technological and financial barriers that have kept the power of data warehouses and decision support out of the hands of the masses, but will also exacerbate conditions that have already proved to be a considerable challenge to leading-edge enterprises.

Partnerships with world-class decision support suppliers can ensure success in meeting the challenges that the Web, Relational OLAP, and powerful parallel-scalable servers have introduced. These combined forces, Aberdeen believes, should encourage IS decision makers and planners to ponder new technology-driven enterprise competitive behavior and build systems at a cost that may be the only thing conservative about them.
