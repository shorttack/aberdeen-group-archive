Year 2000 Transition:
An Opportunity for the Creative Destruction of
Today's Information Systems
Many of today's enterprise computing systems will not be able to properly
handle the year-date "2000". While all IS departments are busily putting in
place workarounds to problems as they are surfacing, others are funding
massive conversions of existing applications. But is this really the wise
approach? In this Viewpoint, Aberdeen will present the management case for
replacing as many Year 2000 questionable business-critical applications as
possible as soon as possible.
Executive Summary
The Year 2000 problem is leaving senior enterprise executives with no easy
choices. There are no clear cut answers to solving the Year 2000 problems that
lie buried in most enterprises' information infrastructures. However, once
executives establish a framework with which to understand and work through
the complex Year 2000 transition issues, they will be able to better manage the
process.
The overly simplistic approach to solving Year 2000 issues often being
employed today is simply to attempt to convert business-critical applications to
be Year 2000 accurate and consistent. But this approach has several drawbacks.
Specifically, because it may not deliver Year 2000 safe operating environments;
there is a risk the programs will not be converted properly; and the program
files produced may not be interchangeable with business partners. And the
resulting applications themselves will provide no new functional benefits even
though the conversion will consume a considerable amount of money and IS
staff time. Please note that for European companies and those doing business in
Europe, there is a requirement that the new ECU (European Currency Unit) be
supported by 1999 - a programming problem that is similar in accuracy
requirements and potential business disruption, although smaller in scope, to
the Year 2000 issue - and this functionality must be added at the same time.
A better way to approach Year 2000 issues is to analyze what next-generation
applications an enterprise believes would be most effective 2-3 years out and
then begin the process of implementing them, including full systems testing,
before 1/1/2000. Starting from this perspective, the Year 2000 problem
becomes the trigger to start the "creative destruction" of many critical but hard-
to-maintain, last-generation-functionality, legacy applications.
Creative destruction is the process of replacing items that work with ones that
perform even better. As consumers, we participate in this process all the time as
we replace older automobiles that will get us around the town - although with a
high maintenance cost and a requirement for maintaining patience and handling
inconvenience when they unexpectedly break down - with newer, more
reliable, more comfortable models. This same process of creative destruction
now needs to be applied to many of today's production applications.
Aberdeen recommends that managers categorize their applications from a Year
2000 perspective. Some applications are already Year 2000 safe and up-to-date
and will be used long into the future; others are no longer useful and should be
discarded. Applications that manage internal processes and facilitate
organizational effectiveness can be replaced or modified as resources and time
allow. But those applications that touch the enterprise's customer (Customer
Responsive) and/or create files to be exchanged with customers, suppliers, and
government agencies must be perfectly accurate and may be prime candidates
for replacement.
Please note that because the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) is
still insisting that Year 2000 conversion efforts be expensed, not capitalized, an
enterprise's income statements may be better served through replacement -
which can be capitalized - than conversion - which must be expensed.
Finally, the time to start the creative destruction process is now. For IS
professionals to be able to properly customize new applications to meet an
enterprise's unique business requirements, follow prudent testing procedures for
bet-the-enterprise accuracy and reliability, and be confident that 1/1/2000 will
be just another day, implementation efforts should be started in 1997 at the
latest. And that means strategic planning and supplier selection should have
been started yesterday!

Check And Correct Your Operating Environment
First
Too many Year 2000 projects are focusing primarily on making existing
applications Year 2000 compliant. Yet even if these applications can accurately
handle dates, they will be worthless if the hardware, systems software, and
networking components on which they depend are not Year 2000 safe.
Aberdeen's research indicates that if there is one aspect of the Year 2000
quagmire where answers are elusive or contradictory, it is the responses from
technology suppliers to the questions, "Are the products I purchased from you
Year 2000 safe? And will they interoperate correctly after 1/1/2000 with the
technology components I acquired from other suppliers?" Quite frankly, most
suppliers do not know and/or are fearful of the legal consequences of stating
that their past products are Year 2000 safe - especially in light of the fact that
their products are operating in conjunction with technology components
acquired from numerous other suppliers over which they have no control.
And if there is one major concern that senior IS executives hold, it is for the in-
house developed systems management aids that have been created and relied
upon over the years. These tools have a high probability of both only using
two-digit dates and not being well documented. In other words, these IS-critical
tools may be ready to break and the efforts required to fix them may be more
extensive than anybody wishes to really contemplate.
The only safe solution to this aspect of a Year 2000 transition is to inventory all
the components of an enterprise's information infrastructure and request that
the external suppliers or in-house development groups certify that they can
handle four-digit dates correctly and what the format of those four-digit date
fields must be.
Note a big flag of caution, just because a system is using Unix or a PC does not
automatically make it safe. Many Unix operating systems and PC BIOSs were
not originally designed to effectively handle four-digit dates.
In addition, older systems from hardware suppliers that have withdrawn their
operating environments from the marketplace and now provide minimal
support may be riddled with Year 2000 problems that will be expensive, or
simply impossible, to fix.
Aberdeen believes that many enterprises that analyze the real and imputed costs
of - 1) upgrading their existing operating environments, 2) converting their
existing applications, 3) assuming a relatively high level of risk that they will
not have corrected all Year 2000 operating environment and application
problems, and 4) not gaining any productivity benefits for the expense and
effort - will begin to understand the advantages of replacing specific legacy
applications. However, new operating environments also require an enterprise's
IS organization to take a new approach to managing applications.
Check Your Customers' and Suppliers' Application
Environments
Begin by asking this leading question of your IS operations, "How many
electronic interchange files, on tape or through a network, does the enterprise
receive and send out each week? To whom? And based on what applications?"
Expect an answer of over 1,000, such as payroll records or inventory when-
expected delivery schedules, for a typical enterprise of over $200 million,
depending upon the industry.
These files must be Year 2000 and beyond accurate and they must use a
compatible four-digit date code. If not, you will be corrupting the data
processing operation of your most important business partners - or they will be
damaging yours!
Six Application Categories
From the Year 2000 management perspective, applications can be categorized
into the following six general categories in order of least criticality to most:
• Year 2000 safe already: applications that have been installed over the last
several years where the developers included four-digit date codes and have
certified that the application is Year 2000 safe. However, users must
ensure that the operating environment on which these applications run is
also Year 2000 safe.
• Shelfware: programs that at one time had a purpose but no longer are
needed. Some organizations have found that shelfware may account for up
to 80% of their application inventory. Best made Year 2000 safe by
discarding.
• Organizational effectiveness: applications that facilitate communications,
planning, and operations among employees, customers, and suppliers as

well as provide management reporting and results analysis. The failure of
these applications would be highly disruptive and costly but the
organization could still function.
Many organizational effectiveness applications, especially e-mail,
groupware, data warehousing and decision support, are relatively new. As
a result, they are the least likely to have Year 2000 problems. However,
while those problems that do occur will prove to be terribly frustrating to
an enterprise's personnel, they will not contain the potential for disaster
that the following categories hold.
• Internal processes management: all the applications required to run a
business, such as payroll, manufacturing scheduling, cash management,
sales ordering, warehouse inventory, customer history, patient records, etc.
These applications must handle the Year 2000 accurately but their related
program screens and reports may inaccurately refer to the Year 2000 as
1900. As long as all employees are educated about the 100 year
discrepancy, this problem can be cleaned up over time with no serious
consequences.
The key for internal process management applications is that they must be
accurate. Many mainframe application conversion efforts are spending the
bulk of their resources ensuring this internal accuracy. However screens
and internal reports may still show the date "1/1/2000" as "1/1/1900"
simply because there is not enough time and resources to fix the cosmetics
and employees can be trained to do the mental translation.
• External information exchanges: any electronic linkages and
interoperability with customers, suppliers, government agencies, third-
party payers, etc. As independent companies have meshed their operations
together to increase the efficiency of the value chain in which they
participate, they have also created date dependencies within their
applications which may be incompatible with the conversion (or lack of)
to new Year 2000 safe date formats.
If external information exchange applications are not correct, it will force
customers - the source of every company's revenue and livelihood - to
seek new suppliers and establish new value-chain relationships. In
addition, there may be substantial financial penalties if funds transfers are
not transacted in a timely manner or required information is not provided
to the proper government regulatory agencies.
• Customer responsive: any application which is used to take customers'
orders, schedule the shipment of product, calculate time-dependent
payments, create accurate invoices, acknowledge acceptance of payment,
etc. All aspects of these applications need to be Year 2000 and beyond
perfect as soon as possible to maintain customer confidence and trust.
Aberdeen believes that in the worst case senior executives and their
enterprises may face liability litigation, if customer responsive
applications are not Year 2000 correct. And in the best case, companies
with Year 2000 accurate customer responsive applications will have a
noticeable and immediate advantage over competitors who do not. This
class of applications must be correct -anything less would be dereliction of
duty.
Why Testing and Not Testing Are High Risk And
Cost
The logical solution to managing through the Year 2000 transition would seem
to be fix, test, correct, and retest. And self-contained operations are taking that
course of action, often with very unexpected results. For example, one
company found that its installed software reported that the license the user
acquired was no longer valid. And a British bank related how its system began
printing out reports in older formats that included the now obsolete schilling.
But the real problems to testing for most enterprises are: 1) any test on
production systems creates a risk of corrupting business-critical applications
and data, and 2) the cost and effort to exactly replicate an enterprise's operating
environment to test for Year 2000 problems is nearly equivalent to that
necessary to originally put it in place.
As a result, most enterprises will be testing their information infrastructure's
Year 2000 compliance starting on 1/1/2000. And as many may recall, the 1996
Atlanta Olympics were forced to take the same information systems approach
to testing - the only real test was the first day of the Olympics. While 95%+ of
the systems worked as promised, the applications that were developed to
support one major and vocal customer group - journalists - did not work as
anticipated. The black-eye that resulted will be remembered for many years to
come.
This inability to effectively test legacy systems for Year 2000 compliance and
accuracy before they become Year 2000 production systems should be of deep
concern to executives. The risk associated with the inability to comprehensively

test converted systems is yet another reason Aberdeen has come to believe the
creative destruction approach has tremendous appeal when it can be followed.
Replace or Convert: The Pros and Cons
To ensure that executives have an objective perspective on approaches to the
Year 2000 transition management issues, they need to be familiar with the most
significant advantages and disadvantages of both the replacement and
conversion options.
The key realistic reasons for converting applications are:
• There are no application packages available from independent software
vendors (ISVs) that can meet the enterprise's functional requirements;
• The IS department cannot recreate the business logic contained in the
existing application and therefore must do a conversion to maintain
current business rules within the organization;
• The application's functionality has already been frozen and it is more
convenient and less expensive to do a quick and dirty conversion than to
redesign associated business processes with a new ISV-supplied
application.
The negatives for conversion are:
• High and unascertainable cost that will be immediately reflected on the
enterprise's income statement - with no associated enterprise benefit or
improvement;
• Relatively high risk that the systems software upon which the converted
application runs and other software with which the application
interoperates will not be Year 2000 trustworthy;
• The lost opportunity cost could be very damaging. As competitors are
improving their information systems to lower internal process costs or
better meet market demands, the company that is focusing its resources on
application conversion is losing its relative competitive competency in
information systems.
Taking the same summary approach to the creative destruction/replacement
approach, the pros are:
• If implemented correctly with more effective processes, the enterprise
will gain an intermediate-to-long-term operational advantage;
• Much lower risk that an underlying systems software problem will create
a systems failure that will disrupt or stop operations;
• May well prove to have a better ROI in terms of application acquisition,
on-going maintenance, internal business disruption, customer/supplier
relationships, and potential liability or damages litigation.
The cons to replacement are:
• Requires a once-in-a-generation change in the way the enterprise
operates to adopt the best practices that are included in today's latest-and-
greatest database-centric applications;
• Requires IS to make strategic information infrastructure choices now - at
a time when technology is changing at a more rapid rate than ever before -
and acquire the necessary skill sets to successfully implement these
changes;
• The investment costs of replacement may be higher than the initially
projected expense costs of conversion.
Please note that the replacement approach becomes a management issue while
the conversion approach is being typically relegated to an IS technology and
financial problem.

Aberdeen's Recommended Course of Actions - and
Why
The first action an executive needs to take is to assess his or her enterprise's
Year 2000 vulnerabilities and options. This assessment should best be
performed with the assistance of an objective, knowledgeable third-party
professional service organization. Unfortunately, internal organizations tend to
focus primarily on repairing existing applications and to underestimate total
conversion costs, including updating operating environments. For example, a
large utility's internal IS organization initially estimated a $5 million Year 2000
transition cost but this was soon upped to a $15 million estimate after a more
experienced assessment team performed an expanded evaluation of the
situation.
The next step is to consider the opportunities presented by redesigning the
enterprise's applications to be the way they should be to compete in tomorrow's
business environment - and the threats created, if others do so first.
Then the key question becomes clear. Can the organization afford the delay of
conversion (essentially a risky two-year moratorium on advancing enterprise IS
capabilities) and the resulting delay in improving organizational effectiveness
compared to replacement? Aberdeen believes that from a business perspective,
replacement will often be the obvious choice.
Very importantly, now the issues of selecting technology components, time
scheduling, budgeting, and funding come into the picture. But the one deadline
that must be met will be to have Year 2000 safe, operational and tested
production systems before 1/1/2000.
Information Infrastructure Considerations
The larger an organization, the more complex its information infrastructure of
inter-dependent applications, databases, networking, management-control
systems and procedures, and staff specialties. Changing and realigning the
infrastructure within a few years may be possible in smaller-to-medium-sized
organizations but may be impossible for Global 2000-sized enterprises.
As a result, much of the creative destruction process associated with the Year
2000 should be application focused. It should center on business-critical
applications and the specific operating environment platforms they will run on.
Attempting to successfully transform the entire information infrastructure of a
very large organization between now and 1/1/2000 would be an unrealistic
objective.
Summary Observations
Many of today's leading manufacturing companies have already begun the
process of implementing a new generation of process-integrated enterprise
resource planning applications to lower costs and decrease cycle times -
especially before the competition does. As a result, many components, but not
all, within their information infrastructures are already being upgraded to Year
2000 compliance.
What others can learn from the experiences of these manufacturing companies
is that internal staff simply cannot manage and conduct a major enterprise-
wide, production application implementation project without the assistance of
objective, experienced third parties. And some organizations are particularly
resistant to change - to the point of being unable to successfully accomplish the
transition. Please see Aberdeen's Executive Viewpoint, How Enterprise
Executives Play a Pivotal Role in the Year 2000 Solution, 10/3/96, for a
comprehensive framework of best practice change recommendations for Year
2000 transitions.
A second observation is that most - not all - hardware and software suppliers do
not seem to be proactively helping their installed base of customers understand
the Year 2000 issues as they relate to their products. In reality, information

technology suppliers do not want to be the bearer of bad news to business unit
executives who have for too long ignored or not heard their own IS
organization's warnings about impending Year 2000 problems. This is
unfortunate because it will destroy much of the trust and confidence that has
been established between suppliers and customers.
A third comment is that managing the Year 2000 transition will be difficult for
any individual. Yet there is a tremendous amount of personal responsibility
being delegated to the individuals charged with ensuring that their enterprises
will be functioning smoothly while wisely correcting the entire two-digit date
problem. And on 1/1/2000, all the senior officers of an enterprise, not just IS
managers, will be viewed as being responsible for the success or failure of their
Year 2000 transition.
A fourth consideration is that for European companies, and those doing
business in Europe, the Year 2000 transition also means the requirement to
concurrently handle both national currencies and ECUs in their applications.
This dramatically increases the programming efforts required for converting
enterprise-wide, production applications to be viable in the future.
And finally, the technology aspects of cutting over to new Year 2000 safe
systems will take time. The latest that new production systems should be in
place and running an enterprise's operations is December 1, 1998 to permit 13
month's of operations (the typical annual financial cycle) until 1/1/2000. And
these systems need to be running in parallel with existing production systems
for at least 3 months prior to crossover - or September 1, 1998. And it will take
at least 12-18 months to customize the new applications to meet the
organization's optimum process requirements and to train employees on new
methods. This means that acquisition decisions must be made before September
1997. There is only a limited time to evaluate your options.
Aberdeen Conclusions
Aberdeen views the Year 2000 issue like a slowly rising spring flood from the
winter snow run-offs and early showers. The problems are already surfacing in
the form of applications that simply cannot handle dates starting with "20" - the
initial rising of the waters. Yet looking upstream, one can see that the river
banks (professional IS staff, both internal and external) do not have the capacity
to handle all the water (management and technology issues surrounding an
enterprise's Year 2000 transition) that is coming down. After the flood
(1/1/2000), the weaker structures (enterprises) are typically washed away or
damaged beyond usefulness while the surviving ones, typically built by their
owners to withstand flooding in the first place, will be improved even more.
Aberdeen believes that in the face of the Year 2000 problem, when possible, it
is time for enterprises to tear down their old but functioning applications - and
replace them with strong, modern ones. Through the process of creative
destruction now, the enterprise will be prepared to satisfy the unmet customer
and market needs left by enterprises that are not successful at managing their
Year 2000 transition within three short years.

### Abstract

Aberdeen Group argues that the Year 2000 computing problem presents enterprises with a strategic opportunity to replace outdated legacy applications rather than merely patching them—a process it terms 'creative destruction.' The study provides a six-category framework for classifying applications by Y2K risk and business criticality, recommends application replacement over conversion where possible, and sets out a critical timeline requiring acquisition decisions by September 1997 and production cutover by December 1998.
