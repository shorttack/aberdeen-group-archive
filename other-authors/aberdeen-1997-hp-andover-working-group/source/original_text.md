## HP's Andover Working Group Makes Significant Headway in Establishing Interoperability in Healthcare Computing

**April 7, 1997** — Hewlett-Packard recently announced the 1996 accomplishments of The Andover Working Group, an initiative comprised of technology suppliers, medical equipment vendors, pharmaceutical companies and healthcare providers. Increasing competitive pressures in healthcare are putting a premium on information availability and the effective implementation of information systems standards. The Andover Working Group is bringing long-awaited standards-based interoperability to the healthcare industry. Since its inception in March 1996, the Group's mantra has been to enhance and improve existing information interchange standards. Aberdeen believes that the Group's efforts promise to vastly improve the flow of data across the healthcare continuum - all contributing to a higher level of care quality and reduced costs.

HP spearheaded the Andover Working Group's launch and the company's strong leadership has helped the Group grow in both size and influence. Several key objectives have been reached by the Andover Working Group in the past 12 months. They include:

- **Delivering on the Promise** - Alpha software for the HL7 standard was distributed to core members in August as promised, and the first proof-of-concept demonstration took place as planned in October. HP's ability to create a truly collaborative environment among the Group's core members was a key success factor in its ability to meet all release dates.
- **Exponential Membership Growth** - Today the Andover Working Group is comprised of 18 core members and nearly 200 supporting members - up from 12 and 18 respectively in March 1996. Just as important, its members represent a wide range of healthcare market segments and include numerous influential market leaders such as Eli Lilly, Andersen Consulting, MedicaLogic, and Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, with Microsoft on board as a supporting member.
- **Setting the Stage for Tomorrow** - While establishing a solid technical foundation and garnering significant industry support in 1996, the Group also set its sights on two additional standards initiatives for 1997 that include DICOM for imaging and IEEE MIB for medical devices.

In addition to discussing the Andover Working Group's vision for IS compatibility in the healthcare industry, this Aberdeen Announcement Profile addresses what Aberdeen believes is HP's strong value proposition in healthcare above and beyond its role as the catalyst in the Andover Working Group's success.

### State of the Industry

The ultimate objective of healthcare information systems interoperability is clear and compelling - to enable healthcare providers to transmit appropriate and relevant information to other locations at the right time (and at lower costs than current integration methods), thereby improving care quality. The problem has always been that information interchange standards have not always translated into real-world communication between applications.

Two monumental challenges face the healthcare community in its effort to establish information systems compatibility. The first is that healthcare environments are extremely distributed. In every local community there are multiple sites - hospitals, clinics, labs, pharmacies, etc. - that conduct numerous clinical and administrative activities. Compounding the challenge is that these computing infrastructures are rarely the same. The continued consolidation among care-providing organizations only serves to increase the pressure on sharing data across multiple disparate information systems infrastructures and applications.

A second challenge involves the implementation of standards. Incomplete standards have been the norm in the past because user-defined variables within specific applications make plug-and-play data interchange impossible. Even if two applications are built according to an existing standard, user-defined modifications to the applications often prohibit communication between the two. HP organized the Andover Working Group to make existing data interchange specifications relevant and useful by enabling standardization to take place at the implementation level.

### The Andover Working Group - Turning Vision into Deliverables

HP and the Andover Working Group created a model, the Enterprise Communication Framework (ECF), by which these standards can be made effective and put to work in today's changing healthcare settings. In March 1996 HP set out to develop an ECF that encapsulates existing healthcare data interchange standards. Within this framework are Information Models whose rules ensure consistency within the user-defined fields that in the past have prevented effective communication between applications.

The ECF eliminates several levels of development complexity - a major obstacle in previous standardization efforts. Existing standards do facilitate the exchange of data, however direct communication is not possible between applications that have different implementations of the standard. By encapsulating existing standard message profile specifications into software objects called Enterprise Communicators, application developers do not need to completely understand the complex standards and rewrite them into their applications. This code is packaged within the Enterprise Communicator and embedded into third-party applications as a software component. This not only dramatically increases the likelihood of true interoperability between applications, but also significantly reduces application development and systems integration timeframes.

Last year Aberdeen predicted that the Andover Working Group's Enterprise Communicators would bring application interoperability closer to becoming a reality than ever before. In an aggressive rollout strategy, the Group initially targeted Health Level 7 (HL7), the existing standard for formatting, transmitting, and receiving textual data as the first standard to be incorporated into the ECF Version 1.0. This goal was accomplished when alpha software was delivered to the core members in August 1996 and included the Information Model that ensures implementation, not just specification, consistency.

### High Expectations for 1997 and Beyond

The ECF for HL7 today sits in the hands of the Group's charter core members. The collaborative development efforts of these organizations will allow the ECF to be commercially available by the end of this year and fully embedded within the applications of those leading healthcare information systems suppliers in 1998. The core membership for 1997 has expanded and now boasts the clout of HBO & Company, KPMG Peat Marwick, Lockheed Martin Communications, and six others to date. Each core member organization commits a full-time senior architect to the Andover Working Group for one year and agrees to use the ECF in its products.

At the HIMSS '97 Exhibition in February, the Andover Working Group revealed its plans to address two additional standards beginning in 1997:

- **DICOM** - The Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine standard outlines the means of formatting and exchanging images such as X-rays and ultrasound images. Aberdeen recommends that those ISVs developing electronic patient records take a close look at the Andover Working Group's activities, as the simultaneous implementation of HL7 and DICOM specifications adds a new - and highly desirable - dimension to the electronic patient record.
- **IEEE MIB** - The IEEE Medical Information Bus specifies the standards for establishing the interconnectivity and interoperability of medical devices and computerized hospital information systems. Aberdeen research indicates that the ability to better manage and share data that originates within medical devices is an important item of interest for care-providing CIOs.

### Microsoft's Healthcare Users Group Endorses the Andover Working Group

Microsoft's Healthcare Users Group (MS-HUG) and the Andover Working Group have formed an active affiliation that calls for the Andover Working Group to share its HL7 ECF specifications with MS-HUG's ActiveX for Healthcare Committee. ActiveX plays a significant role in the Andover Working Group's efforts to deliver plug-and-play interoperability through standardized Enterprise Communicators.

Aberdeen believes that this relationship transcends what have been common interoperability interests in past standardization efforts. The two highly respected and considerably influential groups have taken a monumental step in consolidating both the vision and technical specifications for standardization in the healthcare industry, and the Andover Working Group has already established an active coordination with the HL7 committee.

### HP in Healthcare — A Total Solutions Provider

HP's Medical Products Group has long been recognized as a market leader in providing medical equipment. HP has devoted a portion of its Computer Systems Organization to this explosive industry — enabling HP to further enhance its position as a total solutions provider. Aberdeen believes that this change strengthens Hewlett-Packard's already strong position in the healthcare market.

HP's healthcare solutions can be organized into three groups:

- **Clinical Information Management Solutions** - HP provides departmental information solutions at the point of care for critical care, cardiology, surgery, perinatal and laboratory applications.
- **Medical Diagnostic Equipment** - HP has long been a leader in providing point-of-care and back-end diagnostic equipment. The company's strengths include ultrasound imaging and patient monitoring equipment, as well as cardiology and obstetrical systems.
- **Computing Technologies & Professional Services** - HP turns the applications of its healthcare business partners into complete solutions by adding a combination of market leading computing technologies and advanced professional service offerings. HP offers its healthcare customers reliable, scalable, and secure computing platforms. HP's open systems have emerged as a leading platform for Internet/intranet solutions in healthcare.

### Aberdeen Conclusions

In 12 months the Andover Working Group has made exceptional progress and, by creating a product which simplifies complex standards and makes them easier to use and implement, has taken a significant step in establishing a program to take healthcare standardization to the next level. HP continues to be a leader in the development and implementation of open systems and communication standards throughout the IT industry as a whole.

The stability and leadership that HP has demonstrated with the Andover Working Group is not surprising - HP has long provided tremendous value in healthcare outside the lines of the Andover Working Group. Look for Hewlett-Packard to continue to be a systems-supplier-of-choice in the healthcare market and for the Andover Working Group to continue to gain momentum in bringing standardization to healthcare.
