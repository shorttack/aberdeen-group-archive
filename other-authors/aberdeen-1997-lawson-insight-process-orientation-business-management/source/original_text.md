# Lawson Insight: Where Process-Orientation Makes A Difference For Business Management

### Preface

The past five years has witnessed more dramatic and fundamental change in the market for Enterprise Business Applications (EBAs) than in any prior period. In that time, enterprises have driven suppliers to rapidly integrate new more flexible approaches and capabilities into their business solutions. These same suppliers were also measured on advanced technology exploitation both in terms of how well and with which ones. This time of change is also characterized the emergence of new management-driven interests, among them: business process reengineering (BPR); supply chain management; business best practices; open systems; client-server architectures; and the Internet.

This changing market requirements and technology dynamism has challenged many heritage suppliers and shown to be a difficult course to traverse. Conversely, over this same time frame, new suppliers leveraging technology to overcome barriers of market entry have now become the dominant players. Of all the suppliers that Aberdeen follows, Lawson Software continues to stand out as a company able to continually reinvent itself, its business solutions, its integration of leading-edge technology and its market value proposition.

### Executive Summary

Lawson Software has consistently recognized key emerging business and technology trends — especially those that would serve to add value beyond core business functionality. This recognition is evident today in the company's leadership in delivering true Web-deployable business-extending applications and continues to result in many tangible benefits for Lawson customers.

The four cornerstones of the Lawson Insight Business Management System are:

1. **Process Orientation**
2. **Event-based Management**
3. **Continual Improvement**
4. **Ubiquitous Information Access**

Lawson Insight's process orientation is manifested in the company's Lawson Process Suites. Leading enterprises have concluded that the functional orientation of yesterday's business applications prevented them from effectively integrating their critical business operations and their information and stood as a roadblock in the pursuit of competitive industry best practices. Aberdeen research shows that the high-value lesson of BPR is that business operations are cross-functional activities and that enterprise business applications need to be designed and deployable across that operational landscape. The Lawson Process Suites — Financials, Human Resources, Procurement and Supply Chain — meet those enterprise requirements head-on.

### Market Position and Strategy

Over the past five years, Lawson Software has been successfully executing a go-up-market business and technology strategy. Corporate fiscal year revenues for 1996 were reported as $101.8 million, an increase of 46% over the prior year. The company has over 2300 customers operating across a full spectrum of operating environments from the IBM AS/400, to leading Unix systems, and an increasing base of sites operating Microsoft Windows NT.

Aberdeen believes that Lawson is well positioned to address the non-manufacturing, flexible, process-oriented business management requirements of enterprises across the economic spectrum. From this core competency, the company has honed its messages and capabilities by focusing on exploiting key vertical markets. The first and highly successful vertical industry efforts have been devoted to the healthcare market.

Aberdeen understands that Lawson's vertical strategy will diversify in 1997 to address multi-tier retail, hospitality and leisure, and publishing markets — markets that demand flexibility and process orientation to establish competitive edge.

### Key Technologies and Products

The evolving capabilities of Lawson Insight are built upon a five-pillar technology foundation that supports process-centric applications, activity based management, Web-deployable self service and business commerce, integrated workflow and systems platform independence.

The Release 7.0 features and technologies that make Lawson's process-centric approach work begin with the Active Object Repository. The Repository maintains the methods that define the Lawson Insight business objects. These objects are shared across the multiple application suites.

#### Activity-Based Management and the Intelligent Data Warehouse

A dilemma for many organizations is effectively reconciling financial and operational information for the purpose of making critical business decisions. Lawson has attacked the problem from a combined business practice and technology perspective. It begins with the implementation of Activity-Based Costing (ABC) which makes new and more powerful management methods available across a much broader range of business activities.

To resolve this dilemma, Lawson has created an Activity Repository that functions essentially as an intelligent data warehouse — synchronized for simultaneous update with GL transactions, yet unconstrained by the metadata model of the ledger or any other business feeder systems. This approach supports process-based ABM analysis and creates flexibility in deriving new measures as business processes rapidly change.

#### Web-deployable Applications

Aberdeen research shows that just as systems and network management provided the first technical context for the deployment of object-oriented technologies, the Internet will provide the first business context for the widespread adoption of object-oriented line-of-business solutions. Now that the long-anticipated distributed interprocess communications and connectivity technologies — like OLE Automation and Controls (OCX/ActiveX), CORBA and JAVA — are reaching the market, the realization of distributed, component-based, on-the-fly applications is within reach.

Due to its distributable layered architecture, Lawson Insight is ideally suited for Internet or Intranet implementations. This is demonstrated in the unique capabilities offered by the WebDesktop, Web Service Centers and the advanced technology development environment that makes them possible.

Now in its second release, the WebDesktop allows Insight users to access the full range of Business Management System functionality from their web browser of choice. Insight transaction screens are delivered as JAVA forms.

### Competition

Enterprise suppliers like Oracle, PeopleSoft, and SAP, are getting surprised. Lawson has been able to anticipate key business trends and requirements and deliver solutions and technology to market ahead of its high-end competitors — many of whom are still developing or sporadically introducing products today.

Competitive midrange suppliers like System Software Associates, J.D. Edwards, and Ross, have a dual quandary. First, Lawson's successful product migration to open systems in the late 1980's gives them nearly a decade more experience in advanced, distributable multi-tier technologies and architectures. Second, Lawson has been able to provide an evolutionary technology path for its midrange customers that does not present high-risk, disruptive business- or multi-generational technology-changing choices.

Desktop-oriented suppliers, like Great Plains, State Of The Art and Solomon, with desires to move up the enterprise food chain through their focus and commitment to Windows NT and primarily offering financial products, site Lawson as a key competitor.

Lawson Software stands out as a company able to continually reinvent itself, its business solutions, its integration of leading-edge technology and its market value proposition. Aberdeen believes that Lawson is well positioned to address the non-manufacturing, flexible, process-oriented business management requirements of enterprises across the economic spectrum.

Evaluators of Lawson Insight will find an architectural and technological foundation supporting Lawson's market and business value proposition, its: intrinsically process-centric applications; fully integrated support for activity based management; business commerce and self service Web-deployability; multi-level and cross-organizational integrated workflow; and n-tier, distributable architecture and systems platform independence.
