Aberdeen Picks The Right Tools for Year 2000
IS buyers have a right to be skeptical about the Year 2000
"solutions" that they are seeing. It is hard to understand how
something as small as making a date two digits instead of four
could cause such a massive problem, or why this problem is so
difficult to fix. It is especially hard to pierce the fog of supplier
hype and expert alarmism and figure out how effective the
solutions are.
The Year 2000 problem is also tough to solve:
It is large and pervasive, involving a minimum of 100
billion lines of code to check.
It is hard to detect, involving many programming styles,
languages, and environments that often lack key
information such as documentation or source code.
It is time consuming to fix – it can take over 1000 person
years to check the 200 million lines of code in the typical
large enterprise manually, and the interdependence of
systems makes it difficult to fix them in parallel.
It is irresponsible not to fix, involving bottom-line
negative impact on the company, its suppliers, and its
customers. For example, a system with a Year-2000
problem can "lose" transactions or data – such as
information critical to balancing the books. It may be
difficult to determine exactly what is missing or how to
recover – and the lawyers are waiting for any mistakes.
It is typically expensive to fix, requiring a major
allocation of programmers.
The good news is that a powerful Year-2000 toolset can help
dramatically. Choosing the right tool for each phase of the date
conversion process – a tool that automates the process as much
as possible – should cut down on complexity, reduce the risk
of oversights, and significantly reduce the labor, cost, and
time. The key is to pick the right tools.
Aberdeen research shows that Year 2000 tools can deliver
major benefits in the Year 2000 conversion process. IS will be
able to automate only around 30% of the Year 2000 conversion
process with such tools – the rest remains people-intensive.
But automating that 30% and feeding tool-generated
information into the other 70% dramatically reduces the risks
of failure. Problems are more thoroughly detected, fixes are
more thoroughly tested, conversion time slips are strictly
controlled, and undoing conversion mistakes is far easier and
quicker.
To achieve these benefits, IS must pick best-of-breed tools that
can be integrated with each other and fitted into today's best-
of-breed Year 2000 processes. However, real-world experience
is proving that effective tool-supported processes do far better
than manual ones. This is a no-brainer; IS should upgrade its
Year 2000 tools now.
Karen Moser
