# Silknet Software: Changing the Rules for Next-Generation Customer Support Applications

Silknet Software is the first viable independent software vendor (ISV) to introduce a true internet-based customer service application. As such, the company is able to deliver a value proposition unrivaled by any other supplier of customer interaction software (CIS) today. Given the inherent advantages of the new software architecture utilized by the company — as well as the unique functionality of the Silknet eService application — Aberdeen believes Silknet is in a position to radically redefine the customer service software market.

This Product Viewpoint describes how increasing demands on customer service operations are rapidly changing the standard of excellence — forcing operational and IT professionals to reexamine the applications used to cultivate and retain high-profit clients. Internet-based support is one mechanism with a high degree of interest, giving Silknet the opportunity to define an entirely new segment within the CIS market.

### Executive Summary

Over the last several years, the market for client-server-based CIS applications has exploded. In 1996, total revenues for CIS software suppliers topped $1 billion — a 68% increase over the previous year. Aberdeen expects this growth to continue for the next several years, with the total CIS ISV market reaching $3.4 billion by the year 2000.

Aberdeen notes that over 60% of the CIS market is comprised of customer support/help desk software, typically deployed in a traditional call center. Most of these applications are geared to help support analysts log and track customer calls and incidents through to closure.

Companies seeking differentiation and competitive advantage are looking to take support to the next level. They want to actively involve customers, business partners and experts across the entire enterprise in the support process — making use of the unique knowledge each has to offer. The goal is to proactively respond to customers with greater accuracy and in less time.

However, traditional client-server applications deployed in call centers may not be best suited for this challenge. Because universal access to multimedia support information is so critical to this new model, forward looking users are exploring the use of internet technologies.

On March 10, 1997, Silknet Software introduced Silknet eService — a customer support solution solely designed to be deployed in an inter/intranet environment. By making use of a thin-client architecture, eService can be deployed to anyone with a browser — be they support professional, business partner or customer. As a result, organizations can use the same application no matter what channel is used to deliver customer support. With eService support organizations are no longer constrained to the limitations of the call center.

First out of the gate and closely aligned with the business requirements of advanced support operations, eService has given Silknet a definite market advantage — causing a desperate scramble among competitors to catch-up.

### Moving to the Next-Generation of Customer Support Applications

Aberdeen's ongoing field research with advanced customer support organizations has revealed that their standard of excellence for CIS applications is rapidly evolving. Specifically, Aberdeen has identified four major trends that are defining customer support best practices and changing the criteria for selecting next-generation support applications:

1. **Transition from call tracking to problem resolution/service delivery.** With call tracking, support organizations always have an up-to-the-minute status on every customer incident. However, call tracking by itself does not directly provide a support agent with the information he or she needs to solve a customer's problem. Support organizations are complementing call tracking applications with sophisticated problem resolution software backed by comprehensive knowledgebases.

2. **Customer self-service gains prominence.** Many customers are demanding more control over the support process. As a result, companies are now providing direct access to the support application, allowing customers — as well as business partners — to bypass the support call center entirely. This eliminates one of the most common support complaints — waiting on hold.

3. **Integration with complementary enterprise-class applications.** Support activities are enhanced by providing access to other CIS applications, such as field service management and sales force automation (SFA) software. Additionally, integration with core operational systems such as manufacturing, logistics and billing is often mandatory.

4. **Increasing use of multimedia.** Today's customer support call centers limit the interaction between the support analyst and the customer to a voice over the telephone. This is often frustrating to both parties when trying to explain complex problems or solutions. In many cases, the ability to share images, schematics, or video information on a realtime basis provides significant enhancements to the support process.

### Customer Self-service Goes Prime Time

A recurring theme encountered by Aberdeen in talking to individuals in charge of support operations is the desire to give customers the means to help themselves. Most companies cite the fact that the majority of support calls they receive have to do with routine problems and issues that have already been solved. Rather than involve a support agent, it makes more sense to have the customer submit a description of their problem or issue to the support application directly.

The major motivation behind customer self-service is its ability to increase a company's responsiveness. With self-service, customers are no longer frustrated with long call queues during peak hours, and instead are assured consistent access to the support information they need. Direct customer access to support applications also enables organizations to provide 24x7 service without having to staff a call center around the clock.

### Customer Self-service Requires a Different Design Point

Unfortunately, most existing call-center-based applications are not inherently designed for customer self-service. These call tracking-centric systems are for use by a trained support analyst and are chiefly concerned with gathering information regarding customer and service histories. Most of the screen real estate is taken up by irrelevant information from a self-service perspective — customer address, customer ID, sales/service territory, or date of last purchase. Additionally, these systems are often complex to navigate and require specialized training.

Aberdeen believes that the successful ISVs will be the ones who provide systems designed around the following three tenets:

- **Ease-of-Use** — Unless the interface is completely intuitive, customers will reject using the new system and revert to phoning the call center.
- **Solution/Information Access** — Customers need to be able to submit a natural language description of a problem and have the system automatically guide them to the appropriate solution.
- **Customer Communication** — The system must provide secure access to all other enterprise-wide applications needed to support the customer. Additionally, this new communications channel is an excellent vehicle for sales and marketing activities.

### CIS and the Web — A Natural Combination

Internet-based customer support solutions have inherent advantages over applications adhering to other architectures. The most obvious reason for adopting internet technology is that it is an elegant means for enabling customer self-service. Any individual with a browser and authorization can access a Web-based support application.

Internet-based applications are also easily deployed to business partners as well as to any user across the entire enterprise. This means that a company can ensure consistent levels of support whether it is delivered by means of a dedicated internal call center, a virtual call center linking experts from across the company, or through the organization's indirect distribution channels.

Finally, there is the opportunity to establish customer-directed support forums in which clients offer advice and support to their peers. This not only provides a virtually free support mechanism, it also gives suppliers access to new solutions that they might not have devised on their own.

### Silknet Takes the Web CIS Lead

Silknet Software (603-625-0070, www.silknet.com) introduced its internet-based eService product in March, 1997. Having raised $7.3 million in venture capital funding, the company is now concentrating on making eService the premiere Web-based customer support application. Aberdeen views Silknet as the first viable supplier to offer a solution built from the ground up with internet technologies and fundamentally designed for deployment over the Web.

In developing eService, Silknet picked customers, not call center-based support agents, as the primary intended end-users. Built from the ground up with the needs of customer self-service in mind, eService incorporates several innovations that distinguish it from call tracking software that have had a Web interface bolted on as an afterthought.

The primary objective of eService is to help customers find the knowledge they need from any place at any time. Users are presented with an intuitive interface that assists them in locating and retrieving the proper solution to the issue at hand. And because eService is able to identify the user, the interface can be customized to the unique needs of each individual customer to deliver personalized service.

If customers are unable to resolve a problem by themselves, they can use eService to submit a case on the Web or initiate a telephone call with the customer support center. eService allows each party to take remote control of the other's system so that they can more easily share the symptoms of — and solution to — a problem. Finally, customers and service agents can dynamically share Java applets via eService.

### Advantages of Silknet's Architecture

Silknet's Architecture adheres to a thin-client design model. Using a Netscape or Microsoft Explorer browser over the Internet/intranet, users obtain secure access to eService through a firewall. The application logic of eService consists of object-oriented code written in ActiveX. Silknet is presently adding Java capabilities and expects this work to be completed by Q4 1997.

Silknet's thin-client approach means that eService can be administered from a single centralized site. The latest version of the application is automatically downloaded to the client platform each time the end-user begins an eService session. End-users are always assured of having the latest eService version. Administrators can support worldwide deployments as easily as a single local system.

### The Dilemma for Traditional Client-Server CIS Suppliers

Faced with the prospect of competing against advanced, internet-architected customer support solutions, existing client-server software suppliers are in a quandary. On the one hand, they must continue to profess their belief in client-server-based solutions to maintain their installed base and associated revenue stream. At the same time, they are keenly aware that consideration for many new installations will require that they have a pure inter/intranet solution.

Most offer a one-size-fits-all Web access product at a single price point. The fact that they have yet to institute a flexible pricing model based on usage levels indicates they have yet to fully come to terms with this new market.

### Conclusions

Aberdeen believes that Silknet Software is in an ideal position to capitalize on this market transition. The company has no legacy baggage to bring forward and has secured the funding needed to execute on its business plan. Its eService offering is an advanced solution that combines the functionality demanded by users with the technology and architecture required for next-generation, network-centric applications.

Silknet is quickly changing the rules in the customer support application space — in the process forcing competitors to reevaluate their product, pricing and technology strategies. Unless competitors move fast, leadership in this new market is Silknet's for the taking.
