Executive Viewpoint examines fast changing computer industry trends and conditions from the dual vantagepoint of many years of industrial executive experience and institutional research analysis. The viewpoint is qualitative and seeks to identify major trends of significance to institutional investors.

ARE HYPERGROWTH P&L'S REAL? - OR ARE YOU SEEING MORE THAN YOU PAID FOR

In EVP 1, 2 and 3 we reviewed the structural aspects of hypergrowth. In this EVP we analyze the P&L implications of speeding in industry's fast lane, since what you see can be more than what you think you're getting.

REVENUE PULL VERSUS COST PUSH

There are two fundamental ways of running fast-track corporations:
o Revenue Pull - managing the business to keep a smooth order and shipment flow, even if this means loss of market share. Revenue Pull means trading off growth for presumed better cost and revenue visibility. The operational objective is to optimize factory loading and derive the financial benefits thereof. Emphasis is on cost control, summarized as "To make money, cut costs." In real life, it hardly ever works that way - customers and competitors get in the way of smooth operations.
o Cost Push - Operating the business to gain new sales as quickly as possible by ramping up new product development, marketing and plant capacity somewhat in advance of hard orders. The objective is increased market share and the ensuing financial benefits. Emphasis is on optimal marketing, typified as "To make money, spend money". Most fast-track firms tend to operate this way at least during a portion of their hypergrowth.

In actual practice, firms strive for a balance of these two methods. But management typically finds itself trapped on the quarterly earnings treadmill, and like it or not, will tend towards the cost-push mode. (See TECH GROUP report on Tandem Computers of December 16, 1982 for a recent example).

AN EXPENSIVE SURPRISE OR TWO

Comes the middle of the second quarter and GOH! says there is some weakness out there in Cleveland and Detroit and Pittsburgh, what with the recession and the Japanese attack on domestic autos. MBA recites the litany of reasons for making the revenue goal (The Board; Wall Street; employees; bonuses; and not necessarily in that order). So GOH! calls in some favors from Europe and California and Texas and who knows where else and is nearly back on track when CFO says "Wait! There's a big surprise in G&A this quarter". FTI's rudimentary control system somehow lost track of some $2 million in advertising expenses that are due this quarter, having already been pushed into Q2 from Q1.

CFO explains the problem: If we just make the $110 revenue goal we agreed upon, we have $2 million worth of expenses that will impact the bottom line to the tune of 6 cents, or, the same earnings as Q1. So, in the waning days of February (FTI is on a typical fast track fiscal year - in this case August), GOH! pulls out all stops, and CFO, preoccupied with the new last minute burdens, skips his customary visit to the factory on the last day of the quarter.

A MISUNDERSTANDING ABOUT LEAP YEARS

It's just as well, since factory management labors under the belief that all even numbered years are leap years and had thus planned to make a real push on February 29th, 1982. And so they did, with some shipments even going out during the wee hours of February 30th! Conveniently, the shipping dock date stamp got stuck on February 28th.

Well, they made the quarter. As the word went out over the broad tape on the Ides of March of "another record quarter of sales and earnings", Wall Street applauded and breathed a collective sigh of relief. Earnings were exactly on target, with revenues slightly above Street expectations. Confident Fortyish Analyst (CFA), FTI's leading sell side advocate, gets the details of the quarter from CFO and asks his usual penetrating questions. "What happened to the margin components?". Until now changes of more than a tenth of a percentage point or two per line item were unusual.

CFO tells CFA that revenues were somewhat better than plan, owing to some last minute strength in Europe and the Southwest, which helped gross margins by six tenths of a percentage point. R&D accordingly was off a tenth or two since these programs tend to be planned in dollar amounts rather than as a percentage of sales.

But what happened to SG&A? CFO says that the results of the advertising program were paying off so well that they decided to spend even more, that there were some unexpected additional startup expenses on the new offshore manufacturing facility, and then there were some other G&A items that added up to the final total. "Can you break these out", asks CFA? Not really. "Then, when can we expect to get operating margins back to the 20.0 percent level?". "We never said 20.0 percent, but indicated a range of 18 to 22, and we are well within that. Besides, you analysts try to read too much precision into numbers. Do you realize that one tenth of a percentage point this quarter was only $112 thousand dollars, the price of a few pages of advertising in the Wall Street Journal?", replies CFO. And finally, what about the auspicious decrease in tax rate? "As we've said, it could vary by a point or two this year depending upon international business mix, R&DTC, ITC, and recent tax legislation".

DIGGING A DEEPER PIT

So much for the second quarter; Wall Street is on to Q3 (near term) and the year (long term) and FY83 (forever).

GOH's field force is taking a well deserved week or two off. Particularly since little business is done in the first week of any quarter. They need to recharge their batteries in preparation for another tough quarter since the secular decline in the Midwest is not going to turn by May, the last month of Q3.

Having really pushed just to make the stated $110 goal by borrowing from future sales, and then borrowing $2 million more to bail out the control system, GOH! is hard pressed to make Q1's $112, let alone the $120 goal. And CFA and other analysts will probably kill the stock if earnings aren't up sequentially at least a nickel.

And CFO, speaking forcefully to MBA, says that it is getting more dangerous to operate with the rudimentary control system installed by his predecessor. Another $1-$2 million oversight could pop up again at any time. MBA agrees, but how about holding off until fiscal 1984? All agree that thinking the unthinkable - scaling back to a Revenue Pull mode - couldn't be done this year, what with the company and the stock on a big roll.

Well, there are a number of businesslike ways out of this cost-push dilemma and all have been used at one time or another by most hypergrowth companies:
o Sales promotions, discounting, sales compensation adders, and other practices to stimulate sales.
o Borrow sales from future periods by increasingly dipping into typically smallish backlogs.
o New product introductions, mid-life kickers, customer incentives to order and take delivery now, and extended payment terms.

Now sometimes all the corporate tactics above have been exhausted, and management must decide whether to face up to a bad quarter or to wing it one more time. If the latter, then there are some measures that do not conform to generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP), but have been said to be used upon occasion:
o "Cover the clock" at quarter end, and hope to pay back the borrowed shipments next quarter.
o Resort to artifice and book as sales some shipments where there is a material question as to the adequacy of the paperwork.
o Extend this further by taking into revenue some contingency sales and hope the auditors don't pick it up; besides, the quarterly review is less stringent than the annual audit, by which time all will have been fixed.
o Go the whole way and book as sales, items which are not in fact sold, using intermediaries to take delivery and other subterfuges.

CROSSING THE RUBICON AND RETURNING BARELY UNSCATHED

After some moderate soul searching and assessing the possibility of a sales upturn within the next six months, FTI decides to wing it. So, virtually undetected by investors, FTI indulges in some questionable practices. And, as luck would have it, sales do pick up somewhat during the final fiscal quarter as a federal agency cleans out its budget and a major auto maker decides it must modernize or die. So FTI brings in another record year, with slightly depressed margins but making the original $2.75 EPS goal, down to the penny each quarter.

QUESTIONS REMAIN - FISCAL 1983?

CFA and fellow analysts are a bit uneasy about FTI's margin erosion and the new unevenness in margin components. However, all they see are the actual results, not the original plan, which had envisioned a "modest" year-to-year sales gain of 50 percent with all margin components remaining intact. What they cannot see is that, expense items were running ahead of sales even faster than planned, necessitating nearly $20 million of incremental sales just to cover the incremental expenses.

But with FTI's high gross margins, it was far easier to cover the added expenses than it would be for FTI's major competitor who had poor gross margins. So FTI gained market share as well, reinforcing investor and FTI alike in the soundness of their judgements.

What is the moral of the story? Simply that FTI and its investors alike got what they paid for: another banner growth year with very heavy emphasis on earnings per share. There is much mutual admiration all around, and explicit urgings to make FY83 even better.

INVESTOR RELEVANCE

Now one asks, could CFA or any of his peers have been able to detect the real issue - poor expense visibility and controls? Could the auditors? NO in the first case for the obvious reason of lack of access. Even Beady Eyed Avid Numberpusher (BEAN), streetwise and cynical of all "management types", only suspected. Were there any strong hints? Yes, but none that were conclusive; variations in margin components reflect events in the real world.

What of the varying tax rate? As we know, taxes are a combination of a tax rate and various offsets and credits. While CFO could estimate each quarter what the annual rate might be, he was required to use the quarterly rate to make up for over- or underestimating. With only a 1.4 percentage point excursion, FTI's rate was well within accepted norms.

As to the auditors, there is simply no issue as to the $2 million advertising expenditure in Q2: it was incurred and duly reported. And so it was with other expense items; they were reported as incurred. FTI's auditors, Coppers, Petersen and Airwick (CPA) are not paid to tell management how to smooth earnings, quite the contrary!

As to the stopped clock, if the documentation is in order, only an exhaustive investigation would detect it. And CPA is not paid to do that, absent a serious discrepancy arising in the quarterly review. (Investors should note the quarterly financial statements, technically "interim statements" are neither audited nor carry the same weight as annual statements. In part this is because many annual expenses such as employee benefits and annual report costs occur in peaks but are spread over the quarters in keeping with the basic "matching principle" of accounting).

But CPA did note on their year end review that Q3 sales paperwork was not as tidy as they liked, but that by the end of Q4 the problem was mostly resolved. There was some question of restatement of revenues, and it was a close call. CPA recommended to the Audit Committee of the Board that FTI install better controls, particularly since it was reaching the size where a Management Statement attesting to adequacy of controls would be required.

NEXT - SOME BALANCE SHEET EFFECTS

So much for a cursory review of FTI's income statement, which provided some clues but not hard evidence of the severity of Cost Push. Will the balance sheet provide any better answers? Yes, but paradoxically, some exaggerated ones.
