THE MARCH QUARTER BLUES - OR, WERE WE EXPECTING TOO MUCH, TOO SOON?

Wall Street's euphoric bidding up of high-tech shares: earnings estimates scaled up by 
pennies/nickels/dimes to justify lofty P/E multiples. Cannot change numerator in runaway 
market - only lever is EPS denominator. Mixed results appearing for March quarters.

NO MYSTERY - JUST PLAIN BUSINESS SENSE
Four factors making March quarter moderate:
1. Major customers on calendar year: January nullity, February signs of life, March bookings.
2. New budgets not turned on full force; period of reflection; "squirrel away a penny or two."
3. Informal institutionalization: sales conferences, vacations for heavy hitters (70-80% of business). 
4. New Year's Resolution effect: think it through rather than leap onto quarterly treadmill.

MARCH QUARTER + PROTRACTED RECESSION + BULL MARKET = POTENTIAL DISASTER
Recent Dismalness = worst recession in fifty years. High Tech America did outstanding job: 
hunkered down, invested for future (depressing near-term margins), prepared for recovery.
Led analysts to call some firms "recession proof or recession resistant."
Investor logic: if they did well in recession, recovery will bring massive sales + margin expansion.
Reality: real world not so kind. Recent March quarter preliminary results proving this.

FOUR FACTORS PREVENTING INSTANT MARGIN BOUNCEBACK
1. Deferred maintenance/investment must be restored before competitors gain ground.
2. 20%+ revenue growth = minimum for high-tech group; requires new hires (cost money).
3. Career plans in tech require headcount growth; static headcount hurts morale.
4. If all competitors in same boat, rational to invest for long term; fine line exists.

A few companies have snapped a too-tightly-stretched cord. Stock prices punished for 
missing supposedly discounted calendar 1983 earnings.

Meanwhile, quite positive about longer-term prospects of high tech sector, but suggest that 
a raging bull leg of the market has led many to expect too much too soon from too many.

### Abstract

EVP #7 analyzes the structural and cyclical reasons why March quarters systematically disappoint investors in high-tech stocks, identifying four business-cycle factors (customer budget timing, new-year caution, informal industry institutionalization, and psychological reset) compounded by the worst recession in fifty years and the resulting Wall Street euphoria that inflated P/E multiples beyond what earnings could justify. Casale argues that instant margin expansion post-recession is structurally impossible and that some companies have already snapped from the strain of maintaining earnings to justify lofty multiples.
