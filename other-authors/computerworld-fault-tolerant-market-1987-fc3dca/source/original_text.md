### Abstract

March 1987 Computerworld news feature on the U.S. fault-tolerant computer market, described as a 'two-man fight' between Marlboro MA-based Stratus Computer, Inc. and Cupertino CA-based Tandem Computers, Inc., with IBM 'on the edge' and unable to land a solid punch. Pete Kastner — at the time manager of marketing development at Stratus (more than a year before he co-founded Aberdeen Group in 1988) — is quoted predicting the U.S. fault-tolerant market will reach $2 billion in 1987 and grow 40-50% per year through 1990, driven by financial services, brokerage, point-of-sale, and shop-floor applications. Dataquest's Van Weathers offers a parallel analysis citing expansion of target markets (POS, telecommunications) and continuing price/performance pressure. International Resource Development (Norwalk CT) gives a more conservative $1B for 1987 rising to $1.2B by 1990. The article also chronicles NCR's push into low-cost fault-tolerant POS systems ($25K-$45K), Tandem's introduction of low-cost expandable systems, Parallel Computers Co. (Santa Cruz CA) targeting Unix-based systems at telecom and niche markets like law enforcement, and new entrant Tolerant Systems Inc. (San Jose CA) aiming low-cost systems at banking, telecom, manufacturing and federal government. Illinois Bell's Networker telephone-network monitoring system is described as built on a Parallel Computers fault-tolerant system. This is a rare PRE-Aberdeen Kastner artifact documenting his industry-forecasting voice from the Stratus marketing-development era.
