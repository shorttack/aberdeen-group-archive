Videoconferencing Booms on SARS Fears

By Ry Crozier, Electronics News Australia -- Electronic News, 5/14/2003

Severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS) is more than just a threat to the health of sufferers, it is also casting a shadow over the semiconductor industry. After championing China as the new manufacturing darling, over-dependence on Southeast Asia may now lead to supply chain disruptions and another mini down-cycle for an already challenged industry. 
 Already the casualties are appearing. Motorola Inc. temporarily closed a mobile phone manufacturing facility in Singapore after its 305 workers were quarantined due to SARS exposure. And Microchip Technology -- which houses test facilities in Thailand -- is also seeing the effects. “The spread throughout Asia of SARS is inhibiting business travel and, in some cases, purchasing decisions, as many customers are temporarily closing plants to avoid the spread of the disease,” said Microchip’s CEO Steve Sanghi, in a statement. 

 Intel Corp. has warned that CEO Craig Barrett will skip an appearance at the Intel Developers Forum. And in a setback to intense competition from rival Nvidia, ATI Technologies CEO K.Y. Ho has postponed a trip to launch ATI’s new graphics processors. Both Sunplus Technologies and laptop manufacturer Compal have barred employees from visiting affected countries until the disease is under control. With applications engineers in the region, Broadcom, Cirrus Logic, Conexant and National Semiconductor have also been implicated. 

 But with many companies canceling travel plans and airlines cutting routes and staff numbers, in turn, other sectors are booming. Just as the security industry latched onto the hysteria attached to September 11, companies affected by travel bans are turning to videoconferencing in their droves. 

 Integrated Vision, one of Australia’s largest videoconferencing providers, has reported a 44 percent increase in queries since the outbreak, from an average of 90 calls per day up to 130. “Of those, 20 specifically identified SARS as the reason for wanting to use videoconferencing,” Managing Director Graham Williams told Electronics News Australia . “The spike after September 11 lasted only a couple of days -- this is much more sustained. The only other time this has happened is when Ansett collapsed.” 

 Sydney-based provider ServicePoint expects a similar result. “There hasn’t been an immediate increase to the effect of September 11 or Bali, but it’s still too early to tell,” Bill Warren, ServicePoint operations manager, also told Electronics News Australia . “I think any travel threat effectively increases companies’ use of videoconferencing so we’d expect demand to eventually filter through.” Tom Bianchi, Microchip Australia and New Zealand district sales manager, has already indicated his intention to harness videoconferencing. “We’re looking to install videoconferencing equipment to get around this inability to meet face-to-face,” Bianchi explains. 

 Despite opportunistic effects -- add medical masks and diagnosis data to that list -- the overall effects on an already fragile industry are difficult to quantify. Lower production and R&D costs sent Western semiconductor manufacturers to low-cost China in their droves. Now many face an anxious wait, as supply chain disruptions look more and more likely as the country is in the grip of the disease. 

 “The entire electronics assembly industry has a critical, no-second-source dependence -- for the moment -- on components made in China,” Aberdeen Group Analysts Russ Craig and Peter Kastner state. “The dependable supply of key component building blocks is threatened by SARS, not just the assembly plants in China. Our assumption is that the disease can and will be halted by aggressive public health actions going forward. If not, then the electronics industry will be a tiny part of humanity’s problem.”
