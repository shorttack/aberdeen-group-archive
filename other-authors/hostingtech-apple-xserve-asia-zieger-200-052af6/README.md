## Abstract

HostingTech magazine (November 2002) by Anne Zieger profiles Apple's campaign to use the Xserve 1U rack-mount server (running Mac OS X Unix-based OS) to break into Asian datacenter markets — its niche markets being media/entertainment, broadband/Internet services, education/research, and biotechnology/life sciences. Asian server market (excluding Japan) Q1 2002: $1.3B, down from $1.4B prior year (-7%) per IDC. Apple Q&A: CEO Steve Jobs; Cupertino CA; founded 1977; ~9,612 employees; 2001 revenues $5.36B; URL apple.com. Quotes from multiple analysts. Tim Bajarin (Creative Strategies): 'Apple is quite committed to going after this server marketplace, and over time could be a worthy competitor.... It is our opinion that the Asian market could be Apple's best place to gain initial ground.' Tim Deal (analyst): 'It's easy to use, and physically easy to maintain. It should appeal to both the veteran I.T. manager and to new I.T. managers.' Daniel Duerr (Grey Zone — Xserve channel partner): 'The cost for disk storage is unprecedented. This is one of the big things Apple did right.' Quote from Peter Kastner, chief research officer with Aberdeen Group: 'For Apple's computer-intensive imaging customers, the Xserve offers a less expensive and [more scalable product] not available from Apple until now, with the ability to port many Unix/Linux applications easily.' Closing Kastner caution: 'No one should expect miracles overnight. Apple has a good product but needs to build channels and acceptance, while worldwide market conditions are poor at best.' Storage cost analysis: Sony 480 GB NAS retails $4,100-$4,400; Maxtor 480 GB ~$3,600; fully-loaded Xserve gives same storage plus more features for similar price.
