Introduction 
This report consists of descriptions of the internal quality assurance (QA) programs of 
eight major, national information technology (IT) professional services suppliers that 
provide systems integration and custom application services and an overall summary of 
the research findings.  This research project was undertaken at the request of Peter 
Kastner, Executive Vice President of the Aberdeen Group, Inc., for the purpose of 
assembling background information on the subject of industry QA standards and 
practices.   
The author of this research note is Stephen Lane, Aberdeen Group Senior Analyst.  Mr. 
Lane has more than twenty years of experience in the computer industry, chiefly as 
product manager and consultant.  In his role as an Aberdeen analyst covering the IT 
professional services industry, Mr. Lane researches the market positions and service lines 
of consulting and systems integration service suppliers, in addition to the methodologies 
and best practices that characterize such firms’ service delivery models.   
With the exception of the description of Andersen Consulting’s QA practices, the 
information summarized herein was collected during telephone interviews with QA 
practice leaders or managers from the following IT professional services suppliers: Ernst 
and Young LLP (E&Y); Computer Sciences Corporation (CSC); Deloitte Consulting; 
KPMG; Electronic Data Systems (EDS); Keane Inc.; and PricewaterhouseCoopers.  The 
Andersen Consulting description is based on a thorough review of the firm’s Client 
Quality Management Reference Manual and related documents from the litigation.

Summary Findings 
Six of the eight firms described in this report have centralized organizations that are 
responsible for developing, maintaining, conducting, and promoting QA and related 
corporate standards (i.e., best practices) and processes.  These organizations typically act 
as independent reviewers to periodically carry out QA reviews of active projects.  The other 
two of the eight firms profiled firms also have centralized organizations whose roles are to 
develop and promote QA and other standards, but members of these groups do not actively 
participate in project reviews.  Rather, senior partners within the relevant practice areas 
carry out such reviews.  These partners are not members of engagement teams.  Among all 
eight firms, the actual responsibility for ensuring adherence to the firm’s quality 
standards and practices rests with the service delivery teams and goes up the practice area 
management, or line of business management reporting chain of command. 
• 
As a rule, independent QA organizations do not become involved with a potential 
client during the engagement opportunity assessment and proposal stages, although 
some firms do have guidelines that specify when such involvement can or must 
occur.  Such guidelines are generally based on pre-defined criteria such as risk; 
revenue potential; client gain/loss potential; match between engagement goals and 
agreed-upon deliverables and service supplier capabilities and expertise; inclusion of 
new or untested technologies; and a new client-relationship opportunity.  None of the 
suppliers interviewed for this report share pre-engagement risk assessments with 
potential clients.  Typically, pre-engagement risk assessments are used by 
professional service organizations to determine whether or not they can meet client 
expectations and deliverables while maximizing their own project profitability. 
• 
Suppliers use internal QA reviews and related activities as self-improvement 
mechanisms — “What are we doing that we can do better.”  They are also used to 
ensure that engagement managers conduct projects in such a manner as to maximize 
profitability and that they make effective use of internal resources.  For this reason, 
most QA reviews are, by design, exercises in self-criticism.  However, to encourage 
openness, the majority of firms do not use QA reviews as a means for determining

employee performance.  Most interviewees stressed the critical need for openness, 
and stated that identifying and focusing on problems identified through the QA 
process in a non-confrontational manner is the best way to ensure successful projects 
and quality deliverables.  
• 
While clients are typically interviewed during QA reviews, the purpose of such 
interactions is both to determine the state of the working relationship between 
supplier and client team members and to understand the client’s views on the 
effectiveness of project activities and processes.  Therefore, the aim of such interviews 
is to gauge the level of client satisfaction in terms of how a project is proceeding, not 
the quality of the deliverables.  Measures of the latter are based on acceptance tests, 
joint project reviews, and ultimately, client-satisfaction surveys.  The real measure of 
the effectiveness of internal QA reviews is whether or not recommendations resulting 
from them are carried out in a timely fashion.  This is typically done through follow-
up reviews scheduled in accordance with the severity of the problems identified. 
• 
The firms researched tend not to mandate internal QA reviews for each and every 
engagement.  In this sense, Andersen’s requirement that all projects undergo at least 
one such review is above industry norms.  Typically, the extent and frequency of 
internal QA involvement in engagements is based on risk, revenue opportunity, and 
the other criteria described above.  
• 
As a rule, suppliers do not share internal QA reports with clients except in edited or 
summary form.  Of the eight leading firms researched this report, only Deloitte, 
Keane, and CSC share complete reports with clients as a general practice.  EDS and 
PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC), on the other hand, make a practice of not sharing 
QA findings with clients unless specifically requested to do so, and then the firms 
deliver only summary reports.  Furthermore, it is PwC’s policy to destroy QA reports 
a year after projects are completed.  It is worth noting that none of the individuals 
interviewed for this report could recall a single instance where internal QA 
documents were used in legal actions.

Supplier QA Descriptions 
Ernst and Young LLP (E&Y) 
E&Y’s Quality Advisor program was started in 1992.  The program was an outgrowth of 
an internal restructuring process and was triggered by a few “unhappy client” 
engagements that were blamed, in part, on growth and integration problems stemming 
from the merger of Ernst and Whinney and Arthur Young that created E&Y in 1989.  The 
focus of the program is to create a collaborative partnership with major clients by 
reducing client risk, thereby ensuring a healthy, long-term relationship between the client 
and the firm. 
E&Y’s Director of Risk Management in the U.S., who coordinates program activities with 
the heads of the firm’s practice areas, manages the Quality Advisor program.  The 
manager of the practice area under which the engagement falls assigns a Quality Advisor 
to all engagements with revenues above $1M.  The Quality Advisor reports to the practice 
manager and is typically at the partner level or higher and is a recognized expert in his or 
her practice area.  The role is a line function and does not report to either engagement 
managers or client relationship partners. 
E&Y’s program methodology was developed in-house and is not based on any particular 
set of external  guidelines.  Quality Advisors are briefed about an engagement during the 
pre-proposal and start-up stages.  Quality Advisors review project plans, resource 
assignments, planned deliverables, and milestone schedules.  Throughout the course of an 
engagement, a Quality Advisor will interview client personnel and E&Y team members.  
He or she will then conduct quarterly reviews in which both client and E&Y project team 
members participate.  The reviews can last up to two or three days and can be quite 
intensive ⎯ including reviews of reports from software testers and integration specialists.

These quarterly reports cover such topics as engagement status, resource allocation, 
problems, issues, and customer satisfaction levels.  Report findings are characterized by 
red (problem or issue with an immediate follow-up required), yellow (item needs to be 
improved, status to be tracked), and green flags (item meets measurement criteria).  
Neither engagement managers nor relationship managers participate in the quarterly 
reviews other than to provide required status information, prepare responses to previous 
reviews, or to carry out action items based on flagged issues.   
Actions are driven by risk assessments.  Although the program is structured to provide 
detailed, drill-down analysis of problems and issues, its primary focus seems to be on 
maintaining client satisfaction.  Acting on the advice of internal counsel, Ernst and Young 
provides summary, but not detailed, reports of the quarterly review to its clients.  Since the 
Quality Advisor program was introduced, E&Y has made an effort to internalize its 
practices into the company culture.  A poor QA review can negatively affect an 
engagement manager’s or relationship partner’s bonus.   
The Quality Advisor program is described in E&Y’s pre-sales materials and proposals. 
Computer Sciences Corporation (CSC) 
CSC’s Technology Management Group (TMG) is responsible for process improvement 
and quality assurance (QA) consulting and oversight among the firm’s commercial 
business units.  The function reports to the President of the Application Services 
Organization (commercial software development and systems integration outsourcing) 
and has a Software Engineering Institute (SEI) Capability Maturity Model (CMM) Level 
2 (repeatability) rating.  It is currently working toward a Level 3 rating.  
CSC’s Federal Business Unit has a Level 4 rating overall and two business units within 
the Federal organization ⎯ Systems Engineering and Analysis Support (SEAS) and 
Aerospace Information Technology ⎯ are rated at Level 5.  SEAS also earned an 
International Organization for Standardization (ISO) 9001 certification in 1997.  CSC is 
the only organization in the professional services industry with two business units rated at 
Level 5.  SEI does not rate companies as a whole — only specific business units.

TMG has been overseeing software development and systems integration QA for CSC’s 
commercial business units since 1996.  The organization does not conduct QA testing on 
individual projects but it does get involved at the due diligence assessment phases of all 
projects.  Its role is to ensure that project processes and procedures are modeled after SEI 
practices.  The group also oversees the development of test plans and performs scheduled 
(vary by project) process checks to ensure that the plans are followed ⎯ e.g., that errors 
are documented, that actions are followed up, and that all activities are documented.  In 
this role, the organization serves as an internal review function. 
Project teams are responsible for submitting QA reports to clients.  Reporting activities are 
based on the Balanced Scorecard approach.  The reports track issues such as number of 
errors, number corrected, progress on outstanding errors, etc.  CSC software projects are 
performed on a release-based schedule.  Therefore, the QA reports are important for 
deciding (with clients) whether or not releases will be submitted as scheduled based on 
severity of errors and availability of workarounds.  Clients are involved in acceptance 
testing and have final approval. 
TMG is actively involved in promoting SEI/CMM key processes throughout CSC.  Several 
Engineering Process Groups have been established in CSC’s business units.  The 
company also has a Corporate Knowledge Program, supported by a Lotus Notes database, 
that captures plans, processes, and procedures ⎯ including those related to QA activities 
⎯ making them available to CSC business units and project teams worldwide. 
CSC encourages openness and the sharing of information.  Therefore, TMG does not see 
itself as a “hammer” to enforce compliance with corporate or business unit QA practices. 
There is no information available about law suits involving CSC software 
development/systems integration projects prior to the company’s implementation of SEI 
practices in 1996, although there are instances where CSC ended up having to fix 
problems for clients at no charge.  The interviewee could not recall a single legal action 
against CSC having to do with software projects since 1996, the year the TMG 
organization and its processes were put into place.

KPMG Consulting 
In 1996 KPMG moved from a post-engagement quality review model to a more proactive 
QA model.  The move coincided with the company’s reorganization of its consulting 
practices.  It was also based on the recognition that as KPMG took on larger, more 
complex engagements on a national level, the company had to keep better track of quality 
and risk issues on an ongoing basis.   
The current Quality Assurance Program (QAP) is under the direction of the National 
Risk Management Partner for Consulting and is staffed by three senior partners with 
decades of collective consulting experience.  As KPMG is organized by industry practices, 
the practice leaders own the ultimate responsibility for all engagements in their respective 
areas.  Thus, the QAP team members serve as a “second set of eyes” for these individuals. 
The team meets by phone or in person on a monthly basis to review a list of open 
engagements, identify those that qualify for review, and select and schedule those that 
qualify.  Any engagement with a contract value of $1 million or higher is assigned to a 
QAP team member.  Other engagement selection criteria include complexity, association 
with a new KPMG services, and size.  In addition, an engagement may be selected for a 
QAP review at the request of its industry practice leader.   
A QAP reviewer typically does not meet with the engagement team until the engagement is 
under way, but will always schedule a meeting within the first three months of its start.  
Prior to the meeting, the QAP representative will request background material, such as 
contract or statement of work, project plans, budget, etc., from the engagement team.  The 
reviewer will then meet with the engagement team and conduct interviews with between 
two-to-four key client personnel.  KPMG stresses that reviewers meet with client personnel, 
not to defend or explain, but to obtain the client’s views on how the firm has performed, 
how the personnel assigned to the project are performing, and identify activities that they 
can do better.   
Specific areas that a reviewer will study include engagement management (e.g., budgeting, 
resource utilization, etc.), quality (of actual deliverables as well as client perception of the

deliverables), and financial management (e.g., where is the project relative to the budget).  
During the review process, the QAP team member will meet with the engagement team to 
discuss findings and make recommendations.  A typical review will last four-to-five days, 
including report preparation. 
Engagements are assigned red, yellow, or green risk flags.  A red flag is taken as an 
indication to the industry practice leader that there are conditions that require his or her 
direct, immediate attention.  Yellow flagged engagements contain items that require direct 
action by the delivery team.  Green flag reports list the reviewer’s comments and 
suggestions.  Report copies, regardless of how they are flagged, are sent to the engagement 
team, the industry practice leader, and the KPMG executive team.   
The QAP team reviewer will conduct monthly, follow-up reviews for any project that 
receives a red flag.  This process will continue for as long as critical issues remain.  Yellow 
flagged engagements may undergo additional reviews by the QAP team member but, as a 
rule, follow-up activities are the responsibility of the appropriate industry practice leader. 
KPMG follows an internal, situational release-based model for application development 
and systems integration projects.  Project plans and schedules include acceptance tests 
and the company will work with clients on release schedules to decide whether or not a 
particular risk or the absence of certain functionality should or should not cause a 
schedule change.  KPMG is in the process of a phased rollout of a knowledge management 
tool to consolidate engagement histories and best practices with industry practice specific 
templates.  
Deloitte Consulting 
Deloitte Consulting divides Risk Management and Quality Assurance/Quality Control 
into entirely separate functions.  Risk Management exists as a corporate function with a 
National Partner assigned to oversee all economic and market condition risks associated 
with engagements of a certain revenue size ⎯ typically more than $1 million.  At the 
beginning of every engagement or project, the leader or responsible partner completes a 
formalized Risk Questionnaire.  Only those projects receiving a pre-defined, weighted

average score are assigned to the process.  If a weighted score is high enough, the Risk 
Management Partner can veto the proposed engagement.  
QA activities for large engagements are managed at the practice level — typically by a 
Senior Partner within the practice who is not a member of the project team.  It is worth 
noting that Deloitte rarely engages in projects where its employees are wholly responsible 
for the end product or deliverables.  Rather, project team members work with the client’s 
staff and projects tend to be jointly managed.  In addition, Deloitte believes that rather 
than have QA assessments performed by people outside of a particular practice; 
individuals within the practice have more to offer in terms of practical experience.  With 
those facts in mind, the following describes the role and activities of the project QA 
function. 
Deloitte follows its own internal methodologies and best practices to create detailed project 
plans.  Engagement managers pay particular attention to defining deliverables and 
milestones, especially completion criteria for the deliverables.  A partner assigned to the 
project QA function will typically get involved early in the engagement but only after the 
project leader and team (including client team) have developed and approved the required 
project documentation.  However, a QA partner will occasionally get involved with a 
prospective engagement during the RFP (request for proposal) or pre-initiation activity, if 
the engagement is large or complex enough to warrant an additional viewpoint from the 
project management perspective.   
If the QA partner gets involved after project initiation, he or she will meet with the delivery 
team after reviewing the project documentation.  The purpose is for the partner to discuss 
any preliminary observations or recommendations that the team (including client staff) 
may have already considered but decided not to carry through into the project plan.  The 
idea is also for the delivery team and QA partner to test out any ideas or 
recommendations before the partner meets with the client. 
The QA partner then meets with the client sponsor and/or client project director.  The 
partner presents his/her high-level documented observations and recommendations to the

client, paying particular attention to issues that may have an immediate impact on the 
project.  The partner then delivers a detailed written report to the engagement manager 
who is responsible for ensuring that any issues identified during the course of the QA 
activity are addressed.  Because Deloitte takes a collaborative approach to engagements, 
the client sponsor or project leader receives a copy of the final report.  
Deloitte’s European and Canadian practice organizations have adopted the ISO 9000 
project management model and are ISO 9000 certified through independent organizations 
outside of ISO.  Some Asia/Pacific practices, notably Australia, are also ISO 9000 
certified.  The U.S. practices have not adopted ISO 9000 but are evaluating the 
appropriateness of the SEI CMMs as guidelines.  In developing its own internal 
methodologies and practices, Deloitte in the U.S. has aligned itself with the Project 
Management Institute and is considering PMI certification.  Other sources Deloitte 
reviews include the Methodology Group and the Risk Management Group. 
In the U.S., Deloitte is just now in the process of formalizing its QA methodologies across 
all practice areas under a Senior Partner, although responsibility for carrying them out 
will probably remain within each practice.  The company has also established an initiative 
to create consistent Risk Management reviews with templates on a global basis. 
Keane, Inc. 
Keane has a corporate-level quality assurance organization headed by a Director of QA.  
The organization is responsible for defining and disseminating QA practices throughout 
the corporation, providing mentoring and education to practice groups and individuals, 
and, above all, for ensuring that the firm’s QA practices are carried out at the individual 
project level.   
Keane is organized into five geographic areas; each headed by a vice president.  Prior to 
the establishment of the corporate organization in 1994, QA was handled at the area vice 
president level.  As Keane began entering a period of rapid growth in 1997, corporate 
management recognized the need to have standard practices throughout the corporation.  
Thus, the area QA functions were rolled up into a corporate-organization.  Today, there is

a QA manager in each area who reports directly to the corporate Director of QA and who 
has a dotted-line reporting connection to the area VP.   
Keane’s practices in the United Kingdom are ISO 9001 certified, due in large part to the 
fact that such certification is required to do business in most European countries.  It has 
also elected to pursue SEI CMM Level 3 certification on a project-by-project basis.  The 
company typically achieves Level 3 certification within 8-to-12 months from start to finish 
of the process. 
The primary focus of Keane’s QA organization is on deliverables-based projects, although 
the group will occasionally get involved with strategic consulting engagements.  In 
addition to being responsible for developing and disseminating internal QA standards, the 
QA group is responsible for their enforcement through the company’s Project Control and 
Reporting Process (PCRP).  The PCRP is a set of prescribed activities where project 
elements ⎯ e.g., financials, schedules, milestones, deliverables, etc., ⎯ are tracked.  
Results are reported up through the corporate management chain.  In this way, every 
deliverables-based project receives visibility throughout the company. 
Every deliverables-based project in the company is reviewed by the QA organization on a 
quarterly basis.  Depending on the scope and complexity of the project, such reviews can 
last from one day to two weeks.  Prior to a review, the area QA manager collects and 
analyzes the project documentation.  He or she interviews project staff in addition to 
important client contacts.  The QA function does not conduct separate testing of project 
deliverables, but does ensure that such testing is part of the project plan and that it is 
carried out according to corporate QA practices.   
Following the review process, the QA manager conducts an informal debriefing with the 
project team to discuss findings and to develop remediation strategies, if necessary.  The 
QA manager also writes a formal review report that is made available to both Keane 
management and client staff.  Typically, clients receive a formal presentation of findings 
in addition to a report.  The purpose of both monthly reports and quarterly reviews is to 
proactively identify actual or potential problems and develop solutions for them.

Electronic Data Systems (EDS) 
QA and review activities at EDS reflect the size and diversity of the corporation itself.  
Specific responsibility for such functions rests primarily with each of EDS’ more than 75 
solutions centers, strategic account groups, and strategic business units (SBUs).  QA 
standards and practices are not mandated from the corporate level.  However, there is a 
Corporate Methods and Processes (CM&P) organization that has been in place since 
1988.  Its role is to develop and disseminate high-level methodologies, guidelines, and 
templates and to act as a resource center for business groups looking to design quality 
activities and functions into organizations and projects.  CM&P has created two 
methodologies in which QA issues and standards are addressed and defined ⎯ Project 
Management Methodology and System Life Cycle Methodology.  The former methodology 
is influenced by ISO 9000 standards and is in its second generation (PM2).  The latter 
methodology was developed from SEI’s core principles and is in its third iteration (SC3), 
the first having been developed in 1988.   
The PM2 and SC3 methodologies are not CMM certified or ISO compliant, nor is their 
use mandated throughout the corporation.  However, there is currently a corporate-wide 
initiative under way to ensure the SBUs and solutions centers conform to PM2 
methodology standards at a minimum.  Today, roughly half of the more than 75 solutions 
centers follow PM2 standards in client projects and three are using the guidelines 
established by SC3.  Again, these methodologies consist of guidelines and templates and 
are, of necessity, flexible enough to be used by EDS’ highly diverse SBUs and solutions

centers.  For example, corporate guidelines describe the role of QA, how to develop a QA 
plan, and the steps involved in an overall QA process.  They do not, however, describe 
specific steps, activities, and deliverables.   
However, there are organizations throughout EDS that follow project management and 
QA practices that ⎯ due to client requirements, internal organizational practices, or 
geographic location ⎯ adhere to processes that are more stringent than the PM2 and SC3 
suggested methodologies.  Many, in fact, use processes that are ISO or SEI-certified or 
that have SEI-authorized assessors responsible for assessing internal processes.  Some 
predate the corporate methodologies, some were developed independently, and some are 
variations based on overall internal guidelines.  This speaks to the highly autonomous 
nature of EDS SBUs and solutions centers and the fact that while standards adherence 
may not always be mandated internally, it can often be driven by customer requirements.   
Although practices vary ⎯ in some cases, considerably ⎯ from one SBU or Solutions 
Center to another, EDS Service Quality Assurance (SQA) activities can be roughly 
characterized by the following example of the Process Improvement Group that is part of 
EDS’ Xerox strategic account organization.  The group has four or five SQA personnel 
assigned to work with the roughly 450 systems engineers that make up the EDS project 
teams that serve the Xerox account worldwide.  One of these individuals will typically be 
assigned to a project at the acceptance stage.  His or her responsibility is to assist in the 
formulation of the project QA plan, conduct coaching or mentoring activities as needed, 
and conduct periodic reviews as agree to in the overall project plan.  The group does not 
do independent testing, but it does work to ensure that test plans and acceptance criteria 
are part of every project, that test details are documented, and those tests are scheduled 
and carried out.   
SQA personnel review projects at a minimum of once a month.  They review hard and soft 
copy deliverables, interview project team members, and conduct debriefing meetings with 
EDS team members.  Typically, client personnel are not interviewed as part of the ongoing 
project review process.  However, they may be interviewed by SQA as the result of issues 
raised during post-project customer satisfaction reviews.  Project managers, who typically

every QA activity and finding having to do with projects for which they are directly 
responsible.  SQA reviewers also send edited and summarized monthly reports to SBU 
senior managers and program managers.  Reports are not delivered to client personnel.  
However, if issues that require interaction with clients are identified during reviews, 
project managers are responsible for meeting with client personnel to resolve such issues.  
Failing resolution at the client-project manager level, program managers are brought into 
the discussions.   
In responding to potential client RFPs or bidding on contracts, SBUs and solutions 
centers include QA practice descriptions and activities in pre-sales materials and 
presentations.  Among those organizations that adhere to corporate, or strict internal 
guidelines, QA activities and deliverables are also detailed in statements of work and 
individual project plans.  To the extent that QA results play a role in performance reviews, 
the tendency is to use them as one of the criteria to measure the effectiveness of individual 
project leaders and, to a lesser extent, program managers.  More and more, adherence to 
pre-defined processes at the organizational level (and, although less so, at the corporate 
level) are used as key performance measures and, as such, influence bonuses and other 
remuneration plans.   
Most SQA groups have internal networks wherein they share data and experiences.  
Increasingly, reports (minus client names), shared information, and the cross-
organizational working groups are contributing to a body of corporate knowledge.  
Information from such sources plays a major role in the activities of the CM&P 
organization as it works to create best practice models while allowing each SBU and 
solutions center to identify and document its own particular process and activity 
requirements.

operational level.  The biggest difference is in the diversity of internal standards and 
practices used throughout the firm.  While there is an effort under way to establish 
overarching methodologies to be used as the basis for all such practices, the details of how

PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) 
Within PwC, responsibility for QA and risk management functions resides with the P&L 
business units.  At the same time, each of the firm’s three major geographic regions ⎯ the 
Americas; Europe, the Middle East, and Africa; and Asia/Pacific ⎯ has an organization 
responsible for overall QA and risk management standards and practices.  The functions 
of the regional QA organizations are in the areas of review and advisory services, best 
practices development, and the coordination of internal practices to ensure consistency of 
QA and risk management at both the regional and corporate levels.  
The history of PwC’s regional QA organizations goes back to the pre-merger days of Price 
Waterhouse, Inc.  Providing a centralized repository of information and expertise, the 
practice of having separate QA oversight functions became formalized in 1988.   
Today, each PwC regional QA group is made up of a combination of permanent and 
rotating staff that tend to be senior consultants.  Members report directly to business unit 
heads and, through dotted-line organization structures, to the regional QA heads.  
Depending on the financial scope and, in some cases, the complexity of a particular 
engagement, QA staff can get involved in projects as early as the request for proposal 
(RFP) review and response stage.  They are responsible for recognizing and 
understanding risk issues and potential quality barriers up front in order to ensure that 
engagements are organized to address them.  Engagement teams are responsible for 
following QA processes and the ultimate quality of deliverables.  QA teams are responsible 
for ensuring that engagement teams put such processes in place and for determining 
whether or not they are being followed. 
Regardless of when QA staff are assigned to engagements, reviews are supposed to occur 
on a quarterly basis.  The process starts with a review of relevant documentation ⎯ 
proposals, contracts, project plans, deliverables, and issues logs.  These last are kept by

Page 121
engagement teams and are part of the standard PwC QA process.  Items reviewed include 
milestones and dependencies in addition to quality control test plans and acceptance 
criteria.  The QA staff member will then meet with the engagement team and one or two 
client sponsors or decision-makers.  PwCs objective for such reviews is to identify issues, 
put them in writing, and assign responsibility for their resolution.  During this phase, a 
QA review may decide that additional reviews be conducted on a more frequent schedule 
than the normal quarterly cycle ⎯ the goal being to proactively identify problem areas 
and develop resolution plans. 
As a rule, QA reviews seldom identify issues that have not already been noted by the 
engagement teams.  The same holds true for recommendations ⎯ those that are identified 
during the QA review have typically been proposed and implemented and/or rejected by

Andersen’s corporate-wide Quality Management System (QMS) covers all aspects of
