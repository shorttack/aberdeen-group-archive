"What's the price/performance?" is
the first question users ask when they
consider buying a new computer. Cal­
culating the price is a pretty straight­
forward process—though rampant
discounting has made it more difficult
—but calculating the performance is
another matter entirely.
The usual performance measure­
ment for transaction-processing sys­
tems is transactions per second, or
TPS, per processor. While TPS com­
parison among products in a single
vendor's line are usually reliable, the
differences in hardware and software
usually render comparison among
vendors difficult, even meaningless.
The industry has "agreed" on a
standard transaction, known various­
ly as a TP1, or an ET1, transaction, or-
a debit-credit benchmark. Determin­
ing the TPS rating of a system should
just be a matter of feeding it TP1 trans-
\j performance figures-, rekindle
actions and measuring how many it
can do.
But this isn't so, says Omri Serlin of
ITOM International Co., in Los Altos,
Calif. Such measurements, he says,
"are, at best, ambiguous and always
the subject of intense controversy."
Unless a number of other factors—
such as response time—are held con­
stant, benchmark comparisons of
equipment from various vendors pro­
vides only a crude guideline. Since
there is no independent testing labo­
ratory for transaction-processing sys­
tems, Serlin and other analysts de­
pend on figures produced by the
vendors themselves. Serlin regularly
reports these internal corporate
benchmarks in a newsletter, noting
that he has no independent means to
check them.

sons became even more controver-

Stratus that would give its system a

Tolerant says the figures it used
represent comparable hardware and
software configurations. The competi­
tors say it doesn't. Serlin believes
there isn't really enough data avail­
able to be sure what performance fig­
ures are comparable. He printed the
figures Tolerant used, but says that he
just publishes the performance bench­
marks; he doesn't endorse them or
ensure their comparability or validity.
Serlin suggests users run their own
benchmarks, with transactions of the
type they expect to use.
While the price/performance com­
parisons Tolerant released last week
are debatable, the product it unveiled
is a clear, if not belated, improvement
over its earlier system. In 1983,
founder Eli Alon promised that Toler­
ant Transaction Systems Inc. would
deliver fault-tolerant Unix-based
processing in 1985. Since then, the
firm dropped Alon from its ranks
and the word "transaction" from its
name. But last week it finally ful­
filled his promise, at least six months
late, by introducing the software and
hardware needed to convert its sin­
gle-processor systems into fault-tol­
erant multiprocessor systems.

There are both differences and simi­
larities between the Tolerant ap­
proach to fault tolerance and that of
market leader Tandem and its distant
second-place competitor Stratus. Like
Tandem, Tolerant achieves fault toler­
ance with a combination of hardware
and software. Like Stratus, it offers
multiple-microprocessor building
blocks. Like both, it offers linear ex­
pandability. Unlike Tandem, it will
not sell many of its computers directly
to end users.
Major vendors that have invested in
Tolerant—they won't allow their
names to be mentioned yet—plan to be
its resellers, says Shirley Henry, Toler­
ant's marketing director. The only ex­
ceptions will be a few technically so­
phisticated end users.
Like Tandem, Tolerant plans to
stress the transactions per second per
dollar available from its systems—us­
ing whatever controversial figures
come to hand. Tolerant will offer fault
tolerance, but downplay it. Tandem
can get away with that because nearly
everyone now believes its systems are
fault tolerant. Tolerant may someday
finesse the fault-tolerant question, but
last week's disagreements indicate
that neither Tolerant nor any other
vendor is likely to satisfy users with
its price/performance calculations any
time soon. —Paul E. Schindler Jr.
