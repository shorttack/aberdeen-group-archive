 May 4, 1998

Videoconferencing Boost

Intel's TeamStation room system promises PC-like ease of use and pricing

By 
 Mary E. Thyfault

ntel is looking to bring the ease and integrated applications of desktop videoconferencing to
room systems.

The vendor's new TeamStation system, based on standard PC technology, will let conference
attendees share PC applications and access network applications, including those on the
Internet. Until now, room videoconferencing systems were based on proprietary hardware and
often required participants to perform cumbersome tasks to share applications. 

"People have had this wonderful suite of applications at their desktop, then you walk into a
conference room and you're faced with 1950s technology," says Michael Witteman, director of
product development for Intel's business communications unit in Hillsboro, Ore. Many people
don't finish business during videoconferences, Witteman says, because they don't have PC app
tools with them. "People are constantly breaking out for five-minute cycles of non-productivity
time," he says. "With TeamStation, people can meet in the conference room and get all of their
work done." 

Caterpillar Financial Services Corp. is installing TeamStation in six North American cities, five
European cities, and Singapore. "We're hoping that people can be looking at an Excel spreadsheet
and comment on it at the same time," says Gwen Leuthold, performance consultant for
Caterpillar Financial, a Nashville, Tenn., unit of farm-equipment maker Caterpillar.

The parent company wants to eventually enable most employees to easily use
either desktop or
room videoconferencing. The company projects it will have 1,000 videoconferencing users by
mid-1999. Caterpillar has already installed more than 30 TeamStations.

Using a Web interface, conference participants can download presentations from their desktop to
the room system, which is attached to the corporate network. TeamStation runs the Pentium II
microprocessor and filtering software to compress the video signal, send it over the
communications line, and decompress it at the other end. This creates a less jerky video
presentation over low-speed lines, says Peter Kastner, an analyst with the Aberdeen Group.

By relying on standard PC technology, Intel is able to price TeamStation below proprietary room
videoconferencing systems. TeamStation is priced at $9,999, or $11,999 including monitor; until
recently, full-featured room systems have been priced closer to $20,000. "As the costs come
down, this technology will be fundamental to communications in the 21st century," Kastner
says.

But price isn't all that's important to customers. "The most important feature of TeamStation is
its ease of use," says Gus Otto, director of videoconferencing at Caterpillar. "It's eight easy
steps to put together out of the box."

TeamStation also interconnects with existing videoconferencing systems that rely on the H.323
ISDN standard and systems based on the newer H.320 IP videoconferencing standard.
