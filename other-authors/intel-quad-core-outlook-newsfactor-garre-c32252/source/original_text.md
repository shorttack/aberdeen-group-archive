 The Outlook on Intel's Quad-Core Chips 

 By David Garrett 

 November 15, 2006 8:37AM 

 With the release of the Core 2 Extreme and Xeon 5300 quad-core processors, Intel will regain some of the thunder lost to rival AMD, which has enjoyed a string of major announcements in the past two months, among them a new line of Dell servers using AMD's highly regarded Opteron processors.

 Intel's new quad-core processors are fast, but how fast will they move off the shelves? According to Peter Kastner, vice-president and research director for enterprise technology at the Aberdeen Group, the answer bodes well for Intel. 

"I see a rapid adoption reaching mainstream in Q2 of next year," he said. "These should ramp up very rapidly because Intel has priced them to drive market acceptance."

Intel's new quad-cores come in two flavors. The first, the Xeon 5300 series for servers, offers performance up to 50 percent better than Intel's dual-core Xeon 5100 series. The Core 2 Extreme series, designed for desktop use with companies and consumers in mind, offers up to 80 percent faster performance for highly threaded apps, according to Intel.

In an interview with the Financial Times, Intel's Pat Gelsinger said, "In one year, we've achieved a four-fold increase in performance. That is stunning."

Serving Up Servers

Kastner noted that not every market will adopt the blazing new CPUs at once, but some, such as the business server market, are ready for new chips.

"On a server, which can use multiple cores, almost by nature this should be very attractive for a huge percentage of volume server shipments," he said.

That means that Intel will regain some of the thunder lost to rival AMD, which has enjoyed a string of major announcements in the past two months, among them a new line of Dell servers using AMD's highly regarded Opteron processors. For years, Dell had been one of Intel's most loyal customers, refusing to launch Opteron-based servers even though HP, Sun, and others had long since done so.

According to Kastner, companies seeking to reduce the number of servers they maintain -- and to reduce, in turn, not only the cost of maintaining but also the cost of cooling those machines -- will see promise in Intel's new wares.

"Quad-core becomes really exciting for the many I.T. organizations that are looking at server consolidation through virtualization ," he said.

Beyond Servers

Quad-core chips also will be used in high-end business workstations where power is key, including engineering and CAD applications, not to mention a growing number of product-design functions where simulation and 3D modeling have become standards in bringing products from the research bench to retail shelves.

And consumers will cast a close and quite possibly hungry eye at Intel's new chips. Kastner described the performance of quad-core processors in gaming, multimedia, and high-end consumer apps as "awesome."

"All the reviews I've read have said this is your Christmas dream," he concluded.
