The race is on to create a future based on Windows NT clustering.Bill Gates is trying to retain his place as top dog of Wolfpack, the groupof vendors set up to champion the cause. Ian Ashbridge reports. Written by sarah petrie Computing , 23 Nov 1996 Bill Gates bets. He bets big. When jockey Frankie Dettori won seven races in a row at an Ascot race meeting last month it seemed incredible that one man could steer so many horses to victory. Indeed, it was a 25,000 to 1 shot. With Windows NT clustering, Gates is betting that he can lead a pack of dogs - a Wolfpack to be precise - to victory. His gamble is that he can remain in charge of a group of vendors, known as the Wolfpack. It aims to deliver the hardware, applications, databases, tools and everything else which will make Windows NT clusters running on parallel Intel machines the dominant enterprise operating system within 10 years. So, can he pull it off? There is always an element of uncertainty in long-range predictions. But like every other PC company in the world, Gates is looking to the server market for growth, especially as the Internet has thrown his PC plans into disarray, forcing him to promise a version of Windows to run on stripped down PCs. Gates knows that, at this level, Microsoft has little hope of becoming a company worth tens of billions of dollars, something which Compaq has said it will achieve. The company has set a target of $40bn-worth (#24bn) of annual revenues by the year 2000. The vendors in Wolfpack aim to deliver the standards for different parts of the NT clustering platform. This first phase will take two years. Windows NT clustering itself will hit the streets in the first quarter of 1997 as a two-node system. It will not be ready to scale to four or eight nodes until at least six months later, and will deliver a failover solution described as 'warm start' or back-up which will run in 15 to 30 minutes. Exactly which software will run on it has yet to be defined, but Oracle and others are trying to bend their software into the clustering mould. However, clustering, in the true sense of the term, means high availability, scalability, shared facilities, and savings in resource management. Peter Kastner, senior vice president of research firm the Aberdeen Group, says: 'In reality, no one company in the Wolfpack has the whole toolkit. They're all at the starting gate. The race is on.' And developing hardware and software for clusters with NT as an operating system is no stroll. But the success of NT on Intel in standalone servers means analysts' predicted market size only has to be close to the mark for the stakes to be huge. 'One in seven people investing in NT are buying for clustering,' says Kastner. 'But we are warning users against the siren song of Microsoft Wolfpack. If you believe you can run on Microsoft only, wait for two years.' The spilt within Wolfpack seems fairly clear. Favouring Tandem's Servernet technology are firms including Compaq, Dell, and, not surprisingly, Tandem, which may also have bagged IBM. They are up against Digital, the original Windows NT partner, which has been running NT for three years on Intel and its Alpha platform in the hope of breathing some life into its sadly moribund architecture. Digital already has Digital Clusters for Windows NT, a package of services for PC LANs. In its technical white paper published in April, the company set out its stall for its clustering technology. In it, Digital states that there are two data access models used in clustering solutions - the partitioned and the symmetric. The difference between them is that under the partitioned model, only one cluster member can access data at any time, while symmetric data access used in VAX clusters delivers shared facilities all the time. The section on cluster data access models concludes: 'While the symmetric data access model may have benefits for future versions of Digital Clusters, it is not supported in the current version.' So Digital feels it already has the technology to make clustering work as it should. This is as strident as you would ever expect Digital to be, but scratch a little deeper into Wolfpack and the fangs start to appear. Ian MacKenzie, marketing director at Digital UK says: 'Tandem is just jumping on the NT bandwagon, Compaq's solutions for clusters need special hardware and none of these companies possess Digital's support infrastructure. There have been no decisions about which technology will be adopted by Wolfpack.' According to Hugh Jenkins, group systems product manager at Compaq UK, standards are key. He says: 'The issue of standards will make or break clustering. In the past, clustering has always been proprietary. We were one of the initial drivers behind this, and we are getting people to define a standard. There is a lot of work to be done, but it is an ongoing development effort.' The Servernet backers don't all agree with each other, however. 'Initially, clustering will not be about scalability it will be about protection against single-server failure,' claims Jenkins, speaking as a man with his eye firmly on the workgroup server market in banks and building societies. Clustering would allow Compaq to send a clear message to these companies that it can be trusted to deliver systems running mission-critical applications for big companies. Norwich Union would be the typical target market for NT clusters. It is an IBM mainframe shop running ES9000s, 45 IBM and Sequent Unix servers and about 120 NT servers from Compaq carrying out file, print and some call-centre mission-critical applications at department level. Clustering would be an ideal solution, but its IT department can already deliver a form of hot boxing, because it has invested in standby equipment with disk mirroring and redundant array of independent disk (Raid) technology. Consequently, it currently has no plans to cluster its NT boxes. Allan Dornan, group distributed computing manager at Norwich Union, says: 'For most users we can create warm standby. They already get transparent access to more than one server. In high availability, if the server falls over, you want seamless carry-on and that is not the case with NT clusters.' Unlike Compaq, Tandem specialises in back-office technology, where its reputation for fault-tolerant systems running the world's banks, gained over 20 years, is second to none. And while it wants to move into the low-end server market - as revealed by its first foray into the Intel market with its launch of a whole rack of servers on 30 October - it wants NT clusters to be scalable, bringing lots of applications to its core customer base. Chris Miller, IT manager at insurance brokers Axa Equity, leads a well-established in-house IT section running Tandem Hymalaya mainframe-class servers. Axa runs two sites, one with a 12-processor Tandem Hymalaya and a six-processor version as a hot standby in Coventry, which also runs dedicated services for that office. A few years ago, the smaller machine crashed and everything was dumped on Miller's lap. It happened on a Friday, and he claims that by Monday everyone at the Coventry office turned up for work and logged on as usual. It was still a major headache, in spite of using proven fault-tolerant technology. 'I am extremely suspicious of long-term predictions,' says Miller. 'If in 10-years time the world is running NT we will not be unhappy, but as far as Wolfpack is concerned, I'd be very impressed if they delivered it in 1998.' Miller refuses to predict whether he will ever run his core business on NT. Tandem now admits that, in future, its machines will run on platforms for which there are the largest number of applications. It is licensing its technology for the first time to give it a stake in the Wintel future. Would Compaq and Dell have signed up with Tandem's Servernet if the company already had a presence in the workgroup server market - the very space both are hoping to move into next on the back of ever more powerful Intel processors? They could have opted for Digital technology or even NCR's Lifekeeper fault-tolerant software, but these firms would be a more immediate threat at the low-end server level. Digital once reaped rich rewards through its VAX clusters. In fact, it was only one year ago that research company Forrester said the company offered the best NT clustering product and that it would leave the competition standing. But if Wolfpack is about developing standards, then Microsoft, coming from a PC world into a commoditised server market alongside Intel, is bound to listen to the hardware vendors which helped make it the world's most successful software company. Compaq needs NT clustering to work if it is to significantly increase its world standing, Digital needs it too, but on its own terms. For some of the others, such as Tandem, it is also vitally important. Those feeling secure, such as Hewlett-Packard (HP) and IBM, are relaxed about NT clustering. HP is taking a Unix and Windows NT stand as it doesn't want to be caught short. As usual, with IBM you could get as many answers as there are divisions within the computer giant. Sun, arrogant as ever, merely calls them all failed system houses and Intel resellers. Never short of an alternative, Sun's message is of Interactive Internet Computing, a new paradigm where NT clusters don't matter. In any case, it says it already has scalability in Unix, which has been around for 20 years, and is now proven as a server operating system. Jenkins claims: 'Sun is just about the only company without an NT strategy. It is a Unix bigot and this will be its downfall.' But Dornan claims a lot of people in the user community still consider Sun a good long-term bet. Within the Wolfpack consortium, it will take all of Bill Gates' influence to stop this drive for openness through dominance to collapse. It happened to ACE and all the other Unix standards bodies of the early 1990s when just about every organisation suddenly declared themselves an open systems company. Of course, this time, there is no choice for those in the consortium as it is a battle for survival, and fear is a great motivator. Perhaps there is even a place for Digital. Finally forced to accept that it failed to convince the world of the power of Alpha, it will become a dedicated Intel shop like Tandem before finally becoming the services company it said it was in 1993. Microsoft owns the operating system, and, by 1998, the NT clustering standards should have been agreed. Each company already has other proprietary offerings which they are selling for NT, but most of these will not be compatible with the eventual Wolfpack standard. Established users are likely to be forced ultimately into adopting a migration strategy which no big user currently wants to contemplate. Both Miller and Dornan say that, even when NT clustering has been proved to work and every application runs seamlessly, it will still be years before they expect to start ditching Unix and mainframe package solutions or start porting all of their bespoke applications to run on it. If yours is a small to medium-sized company running a lot of PC-based applications, NT clusters are probably the perfect path to take, but for big corporations the jury will be out for at least another two years. 'Microsoft has still got a load of AS/400s running its own business at the moment,' says Kastner. 'Microsoft is a software factory, it knows nothing about support and this technology is going to be sold through partners,' he says. 'When Microsoft is ready to run its own $8bn business on Windows NT clusters and rely on its partners for support, it will be time to consider it as a mission-critical operating system. Until that time, hedge your bets.' Kastner warns that no single Wolfpack vendor has a complete clustering toolkit, that one in seven NT investments is aimed at clustering, and cautions enterprises against the 'siren song' of Microsoft-only Wolfpack deployments pending two years of maturation. Article maps the internal Wolfpack split between Tandem Servernet backers (Compaq, Dell, Tandem) and Microsoft-aligned vendors.
