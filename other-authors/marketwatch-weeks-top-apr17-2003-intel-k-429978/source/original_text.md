By CBS.MarketWatch.com

It was a topsy-turvy week on Wall Street, with equal and opposite forces -- from earnings news to world events, from tax cuts to tax angst, from rumored acquisitions to completed mergers, and from legal wrangling to regulatory shuffling -- exerting themselves on investors and on stocks and vying for supremacy. 

With the Cubs and Royals in first place and the defending World Series champion 
 Angels being dealt , it would be more than fair to question the order of things.

Fortunately for those wanting to put a definitive up or down arrow on the cockeyed week, the three main stock indexes were willing to choose a direction, though they hardly moved in lockstep. The Nasdaq ended the week undefeated, climbing to a three-month high, while the S&P 500 and the Dow ran up 3-1 records.

WASHINGTON (CBS.MW) -- The U.S. economy "urgently" needs at least $550 billion in tax cuts, President Bush said Tuesday, and it needs it "quick." Labeling his plan a "jobs and growth" package and seeking to overcome congressional opposition to a such a costly tax reduction, Bush argued that the initiative "was urgent in January" and "is even more urgent now."

 A brave new WorldCom?

ASHBURN, Va. (CBS.MW) -- WorldCom surprised Wall Street Monday with a relatively quick agreement on a bankruptcy reorganization plan, featuring a new name and a new headquarters, but the long-distance giant faces obstacles in its recovery from the biggest corporate scandal in U.S. history. 
 See full story. 

 The cost of trading with the enemy

WASHINGTON (CBS.MW) -- When individual Americans are accused of aiding terrorists, they're thrown in jail and their names are dragged through the mud. But when major U.S. corporations are caught trading with the enemy, they get only a slap on the wrist. 
 See full story. 

 Slow adoption of free IRS e-filing

SAN FRANCISCO (CBS.MW) -- With the tax-filing deadline mere days away, just 2.3 percent of those eligible had taken advantage of the IRS's free e-filing program. While tax officials claimed satisfaction with the adoption rate -- which, they said, exceeded an internal goal of 2 million free filings -- a low-income-taxpayer advocate offered a different take: "A lot of the people that would need this kind of service don't have access to the Internet, and don't have the computer literacy, or the literacy skills to do this," said Julie Kruse, director of the National Community Tax Coalition, provider of free tax preparation to low-income taxpayers. 
 See full story. 

 Real Estate: Zoning out

LOS ANGELES (CBS.MW) -- Millions of Americans can't find affordable housing, forcing them to pay more of their income toward rent or mortgage than is prudent or to commute ever-longer distances. Among the reasons are zoning restrictions that all but outlaw affordable housing in far too many municipalities. 
 See Resident Authority by Steve Kerch. 

 Commentary: Back to our regularly scheduled bear market

SAN FRANCISCO (CBS.MW) -- The much-anticipated "war rally" turns out to have had all the power of an errant Iraqi Scud. And now that Wall Street has shifted its attention from the shock and awe of Baghdad bombings to the collateral damage of the economy and corporate earnings, investors don't much like what they see.
