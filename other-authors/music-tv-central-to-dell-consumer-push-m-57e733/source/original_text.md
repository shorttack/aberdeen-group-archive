In a move that was highly anticipated, Dell /quotes/comstock/15*!dell /quotes/nls/dell ( DELL 12.77 , -0.10 , -0.78% ) held a conference to show off its first company-branded digital music player, a digital music store, a flat-screen television-computer monitor, a new PDA, a revamped gaming computer and a video projector. Additionally, Dell gave a demonstration of its remodeled online store, which the company said would be active in mid-October. "What we're doing is responding to the demands and needs of our customers," Dell said. "We have a $5 billion software peripheral business that gives us great insight into what customers want." Among the products Dell intends to sell in time for the Christmas shopping season is its music player, the Dell Digital Jukebox. The company believes that Digital Jukebox buyers will also find it convenient to use the device to download songs from its new digital music store. Dell's new consumer push includes a 17-inch flat-screen television, which can also be used as a computer monitor, a new Axim X3 PDA that is lighter and thinner than the company's Axim X5 model, an upgraded Dimension XPS gaming PC, and the Dell 2200MP projector, which the company is touting for use with DVD players. However, while Dell posted images of its new products on its Web site, there were no technological details, pricing information, or specific release dates for any of the items. When asked if Dell had any arrangements with the leading music companies, Michael Dell only said that there was "no reason" for any music companies to not make their songs available through Dell's store. With regard to the pricing and availability of its music player, PDA and other products, Dell said that information would be available when the devices are ready to go on sale. Analysts said Dell's moves signal that the company has realized there has been a change in the role of the personal computer for the retail customer, and that Dell also needs to break free from its image of being a business-PC company. "This is their way of putting a stake in the ground in the mass consumer market," said Tim Bajarin, president of research firm Creative Strategies. "They're reflecting something that's been going on with the PC for the past two years; it's become the center of the digital lifestyle." While Dell claimed to be responding solely to the demands of its customers, it would be almost impossible for the company to not see what its competitors are doing and want to get in on the game. Hewlett-Packard /quotes/comstock/13*!hpq /quotes/nls/hpq ( HPQ 36.82 , -0.02 , -0.05% ) recently launched more than 100 new consumer products, Gateway /quotes/comstock/11i!asiyf ( ASIYF 1.76 , -0.01 , -0.64% ) has spent most of the year adding flat-screen TVs, digital cameras and other items to its product line, and Apple Computer /quotes/comstock/15*!aapl /quotes/nls/aapl ( AAPL 136.42 , +0.07 , +0.05% ) pulled off a coup with the first online music store to garner the support of the major music companies, and has also upgraded its IPod music players and notebook computers. Peter Kastner, of the Aberdeen Group, said that the state of the market, and Dell's experience with its low-cost business model, should pay off, even though its competitors have a head start. "What is working for Gateway and H-P can certainly work for Dell too, with Dell.com becoming even more of an online electronics superstore," Kastner said. As expected, competitors were diplomatic, but also unmoved about Dell's new consumer products foray. "There's a lot more (in the consumer space) that just selling music or TVs," said Mike Moeller, an H-P spokesman. "Trying to figure out how to integrate all the parts together is a bit of a challenge." Dell shares rose 2 cents to close at $33.92. Rex Crum is a reporter for CBS.MarketWatch.com in San Francisco.
