 Vista: How Soon Until Deployment? 

 By David Garrett 

 November 3, 2006 8:52AM 

 With Windows Vista availabel to businesses on November 30, the question on many minds is how soon companies can or should upgrade to the new operating system. Some analysts are saying Vista rollouts in enterprise settings won't be complete for many years, while others are saying Vista can be deployed quickly.

 The average Joe will have to wait until 2007 to get a copy of Windows Vista, but companies will have it as early as November 30, along with new versions of Office and Exchange, according to Microsoft . That's less than a month away, yet it could take years for companies to start -- much less finish -- deployments.

Peter Kastner, vice-president and research director for enterprise technology at Aberdeen, noted that companies on a three-year hardware lifecycle (a fairly standard plan) buy a fraction of their total hardware needs per month. "It's a cadence," he said. "That means Vista will be fully deployed sometime in 2010 as new, Vista-laden machines replace old XP machines."

If Redmond-based Microsoft hopes that companies will pick up the pace -- or ditch their long-standing caution -- it's out of luck. "There's no light-switch conversion where everyone turns on Vista one morning," said Kastner.

Forced Upgrades?

Beyond hope, Microsoft does have a way to push the adoption cycle forward. "Sometime in 2007, Microsoft will likely stop availability of Windows XP in small business systems sold at retail, forcing a Vista switch," said Kastner. 

Microsoft also can cut support or slow the rate at which it patches XP -- although, if past behavior is any guide, it will be a long time before Ballmer & Co. take those steps.

Answers to certain key questions might have an impact on how quickly Vista is adopted in the enterprise. First, of course, is how well it works -- or rather, how well other applications, including hardware drivers, work with Vista.

Gartner analysts Michael Silver and Neil MacDonald, in a statement on Gartner's Web site, said they believe it will take "about 18 months from the time Windows Vista ships to test applications, get independent software vendors to support applications, build images and run pilots." 

They also noted that companies must start the testing process now to be ready for rollout by 2008.

Better Sooner?

But Aberdeen's Kastner said he thinks it can be done sooner. "We disagree with Gartner that business needs to wait until 2008 to start deploying Vista," he said, adding that bugs and software incompatibilities will be patched regularly. "Fixes come out every month, not just in service packs," he said.

Another key question? Features like Vista's much-hyped Aero interface, with semi-opaque windows and other eye candy, need more horsepower than most business machines currently have. How much power , exactly? 

In response to the question, Microsoft has split Vista machines into two categories: "Vista Capable" PCs that run Vista's core functions and "Vista Premium Ready" PCs that can handle all the bells and whistles.

At a bare minimum, "Vista Capable" machines must have an 800-MHz processor and 512 MB of RAM. "Vista Premium Ready" machines need 1-GHz processors with 1 GB of RAM and at least 128 MB of graphics memory -- a good deal more than many of today's business PCs.
