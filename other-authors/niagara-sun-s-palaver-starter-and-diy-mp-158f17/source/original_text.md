Niagara: Sun's palaver starter? (and DIY MP3) Published on Computerworld Blogs ( http://blogs.computerworld.com ) Niagara: Sun's palaver starter? (and DIY MP3) By IT Blogwatch Created Nov 15 2005 - 7:46am In today's IT Blogwatch , we look at Sun's UltraSparc T1 chip - Niagara. Not to mention a howto for making your own flash MP3 player. Sun has some good news for the environment and customers wanting more powerful CPU's that use less electricity but is it enough to bring Sun back into the "sun light"? Tom Krazit [1] reports: " Sun Microsystems Inc. will try to convince legions of users who have switched to low-cost Windows and Linux x86 hardware for Web serving to switch back to the company's forthcoming UltraSparc T1 servers ... will be available with either four, six, or eight cores, and each of those cores will be capable of running eight threads ... maximum power consumption of 70 watts [per server] ... Sun now hopes to recapture those users by focusing on two areas: multithreading and power consumption." » Colm Smyth [2] gives a good description of what it can offer: "If a CPU core can run 3 times faster than memory, then it would spend one unit of time doing something useful (data processing) and then 3 units of time waiting for memory; that's 3/4 of the time waiting! So with CMT, the core does a neat juggling act - while the first thread is waiting three units of time for the request from memory to complete, three other cores can each run for one unit of time (and of course, each of the other cores has to wait for memory too). But the bottom line is that even though the threads are waiting for memory 3/4 of the time, the core is actually running all four threads at their maximum speed! Now the next brain-twister about CMT is that it's effectively a higher powered CPU that need s less electrical power; a four-thread core needs little more power than a single-threaded one even though it's doing 4 times the work. Less power means less heat generated, which leads to smaller fans (which in turn use less power). For mobile users, it means longer life batteries ... I like Bill Moffit's CMT waterfall analogy [3] from way back - adding cores to run threads is like getting a wider waterfall, adding processor speed is like a higher one." » Barton Bank [4] says he's, "always skeptical of Sun. Big on hype, short on delivery. But judging by IBM's poison pen letter, Sun's Niagara chip is worth watching." He then goes on to quote a PR FUD-o-gram from IBM: "Sun has been beating the Niagara drum over the last two weeks in an attempt to revive its floundering server business. Since Sun now gives away its software, the foundation of its business is built on it server products. It's becoming clear that Sun is betting its business on Niagara, but as it once again tries to buy time and momentum with an early product release, a number of concerns remain." » Peter S. Kastner [5]: "I recall being briefed on the Niagara roadmap circa 2000. Sun has had a poor delivery record in Sparc over the past several years. That has hurt the company; some say it has hurt badly. Wouldn't this technology have been market-turning, say, mid-2002? With today's big Wall Street Journal story on computer power consumption, Sun's eight cores in a 70-watt envelope is the right recipe. Niagara is a big help to Sun's Sparc base, but I doubt it will shake the market's penchant for x64-based servers, in Sun's case, AMD Opterons." » Gil Friend [6]: "The hook: new energy efficient products from Sun. The angle: top minds from inside and outside Sun, including Christine Ervin (former Assistant Secretary of Energy, former head of US GBC), Jonathan Koomey (Stanford University and Lawrence Berkeley Lab), Noah Horowitz (NRDC), Amory Lovins (RMI), Greg Papadopoulis (Sun EVP). Michael Krasny (KQED Forum) hosting. McNealy talked about efficient servers and thin-client computing ... (Exciting to see this, since we [Natural Logic] wrote Sun's first environmental policy statement - back in the early 1990s - which focused on linking Sun's environmetal performance with their brand and their products' price/performance value proposition.)" » Quark, 2Technology [7]: "It expects to ship systems based on the processor by December-end." [We'll look forward to seeing if they meet that deadline] "Sun claimed that the chip gives the company a five-year leap on IBM's 'Power' and Intel's 'Xeon' processors ...
