BORDER=0 WIDTH=160 HEIGHT=600 ALT="Click Here"></A> </NOSCRIPT> The recent partnership between Microsoft and Novell is good business for Microsoft and Novell, or the former rivals would never have signed what some have called a landmark deal. But is it good business for their customers, the enterprise clients who rely on Windows and SuSE Linux to run their companies? According to Peter Kastner, vice president and research director for enterprise technology at the Aberdeen Group, there's at least one benefit to be seen from the Microsoft-Novell move, as well as Oracle's recent announcement that it will support Linux for lower fees than Red Hat does. "The real interest by large I.T. organizations in the recent Oracle and Microsoft announcements is in Tier One support," he said. "Enterprises want first-class Linux support without bringing more vendors to the table." Support, of course, is how most open-source firms make their money. Red Hat's version of Linux is free to download, for instance, but a call to the company's support lines costs money. More and more, enterprise clients want to reduce the number of calls they need to make when something goes wrong -- the "one throat to choke" approach -- and want to know those calls will be answered by marquee names with enough money to provide high-end support. "Oracle and Microsoft fit that role," said Kastner. Conflict Reduction Microsoft and Novell, of course, see their move as a win for customers, and in announcing it they lined up customers of all stripes to cheerlead. "This new Microsoft-Novell collaboration agreement could allow significant virtualization of servers, reducing the number of physical platforms we'd have to maintain," said Bill Schrier, Seattle's chief technology officer, in a prepared statement. He even said the Microsoft-Novell move would "save taxpayer dollars." "This collaboration of open source and proprietary software is a win-win for the I.T. industry and our customers because it provides greater choice when developing comprehensive solutions," added Jodi Honore, vice president of vendor management for Ingram Micro North America. Indeed, the notion that Windows and Linux products could work better together, reducing the number of conflicts and the amount of staff it takes to install and maintain them, could leave CIOs with grins on their faces. Microsoft Intentions? Part of the Microsoft-Novell partnership, for instance, includes a plan to develop document converters to pass data between the two platforms. But part of it also includes a covenant not to sue each other's customers for patent and copyright infringement, which has left some members of the open-source community wary of Microsoft's intentions. "This is apparently some kind of a covenant not to sue, not a true cross-licensing deal," wrote blogger and journalist Pamela Jones of Groklaw, a site that tracks legal issues relating to Linux and other open-source software. "I think that's how they plan to step over and around the GPL," she added, referring to the General Public License that accompanies some open source software, allowing it to be copied, modified, and distributed, free of charge.
