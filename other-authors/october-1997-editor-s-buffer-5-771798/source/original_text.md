October 1997 Editor's Buffer Dialing for Hubris Having bitten the forbidden fruit, vendors look for salvation A fter days of summer rain, Boston emerged refreshed on August 6: bright, clear, full of sun. It was the final day of the Object/Relational Summit--and across town, opening day for MacWorld Expo. In just a few hours, Apple Computer cofounder Steve Jobs would be on stage deferring to a giant video image of Microsoft CEO Bill Gates. Beamed in from Redmond, Gates would announce Microsoft's $150 million investment in Apple. Macintosh partisans would boo, hiss, groan, and grimace. But pushed to the brink of extinction, they would know in their hearts that Jobs had done the right thing. Time had come to turn the page and begin a new day. The Apple-Microsoft deal instantly became front-page news. Apple's long-running saga has captivated the country in a manner usually reserved for celebrity murder trials. Although other hardware and software companies have undoubtedly had a larger impact on people's lives, none has been such the object of love and emotional angst. Particularly for users outside the computer and IT industries, the Macintosh literally brought home the power of computing--not to mention publishing, computer graphics, and other activities formerly in the domain of specialists. Apple's fateful decision not to license its operating system and graphical interface--generally regarded as disastrous--merely made the company more beloved. The rest of the world bowed to the Microsoft conquerors; Apple did not. The Macintosh was well on its way to becoming computer technology's first true cult product. But just before the martyred Mac transcended business reality and floated into the heavens, Jobs, the business-savvy cofounder, pulled Apple back down to earth. "Like an old-time farming community, much of Silicon Valley is getting together in an effort to rebuild the fallen Apple Computer," reported The New York Times. The paper's August 7 edition called Microsoft's investment and the new board of directors--which includes Oracle's Larry Ellison, former IBM CFO Jerome York, and other luminaries--a "high-technology barn raising." Apple, as a national treasure, must not be allowed to fail. This seemed to be the prevailing view of many news providers: that only Apple can make people love computers. The computer industry simply couldn't afford to lose its most beloved product and maker. The following day, George Gilder, a well-known commentator on information technology economics, provided a more "real-politic" view of events. Writing in the August 8 edition of The Wall Street Journal, Gilder described the deal as a mere footnote in a "battle between the past and future, the war between the existing configuration of industries and the industries that will soon replace them." To Gilder, Microsoft and Apple represent the past. Microsoft is in the early stages of a desperate struggle against the erosion of its empire caused by a tidal shift toward Java-based network computing. With his investment, Gilder notes, Bill Gates may have thrown off the "antitrust constabulary." But he has not "shifted the tides of change. [Gates] has merely embarrassed Oracle's Ellison by increasing the price of any purchase of Apple, and he has trumped Netscape by buying dominance for the Microsoft browser in the next Apple operating system." Gilder concludes that "Gates can only win the future by fully embracing Java and programming for the network computer." It's not difficult to imagine Gates and his colleagues having a hearty laugh over that last sentence. The Apple investment makes good business sense for Microsoft, given the profits it takes as the only major business software supplier for the Macintosh platform. But will they have the last laugh? Will pride goeth before the fall for Gates' Windows empire? Back at the Object/Relational Summit, hubris was in a more advanced stage. A year ago, Informix Software was leading the pack in the race to capture new applications demanding universal, extended relational abilities. Michael Stonebraker, chief technical officer of Informix and creator of the Illustra DataBlade technology, was the preeminent object/relational visionary. CEO Phil White was leading the company into the billion-dollar revenue circle. Oracle was barely out of the object/relational starting blocks. But as they say, what a difference a year makes. On the opening day of the company's annual user group meeting, Informix announced the appointment of Robert J. Finocchio, Jr. as president and CEO, with White remaining in an Ellison-like capacity as chairman of the board. As White explained at a press conference, he would set the vision and close the big deals, while Finocchio righted the company's sales and operations. But by the time of the Summit two weeks later, White was gone. For all his charms, Wall Street and Informix shareholders could not forgive White for his rosy scenarios and plummeting revenues. Charles Phillips, a leading analyst with Morgan Stanley Dean Witter, wrote: "Informix has set a record for how quickly a company can go from being a factor to being fiction in investor's minds. At this stage, the only thing that counts is what sort of Marshall Plan the new CEO can orchestrate and how quickly he can implement ý he has to get up the learning curve fast, reduce headcount while retaining the superstars, plug the cash flow drain, and put some controls in place. The toughest of those tasks will be keeping the good people since the demand for talent at emerging companies in the Bay Area is insatiable. Moreover, many employees have seen long-time associates or supervisors jump ship; others may not be up for the long rebuilding process with Microsoft and Oracle waiting for them at the end of that long journey." Earlier in the year, White blamed himself for his company's obsessive fascination with the object/relational DataBlade technology and lust for primacy in the emerging universal database market. In his presentation to the Summit, Informix CTO Stonebraker was far less repentant. He described content management as a wide-open field ready to explode with hugely profitable applications. As to be expected, he blasted Oracle8, offered guarded praise for IBM's DB2 extender technology, and dismissed Microsoft's OLE DB-driven attempts at object/relationalness. Informix is in the early adopter stage, and "basically things are going just fine," Stonebraker said. "DataBlades are new software; and as with any new software, DBAs will have to test the heck out of it." Hovering in the air, however, was the question of whether DBAs and their IT organizations dare commit precious resources to leading-edge Informix applications given the company's delicate financial condition. Andy Mendelsohn, Oracle's vice president of Languages and Object/Relational Technology, offered an interesting historical view of database technology, ending with today's need for rich data type support and an object/relational model. While Informix concentrates on type extension and providing database engine support for as many third-party DataBlade vendors as possible, Oracle is focusing on enabling users to build an object/relational data model that rests on the concept of business objects. As Mendelsohn described, Oracle employs "object views" to provide relational database access for object-based applications. In the halls and foyers at the Object/Relational Summit, attendees and speakers debated the virtues of native objects versus object views, the use of object caching, and navigational "pointer" references versus joins. Discussion and debates were lively. Jim Melton, chief editor of the ANSI SQL standard, did a terrific presentation on SQL3--another topic of hot debate at the Summit. And Chris Date, recoiling from various speakers' references to the "venerable, but fading" relational model, criticized the navigational drift of commercial object/relational models and presented his own object/relational manifesto. His concerns and prescriptions were well received. As the Summit displayed amply, the object/relational space is full of interesting, leading-edge technology and applications. The solutions being offered are both novel and practical, and far less beholden to religious "object versus relational" issues than one year ago. In the coming months, we plan to feature discussion of these issues in Database Programming & Design, leading up to the 1998 edition of the Object/Relational Summit. But beyond the technical issues, the state of the database market occupied the minds of many. The Aberdeen Group's Peter Kastner described well the tough competition facing database vendors not named IBM, Oracle, or Microsoft. Thomas Atwood, one of the founding fathers of object database, cited economics and marketing as the major reasons why ODBMS has yet to impact the mainstream. Even the rise of Java, which Atwood thought offered ODBMS vendors an excellent opportunity, has been compromised by economic necessity with JDBC and JSQL. Relational vendors love to describe the ODBMS world as that "zero-billion dollar" industry. However, attendees who stayed for Doug Barry's closing presentation might wonder whether ODBMS prospects are as bad as they seem. Cautioning listeners to set strategy before making product selections, Barry described a number of successful ODBMS applications. Having suffered their taste of humble pie, the ODBMS world appears to be moving forward. As Apple's painful decline shows, no company in this field can dare be too proud--not even the beloved. In the database market, the mainstream is definitely consolidating. However, the consolidation will also open up the terrain to new solutions that could ultimately rechannel the mainstream. search - home - archives - contacts - site index Copyright 1997 Miller Freeman Inc.
