Open: TPC Database Benchmarks TRUTH AND BENCHMARKS The Transaction Processing Performance Council has become an oracle (no pun intended) for buyers seeking objective performance data. by Nancy Cohen March 14, 2005 W hether the vendor’s product is house-paint or hair dye, consumers know that vendor-driven product descriptions are written through rose-colored glasses. To figure out if the product is actually worth their dimes and quarters, consumers resort to explanations of in black and white: Consumer Reports or at the least, word of mouth from the nice people next door. The stakes in performance and dollar totals are enormously higher for managers who need to make decisions about database systems, servers, and mission-critical software platforms based on objective findings. Reliance on vendor-driven benchmarks or what’s overheard over lunch at a trade show won’t give decision makers the reliable answers they need unless they can see some benchmarks fairly designed for real-world settings. Is there such a deal? Corporate IT managers know the answer is a resounding ‘yes.’ The Transaction Processing Performance Council (TPC) has become somewhat of a consumer guide for IT buyers coping with big buying decisions about server and database systems. Now the TPC has a number of new benchmarks in the wings that reflect the changing realities of work environments. But how do IT buyers feel about benchmarks generally? Do they really mean anything to them? The answer is a definite yes—that is, good benchmarks, says Peter Kastner, analyst for Vericours, He says the TPC is a place where business IT seekers can go with greater confidence for numbers without fear of stacked configuration decks and misleading numbers. That, he says, was not always the case: Before the council was founded in 1988, there were no standardized benchmarks for commercial computing. Think about it. Figuring out which shampoo one should buy is not going to be easily resolved by just consulting one shampoo vendor. In the high-stakes business of transaction processing, vendors’ claims that their systems will outperform competitors’ created even more serious room for pause. As information technology took hold of business processes in the early 1980s, a new growth industry in on-line transaction processing brought with it a need for business buyers to figure out which vendors had the best answers. Benchmarks such as TPC were developed. “Every vendor had specific tests,” says Kastner, “but buyers were confused and sometimes misled. There was lots of truth in the maxim, ‘Lies, damned lies, and benchmarks.’” Kastner credits the TPC as a lever of independence that has kept vendors on their toes and in turn on a path of continued improvements. The TPC’s membership is largely made up of vendors themselves. When they come to the TPC table, it is for the common good of working on industry standards. Even with the dominance of vendors in the TPC, the process is one in which competitors keep each other honest. As has been often said, notes the TPC’s FAQ page on its web site, “It's not a system of the foxes guarding the henhouse, but of the foxes guarding the foxes.” All TPC benchmarks, moreover, must be approved by a TPC auditor. As a result of TPC-induced competition,” he says, “the industry has seen application-level performance improvements of 100-fold.” What makes the benchmarks so very useful, explains Kastner, is that they are indeed well thought-out. “The secret to the success of TPC benchmarks,” says Kastner, “is that the specifications are set by a committee of benchmark-jaded engineers from the likes of Dell, HP, IBM, Sun, Oracle, and Microsoft. These men and women fight competitively every day –and lots of nights. “That competition drives the TPC to scrutinize every benchmark as a level playing field, with room for innovation and technology advancement, but with a detailed set of requirements. The actual TPC tests must be viewed and certified by an independent auditor, and a full-disclosure report submitted for peer review by the full TPC. The combination of detailed benchmark standards, an independent audit, and in-the-sunlight peer review makes the TPC benchmarks the gold standard of commercial benchmarks.” The Future The TPC continues to evolve its benchmarks to keep pace both with technological changes and the changing requirements of benchmark users. In addition to refining and fine tuning the existing benchmarks, the TPC has begun development of new benchmarks: The OLTP benchmark—TPC-E—which will provide an expanded workload modeled on brokerage houses, reflecting more contemporary workloads than TPC-C. A new Web-service benchmark consisting of transactions involving database interactions and durable message-queue operations. It will display ACID properties running against a commercial application server. A new decision-support benchmark to measure a randomized set of transactions.
