PC Replacement Survey: One Size Fits None (Peter Kastner) About half of InternetWeek readers surveyed wait for something to break before replacing PCs, according to a joint survey conducted with the Aberdeen Group. But waiting for something to break is a good recipe for trouble, argues Aberdeen analyst Peter Kastner. By Peter Kastner, the Aberdeen Group InternetWeek December 23, 2003 09:00 AM A recent survey of InternetWeek readers conducted by Aberdeen Group and InternetWeek reveals sharp dichotomies of approaches to PC replacements. Our survey was completed by over 250 readers who manage about 750,000 business PCs. The top-10 respondents alone have the joy of dealing with over 550,000 users, and there are lots of well known North American companies on the list. As many of you hone those budgets for 2004, here's my take on the state of the industry regarding desktop and laptop buying trends based on the survey data. Purchase Orders Now Being Approved The first good news is that big companies are opening up their wallets. While six deals of 10,000 PCs or over representing 70,000 PCs were done in the last twelve months, three companies are now buying 30,000 PCs each and another handful of big orders bring the forward-looking total to 160,000. Judging by Intel's buoyant Q3 guidance and capacity-driven memory price increases in the past couple of months, it's fair to say the buying drought of the past couple of years is fading away. Yankee Philosophy: If it's not Broken, Don't Replace It The biggest finding of the survey is how many InternetWeek readers lack a schedule for replacing PCs and laptops. About 43% of desktops and 54% of laptops are replaced with a philosophy of "when the need is justified", "when they break", or "when a new application requires it". Throw in another 5% for desktops whose scheduled replacement is greater than 4.5 years, and we're at about half - a stalemate with the process-by-schedule enterprises I'll touch on in a moment. The industries with the stingiest Yankees are healthcare and retail. Manufacturing is around the average, and financial services and - gasp - governments are least likely to wait for something to go wrong. Frankly, I am disappointed that more of you are not able to break the yoke of unscheduled PC replacements. I grew up a Yankee, so I know how to pinch a penny and I like to maintain a tool as long as it lasts. But I've already said my piece on the subject: get rid of those Windows 9x and NT liabilities before the software breaks for good, probably with a virus or worm attack. Those who replace PCs on a catch-as-catch-can basis also experience higher costs of ownership, according to my research, due to higher support costs. The TCO gap will widen further in the future as the benefits of stable image platforms are not attained by this class. The Schedule is 36 Months For the half who schedule PC replacements, the data are quite interesting, and mostly good. In comparing past desktop life cycles to future ones, the trend is clearly to move towards a center point of 36-42 months. Those with shorter schedules in the past are getting longer replacement cycles and vice versa, leading to a nice convergence around 36 to 42 months. I am not alone in saying that 3 years is about right and 3.5 is OK. Get much longer and costs go up. So, it's good to see so many buyers agreeing from experience. The schedule for laptops is also converging on 36 months. That's troubling since the wear-and-tear of laptop use puts the useful life of a road machine at closer to two years - but then laptops cost twice what desktops do. So even the schedulers are hiding the true costs: perhaps that's why over half of the survey say they don't have a fixed replacement schedule for laptops. Another explanation is that the latest generation of laptop users are corridor cruisers, who hop from meeting to meeting lugging a desktop-replacement laptop, which gets less wear and tear than a road machine. No Surprises in Price and Configuration About 15% of desktops are configured at each of four speed bumps: 2.0, 2.2, 2.4 and 2.6 GHz. A typical system has a 17" CRT, so those fancy LCDs are only going to 1/6th of you. Nothing unusual in memory, disk capacity, or LAN specs. CD-ROM is the most common optical, but CD-writers and DVDs are quite popular (and harder to justify). But could the 80% who still order floppy disk drives explain why? It also appears that Windows 2000 was a very good vintage: a third will use Win2K going forward even though its scheduled end of life starts in less than two years. As to desktop price, 37% demand all this hardware and software for $1,000 or less. Another 30% spend between $1,000 and $1,200. While there are some large companies with broad laptop deployments, half the survey use a PC mix with 10% or fewer laptops. In the U.S., overall laptop use in business is over 25%, so our survey does not accurately reflect the overall market. For laptop configurations, a 1.6 - 2.4 GHz processor, 14" - 15" screen, and a CD-RW for about $1,600 are at the hump of the bell curve. But 23% get DVDs, perhaps because 23% of you are spending over $2,000 on a laptop. Lastly, few see more than a few percent of deployments in tablet PCs, and many see none. As to whom do you buy from, Dell beats IBM by a modest 10,000 units and HP by 20,000 units - not a big difference in a sample of 750,000. Thanks to all of you who took part in the survey. We'll do this again. Peter S. Kastner is executive vice president and chief research officer for the Consumer Digital Technology Practice at Aberdeen Group. Kastner leads Aberdeen's research in hardware platforms, pervasive computing, and semiconductors. His primary research focus is on workgroup, departmental, and Internet edge servers; blade servers; Infiniband; personal computing; and consumer IT products. He has more than 30 years of IT industry experience in both user and vendor organizations.
