Five minutes into the talk, which is being broadcast via synchronized video and audio over a wireless network, you decide to communicate a particularly interesting slide projected on-screen to a colleague in New York. You write into your Tablet, "This makes sense," and then email it to the colleague. The device, which is also connected to the wireless network, instantly produces the PowerPoint slide, together with the presenter's synchronized audio, when your colleague clicks the emailed note using his own Tablet PC. A Vision Built On Collaboration Sound intriguing? Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer thinks so. And, as he explained during a wide-ranging talk on the launch of Microsoft Office 2003 last October, this kind of functionality is just a taste of things to come. Indeed, Microsoft is spearheading a slew of initiatives aimed, as the software company is now fond of saying in ad campaigns, at helping companies realize "great moments at work" by leveraging more powerful collaborative tools. "The next frontier [in computing]," said Bill Gates, Microsoft chairman and chief software architect, at the October 2003 launch, will see "people working together more effectively. Every one of our new products is about sharing information and collaborating. We are far short of what's possible there." To help realize that vision, many of Microsoft's upcoming releases, as in 2003, will rely heavily on XML-based Web services and other standards that applications can use to access and share data across multiple platforms (for example, the next desktop OS, codenamed Longhorn, expected to be released in 2006, and the next iteration of Visual Studio, codenamed Whidbey, expected to be delivered to developers in beta version later this year). Also to be unveiled in the first half of this year is an Intelligent Message Filter for Microsoft Exchange Server 2003 and new versions of MSN. The filter incorporates SmartScreen Technology, a machine-based filtering technology that works with other spam-prevention technologies to eliminate as much as 80% of spam. (For more information on SmartScreen see the " Buffing Up Its Image " article.) Dovetailing with the information-sharing theme is Microsoft's Live Communications Server, a real-time collaboration platform that includes instant-messaging and Web-conferencing capabilities. The 2004 iteration of the solution will feature, among other improvements, support for "federation scenarios," enabling companies to link Live Communications Server deployments. Both the server and Microsoft's Web-conferencing service, Microsoft Office Live Meeting, will also be more tightly integrated with Office. Additionally slated for rollout this year is the ISA (Internet Security and Acceleration) Server, which will provide new levels of application layer security, simplified management, and fast Web access. The next release of SQL Server, codenamed Yukon, will feature enhanced scalability, manageability, availability, and business intelligence functionality, plus deeper integration with developer tools. You can also look forward to MOM (Microsoft Operations Manager) this summer and Microsoft System Center soon thereafter. The solutions will deliver application and system management of everything from devices to the data center for enterprises of all sizes. By midyear, Microsoft will also launch Virtual Server, software that lets users run multiple OSes on a single server, plus Software Update Services 2.0, a security patch management system for maintaining network security. Challenges For Microsoft Observers generally applaud the technology initiatives and Microsoft's heightened focus on helping customers use their products to implement qualitative processes (for example, compliance with the 2002 Sarbanes-Oxley Act). But experts also warn of pitfalls ahead for the company. They point, for example, to product gaps in Microsoft's Business Solutions Division. The unit, whose solutions encompass analytics, reporting, customer relationship management, ecommerce, supply chain management, and other functionality, accounts for a still small share of company revenue. Yet, while ramping up Business Solutions, Microsoft is likely to avoid areas, such as complex CRM packages for large enterprises, which entail comparatively few installs and high support costs. The company can be expected instead to focus efforts on products that lend themselves to high sales volumes, such as transactional applications for PC-based cash registers and financial accounting software, similar to Intuit's QuickBooks, for small businesses. "A product like QuickBooks is more attractive to Microsoft than the spaces that SAP, PeopleSoft, or Oracle compete in because you can sell tens of millions of units around the world," says Peter Kastner, a chief research officer at Aberdeen Group. "Competing against, say, SAP over a few thousand implementations would break Microsoft's business model." That business model has long hinged on another pillar of the Microsoft juggernaut, the all-pervasive Windows OSes, which now reside on more than 90% of SMB desktops. Bill Gates and company have dropped no hint of doing what many believe Microsoft must do in the face of the growing threat from Linux: making Windows' source code freely available to developers and porting Microsoft applications, such as the Office suite, to Linux. Analysts differ as to whether Microsoft's continuing resistance on this front will be a net positive or negative for the firm. "A lot of companies are starting to experiment not only with Linux on the server, but also Linux on the desktop," says Chad Robinson, a senior business analyst at Robert Frances Group, an IT advisory firm. "To hang onto Windows at a time when companies like CodeWeaver have created compatibility layers so you can run Office on Linux just doesn't make sense anymore. Microsoft's decision is going to hurt them in the eyes of customers." Kastner disagrees, noting that there is no proven mass market for Linux. "You'd be asking Microsoft to lose a lot of money in helping a competitor operating system," he says. "No sane business person would make this decision." Linux-compatible or not, many Microsoft products still get less-than-sterling ratings from customers. That's a finding of Mika Krammer, a vice president of research at Gartner. When measured in terms of usability, affordability, and support, for instance, Microsoft solutions score lower in surveys than competitive offerings from Intuit and salesforce.com, she observes. Raising the perception bar, she adds, will hinge in part on the reception of Project Green, an initiative to link to Longhorn the business management applications that Microsoft acquired from Great Plains, Navision, and Solomon. But the delay in unveiling Longhorn (not expected to ship before 2006) affords impatient businesses an extended opportunity to bolt to Linux. Add to this disgruntlement over Microsoft's Software Assurance program, which requires customers to pay a yearly licensing fee to annually upgrade Windows and Windows-based products. Observes Robinson, "The people who upgrade over long cycles really got shafted [by Software Assurance] because they no longer enjoy discounted pricing for upgrades. Microsoft needs to fix this initiative." Mending Fences The company might also do well to mend its channel program. Many of the tens of thousands of VARs, systems integrators, ISVs, and consultants on which Microsoft depends to market to SMBs are clamoring for streamlined management and better rewards, says Krammer. One place to start: entering all channel partners into a common database, as IBM and Cisco now do. "Without the right compensation and support, channel partners might be willing to reconsider who they sell for," says Krammer. "Also, Microsoft needs to beef up requirements for reselling its business applications. Any VAR can now market [Microsoft] CRM. Given the complexity and poor track of the software, that might not be the best thing." One thing is certain: Microsoft's rollouts, performance, and decisions in the year ahead will play out (and be watched) on an unprecedented scale. The stakes for SMB customers couldn't be greater. by Warren S.
