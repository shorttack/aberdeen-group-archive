 Is buying a consulting firm
 the next step for Microsoft?
 By JEFF MEISNER
STAFF WRITER

    With $38 billion in the bank, Microsoft Corp.’s war
 chest is the envy of the software industry.
    One analyst suggests the Redmond giant could spend
 as much as $15 billion in the next three years to buy a ma­
 jor consulting firm like Accenture Ltd. or Pricewater­
 houseCoopers LLP to bolster its presence in the lucrative
 enterprise software market
    Speculation concerning a future tie-up between Mi­
 crosoft and a large consulting firm gained ground in re­
 cent weeks after research firm Gartner Inc. devoted a re­
 port to the possibility.
    But such a scenario divides industry watchers, with
 some calling it is a logical step for the company and others
 predicting it would wreak havoc with other parts of Mi­
 crosoft’s business.
   What is clear is Microsoft has not been shy in the past
when it comes to buying its way into new markets.
   This week the company said it would pay $1.3 billion in
 cash and stock to buy Navision AS of Vedbaek, Denmark,
a 1,300-employee software concern whose applications
help small and medium-sized businesses automate their fi­
nancial, human resources, e-commerce and accounting
data. Navision will become part of Microsoft’s business so­
lutions division, built around the company’s acquisition of
Great Plains Software in December 2000 for $1.1 billion.
