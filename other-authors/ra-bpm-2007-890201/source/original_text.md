                            Executive Summary



T
        he need to support new, agile business processes is the chief pressure driving or-
        ganizations, according to the results from the survey that provide the foundation
        for this report. But there is one glaring surprise from this research: Nearly half of
        the respondents – mostly from small and mid-size organizations ─ say their com-
panies’ ERP systems or best-of-breed supply chain solutions don’t provide adequate
functionality. Some application software, particularly custom applications common in
the supply chain, retail areas, and on shop floor, is not designed to accommodate the
rapid business process change and distributed enterprise models on which businesses op-
erate today.
An overwhelming majority of organizations represented in the survey feel constrained in
their applications’ ability to meet customers’ individual business requirements. Here’s
what they say about their current application portfolios:
   • Only 19% say their applications afford them the desired flexibility businesses say
     they need today, meaning they can adapt quickly to most customer requirements.
     This is where businesses need to be to best meet today’s business demands.
   • Close to half ─ 49% ─ say they employ manual processes to get the job done,
     implying that labor is an important component of something that should be auto-
     mated.
   • About 13% say their applications offer little or no flexibility in meeting individ-
     ual customer service requirements.
   • Close to one-fifth of companies surveyed ─ 19% ─ say their applications force
     them to limit service offerings.
As a result, more than half the survey is turning to BPM to get the process right at
the line-of-business level without having to throw out expensive ERP or custom back-
end applications. And they are using SOA technology as the glue to tie BPM to ERP
and other enterprise applications.

                  SOA and BPM Technology
                  Aberdeen forecasts that 90% of all companies were to at least have SOA strategies in
                  place by the end of 2006, if they have not implemented them already. The main factors
                  driving these implementations are:
                       •   A desire for new capabilities like cross-application process integration;
                       •   Re-use of applications via web services; and
                       •   Better management of IT complexity.
                  The 49% of companies whose applications force them to resort to manual processes to
                  meet customer requirements would be best off looking for vendors to provide composite
                  applications that tie together point solutions to enable a flexible industry- or company-
                  specific business process (One example: an integrated business planning solution that can
                  enable the balancing of supply and demand to maximize profit by bridging sales and op-
                  erations forecasting tools, constraint-based supply chain applications, and financial sys-
                  tems).
                  The 49% may also find an answer in BPM tools. Leading companies are looking to lev-
                  erage these tools to build out front-end applications that are:
                       •   Flexible from an end-user perspective;
                       •   Equipped with a rich user interface that promotes productivity;
                       •   Able to map well to line-of-business process requirements;
                       •   Capable of integrating with their investments in back-end ERP and best-of-breed
                           business process solutions.
                  Over an application’s lifecycle, BPM can deliver dramatically lower software mainte-
                  nance costs due to the ease with which business units can change the process and key
                  process parameters without massive IT reprogramming and testing.
                  Yet, only half of the companies represented in the survey are using BPM.

                  Payback is Slow in Coming
                  These challenges may be preventing companies from realizing the full potential return on

                  Kinks in the Supply Chain
                  For all the benefits BPM and SOA bring, companies that rely heavily on their supply
                  chains ─ manufacturers, retailers, and distribution/logistics organizations ─ are least
                  likely to use BPM tools, even though their benefits are more than apparent.
                  Here are some key survey findings on this group, which made up more than half of the
                  respondent pool:
