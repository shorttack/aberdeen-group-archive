 The (rebate) check is in the mail – or is it? 

    By Jon Van Vlack 

   Consumers hate rebates. That's what they tell me. They would rather just pay discounted prices right at the stores.
   I frequently conduct presentations on consumer issues. Given the opportunity, people like to tell me what works in the marketplace and, more to the point, they like to emphatically tell me what does not work. Rebates are a hot topic under the "doesn't work" category.
   For lots of people, rebates are simply a big rip-off. Many complain that the rebate process is unnecessarily and purposely difficult. A frequent complaint is that rebates take too long to process. Claims of 12-week processing often turn into stories of waiting 6 months for a check to show up. Then again, many people complain that rebate checks never show up at all. 
   I can tell you that rebates are big business. After a marketing lull, rebates returned with a vengeance to retail promotion about three years ago. How big are rebates? According to industry analysts, last year rebate offers amounted to more than $10 billion.

    My first time 
   When I first became a consumer advocate, many years ago, I took quite a few complaints about rebates. Though I have dealt with literally hundreds of rebate situations since then, I still fondly remember my first time.
   A hair-cutting salon in Kingston had just moved to a new location. They decided to celebrate the move with a party for their patrons. To toast their new quarters, the owner bought a case of champagne. That's where the trouble came in – the bubbly came with a $1.50 rebate per bottle. That's an $18 saving on a case.
   Months after the party, the salon owner came to me explaining the wine company denied his rebate. I told him I would look into it. When I contacted the firm, they told me that the consumer never sent in the "proof of purchase." I explained that he had sent the UPC bar code from each bottle, a copy of the sales receipt and the completed rebate form. They agreed, but noted they required the corks as proof.
   "Oh Puh-leeze!" was my response. I questioned how they could seriously expect the consumer to fit a dozen corks in the number 10 envelope required in the rebate instructions. Before they could answer, I asked: "What would a consumer do if the bottles were given as gifts?" I followed up by asking whether the rebate is issued for buying or drinking their product, since a cork is only removed just prior to drinking champagne, not months in advance. 
   Shortly thereafter, the shop-owner called to say he got a rebate check, a letter of apology, and a free case of wine! He asked if I would like to stop by for a drink and I said I would prefer a rebate on my next haircut. Unfortunately, he thought I was joking. 

    The game 
   Rebates are a marketing game. As with any competition, there are rules, strategies and goals. Here's how manufacturers and retailers play to win your money:
    First, they offer a low price in big, bold print, while noting in very small print that the low price is the net price after deducting the rebate from the full price you must pay. This strategy induces the consumer to pay the higher price up-front, while focusing on a potentially lower price.
   Next, they make filing a rebate a tedious task – what with cutting out bar codes, filling out forms, making photocopies and mailing it all back to the manufacturer or a fulfillment firm. Often forms require additional information; set deadlines; state exceptions, exclusions and conditions not noted in the ads; and carry warnings that the rebate may take up to 12 weeks to process.
   Finally, the game is rigged. As Dr. John Zhang, University of Pennsylvania associate professor of marketing notes:
   "Rebates motivate people to buy at full price, and many consumers never send in their rebates for redemption. Manufacturers have the better of two worlds when they use rebates."
   Peter Kastner, an analyst with the Boston-based Aberdeen Group, recently noted that 60 percent of all consumers entitled to a rebate never get the refund. Manufacturers see increased sales, retailers get more sales at full price, and even the government benefits – it collects more sales tax because the tax levy is computed on higher, full-retail prices.

    Winning strategies 
   Many consumers fail to file rebates, and that is simply throwing money away. The Washington Post recently noted that 15 percent of all consumers entitled to a rebate never complete the process – exactly what the marketers are hoping. So file for every rebate you can – complete the process. Other tips:
   -- Never make purchases based on the after-rebate price, unless you are committed to fight for the refund. Keep in mind that the process is often designed to fail – for customers anyway. 
   -- Keep records. Since your rebate is bound to be ignored, lost or denied, you will need a document trail to fight for the refund. Right from the start, deal with rebates skeptically and assume that you will have to recreate your request.
   -- Follow up. Keep track of rebates and contact the company if a rebate check is late. Remember, it's the squeaky wheel that gets the grease and the persistent consumer that gets the rebate check.
   -- Complain, if necessary. Your local consumer affairs office, the selling retailer or your state's attorney general can help you get the rebate you're due – but only if you ask for help.
