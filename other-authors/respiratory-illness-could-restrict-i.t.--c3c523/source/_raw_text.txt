Respiratory Illness Could Restrict I.T. Supply Lines By Gregg Keizer, Techweb News, With Eric Chabrow and Marianne Kolbasuk Mcgee InformationWeek April 7, 2003 12:00 AM (From the April 7, 2003 issue) SARS is already affecting the airline and tourism industries. Could IT be next? Breathing New life into Old CRM Reports App Development That Delivers Business Results The Pain Of E-Discovery Videos Dr. Dobb's Mike Riley talks with Jim Manias of Advanced Systems Concepts. In this conversation, Jim discusses the new ActiveBatch 7 and how it can provide significant productivity gains for application developers and business process owners alike. If infection rates go unchecked, the IT manufacturing supply chain may be broken, says Peter Kastner, an analyst with the Aberdeen Group. "Worst case, it could result in major supply-chain disruptions and another downdraft for an already challenged industry," Kastner wrote in a recent report. That's because many electronics manufacturers, including computer makers, depend on suppliers in the Asia-Pacific region. Guangdong province is home to much of China's electronics and IT hardware industry and the source for most of the world's AC-to-DC power supplies. It's also where SARS (severe acute respiratory syndrome) broke out. Joe Wetz, a project manager and principal of K-Lag Technology, an IT consulting firm, says the potential exists for SARS-related supply-chain delays to affect IT projects that involve hardware manufactured or assembled in Asia. "We're anticipating delays, and we are building that into project time frames," he says. Many projects can withstand hardware delays of a few weeks, he says, but if the problem lingers, Wetz's teams might have to find other sources. The SARS crisis coincides with other global problems. "Considering the world situation, you really need to plan for backups and delays, just in case," Wetz says. Many economists caution that the $10 trillion U.S. economy is too big for one disease to cause havoc unless a real health catastrophe hits.
