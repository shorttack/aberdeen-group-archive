(CBS.MW) - Gateway shares fell Monday after chief executive Ted Waitt said the financially troubled personal-computer maker would reshuffle management and unveil a new business strategy following weak holiday sales. Gateway tapped T. Scott Edwards as executive vice president of its consumer segment and David Turner as senior vice president of sales and marketing. By the time the closing bell had rung, Gateway /quotes/comstock/11i!asiyf ( ASIYF 1.76 , -0.01 , -0.64% ) had fallen 16 cents a share, or 4.6 percent, to close at $3.30 on light volume of 2.3 million shares exchanged. The sell-off followed comments Waitt made in an interview with the Wall Street Journal. Waitt said Gateway was prepared to undertake its second major strategy shift in two years. Waitt didn't give specifics, but said cost cuts were possible. The leases on several of Gateway's 272 retail stores expire this year. Waitt reiterated comments he made in December at a Credit Suisse First Boston technology conference, when he said the market for PCs was "brutal" and called the current market "difficult." "It looks to us as if Gateway's whole business model is on the boardroom table," said Peter Kastner, chief research officer with the Aberdeen Group. Gateway recently reported a third-quarter loss of $50 million, or 15 cents a share, which met the consensus of Wall Street analysts. Revenue was $1.12 billion, down 21 percent from the $1.41 billion Gateway reported for the third quarter of 2001. Company officials lowered their full-year revenue estimates to between $4.3 billion and $4.5 billion from a range of $4.5 billion to $5 billion. Market research firm IDC ranked Gateway No. 3 in U.S. personal computer shipments in the third quarter of 2002. The company's shipments actually fell to 729,000 units from 817,000 in the same period in 2001. Stepping forward, and back Gateway has already taken steps to boost its market share and shore up its stock price. In November, the company began carrying more than 100 new digital products at its retail stores, including a 42-inch plasma TV that costs $2,999, or as much as half that of similar models. See full story. However, Gateway reportedly suffered channel inventory problems during the holiday shopping season when several of its retail stores sold out their stocks of plasma TVs and other products, and had difficulties getting more supplies to meet customer demand. "I think they made a huge impression on the market (by adding new products), but they showed they really need a supply chain that can keep up with the market," said Kastner of the Aberdeen Group. Gateway has also tried to appeal to novice technology buyers by expanding its line of "Survive and Thrive" how-to books, and has cut prices on entry-level PCs to as low as $449. Just before Christmas, Gateway offered a 2-for-1 computer offer in which customers who bought one of the company's high-end desktop or laptop PCs would get a free, low-end PC without a monitor. But nothing has been able to stem the company's slide, and Gateway's shares have fallen 69 percent since hitting a 52-week high of $10.60 on Jan. 7, 2002. In December, Waitt said a "best-case scenario" for Gateway's fourth quarter would be earnings at the low end of its forecast range of 10 cents to 13 cents a share, with full-year revenue of about $4.3 billion. Analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call had predicted Gateway would lose 12 cents a share, with 2002 revenue of $4.39 billion. In November, Gateway said it had been under investigation by the Securities and Exchange Commission for two years regarding its fiscal year 2000 operations. Gateway said the investigation is likely to wrap up in first quarter of 2003. Rex Crum is a reporter for CBS.MarketWatch.com in San Francisco.
