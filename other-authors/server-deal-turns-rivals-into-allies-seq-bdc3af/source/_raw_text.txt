Garvey Issue date: Jan. 27, 1997 Sequent Computer Systems Inc. and NCR Corp. are combining their strengths to fill a gap in providing end-to-end computing for IT customers. The two companies last week announced a three-year agreement under which Sequent will resell NCR WorldMark 4300 midrange servers. The deal gives Sequent customers low-end to midrange server options to complement Sequent's high-end, data-center mach ines. It also adds more hardware support throughout the enterprise for companies migrating to Windows NT. Analysts estimate the deal to be worth $50 million over the next 12 to 18 months. "As people move to Baan, Oracle, and SAP applications, there's a clear separation of tasks between the database and the application, causing increasing demand for NT servers around our own [high-end] servers," says Andre Dahan, senior VP of sales and marketing for Sequent in Beaverton, Ore. In the past, Sequent has bundled Dell and Compaq PC servers with its own systems for some accounts. But with its highly scalable NUMA-based servers rolling out Feb. 11, Sequent is looking to fill a gap above the "low end of entry-level servers," Dahan says. Mark Hurd, VP of marketing for NCR in Dayton, Ohio, says the unusual deal between traditional rivals will benefit large accounts looking to strengthen their NT-based infrastructures, but he says the vendors will continue to battle for customers for high-end systems. "We expect no change in competitiveness [between NCR and Sequent] for the data-warehouse market," he says. Staying On Track As Sequent prepares to unveil its high-end NUMA (Nonuniform Memory Access) systems next month, Dahan says the company doesn't want to be sidetracked trying to develop application and network servers. Hurd says NCR, recently spun off by AT&T, wants to focus on data warehousing and high-end transaction processing for the banking, communications, and retail vertical markets. Dahan pointed out the uniqueness of NCR's midrange computers, albeit in an unintentionally backhanded way. "We wanted an NT vendor that could scale up to eight processors, and NCR was the only one we could find," he says. The WorldMark 4300 currently scales up to four Pentium Pro processors, and NCR expects to achieve support for eight processors by the second quarter. Hurd says the collaboration between the two companies on Intel and Windows NT will help them develop future Intel-based syst ems. "We're both trying to advance the Intel architecture," says Hurd, "and both of us are out to beat the RISC vendors in the market." Peter Kastner, group VP with the Aberdeen Group, a Boston consultancy, says the deal makes sense for both vendors and customers. It lets Sequent leverage R&D, while NCR gains channel opportunities for its products, he says. "There is no downside for Sequent customers because the NCR boxes do tough work in tough places," adds Kastner. "It's also an indication that Sequent is serious about a broad line of products and is not cutting corners for an increasingly commodity marketplace."
