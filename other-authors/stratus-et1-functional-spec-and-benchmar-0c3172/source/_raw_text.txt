1    INTRODUCTION


In order to measure the performance of on-line transaction pro­
cessing systems, a group of computer professionals (including ac­
ademics, vendors, and users)     created a benchmark profiling a
banking transaction.  The benchmark - also known as the. debit­
credit transaction, or ET1, (or mistakenly TP1, which it is not)
has become an oft-quoted standard in the industry after it was
used in 1973 by a large retail bank to select a vendor who could
handle its 1,000 branches, 10,000 tellers, and 10,000,000 ac­
counts at a peak load of 100 transactions per second.

In an effort to evaluate its systems, Stratus created the TP1
benchmark. TP1 - the Stratus-internal standard benchmark is not
based on ET1,    or the debit-credit transaction. The number and
type of I/O operations it performs are different, and the data­
base it operates on is different as compared to the one ET1 oper­
ates on.

Due to various known and unknown reasons, it has become a popular
misconception outside Stratus that TP1 and ET1 are the same.
Vendor comparisons based on the TP1 vs. ET1 results, though done
often, are invalid.    In an attempt to clear up this misconcep­
tion, and enable an 'apples to apples'   comparison, Stratus is
creating and benchmarking the ET1 transaction as described in the
article by 'anon et al' in the April, 1st, 1985 issue of Datama­
tion.

The purpose of this document is to define the Stratus ET1 bench­
mark, its profile, and the environment in which its performance
is measured.




2.   THE TRANSACTION

Begin-Transaction

There will be (per processing module)   :

Two (2) million Account records
Two thousand (2000) Teller records
Two hundred (200) Branch records

The Account files will be a keyed access files (key length of ten
(10)  bytes), the other files will be random access files. All
data files will have record level locking.

All keys will be generated randomly. The keys that are used as
input to the benchmark will be generated such that eighty-five
percent (85%) of the time the transaction is performed on a ran­
dom account at the current branch, and fifteen percent (15%) of
the time the transaction is performed on a random account at a
different branch    (which might be on a different module in the
multi-module case).




4.   RESULTS

In general, performance is quantified by measuring two metrics:
response time, and transactions per second (tps). Response time
is defined as the time taken to perform a single transaction (on
a system under the full Stratus ET1 benchmark load) after the ar­
rival of the last bit of input from the communications line and
before the transmission of the first bit of output to the commu­
nications line.   Transactions per second (tps) is equal to the
total transactions completed during the benchmark divided by the
total elapsed time (in seconds) required to complete the bench­
mark. Hence, tps will include system overhead, line delays, X.25
time, etc.

The results will be from a system tuned to achieve the best per­
formance. There are no restrictions on memory,    or disks,   or
other tuning parameters.

The actual results of running this benchmark are reported sepa­
rately.  Since we expect these results to vary over time due to
differences in operating system release, hardware configuration,
etc., and we expect the ET-1 benchmark to remain the same, we
have only functionally described the benchmark in this document.

          STRATUS TRANSACTION PROCESSING BENCHMARKS

Introduction
There is a tendency in the computer industry today to move away
from general benchmarks and performance measurements that often
in their simplicity do not provide a true comparison between the
combined hardware and software potential of different systems for
their intended purposes.
Measures such as MIPS are often useful in comparing the CPU
powers of similar machines in a single vendor's product line but
seldom are useful in comparing dissimilar machines from different
vendors. With multiprocessor architectures, of which the
Stratus/32 is clearly an example, it is difficult to calculate
the MIPS rating because MIPS will actually change as you add
additional I/O processors, such as line adapters or storage
directors.
Benchmarks such as the Whetstone benchmark are designed to test
only a limited part of an architecture, are usually not
structured for a multiprocessor architecture, and do not measure
disk I/O and communications, two important parts of a transaction
processing environment.
The scenario is further complicated by the reasonable requirement
that with a fault tolerant architecture, the test must be run
both in a "fully operational" state and a "degraded" state. Due
to the hardware-based fault tolerant design of the Stratus/32,
these two states are the same.
Transaction-Based Benchmarks
Just as the Whetstone benchmark was designed to measure the
scientific processing capabilities of a single CPU processor, new
benchmarks were developed to test the transaction throughput of
online transaction processing (OLTP) oriented machines. These
benchmarks generally emulated one specific type of transaction
processing environment and included both CPU and I/O.
In the last few years, two OLTP benchmarks have emerged through
different channels. These are now commonly known as TP1 and ET1.
Although both benchmarks are transaction processing benchmarks,
here the similarity stops.




June 26, 1986                                     Page 1
ET1 and TP1 are not the same benchmark or even a variation on the
same benchmark. Each measures OLTP performance in a different
way. Not understanding the differences between the two
benchmarks has led to false comparisons. Below is a comparison
between the two benchmarks:
          Operation                      ET1       TP1
CPU intensive loop                       0 ms      150 ms
Read from a large indexed file           1           5
Rewrite to the large indexed file        1           2
Read from a small relative file         '2           0
Rewrite to a small relative file         2           0
Write of a sequential log file           1           1

Although the logical I/O ratio of ET1 to TP1 is 7 to 8, the
physical I/O ratio is far more significant. This is because all
of the TP-1 I/O’s are done to large, indexed files, while most of
the ET1 I/O’s are done to small relative files. In most modern
operating systems like Stratus VOS, the disk cache will be able
to eliminate physical I/O’s to a small, relative file far more
easily than to a large, indexed file. The "largeness" of the
indexed file will guarantee at least two physical I/Os per read
while the "smallness" of the relative file will almost guarantee
no physical I/O’s per read. Since both benchmarks do one
sequential log write, this can be safely eliminated from
comparison. TP1 does 12 physical I/O’s per transaction to 3 in
ET-1. Therefore, TP1 does four times as many physical I/O’s as
ET1.
ET1
The ET1 benchmark simulates a banking deposit/withdrawal
transaction. It is described in an article by "anon et al" in
the April 1, 1985 issue of Datamation titled "A Measure of
Transaction Processing Performance".
The ET1 benchmark on a single Stratus module consisted of the
following:
      Read one 100 byte 3270 message from an X.25 comm line
      One indexed read from a 2,000,000 record account database
      One rewrite of that account record
      One read and rewrite of a 2,000 record teller database
      One read and rewrite of a 200 record branch database
      One write append to a sequential log file
      Format a 3270 buffer
      Send one 200 byte X25/3270 message




June 26, 1986                                      Page 2
      Written in PL1 using a requester/server model with Stratus
      TPF.
The Datamation article states that in 85% of the cases, the
branch portion of the account number will match the current
process branch number, and in the remaining 15%, the account
number will reference a different process' branch. In the
multimodule case, these remaining 15% were dispatched equally to
different account files on modules in the system. This
stimulates the model that any customer can arrive at any teller
in any branch, but will go to that customer's local branch 85% of
the time.

TP1
The TP1 benchmark was designed by Stratus based on a specific
international customer's requirements. It has been documented in
Stratus publications and in the press. Here is a summary:
           A 150ms CPU loop simulating screen form handling
           Five indexed reads from a 100,000 record database
           Rewrites of two of those records
           One write append to a sequential log file
           Written in PL1 (requester) and COBOL (server) using
           Stratus TPF.

Conclusion
ET-1 and TP-1 are two measures of transaction processing
performance. ET-1 results in significantly more transactions per
second because it has fewer I/O's to accomplish. The linear
growth of the Stratus architecture is clearly demonstrated by the
ET-1 results, while showing excellent response times.
