Sun's report, falling short of Wall Street analysts' estimates, underscored weak demand for its high-end server and computing hardware, and investors reacted with punishing force. By the time of the closing bell, Sun Micro shares had fallen 92 cents to close at $3.85 and were the most active stock in the nation, with 239 million shares changing hands. Santa Clara, Calif.-based Sun said it earned $12 million, breaking even a per-share basis, compared to $61 million, or 2 cents a share, in the comparable period last year. Revenue totaled $2.98 billion, a 13 percent decrease from the $3.42 billion recorded a year ago. The Thomson First Call consensus estimate was a profit of 2 cents a share on sales of $3.07 billion. Analyst Kevin McCarthy of Credit Suisse First Boston said he was very disappointed with the 20 percent drop in Sun's product revenue. Despite plans for new product launches, "we don't believe they will meaningfully improve Sun's competitive position," he said. McCarthy reiterated his "underperform" rating, took down his 2004 profit estimates on Sun's stock to 4 cents a share, and expressed an ominous outlook for the company. "We believe Sun's franchise is in decline and Sun will likely miss the bulk of any recovery in enterprise IT (information technology) spending," McCarthy said. Analyst Toni Sacconaghi of Bernstein Research was also critical, saying that even with a more aggressive than usual pricing plan in the quarter, Sun "was unable to jumpstart revenue," and it was becoming harder to justify Sun as a longer-term investment. Sacconaghi gives Sun's stock a "market-perform" rating. However, Peter Kastner, a computer hardware analyst with the Aberdeen Group, in Boston, said investors are likely to interpret Sun's results as being "on the edge of respectability" as the company has been hovering near breakeven for three quarters. "We're seeing the second quarter of the year was not a turnaround quarter for Sun," Kastner said. "But anyone who gives up on Sun would be making a big mistake, as they have a huge, loyal base of customers." Sun CEO Scott McNealy touted Sun's advances during its fiscal year, including the release of more than 20 new, low-end servers and other devices. However, he admitted that the company still has a lot of road to make up as it tries to maintain steady profits. "We've had a pretty tough year. We didn't grow the business," McNealy said. "That's something we're not happy with." Despite missing Wall Street's estimates, McNealy tried to remain upbeat about his company's results. "We've made solid progress throughout the fiscal year, delivering on key initiatives," McNealy said. "We're well positioned to take full advantage of market opportunity over the long-term with a strong pipeline of innovations." Listen to Scott McNealy talk about Sun's quarter Going forward, Sun CFO Steve McGowan said the company expects to see a small decline in first-quarter services gross margins, and an increase in research and development spending. However, he would not give first-quarter earnings or sales forecasts. Analysts have forecasted Sun to earn 1 cent a share on sales of $2.8 billion in what is typically Sun's lowest-sales quarter. The quarter put the capper on a year of losses for Sun, which posted a full-year loss of $2.38 billion, or 75 cents a share. Sun's losses ballooned from the $587 million, or 18 cents per share it reported in its 2002 fiscal year, and sales fell almost 9 percent to $11.43 billion from $12.5 billion. Sun did have some positive news from the quarter, as gross margins rose to 43.7 percent, up from 41.3 percent in the year-ago quarter, and the company generated cash from operating activities of $335 million to raise its cash and marketable securities to $5.7 billion. Rex Crum is a reporter for CBS.MarketWatch.com in San Francisco. ### Abstract

MarketWatch news story (Jul 23 2003, 5:51 p.m. EST) by Rex Crum reporting on Sun Microsystems' fiscal Q4 2003 results. Sun earnings dropped 80%, profit of $12M (breakeven EPS) vs $61M (2c EPS) year ago; revenue $2.98B down 13% from $3.42B; Thomson First Call consensus was 2c/$3.07B. Shares fell 92c to close at $3.85 on 239M shares (most active US stock that day). Analysts Kevin McCarthy (CSFB) and Toni Sacconaghi (Bernstein) were harshly critical. Peter Kastner, computer-hardware analyst with Aberdeen Group in Boston, provided the contrarian counter-balance: 'investors are likely to interpret Sun's results as being on the edge of respectability as the company has been hovering near breakeven for three quarters' and 'anyone who gives up on Sun would be making a big mistake, as they have a huge, loyal base of customers.'
