Investors punished Sun , sending its shares down 10 percent from a $4.77 close to $4.30 in euro trading. Santa Clara, Calif.-based Sun said fourth-quarter profit totaled $12 million, or breakeven per share, compared to a profit of $61 million, or 2 cents a share, in the same period last year. Revenue totaled $2.98 billion, a 13 percent decrease from the $3.42 billion in sales it recorded a year ago. The results fell short of the estimates of analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call who had expected a profit of 2 cents a share and sales of $3.07 billion. Peter Kastner, a computer hardware analyst with the Aberdeen Group, in Boston, said investors are likely to interpret Sun's results as being "on the edge of respectability" as the company has been hovering near breakeven for three quarters. "We're seeing the second quarter of the year was not a turnaround quarter for Sun," Kastner said. "But anyone who gives up on Sun would be making a big mistake, as they have a huge, loyal base of customers." Sun CEO Scott McNealy touted Sun's advances during its fiscal year, including the release of more than 20 new, low-end servers and other devices. However, he admitted that the company still has a lot of road to make up as it tries to maintain steady profits. "We've had a pretty tough year. We didn't grow the business,". "That's something we're not happy with." Despite missing Wall Street's estimates, McNealy tried to remain upbeat about his company's results. "We've made solid progress throughout the fiscal year, delivering on key initiatives," McNealy said. "We're well positioned to take full advantage of market opportunity over the long-term with a strong pipeline of innovations." Going forward, Sun CFO Steve McGowan said the company expects to see a small decline in first-quarter services gross margins, and an increase in research and development spending. However, he would not give first-quarter earnings or sales forecasts. Analysts have forecasted Sun to earn 1 cent a share on sales of $2.8 billion in what is typically Sun's lowest-sales quarter. The quarter put the capper on a year of losses for Sun, which posted a full-year loss of $2.38 billion, or 75 cents a share. Sun's losses ballooned from the $587 million, or 18 cents per share it reported in its 2002 fiscal year, and sales fell almost 9 percent to $11.43 billion from $12.5 billion. Sun did have some positive news from the quarter, as gross margins rose to 43.7 percent, up from 41.3 percent in the year-ago quarter, and the company generated cash from operating activities of $335 million to raise its cash and marketable securities to $5.7 billion. Mike Tarsala is a San Francisco-based reporter for CBS.MarketWatch.com.
