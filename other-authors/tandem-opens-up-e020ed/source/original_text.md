On Jan. 8, Tandem named 39-year-old Roel Pieper as president and CEO. Pieper, who has run Tandem's UB Networks subsidiary since 1993, replaces founder and CEO Jim Treybig, who agreed to step down last October after Tandem posted a 72% quarterly earnings drop amid product delays. Tandem, in Cupertino, Calif., faces many of the same problems that other, older computer makers have experienced. "They invest billions of dollars in technology and R&D, but get stuck trying to find a new mission," says Pieper. Customers and analysts say that while Tandem has always had valuable technology, the company has failed to exploit it with products that find a mainstream market. To grow now, Tandem must work more aggressively with third parties, they add. "That's an essential element of recasting the company for growth as a strong and capable supplier," says Peter Burris, VP of open computing for the Meta Group, a consulting firm in Stamford, Conn. Analysts and users think Pieper may be the right choice to steer Tandem in the proper direction. Although he was posted at a subsidiary, Pieper is still an insider who understands Ta ndem's strengths and weaknesses. Also, his background at Unix Systems Labs and Software AG means, "he knows open systems and production computing requirements," says Peter Kastner, an analyst with the Aberdeen Group, a consulting firm in Boston. Pieper says the key to Tandem's long-term success is to build partnerships that let its advanced software run on other manufacturers' computers. Tandem will try to exploit its Non-Stop fault-tolerant capabilities and ServerNet technology in Unix and Windows NT environments. Tandem has already announced partnerships based on Tandem's ServerNet technology with L.M. Ericcson, NEC, and Compaq, and several more are planned. "We want to be more of a component company," Pieper explains. "All of the value of Tandem's product development is tucked into Himalaya [a server] and our Non-Stop architecture." Tandem's new-found openness appeals to some users. "By packaging their technology for other vendors, the company m ay end up with a smaller share of the market," says Wayne Stargardt, VP of marketing at Pinpoint Communications Inc. in Dallas, a provider of two-way wireless vehicle tracking systems that uses Tandem Integrity Servers as communications hubs. ### Abstract

InformationWeek issue #563 news article (Jan 22 1996) by Barbara DePompa on Tandem Computers Inc.'s strategic pivot under new CEO Roel Pieper. Tandem — the pioneer of fault-tolerant NonStop systems — had just named the 39-year-old Pieper president and CEO on Jan 8 1996, replacing founder Jim Treybig, who stepped down after a 72% quarterly-earnings drop amid product delays. Pieper, previously head of Tandem's UB Networks subsidiary since 1993 (and earlier at Unix System Labs and Software AG), aims to shift Tandem toward partnerships and component software that let Tandem's advanced software run on other vendors' hardware. The strategy targets Unix and Windows NT environments. Peter Kastner, analyst with Aberdeen Group Boston, endorses the pick: Pieper 'knows open systems and production computing requirements' thanks to his Unix Systems Labs and Software AG background. Tandem emphasizes NonStop and ServerNet technology partnerships going forward.
