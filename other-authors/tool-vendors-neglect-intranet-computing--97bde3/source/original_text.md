Tool vendors neglect intranet needs

What is the future for the IT industry? Peter Kastner of the AberdeenGroup outlined his predictions to Douglas Hayward.

Written by newmedia newmedia 

 Computing , 30 Oct 1996

Users must push development tool vendors to produce intranet-ready versions of their products, a leading US industry analyst warned last week.

Peter Kastner, group vice president of Aberdeen Group research organisation, warned that the development of intranet environments will be delayed until tools vendors treat browsers as the equal of Windows clients. The warning came amid a series of predictions for the industry over the next three years (see box).

'IT departments don't have enough resources to develop applications that people want, because they are having to develop them twice - once for existing client-server, and once for intranet,' said Kastner

'That's just mad. People are going to have to put pressure on tools developers to create new versions that treat browsers as just another client.'

Kastner, who was in the UK to visit middleware supplier BMC Software, predicted that Novell will struggle over the next few years, partly because the company failed to see the challenge of Windows NT. And also because the intranet revolution will remove the need for users to buy Novell's Directory Services software, often considered to be one of the company's key technology advantages over rivals such as Microsoft.

'You won't necessarily need Novell's Directory Services if you have a corporate intranet with a white pages facility that lists all your users and their email addresses,' Kastner said. 'I am distressed with Novell's inability to see the threats to its position over the last few years.'

Novell needs to respond to the growing trend for organisations to dump LANs based on local servers in favour of a new architecture which connects users together using intranets.

'Delta Airlines recently discovered that 1 in 16 of its employees was involved in activities concerned with the management of its 6,000 Novell servers. That's hundreds of man hours being wasted every year,' he said.

'That sort of thing is why organisations are looking at ways of putting their servers back in central sites, using high-speed networks to connect individual clients.'

Object-oriented and Internet-based technology will help to bring down IT ownership costs in the short term, but costs will initially rise as users experience a lack of skills and tools to use the new technologies, Kastner warned.

'The problem is that we are dealing with new technologies for which the industry doesn't yet have proper methodologies,' he said. 'That means costs will increase briefly as we go through the learning curve, but they will come down significantly over the longer term.'

Kastner flew into London last week with a guilty secret. While working at Chase Manhattan Bank in 1973, he wrote a system which he knew would not be year 2000-compliant.

'We knew all about the problem then, but we never thought the system we were writing would still be working in 20 years' time. We assumed it would be replaced,' he said meekly.

'In the case of another system, for bonds with maturity dates into the next century, we made sure we were prepared for the year 2000. The millennium issue isn't about bugs - it's about planned obsolescence,' he added.

THE ABERDEEN ARGUS

Aberdeen Group's predictions for the next three years:

Hardware

Intel will pick up more market share as its processors become increasingly powerful. Operating systems based on less popular chipsets will experience end-of-life problems.

Operating systems

HP will dominate the Unix pack with its 64-bit version of Unix. Windows NT will sell well but will not scale well beyond eight processors and will not wipe out Unix, MVS or OS/400.

Netscape will survive if it offers better support to corporate users than Microsoft does.

Databases

Oracle will continue to dominate, even though Informix arguably has the best architecture.

Intranet:

Intranet development will be held up by costs, until users force tools vendors into giving browsers the same importance as the Windows client. Network computer vendors will bundle WordPerfect with their devices after Corel rewrites the application in Java.

Objects

The increased use of objects will be held up by the lack of common methodologies. No single request-broker architecture will dominate the industry.

Networking

Users will move from LANs to intranets, allowing them to centralise their servers and simplify their directory services. Novell will enter a period of managed decline.
