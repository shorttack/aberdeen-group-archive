Upgrades Boost Database Market

 Data mining, Internet connections brighten troubled industry
 By
 John Foley

 Issue date: April 15, 1996

Silicon Graphics is jumping into the emerging market for data mining technology
this week with an initiative that places it in competition with another
recent entrant-IBM. Silicon Graphics is introducing an integrated set of
data mining products, clustering software, and third-party relationships
designed to help users dig valuable information out of databases.

That anno
uncement, and several more by Microsoft, Oracle, Sybase, and other
companies at the DB Expo conference in San Francisco, will be bright spots
for a market starved for good news. Industry-leading database suppliers
saw their stocks drop last week following back-to-back forecasts by Sybase
and Informix that last quarter's sales were lower than expected. Informix's
stock plunged 28% in one day.

"A number of individual situations are adding up to a wrong conclusion,"
says Peter Kastner, an analyst with the Aberdeen Group, a consulting firm
in Boston. "These are all good companies and they will continue to
grow at robust double-digit rates." Wall Street seems to agree. Once
Oracle assured analysts that its business remains strong, database stocks
rebounded.

Among the fastest-growing areas in the database market is data mining, which
lets companies extract information from data warehouses.

Silicon Graphics executives say their company's 64-bit Unix systems and
rich gra
phical capabilities are perfectly suited for data mining. The company's
MineSet product line will include data transformation software, visualization
tools for displaying data, and a console to manage the process.

The new Challenge Array clustering software will support up to eight Silicon
Graphics servers and 288 processors. EDS' dbIntellect unit will provide
systems integration.

"Our technology's sweet spot is dealing with big data," says Ihab
Abu-Hakima, director of marketing with Silicon Graphics' Network Systems
Division.

"They've got the tools. They've got the hardware. And they've got the
integration strategy," says Aaron Zornes, executive VP with the Meta
Group, a technology consulting company in Stamford, Conn. "It is one
of a handful of offerings that has it all. Large companies know this is
very important to them."

Credit specialist TRW's Information Systems and Services unit is an early
user of Silicon Graphics' data mining product
s. "The tools are new
to us and to our customers," says Mark Tinucci, director of database
marketing with TRW's Target Marketing Services in Allen, Texas. "We
find them very rich in identifying the vast amount of information we have."

TRW maintains data warehouses for its clients and expects to generate $15
million this year selling value-added services based on data mining. "We
want to offer the data visualization capability with every piece of data
we deliver to our customers," says Tinucci.

Microsoft will use DB Expo to unveil SQL Server 6.5, an upgrade to its database
management system that will ship this month. Enhancements include support
for distributed transactions across multiple databases, an Internet gateway,
tools for remote administration, and improved support for SAP's R/3 client-server
application suite.

SQL Server 6.5 will also come with improved scalability. Jim Ewel, SQL Server
group product manager, says it will support up to 5,000 us
ers and 200 Gbytes
of data on a single server, compared to 4,000 users and 150 Gbytes in the
current release.

 Filling A Need

Microsoft customers say the new capabilities are needed. The AIM Management
Group in Houston has five SQL Server databases-but recently bought an Oracle
database. "It's mostly being done to get some experience in that environment
in case we run out of horsepower on NT," says Ray Walther, AIM's director
of technology. 

Also at DB Expo: Hewlett-Packard will introduce a version of its Intelligent
Warehouse management software that lets users access a data warehouse using
a Web browser. As part of its OpenWarehouse Web program, HP will also announce
new security software, support services, and integration with Web-ready
data warehousing products from nine other companies.

In addition, as part of its "universal connectivity program,"
Sybase will unveil middleware for Internet connectivity. Oracle will demonstrate
a database text-manag
ement system (
 see related story, p. 75
 ). Also, Software AG
will announce products and services to help companies build small data warehouses,
or data marts.
