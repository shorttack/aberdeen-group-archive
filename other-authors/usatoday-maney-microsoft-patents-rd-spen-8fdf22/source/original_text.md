 Microsoft spends a bunch on patents, but is it worth it? 
In 2003, Microsoft spent about $9.5 million to generate each of its 528 patents.

By that measure, Microsoft brings the New York Yankees philosophy to research and development: shell out more in payroll than anyone else, then lose the World Series to the Florida Marlins, a team that spends one-third as much.

Cost per patent might be a shallow way to measure the effectiveness of a research lab, but then business is full of shallow measurements. As if quarterly earnings, managed to the penny, are an accurate portrayal of a company's success.

Cost per patent doesn't seem to be a number any organization has tracked before. This month, Technology Review magazine is publishing its annual list of top patent winners. I simply pulled up the R&D budgets of the companies on the list and divided the expenditures by the number of patents. 

Perhaps we can call it the Patent Expense Efficiency index, or the PEE.

In the tech industry, Microsoft's PEE doesn't stack up too well. Archrival Sun Microsystems spends about $3.2 million a patent — one-third what Microsoft spends.

By the way, I guess Sun isn't exactly Microsoft's archrival since their truce. Sun and Microsoft now seem more like Kato and Inspector Clouseau in the Pink Panther movies. Sun is the sidekick who's supposed to jump Microsoft from behind once in a while just to keep Microsoft sharp.

Anyway, IBM and Xerox do quite well with their PEE. Both spend just $1.4 million a patent. Hewlett-Packard comes in at about $1.9 million a patent.

And then there's Oracle. Who knows what's going on inside that R&D unit? Daily toga parties? Staff hookah meetings? Oracle is spending $12.6 million per patent, the most of the big tech companies.

Microsoft's case is interesting because it now spends more on its research labs than any other U.S. tech company. It set a goal to create — from scratch, starting in 1991 — one of the world's elite labs, on par with Bell Labs in its heyday.

Microsoft reports earnings Thursday. At such times Microsoft's more than $5 billion in annual R&D spending often gets held up by management as a good thing — a hedge against future competition. 

The cost per patent suggests Microsoft isn't doing so hot. But in other measures, Microsoft Research is doing great. This summer, it will have the highest number of scientific papers at most of the major computer science conferences. These papers tend to be fun reading, such as "Best Practices for Convolutional Neural Networks Applied to Visual Document Analysis," which Microsoft submitted last summer. 

 Technology Review 's patent list features a category called "science linkage," which measures how often an organization's discoveries are cited as "prior art" in other patents. That shows who is pushing the boundaries of computer science. Microsoft Research's score is among the highest in tech and is almost triple IBM's score.

"Microsoft has become the strongest and most active industrial computer science research organization in the world," says Randal Bryant, dean of the computer science school at Carnegie Mellon University. He says that the lab is a front-runner in computer graphics, and that has fed into Microsoft's Xbox game. The Microsoft lab in Cambridge, England, "excels in theoretical aspects of program languages," Bryant says.

"Microsoft gets less respect and visibility for what they are doing in pure research than they deserve," says Peter Kastner of market research firm Aberdeen Group.

Others, though, ask a more practical question: For all the money spent, why hasn't it given Microsoft another butt-kicking product? Some discoveries incrementally improve Microsoft's long-standing moneymakers, mainly Windows and Office. Few of Microsoft's newer efforts, whether the Xbox, tablet PC or MSN, have turned into megahits.

"For all its talk about innovation, Microsoft is not a company that has actually figured out how to create important new products," says Jeffrey Tarter, who runs the industry newsletter Softletter . "Microsoft has gotten very little bang for its R&D bucks."

In the end, though, how can you fault a company for hiring the best minds and giving them everything they need to try to advance the science? We'd all be better off if more companies did that. 

One Microsoft researcher, Jim Gray, is building SkyServer, the world's biggest database of astronomical information. He's doing it to try out new technology for handling vast amounts of information, but SkyServer will be available, via the Web, to anyone doing research on the cosmos.

Does that translate into money for Microsoft? "Historically, when we have done these things, we've found uses for them over time," says Rick Rashid, the energetic head of Microsoft Research. 

Sometimes it's years before someone in a Microsoft product group realizes that a research project could be useful. And sometimes, a research project never proves useful.

Computing legend Gordon Bell now works at Microsoft Research, mostly on a project called MyLifeBits. He's trying to figure out how to store every bit of information you encounter — images, documents, conversations — in a PC. If it can then be organized and easily accessed, it might lead to new kinds of memory enhancement.

As a Microsoft product, perhaps that goes nowhere. Or perhaps it will lead to everyone's brain eventually running on some future version of Windows — Windows Cranium Edition.

"In research, the key thing is you don't know if it's going to work," Rashid says. "If you know it's going to work, you should be in product development. In research, we encourage risk."

Which, for better or worse, explains why Microsoft doesn't have a better PEE.

 Kevin Maney covers technology for USA TODAY. His Technology column appears Wednesdays. E-mail him at: kmaney@usatoday.com
