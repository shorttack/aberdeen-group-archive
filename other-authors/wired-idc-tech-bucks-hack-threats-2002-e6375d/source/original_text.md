IDC: Tech Bucks, Hack Threats Up

 
 
 
 

 
 

 
 

 
 

 

 

 
 

 
 
 
 
 
 
 Elisa Batista
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

 
 

 

 

 

 
 
 
 
 

 

 
 

 

 

 

 
 

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 12.23.02
 
 
 
 

 

 

 
 

 

 

 
 

 
 

 

 

 
 
 

 

 
 
 

 
 

 

 

 
 
 
 
 
 

 
 
 
 

 

 
 
 
 
 There's good news and there's bad news for 2003. 
 
 The good news is corporate technology spending is finally expected to rise. The bad news is that U.S. computer networks are as vulnerable as ever to attack. 
 
 In a series of predictions for the new year, IDC analysts said the economy could expect a boost from an increase in corporate IT spending. But the market research firm also expects a coordinated terrorist attack that will bring the Internet "down to its knees for a day or two" as retaliation against the United States if it goes to war with Iraq. 
 
 "We hope to say at the end of the year that this didn't come to pass," said IDC chief research officer John Gantz. 
 
 Every year, IDC, which employs 720 analysts in 43 countries, makes 10 predictions for the upcoming year. In the six years it has made such forecasts, it has usually gotten seven out of 10 predictions right, Gantz said. 
 
 The company thinks it has a finger on the pulse of what's hot and what's not for 2003, although some of its competitors disagree. A few of its predictions -- such as a rebound in the midrange server market and slow but steady adoption of 64-bit computing -- were standard IT analyst fare. Others, like impending terrorist-hack attacks and the expectation that spam will rise to become 40 percent of all e-mail traffic, have implications far beyond enterprise computing. 
 
 A "cyber-terrorism event" will take out the Internet 
 
IDC fears that a war with Iraq will galvanize hackers to use their skills, perhaps in a coordinated way, to create "economic disruptions" through denial-of-service attacks and even physical attacks on key networks. IDC went as far as to say that such an attack would bring the Internet "down to its knees" for a day or two. 
 
 IDC based this prediction on an Oct. 22 DoS attack against 13 "root servers" that provide the primary roadmap for almost all Internet communications. Although investigators considered it the largest and most sophisticated attack ever against the Internet, users worldwide were largely unaffected. Still, IDC considered the attack a "blueprint" for events to come. 
 
 As more reason for concern, IDC cited a threat made by one hacker, Vladimor "Melhacker" Chamlkovic, who said he planned to release a megavirus on the Internet if Iraq is attacked. 
 
 But analysts from competing firms couldn't help but think that IDC was being a bit paranoid and melodramatic. 
 
 Matt Berk, an analyst with Jupiter Research, said he was more concerned about Internet crime like identity theft than a random attack on a company's data system. He pointed to a recent incident, in which thieves stole approximately 30,000 consumer credit reports and used them to order credit cards and raid bank accounts. 
 
 "All you need is someone's Social Security number and the rest is history," Berk said. "There is so much going on. We pay more attention to viruses and worms and DoS attacks than crime like identity theft." 
 
 The corporate IT market will grow by more than 6 percent 
 
 IDC already got egg on its face for predicting a rebound in the 2002 technology market. In 2003, it's looking at an omelet, competitors said. 
 
 While competing analysts agree that companies will invest more money on information technology and telecommunications gear to replace equipment bought in their efforts to stave off the Y2K bug, they don't agree with IDC on the amount of money companies will spend. 
 
 IDC predicts that IT spending will hit $1.9 trillion next year and grow by more than 6 percent. 
 
 "I don't see it bouncing back to the time of the Internet craze," said Craig Lawton, vice president of market research outfit Boston Consulting Group referring to uncertain economic conditions and a possible war with Iraq. 
 
 Peter Kastner, an analyst for Aberdeen Group, placed the growth rate for IT spending at 3 percent. "Six percent of growth in this economy is totally unsustainable," he said.
