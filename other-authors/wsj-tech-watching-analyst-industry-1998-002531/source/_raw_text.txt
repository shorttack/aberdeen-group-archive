Rising Clout
Along with their rising sales, tech re­
searchers wield rising clout. Corporate
buyers of computer systems, often lacking
confidence in their own knowledge in a
complex area, buy technology watchers’
research to determine which products to
buy. “It’s like buying Consumer Reports
when you go to buy a car,” says Rick
O’Coin, a team leader in Aetna Inc.’s
information-technology' department.
And technology vendors say they can’t
afford not to buy the watchers’ reports, lest
their own goods or services wind up nega­
tively assessed by one of the powerful
analysts at the research firms.
“I buy from all of them," says Marsha
Haugen, market research manager for
Hewlett-Packard Co.’s computer division.'

Dollop of Show Business
Most watchers throw in a dollop of
show business at confabs that charge big
admission prices and line up big names as
speakers. Last year, corporate chief-infor­
mation officers paid as much as S2.500
apiece to see Microsoft Corp. Chairman
Bill Gates and Sun Microsytems Inc.
Chairman Scott McNealy hold forth at
Gartner’s annual conference in Orlando.
“It’s a very attractive business model,”
says Sherri Wolf, an analyst with Adams,
Harkness & Hill in Boston who follows the
industry. “They have 80% recurring reve­
nue, and pretax operating margins of 157e
to 207c.”
The crystal balls of watchers can
sometimes be cloudy. In 1986, a Gartner
analyst predicted that in 10 years four
companies would dominate the computer
universe. Only one, International Business
Machines Corp., remains a player, but the
other three-AT&T Corp., Fujitsu Ltd. and
Hitachi Ltd.-have faded badly.
But computer consumers often find
their ratings indispensable. Outdoors gear
maker L.L. Bean Co. relies on Meta Group
for a broad range of advice, including help
on picking the right telephone call-center
systems for its telemarketers. Robert Pa­
quin, vice president of information serv­
ices at Bean, of Freeport, Maine, says
“you’re buying expertise that it doesn’t
make sense to have full time on staff.”
The watchers don’t actually install
computer and software systems the way
matched what other customers were get­
ting. "They see where the floors and
ceilings are in other contracts, and they
share that," says Mr. Paquin. “Without
that, you would be at the mercy of the
vendors.” He declined to provide details of
the contract.
The U.S. General Accounting Office
spends almost 8100,000 a year with
Gartner, mainly to understand what large
private-sector companies are doing. “They
told us we weren’t spending enough on
hardware and that we were contracting out
too much," says John W. Harmon, chief
information officer for the GAO. One out­
growth was a decision to lease personal
computers on a three-year basis, so that
replacement costs won’t become a capital­
spending item - easily deferred by Con­
gress in yearly budget battles.
“Gartner tells us 407o of corporate PCs
will be leased by 2000," says Mr. Harmon.
But he says GAO has to keep contracting
out work. Civil-service pay grades are too
low for the government to hire its own
sophisticated software programmers.
Not Worth the Money
Some corporate computer experts say
such advice isn’t worth the money. Deon
Babiuk, senior technical analyst at IPL
Energy, a big pipeline firm in Calgary,
Alberta, says that two years ago, he de­
cided to cut Gartner’s service, saving
$60,000 annually. He says he can gamer a
fair amount of what Gartner used to tell
him from the Internet.

The distributed Unix systems wouldn’t

Watchers are also facing growing com­
petition as star analysts leave to set up
their own consulting boutiques. Gartner’s
founder, Gideon Gartner, departed and set
up Giga Group in Cambridge, Mass., in
1995. Despite the added competition, Wil­
liam T. Clifford, president of Gartner,
maintains that growth for the industry is
virtually assured because "technology
business is proliferating at a rate that is
unheard of in modem economic times.”
George Colony, Forrester’s president
says that traditional management consult­
ants like McKinsey & Co. "are only selling
their time-they’re like dentists.” But For­
rester and others can boost profit by
limiting face-time and maximizing pub­
lishing and conference revenue which cost
little for each additional subscriber or
attendee. “There’s real leverage in our
business,” Mr. Colony says.
